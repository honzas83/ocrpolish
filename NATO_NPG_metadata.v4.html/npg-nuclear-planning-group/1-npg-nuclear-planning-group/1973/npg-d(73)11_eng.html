Abstract

NATO Operational Concepts and Doctrine Currently Governing Employment of Nuclear Weapons for Tactical Purposes

This report by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) comments on the operational concepts and doctrine currently governing the employment of nuclear weapons for tactical purposes within the North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO). The document discusses a 1971 meeting in Mittenwald where NPG Ministers discussed unresolved problems related to tactical nuclear defence, including the use of lower yield weapons and the combined use of conventional and nuclear capabilities. It references a basic report prepared by the Supreme Headquarters Allied Powers Europe (SHAPE) and comments from the Military Committee. The report emphasizes that these operational concepts are dynamic and responsive to political and military developments, ensuring flexibility for subordinate commanders. It confirms that current planning is compatible with the specific guidance of the Defence Planning Committee (DPC) and basic NATO strategy. The Military Committee endorses the view that these doctrines provide the necessary flexibility to counter threats to Allied Command Europe (ACE), with similar doctrines applying to the Supreme Allied Commander Atlantic (SACLAN) and the Allied Commander-in-Chief, Channel Command (CINCHAN). The report also addresses the political implications of using nuclear weapons to weaken an enemy's will to continue aggression. Finally, it touches upon the technical characteristics of nuclear weapons, such as delivery methods like PERSHING and POSEIDON, and their differences from conventional munitions.

I. PURPOSE

This report comments on NATO operational concepts and doctrine currently governing employment of nuclear weapons for tactical purposes.

II. BACKGROUND

  1. At their meeting in Mittenwald in May 1971, the NPG Ministers discussed a paper(1) on unresolved problems related to tactical nuclear defence which, among other matters, raised the question of possible improvements in concepts and tactics. In this context, the paper mentioned four factors that might be examined: wider use of lower yield battlefield weapons and their combined use with conventional weapons; the possibility of an early combined use of our conventional and nuclear capabilities as soon as the attack is identified as a major aggression and before our conventional forces have suffered substantial losses, and a constrained but continuing combined use if the aggressor persists in his attack; exploitation of the possible advantage of re-structured and deployed defensive forces against the necessarily more concentrated attacker; and possible reduction of undesirable collateral effects through accurately delivered lower yield and/or fusion weapons and the way of their employment. In a later report containing suggestions for investigating the unresolved problems, the Permanent Representatives expressed the view(2) that the NATO Military Authorities might be requested to provide information on existing concepts and doctrine. The NPG Ministers agreed with this suggestion and invited(3) the NATO Military Authorities to prepare a report outlining operational

III. DISCUSSION

General Comment

  1. The SHAPE Report provides information on the operational concepts and doctrine which govern current planning for tactical employment of nuclear weapons within Allied Command Europe (ACE), and which would apply to ACE operations in time of war. The Report identifies the broad political guidance on which the ACE operational concepts and doctrine are based; outlines the general and specific military considerations that are relevant to the development of such concepts and doctrine; and describes these operational concepts and doctrine in considerable detail. In the Report, SHAPE emphasises(3) the dynamic nature of the concepts and doctrine, pointing out that they have evolved over a period of years and that they are kept under continuing review in order to be responsive to political and military developments. They are intended to be detailed enough to provide the necessary direction for effective planning and operations by subordinate commanders without, however, restricting the flexibility of future military operations through rigid contingency planning.

  2. The SHAPE Report observes(4) that the concepts and doctrine are consistent with guidance provided by political and military authorities in NATO. Although the Military Committee does not comment specifically on this point, it has on several past occasions reported(5) that military planning by the Major NATO Commanders for the employment of nuclear weapons conforms

Our examination of the SHAPE Report leads us to concur, subject to clarifying comments later in this report, that the current concepts and doctrine are compatible with the specific DPC guidance on nuclear planning(1). We also consider that they are in line with broader DPC political guidance(2) and basic NATO strategy(3). At the same time, we fully recognise the importance of reviewing and updating the concepts and doctrine to take account not only of refinements in guidance provided by higher authority but also of changes in the nature of the threat or in NATO’s posture that might be brought about by such factors as modifications of force structure, deployment changes and introduction of improved weaponry.

  1. The Military Committee endorses(4) the SACEUR view that the concepts and doctrine outlined in the SHAPE Report provide NATO with the necessary flexibility to respond to potential enemy aggression, and also provide SACEUR and his subordinate commanders with the means to carry out military planning to counter the threat to Allied Command Europe. The other Major NATO Commanders, Supreme Allied Commander Atlantic (SACLAN) and Allied Commander-in-Chief, Channel Command (CINCHAN), have stated that the operational concepts and doctrine currently governing the employment of nuclear weapons for tactical purposes in their respective commands are essentially the same as those set forth in the SHAPE Report and that a suitable basis exists for SACLAN and CINCHAN and their subordinate commanders to carry out military planning to counter the threat to their areas of responsibility. In this context, we note that SACLAN has prepared several documents(5) for the NP Ministers in the past that have discussed the operational concepts and doctrine pertaining to the employment of nuclear weapons for tactical purposes in the maritime environment in considerable detail.

  2. The Military Committee draws attention(6) to one aspect of the possible effects of the use of nuclear weapons in the tactical rôle, namely the objective of weakening the enemy’s will to continue aggression, a point that is covered in the SHAPE Report as well(7).   1. Authority to request nuclear weapon release. The SHAPE Report states(1) that SACEUR's decision to request nuclear weapon release could be based, among other things, on requests of governments. It is, of course, clear that governments always have full freedom to suggest to SACEUR that he make a nuclear weapon release request. It is equally clear that SACEUR would not only have the right to take such governmental views into account in reaching his decision but would be expected to do so. On the other hand, mention of this possibility in the SHAPE Report should not be viewed as any infringement on the option of a government to communicate a nuclear weapon release request direct to the Council/DPC and member governments, as provided for in the General Guidelines for Consultation Procedure on the Use of Nuclear Weapons(2). If a government originates such a release request, it would, of course, ensure that the message was sent simultaneously to the Major NATO Commanders in order that their views and assessments could be made available to the Council/DPC and capitals as soon as possible(3).

  2. Targets. Reference is made(4) in the SHAPE Report to the broad geographical area in which fixed targets have been identified by SHAPE in connection with the targeting of ACE Theatre Strike Forces and to the possibility that some of these fixed targets might be struck with nuclear weapons for tactical purposes. In the light of existing political guidance which places restraints on making strikes on Soviet strategic nuclear forces and targets within Soviet territory itself under selective release conditions(5), it is evident that not all fixed targets within the broad geographical area mentioned in the SHAPE Report would be suitable tactical targets.

Since ADMS are nuclear devices normally used for demolition purposes or to create obstacles and their employment is, therefore, different from nuclear weapons that are used to attack enemy forces on the battlefield, they are not mentioned specifically in the SHAPE examination of the tactical employment of nuclear weapons in the land battle(2). SHAPE use of the term "land battle targets" in this examination refers primarily to enemy targets that are mobile in nature and thus extremely difficult to define and detect, such as combat and service units, field logistic facilities, command and control facilities, and tactical nuclear delivery systems. We agree that this distinction between ADMS and battlefield nuclear weapons is a valid one, and note that it is reflected in the separate issuance of Special Political Guidelines for the Possible Use of Atomic Demolition Munitions(3). Consequently, the omission of ADMS from the discussion of land battle operations does not imply that they are not covered by the operational concepts and doctrine outlined in the SHAPE Report. On the contrary, we have been advised by SHAPE that these operational concepts and doctrine apply wherever appropriate, to the tactical employment of ADMS.

  1. Conditions for Selective Employment. In its discussion of operational doctrine, the SHAPE Report lists(4) a number of situations in which it is visualised that nuclear weapons might be employed for tactical purposes.
