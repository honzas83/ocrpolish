> [!abstract] 
> # Report on Phase II of the Work Programme on Follow-on Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons
> 
> This document serves as a corrigendum to the Working Paper MFG(Staff Group)WP(75)5 (2nd Revision), replacing paragraphs 7 through 14 with new content. The report emphasizes that nuclear weapons are complementary to conventional forces and cannot replace them, highlighting that the strength of NATO conventional forces directly influences the threshold for tactical nuclear employment. It discusses the political and military objectives of follow-on use, stating that the ultimate goal is to persuade an aggressor to cease aggression and withdraw. The text notes that the if initial nuclear use fails to induce a political decision to stop the attack, NATO will inevitably have to take follow-on measures. It further argues that the scale of nuclear use is critical for military effectiveness and for ensuring that political objectives are achieved, as insufficient scale may be perceived as a lack of resolve. The report also mentions that the relative capabilities in weaponry, target acquisition, and command and control are important variables affecting effectiveness. Finally, it notes that the the Phase II Study Team did not define upper limits for follow-on use or examine the relationship between theatre options and limited strategic nuclear strikes.

Conventional Defence and Use of Nuclear Weapons

8. A principal conclusion in the Phase II Study Team Report is(1) that nuclear
weapons are complementary to conventional forces and cannot replace them. This
conclusion, which we endorse, supports current NATO defence concepts(2), and
emphasises the importance of maintaining adequate conventional forces capable of
supporting and exploiting nuclear operations(3). We believe it is important to
recognise that the greater the capability of NATO conventional forces to defend
against an attack, the higher the threshold for the tactic employment of nuclear
weapons by NATO. Similarly, we agree with the Phase II Study Team Report that
conventional forces have important roles to play at every stage of a conflict,
notably conventional defence, and exploitation operations in support of nuclear
operations; they are complementary to nuclear weapons and cannot be replaced by

14. Timing in Follow-on Use. The Phase II Study Team Report states that the
timing for follow-on use would depend upon a combination of military and
political objectives which are ultimately inseparable(1). However, it recognises
that, in narrow terms, political objectives might, in certain circumstances, be
in conflict with military requirements for an early follow-on use, and that
since actual situations cannot be pre-determined(2), only very general guidance
could be given. Such guidance could include consideration of a number of factors
such as:

(a) the need to give the enemy time to perceive NATO political aims balanced
against possible military disadvantages of deliberately introducing delays
between successive nuclear operations(3):

(b) the agreed principle that the use of nuclear weapons should not take place
so late that NATO conventional forces had been already exhausted and would
therefore no longer be in a position to exploit the effects of the use of
nuclear weapons,

(c) the necessity of preserving military effectiveness by timely use where
fleeting targets must be attacked.
