> # Articles on Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons
> 
> This document consists of a notice from the Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) and an attached article by Robert M. Lawrence titled 'On Tactical Nuclear War'. The Chairman, H.B. Seim, notes that the articles are for background purposes and do not necessarily reflect the official positions of the International Staff or the authors' respective nations. The first article by Robert M. Lawrence argues that while strategic nuclear war is widely discussed, tactical nuclear war—the use of nuclear weapons for limited tactical military purposes—has been largely ignored or dismissed as unthinkable. Lawrence examines the failure of conventional military power in Vietnam and suggests that the United States should reconsider its military options to include a credible tactical nuclear capability to maintain international commitments. He critiques the policies of the 1960s, specifically the Kennedy Administration's focus on conventional warfare capabilities to avoid the binary choice between humiliation and total nuclear war. Lawrence contends that the middle ground between conventional and strategic nuclear responses is vacant, leaving the United States with restricted military options in the event of limited aggression.

ON TACTICAL NUCLEAR WAR
Robert M. Lawrence
Although deemed unthinkable by many, strategic nuclear. war has been much
discussed and has stirred considerable political debate, to arouse a need and
demand that all are willing to address. We have felt no constraint in discussing
conventional. war. We have long fought such wars and are now in the throes of
our third major conventional weapon struggle in the last twenty-five years. At
least until misgivings about Vietnam reached recent proportions, the news media
chronicled victories and publicized courageous acts of our fighting men as if
war fought with conventional weapons was a normal, although regrettable aspect
of international relations in a nation state system. Within the last several
years, however, there has been one possible kind of war that has been virtually
impossible to discuss publicly in any reasoned and coherent manner. This is
tactical nuclear war, the use of nuclear weapons for limited tactical military
purposes, a subject that has taken on an almost leprous appearance and seems
essentially unable to stir intellectual curiosity, let alone serious
consideration by students, pundits, or policy makers. Conventional warfare
received a masterful analysis, inter alia, by Karl von Clausewitz (On War).
Modern strategic warfare has been dissected in Herman Kahn's tome (On
Thermnuclear War). But tactical nuclear war has either been declared unthinkable
and infeasible, in the broadest sweeping terms, or has been distorted-out of all
realistic proportion by those who apparently seek only to rationalize their own
existing conclusions. Are we, because of the unpleasant experience of Vietnam,
going to go through a resdressment of our commitments and leave much of the
world to resolve its own defense problems? Or is it possible that we might
decide to maintain important commitments by reaching, should careful
deliberation suggest it, for a military option that we thus far have elected to
deny ourselves, a credible tactical nuclear capability. Despite a seemingly
overwhelming preponderance of conventional military power the United States was
unable in Vietnam to deter the heavy involvement of North Vietnam, to control
the war, or to bring about a satisfactory military

The policies of the 1960's
The Defense policies inaugurated in 1961 by the new Kennedy Administration
rested upon the theory advanced in public that the United States needed
additional military options to provide, in the late President's words, "a wider
choice than humiliation or allot nuclear action." The core of these new policies
was modernization and expansion of conventional warfare capabilities to deal
with a balance of global military power that had changed drastically during the
Eisenhower years.

The logic of Kennedy's statement was unassailable then, and remains so today.
But the Kennedy Administration's conceptualization of the matter neglected one
very significant option, and actions taken to implement the policy statement
have not achieved expected results. Increases in conventional forces raised the
defense budget by some ten billion dollars before any sort of involvement in
Vietnam, and costs would have been even greater had we lived fully up to our
conventional force self-commitments. Since the Vietnam war reached its present
proportions, the defense budget has increased far more to become approximately
twice that of 1961. But all this outlay has not brought the full spectrum of
flexibilities once described as essential to national security.

The new theory was that the threat of responding to limited challenges with
conventional weapons is a credible threat to the aggressor. - much more so at
any rate than a threat to oppose him with nuclear weapons. Therefore, it was
felt that a strategy calling for the use of conventional force makes deterrence
of lower level conflict stronger and more certain. Moreover, conventional
weapons were held to be the "proper" defense against limited military
challenges. Aggression with conventional weapons should be countered with
conventional arms. The use of any nuclear weapons would be an unwarranted over-
response, one sure to bring with it great destruction ("collateral damage"), a
high risk of nuclear escalation, and critical political costs.

Acting on this theory the U.S. has sought to deal with limited, localized
military conflict by using approximately the same level and type of force as
those the enemy threat or use, which is to say with conventional weapons only.
We have been told repeatedly that this is the wisest policy, not only because it
is the safest, but also because it is the most effective, both for deterring
limited war and for defending against whatever limited aggression still might
take place. It has done neither when subjected to major trials.

Rather than providing us with the flexible response promised, our flexibility in
military options has been more restricted, – unnecessarily so. Between a
conventional force response which has not been effective or efficient in Vietnam
or politically successful in the U.S. and abroad, and a strategic nuclear
response which we dare not use unless directly attacked, we have been left with
little practical choice of options. The middle ground is vacant. The tactical
nuclear posture that should occupy this middle ground is not militarily or
politically credible, is not technically up to date, and is therefore not
useful, save perhaps for "demonstration" or for very destructive purposes as a
last desperation resort. In many ways today's military posture seems less
effective than that left by the Eisenhower Administration. Despite a huge
increase in defense expenditures we have experienced a major war in Vietnam,
which failed to yield positive results after large applications of conventional
firepower. The Eisenhower strategy would probably have precluded a solely
conventional war of this magnitude. We have achieved nothing positive in NATO by
our drive to increase conventional options and place reliance on them. That
strategy has contributed substantially to the defection of France and other
dissatisfactions in the Alliance. We may have, despite the increased defense
expenditures, fallen dangerously behind the Soviet Union in technological
progress relating to the strategic military balance. The U.S. strategic
superiority that previously existed has been lost. The coveted flexibility of
response has not materialized. To many observers danger of reliance upon assured
destruction has increased yearly with steady increases in Russian strategic
power. The past optimism of policy makers that a conventional force strategy
would lead to a revitalized NATO, a quick solution to military threats in
Southeast Asia and a more credible and influential American presence globally
has emerged as a serious misjudgment.

One major reason for this retrogression is our deliberate choice to forego the
benefits of technological advances in the field of limited nuclear war. We
cannot do this without paying a significant price.

Rational for achievement of further tactical nuclear weapon development
Twelve years ago, in a somewhat different intellectual climate, a well-received
book on limited war states that "nothing inherent in the wide range of atomic
weapons renders them incompatible with limited war." The author pointed out a
particular need:

"In considering the requirements of a flexible military establishment, the role
of tactical nuclear weapons looms large; for the adaptation of these weapons to
limited war is probably the most crucial problem of weapons and target
limitation that American strategists face today(1)."

After twelve years this statement is not only still valid, but more pertinent
than ever. Despite almost radical technological advances which now make possible
highly discriminate and adaptable nuclear weapons, very little has been done
about their stockpiling, adaptation to limited war, or integration into our
military postures. Indeed, the laboratory technological advances have gone on
despite lack of encouragement and even outright opposition by Department of
State and Defense policy makers. There is not a single truly new weapon in the
current makers. There has been a total refusal to incorporate the advanced
technology which will be described below. This leads to the conclusion that the
Department of Defense has neglected to modernize our tactical nuclear capability
for what must be solely reasons of policy or psychological aversion.

Such an accusation surfaced publicly in the summer of 1968, when one
knowledgable Congressman, Craig Hosmer, a veteran member of the Joint Committee
on Atomic Energy and also a member of its military sub-committee, wrote as
follows to Secretary of Defense Clark Clifford:

"Grave doubts can be expressed regarding the political credibility of our
current tactical stockpile. There is not a single weapon in it for which a
decision was made after 1960. I am also not aware of any weapon in the tactical
nuclear area which has a possibility of being turned over to the military forces
before 1972."

(1) Robert B. Osgood, Limited War : A Challenge to American Strategy (Chicago
University Press, 1957), pp. 248-251.

Certainly, our present tactical stockpile is immense. There are thousands of

nuclear arsenal designed for the most effective military uses should they ever
be required. But in fact, almost the converse is true. Underlying the
composition and positioning of our present nuclear weapons has been a doctrine
based upon hope and expectation that they will never be used. Although we have
this huge nuclear force, we have not really provided a credible means to use it.
As a result, we have seriously limited our ability to fight a reasonable
tactical war anywhere above the nuclear threshold. This self-imposed limitation
seriously erodes the political credibility and deterrent capability of U.S.
tactical nuclear forces.

How does one account for this state of affairs?
One simple fact: that thinking about nuclear weapons hardened early and still
stubbornly resists change. The image of the Hiroshima and Nagasaki bombs and
subsequent emphasis on strategic bombing concealed our thinking at those points.

In the first years of the atomic bomb, the defense establishment was in the main
indifferent to the cause of non-strategic weapon developments. Requirements were
shaped primarily around strategic bombing needs. This was not conducive to free
exploration of potentialities that might lie in other areas of nuclear
application. In such an environment it was difficult to achieve or even
visualize a properly balanced effort. The problem took on the appearance of a
contest between strategic nuclear and conventional weapon factions. The
strategic faction won easily and so the general trend in nuclear weapons
development was toward greater and greater yields.

In 1951 nuclear tests with tactical implications were conducted at lower yields,
but were soon overshadowed by dramatic implications of the "thermonuclear
breakthrough" for strategic weapons which happened about that same time. By the
middle 1950's, lower yield (but hardly low yield) nuclear weapons had been
produced for tactical purposes and assigned to military units. There was,
however, no clear doctrine for their employment, and their use was not really
visualized apart from that of strategic nuclear forces. Massive nuclear
retaliation remained the dominant factor in our defense policy.

What is very important here, and what accounts for much of the current
stultified thinking about tactical nuclear weapons, was an accident of history.
In 1953, the Eisenhower Administration began to form its defense policy
primarily around a nuclear posture, including so-called tactical nuclear
weapons, but at this time, the technical means for this reliance did not yet
exist. By the time opposition to a nuclear emphasis (which began growing toward
the end of the 1950's) took form in a narrow and pronounced conventional
emphasis the technical means for a tactical nuclear doctrine did indeed exist.
But prevailing opposition sentiment was then such as to ignore these new
technological potentials. It still is.

During the decade of the 1950's, we had no weapons that could today reasonably
be called 'tactical nuclear weapons'; we had only nuclear weapons designated for
tactical use. Their yields were large (for tactical land combat purposes), they
derived their energy totally or primarily from fissioning uranium and plutonium,
which involved the release of vast amounts of long-lived radio-activity, and
their effects lacked precision and flexibility. It was difficult to distinguish
them from 'strategic' weapons and none were really suitable for a limited NATO

The escalation arguments, as they have been generally advanced, have remained

therefore assume that these arguments reflect reasoning to prove conclusions
rather than to reach them.

II
Tactical Nuclear Weapons Defined

What is a tactical nuclear weapon? In terms of our current tactical stockpile,
according to the testimony of former Secretary of Defense McNamara, the average
weapon has a destructive power greater than that of all the bombs developed for
the Strategic Air Command during the first years of its existence. They are then
under such a definition, merely existence. To other nuclear weapons assigned to
non-strategic roles. To other observers, tactical nuclear weapons mean those of
such low-yield that they cannot be classified in the strategic category. On the
other hand, some individuals deny that there can be any tactical nuclear weapon.
They hold that the use of any nuclear weapon in tactical warfare will quickly
and inevitably lead to all-out thermonuclear war. However, there is another
approach to the definition of tactical nuclear weapons based on two
qualifications for them that seem to have been given scant attention in most
debates on this subject.

The first is the qualification that the military tactician chooses to apply to
all armaments, nuclear included. The tactician, who must wrestle with the
problem of actually fighting the battle or war, defines a tactical weapon as one
designed to permit him to conduct military operations in line with the greatest
military effectiveness. And, in sharp rebuttal to a widely held view that the
most effective bomb is the most powerful bomb, a careful analysis of this
definition shows that for most applications the most effective tactical
explosive yields tend to be at the very low end of the spectrum. In this sense,
a huge disparity now exists between the warheads that apparently have been
stockpiled and those that would be best suited for the job. Unless current
policies change, this disparity will remain, and may even increase.

The second is a qualification that our policy-makers should have demanded, but
have not. This is the need for weapons that have a larger and more realistic
measure of political credibility both domestically, and abroad. Past stockpiling
decisions have worked toward political incredibility. What is even more
remarkable is that it appears to have been official US policy during recent
years to stress this incredibility to ourselves and to our allies in the hope,
apparently of gaining a more widespread acceptance of the credibility of our
preferred conventional weapon policies. (Yet, the failure to convince our NATO
allies of the need for expanded conventional forces has left us with what we
had, but hardly relished, back in 1961 – namely, massive retaliation.)

In the cited letter to the Secretary of Defense, Congressmen Hosmer clearly
indicated that the Atomic Energy Commission has successfully conducted large
numbers of significant experiments relating to the achievement of tactical
nuclear weapons, whose characteristics conform to the two descriptions just
advanced, but that there has been no policy decision by the Defense Department

requirements and incentive to the AFC research scientists go hand-in-hand. Had
such requirements been established in the past, today's tactical stockpile could
have been considerably more enriched with such weapons than it is.

As a result of the apparent neglect of this technological progress, the weapons
that now comprise our tactical stockpile represent, in effect, improvements and
refinements of 'the bombs that were dropped on Japan twenty seven years ago.
What has taken place during the past score of years is a huge and admittedly
impressive diversification. But it appears that the intrinsic nature of these
weapons and their effects has remained essentially constant, and it is argued
objectively. Quantitatively, this progress has spelled out gains of the first
magnitude. Qualitatively, however, our tactical arsenal of today symbolizes only
variations on a central theme that has been with us since the first nuclear
explosion took place.

From 1946 until 1958, when the nuclear test moratorium began, nuclear weapon
development that related most meaningfully to tactical application was that of
fission weapon improvement. During this period devices using uranium and
plutonium were tested and weaponized with progress toward reduced yields, sizes,
weights, and costs, but they remained primarily fission weapons. Tests that have
occurred since the moratorium ended (1961), which could have changed this
complexion significantly, have not had any real impacts toward altering the
nature of this stockpile. Tactical use of nuclear weapons is still tied
essentially to large field (multiple-kiloton range) fission bombs.

In general the current US stockpile represents a mode of nuclear warfare, the
meaning, consequences, and credibility of which is open to serious question. It
is hardly necessary to describe quantitatively the destructive and radiological
effect of explosions on the order of the Hiroshima level, or higher. Even at
much lower yield levels, e.g., on the order of a kiloton, the effects of fission
weapons are still indicative of a destructive and radiologically contaminating
warfare whose costs could outweih the political goals of the struggle in which
they were used.

Conclusions

It is relatively easy to agree on the requirement for an American defense
posture politically and strategically necessary to support US and allied
interests. Below the strategic force requirements, which are not the subject of
this article, it can be further agreed that effective local forces are important
for deterrence; that the ability to defend, to deny limited objectives, and to
respond to a wide range of threats is a necessary complement to the ability to
threaten credibly massive nuclear punishment; that deterrence by the ability to
defend locally depends upon the deployment of nuclear weapons overseas; that an
assortment of defensive and tactically capable nuclear weapons is necessary. The
priority to be given to these nuclear weapons, their precise role in our
defenses, the conditions for their use, and the type of weapon they should be
are not matters of easy agreement. These questions cannot be fully answered
here. Certain possibilities and requirements can be mentioned with the hope of
provoking a more openminded, informed dialogue that will promote more reasonable
consideration of tactical nuclear weapons.

It is important that this be done immediately. Too many changes are
occurring—technologically, politically, militarily—that demand this. The
respective roles of conventional forces and improved tactical nuclear forces
need to be reassessed. Certainly, we should continue to develop and maintain
capabilities for fighting conventional wars. One can anticipate many
requirements both political and military for these capabilities. However, by no
means is it clear that this uncontestable necessity will be accompanied by a
satisfactory solution for our future tactical warfare problems. And in a world
where major powers are accelerating military technological developments, and
minor powers have shown an ability to frustrate US conventional capabilities,
the magnitude of our current insufficiency will become increasingly obvious.

TACTICAL NUCLEAR WEAPONS FOR CENTRAL EUROPE?
General Heinz Tretter-(ret'd.)
The use of nuclear energy for the manufacture of weapons has completely changed
political and military thinking. The first nuclear weapons were strategic ones.
Their employment – the moral justification for which is still being debated –
was so overwhelmingly successful that it was perfectly natural to wonder whether
atomic energy could not equally be used for conflicts on a smaller scale. The
question was whether it was technically possible to reduce not only the size and
weight but also the effects of this new weapon, and thus make it suitable for
tactical use.

The development of these weapons therefore followed the opposite process to that
of conventional weapons. For the latter, the aim was to increase the effect and
reduce the weight, whereas, for nuclear weapons, it was necessary to harness the
power of the explosion and adapt it for a limited objective. If this could be
done, the use of nuclear energy might save human effort on the battlefield and
perhaps even put an end to the conventional arms race. What was needed was a
tactical nuclear weapon capable of bringing any military conflict to a speedy
and victorious conclusion, even when used in small numbers, unlike conventional
weapons, which do not produce an overwhelming effect and must therefore be
constantly improved.

This new technical problem was resolved remarkably quickly – at great expense,
it is true. By the fifties the United States already possessed tactical nuclear
weapons, both for its own units and for those of its Allies, as well as suitable
vectors in the form of aircraft, missiles and artillery. At that time, the
importance of these arms was to all intents and purposes incalculable. Thanks to
them, it appeared possible to hold much larger Soviet forces in check, while the
aggressor could be overwhelmed in his own country by strategic nuclear weapons.
This concept was simple and clear. It was based on the United States nuclear
monopoly, which still actually existed at the time but was beginning to
disappear. Nuclear warfare was considered to form a whole. Tactical weapons had
the double advantage over strategic ones of being more accurate and less
destructive. These advantages were sought after because tactical operations
would be carried out on the territory of the Allies, or of the satellite
countries, and it was desirable to spare them as much as possible. Technical
progress made it possible to carry out a nuclear war with minimal risk to the
non-combatant population.

II

Meanwhile, the Russians' have brought about a state of balance in the nuclear
armament field. The American monopoly has been nullified. The strategy of
massive retaliation based on this monopoly had to be abandoned, much to the
regret of the Europeans, for whom it had provided almost total security.
However, the incontestable leading power in the Alliance has obliged them to
adapt themselves to the new circumstances and to the concepts of the United
States high command. The idea of the flexible response has replaced that of
massive retaliation. The principle still remains that under no circumstances
will the West begin a war, but henceforth an attack will no longer be met by the
immediate destruction of the aggressor but by a flexible response. From the
American standpoint, this modification of the concept was essential, but at the
same time it was a serious weakening of the deterrent. The attacker, instead of
knowing he will be destroyed, must henceforward remain in doubt concerning the
countermeasures to be expected. As the Soviet strategic nuclear potential has
become largely invulnerable, so that a riposte against the territory of the
United States is always possible, an immediate nuclear broadside by the West in
answer to an attack is no longer credible. The Soviets, however, are armed
against any other kind of response.

The insecurity of the West has therefore increased. Weapon systems have
exchanged roles: nuclear weapons have become the Shield, and conventional
weapons have again become the Sword, of the Atlantic Alliance. At the same time,
the importance of tactical weapons, which hitherto merely supplemented the heavy
nuclear broadside, has also changed.

Edward Teller, the inventor of the hydrogen bomb, was of the opinion that a war
could not be limited in terms of weapons but only as regards territories and
aims. He therefore suggested that tactical weapons should be used even in a
limited war. After being defeated on the battlefield, an aggressor would no
longer wish to extend either the theatre of operations or his war aims. Teller
wanted atomic weapons to be used to halt the Russians, whose conventional
strength is greater. He thought that the West, whose conventional forces are
smaller, had no intention of widening the conflict in any case, nor would the
Russians want to do so after a tactical defeat by nuclear weapons.

To avoid a general war, other American experts recommended slow and carefully
measured escalation which, combined with political measures, would damp down the
flames.

The results of these studies, although made independently, are therefore similar

weapons alone, even on a large scale, gives the defender no advantage. He can
indeed inflict on the attacker, who is obliged to concentrate his forces,
greater absolute losses than his own, but the overall ratio between strengths
will be unfavourable to him. It is obviously possible to cause a local slowing-

first echelon conventional forces will very quickly be exhausted, so that not

conventional weapons. The West, which does not have any second or third echelon

weapons, with the same yields, and that the aggressor will play the game and use
only low-power weapons. This, however, is all the more unrealistic since the
Soviets do not possess small sophisticated atomic weapons and have no reason to
restrict the damage to enemy territory to the minimum. If they respond to the
use of small weapons by employing larger ones, the calculations show that the
defender will be at a still greater disadvantage.

Account must also be taken of political considerations. In a highly developed
territory such as Central Europe, there are so many roads that it would only be
possible to stop all attacks by using a relatively large number of atomic
weapons. There are enough of them in Europe, but if they were used, this highly-
populated area would be transformed into a desert and intolerable losses would
be inflicted on the population. To defend our Continent along these lines would
be to annihilate it, for tactical atomic weapons, in addition to their doubtful
military effect, would have a much greater effect on civilian targets, such as
towns, centres of production, dwellings, centres of supply and communications.
The position of the defender, obliged to fight in his own country, is therefore
completely different from that of the attacker. Apart from military losses
caused by atomic warfare, he will have to suffer inconceivable civilian damage,
which might easily reach such proportions as to cause the complete breakdown of
all political and military order.
