> # Study on Warsaw Pact Politico-Military Strategy and Military Doctrine for the Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons
> 
> This document is a draft report by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) regarding a study on the Warsaw Pact's politico-military strategy and military doctrine for the tactical use of nuclear weapons. The report was initiated following a May 1973 meeting in Ankara where Ministers noted the risks of escalating conflict if NATO were to use nuclear weapons. The study was developed by an ad hoc Study Team comprising representatives from Canada, Denmark, the Federal Republic of Germany, the Netherlands, the Netherlands, the United Kingdom, and the United States, with assistance from the International Staff, International Military Staff, and SHAPE. The report recommends that the Ministers take note of the study, discuss its issues, and forward the report to the Defence Planning Committee (DPC) subject to endorsement by the Nuclear Defence Affairs Committee. It emphasizes that while the Warsaw Pact does not subscribe to a formal strategy of graduated response, it possesses the capability for a wide range of nuclear options, including pre-emptive use or wide-scale retaliation. The document argues that NATO decisions should not be based on a single assumption of Warsaw Pact response and should consider unquantifiable factors such as motivation and political attitudes. Finally, it asserts that the conclusions of the study do not call into question the current NATO strategy or arrangements for nuclear consultation.

Draft Report by the Permanent Representatives
During their consideration of the ACLANT Follow-on Use Study(1) at their meeting
in Ankara in May, 1973, the Ministers noted that political authorities of the
Alliance, in consulting on a selective release request, would have to give very
serious consideration to the Warsaw Pact (WP) response that such use might
provoke and to the risk of escalating the conflict that such use entails. In
this context, they also noted the suggestion(2) in the ACLANT Study that some
insight on this question might be gained in peacetime by assessing the actual
capabilities that the WP possesses and by seeking as clear a knowledge and
understanding as possible of current WP politico-military strategy and military
doctrine which is likely to govern WP tactical use of nuclear weapons. The
Ministers therefore invited(3) the Permanent Representatives to arrange for a
study on this subject to be prepared for their consideration at a future
meeting.

III. COMMENTS
General Evaluation
3. In developing its analysis the Study draws heavily on the assessment of WP
military capabilities and the summary of the strategic military considerations
affecting Soviet decision-making with regard to use of military power that are
contained in the current agreed NATO military intelligence base(1).
Nevertheless, to arrive at a deeper understanding of WP politico-military
strategy, the Study attempted to go beyond the agreed intelligence assessment to
consider the motivation, political attitudes and possible intentions which might
influence Soviet and WP conduct and actions in an actual crisis, and in
particular the possible impact these unquantifiable factors might have on WP
tactical use of nuclear weapons(2). While fully recognizing the need and value
of the NATO agreed military intelligence base, we agree with the Study that an
examination of these unquantifiable factors is worthwhile. We think the Study
makes a significant and useful contribution in this respect.

predict the course of action the WP might take in an actual conflict,

the WP does not subscribe to a formal strategy of graduated nuclear response(2).
On the other hand, the Study concludes from evidence in the NATO agreed
intelligence base that, although the WP does not have the same range of delivery

conflicting) influences that will have critical impact on WP politico-military

compatible with Communist revolutionary aims, and the defensive attitude against
a perceived Western threat(2). Furthermore, the Study emphasizes the difficulty
of divining whether the WP peacetime policy declarations stressing the defensive
nature of the WP are just a cover for aggressive intent, or whether the WP

9. To prevent deliberate WP aggression, the deterrent stance maintained by NATO
in peacetime must convince the WP that NATO has the means to exact an
intolerable price and that the risk that NATO would be willing to employ these
means in its defence is so great as to be unacceptable from a WP viewpoint. Our
deterrent military posture must be both visible and credible. Furthermore, to
present a credible counter to the range of possible military options open to the
WP, NATO forces must have versatile capabilities that, in WP eyes, provide NATO
with feasible responses to meet the possible variety of WP challenges, including
in-being defensive forces that can oppose an attack and deny an aggressor the
fruits of his aggression, and retaliatory forces that can punish him
unacceptably for his aggressive act.

conflict has begun, particularly if the initial fighting is at a relatively low
