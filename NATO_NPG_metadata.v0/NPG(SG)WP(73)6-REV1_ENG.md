> # Consultation Procedures and Facilities: Preliminary Observations on WINTEX 73
> 
> This draft report by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) outlines preliminary observations on the nuclear consultation aspects of the NATO-wide Command Post Exercise WINTEX 73. The exercise, which took place from February 7 to March 16, 1973, was divided into two phases: FIRMSAND and BROKEN PLEDGE. The report focuses on the FIRMSAND phase, which simulated a pre-hostilities crisis period, the outbreak of hostilities, and a conventional warfare phase culminating in a request for the selective release of nuclear weapons for tactical use. It notes that the Defence Planning Committee (DPC) participated actively for a 12-hour period, while other times were represented by a response cell. The document highlights that while artificialities were inherent in the exercise, the experience provided insight into the problems of current interest regarding political consultation in time of war. Specifically, it discusses the timely provision of information to political authorities, noting that SACEUR's additional measures and existing procedures effectively met the needs of political authorities during the exercise. The report also mentions thees use of National Military Representatives (NMR) as a direct link between SHAPE and Ministries of Defence in capitals. The report is a revision of a possible Permanent Representatives' report reflecting Staff Group discussions.

Exercise Outline
3. WINTEX 73 was a large-scale NATO-wide Command Post Exercise involving most
NATO capitols, NATO Headquarters in Brussels and all of the Major NATO
Commanders as well as many of their subordinate commands. The active period of
WINTEX 73 extended from 7th March, 1973 to 16th March, 1973 and was preceded by
a preparatory and pre-exercise period which began on 7th February, 1973. The
active period was divided into two phases: the first lasted approximately 6 days
from 7th March to 13th March and was called FIRMSAND; the second, designated
BROKEN PLEDGE, extended an additional 3 days to the termination of the exercise
on 16th March. Active involvement of NATO Headquarters in Brussels, as well as
most of the political

The scenario in FIRMSAND covered the final stages of the pre-hostilities crisis

Participation by Political Authorities

4. The Defence Planning Committee participated actively during a 12-hour period
on 12th March; at other times during FIRMSAND the Defence Planning Committee was
represented by a directing staff/response cell manned by personnel from the
International Staff. The degree of participation in capitals varied, ranging
from actual play by the responsible officials in Ministries to representation by
exercise teams. National authorities in France and Iceland did not participate.

Exercise Limitations

5. As is the case in most exercises, there were various inherent and planned
artificialities in WINTEX 73 that place practical limits on exercise experience
as a reflection of what might happen in a real situation. For example, to
accommodate live play by political authorities into such an exercise
necessitates establishing well-defined parameters within which the consultation
process can function. Thus, with respect to the nuclear consultation aspects of
WINTEX 73, the exercise arrangements stipulated that, during the period of
active participation by the Defence Planning Committee (which began at 1100
Brussels time on 12th March), only SACEUR would request the release of nuclear
weapons and that only one such request would be played. However, it was agreed
that, if both British and US weapons were involved, this submission could
consist of two separate messages designed to arrive in capitals and at NATO
Headquarters at essentially the same time in order to allow them to be
considered as a single package. The exercise rules also provided that active
play of the Defence Planning Committee would not exceed 36 hours and would end
with the communication of the decisions of the nuclear powers concerned. It is
not possible, of course, to exclude such artificialities, but it is important to
realise how extensive is their effect on the areas commented on in later parts
of this

their inherent limiting effect, meant that the volume of background information
available to political authorities, both before and during the nuclear
consultation process, was considerably reduced from what would be available in
an actual situation, and this in turn naturally affected the momentum of the
consultation process. Furthermore, the degree of involvement of political

in a position to devote all of their time and attention to exercise play in
WINTEX 73, nor could facilities be fully committed to the exercise alone.
Although it is essential that these factors be borne in mind when considering
the comments which follow, the sounding of this general note of caution, and
mention of specific caveats in later paragraphs where appropriate, should not,
however, be interpreted as rendering invalid the experience of WINTEX 73.

Dissemination of Assessments and Evaluations pertinent to Nuclear Consultation

9. The Ministers have noted(1) that nuclear consultation would take place
against the background of the flow of information envisaged in the process of
general consultation, and have expressed some concern about the adequacy of
procedures to ensure that assessments and evaluations pertinent to nuclear
consultation will be available. The Permanent Representatives, in commenting on
this problem, pointed out(2) that the Council Operations and Exercise Committee
had been charged by the Council with the general responsibility for
strengthening capabilities in this regard. (3) The Ministers agreed with the
Permanent Representatives' suggestion that the Nuclear Planning Group might wish
to monitor, in an informal way, progress being made by the Council Operations
and Exercise Committee, paying particular attention to this aspect of the WINTEX
73 play.

Actions Following Nuclear Power Decision to Release Weapons
19.  In the earlier report describing the nuclear
consultation process, the Permanent Representatives noted that
it can be expected that the Major NATO Commander would inform
the
capitals and/Council/DPC when weapons have been used and what
results were achieved.  The Permanent Representatives also pointed
out that a Major NATO Commander would probably inform capitals and
the Council/DPC if for any reason he `did not'
use a weapon
that had been released to him, although there are no directives
that require him to do so(1).  In WINTEX 73 SACEUR reported the
expenditure of the weapons released to him; these messages were
not circulated to delegations in view of the prior agreement to
end active play in NATO Headquarters when the decisions by the
nuclear powers had been taken.  The importance of such reports
to political authorities is easily recognised.  Knowledge of
whether and when released weapons have been employed and of the
results achieved would probably have considerable impact not only
on political consultation on later requests for weapon release
but also on many other Alliance actions.
