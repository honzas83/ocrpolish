WILL TACTICAL NUCLEAR WEAPONS EVER BE USED?
by: Philip W. Dyer
The role of tactical nuclear weapons in warfare is a subject that has generated
numerous arguments and much confusion.¹ Both official and unofficial sources
have contributed substantially to the debate but ultimately they have only
further compounded the confusion. The numerous scenarios beginning "if the
Warsaw Pact nations launched a large-scale conventional war in Europe tomorrow
what would NATO do?" reveal how far planners and strategists still are from
answering that question. One of the principal areas of disagreement in these
various scenarios concerns the role of tactical nuclear weapons.

The United States possesses an elaborate and expensive weapons system for the
proposed use of which, apparently, has not yet been agreed upon by either
policymakers or expert observers. Before considering the possible use of these
weapons it is necessary to examine the sources of the present confusion, which
lie in the origins and nature of the weapons themselves and their relation to
the problems of NATO.

The second part of the paper presents a model of foreign policy decisionmaking
and applies that model to the question of tactical nuclear weapons. The result
of that exercise is to suggest that tactical nuclear weapons will probably not
be used in the defense of Europe; since no other credible scenario for their
employment can be imagined, the final conclusion is that the weapons have no
conceivable role in future ground warfare.

With the growing production of the weapons and their adaptation
to artillery through the army's 280-millimeter cannon, it became increasingly
clear that a more detailed study of their employment would need to be made.
Again, much of the initiative was taken by scientists, who, in 1951, organized a
study group, Project Vista, charged with the investigation of the battlefield
use of atomic weapons, with particular emphasis on the NATO area.9 The findings
of this group have never been made public, but the report, apparently, urged
NATO to build its defenses around tactical atomic weapons, thus compensating for
numerical inferiority with an awesome increase in firepower.

At the time of the Vista project, just two years after the Russians' first
atomic explosion, it was perhaps realistic to consider an environment in which
only the NATO nations had tactical nuclear weapons. Whether realistic or not,
that is what the Project Vista scientists did. By December 1954, when the Vista
proposals were formally adopted by NATO, however, the environment had already
begun to change, and the Russians soon were to have a significant tactical
nuclear capability themselves. Therefore, by the mid-1950s, when NATO was
building the defense of Europe around tactical weapons, the Russians were
developing a sufficiently large force to make the proposed unilateral use of the
weapons unrealistic. This fact, though, took some time to intrude itself on
NATO's consciousness. In 1956 during a full-scale NATO war game simulating the
use of nuclear weapons, tactical atomic weapons were "used" only by the allied
side. Even with this limitation, however, Operation Carte Blanche proved
"devastating" to the German people, because of fallout and excessive blast
effects. Rather than intimidate the Russians, as it was designed to do, it
succeeded in demonstrating to the German people that for them, at least, a
tactical nuclear war fought over their territory might be essentially
indistinguishable from a strategic nuclear war.9

Thus, the early development of tactical nuclear weapons, from the first germ of
the idea in 1946 through their simulated use in a NATO war game in 1956,
contains the origins of most of the problems associated with the weapons system

In addition to the problems surrounding their origin, the nature of the weapons

main problems can be delineated quickly. The seven thousand weapons in Europe at

the bombs raises the upper value of both yield and range.

corps, or army level and, therefore, men in an entirely different chain of
command from the men responsible for America's strategic atomic and

A second problem is that the destructive capacity of the weapons plus the
variety of ranges available ensures that the land area over which a tactical
nuclear war might be fought would be a nuclear wasteland of substantial
proportions. Although technology has continued to make the weapons "cleaner,"
any extensive tactical nuclear war fought for a prolonged period of time,
especially in a densely populated region such as Europe, would undoubtedly take
a fearful toll of the local population.¹³

A third source of problems is a result of the novelty of the weapons. Their role
on the battlefield raises basic questions: Can an army function on a nuclear
battlefield? Do the weapons help the attacker or defender? Do they require more
men or fewer?¹⁴ Thus, the nature of the weapons has contributed problems which
cannot be resolved short of actual employment.

The third factor after origin and nature of the weapons is their connection with
NATO. Even before they became fully operational, tactical atomic weapons were
associated in most observers' minds with NATO. It seemed to make sense to commit
the most sophisticated available weapons system to the area of the world of most
value to the United States. Unfortunately, the problems of the weapons have
become so inextricably bound up with NATO's problems that the analysis of both
has suffered. No attempt will be made to restate all of the strategic problems
confronting the Atlantic Alliance; the literature on NATO is so vast that it

II

Despite past confusion it is still possible to say something about the future
role of these weapons if a different approach, other than a new scenario for war
in Europe, is used. Although the policymaking process may not yet be
sufficiently understood to predict outcomes, enough has been written on the
subject to provide some guidelines. Whether one accepts Schilling's minimal-
decision model.22 Hilsmann's consensus-building one.23 or Simon's satisfying
one.24 there seems to be a basic agreement on certain features of decisionmaking
in general and of the policymaking process in particular. They all seem to
suggest, with some variations, that there are an important set of conditions
under which responsible policymakers will avoid making dramatic or controversial
decisions, but will normally make those decisions which are minimally acceptable
to the relevant participants. The pressure to make such "minimal" or
"consensual" decisions appears to be greatest where expert advice seriously
conflicts, relevant elites apply strong and contradictory pressures, or a
situation is perceived by the decisionmakers to be one of high risk.

It should be understood that a minimal or consensual decision may not
necessarily be the one to do the least but the one which least antagonizes the
various interested elites. For instance, in the debate over developing tactical
nuclear weapons and the hydrogen bomb, the consensual decision was to proceed
with both weapons since that decision antagonized fewer elites than any other
one. Actually, in that case a decision to do nothing would probably have
alienated the most people since the great majority of participants were
convinced that the Russian atomic explosion required some response.

Applying the concept of a consensual decision to a war in Europe, the following
points emerge. Just as there is conflicting expert advice now over the
advisability of using tactical nuclear weapons so, too, after the beginning of
hostilities the advice would undoubtedly continue to conflict. In one of these
peculiar chicken-and-egg dilemmas, the advice would not be unanimous until after
the weapons were used and they would probably not be employed until the advice
was unambiguous.

The second point is less certain. What types of internal and external pressures
would policymakers be subject to in the event of conventional war in Europe? On
the one hand, it is easy to imagine military men and others insisting on the use
of all available firepower, including battlefield atomic weapons. On the other
hand, some domestic groups might well be appalled at the thought of using such
weapons. Even more confusing is the question of the European allies themselves.
Would the political leaders of West Germany and the Benelux nations strongly
urge Washington to employ such weapons in the vicinity of their countries? Would
they be indifferent and not pressure the United States either way? There is no
way of knowing for certain what they would do, but the fact that Europe has
survived two conventional wars already in this century, coupled with England's
horrified reaction to an American statement that atomic weapons were being
"considered" during the Korean war (and Korea is half the world away from
Europe), suggest that our European allies would strongly urge the United States
not to use the weapons. Thus it would seem that there would be substantial
contrary pressures acting on the policymakers.

The third point is much more certain. Almost all participants would identify a
large-scale conventional war or even a smaller-scale use of arms in Europe as a
situation of exceedingly high risk. The various crises over Berlin have
indicated that both sides regard disruption of the status quo in Europe, even
when not violent, as a substantial provocation with portentous implications.

Therefore, not just: one but all three of the conditions which usually provoke
consensual decisions would be present. The pressures for a consensual decision
would be enormous. In the context of Europe, though, what would be a consensual
decision? There are probably only four conceivable courses of action, each with
a spectrum of variations, open to the United States in the event of armed
conflict in Europe: (1) The United States could do nothing. American troops in

One parenthetical point needs to be made. The discussion rests on the assumption
that the ultimate decision to employ these weapons will be made by the
president, not by field commanders. While information is very hard to uncover,
the evidence seems to be that the "fail safe" system for tactical nuclear
weapons, sometimes referred to as Permissive Action Link (PAL), is at least as
effective as it is for strategic weapons.26 Thus, at the present time, it is
unlikely that a field commander on his own initiative could overrule the
president's decision. One further assumption underlying this discussion is that
there will be a certain degree of rationality exercised by decisionmakers even
after hostilities start. Thus while officials may not calmly sit down and
evaluate each of these four options in the heat of battle, the issues are such
that they can be, and hopefully have been, analyzed in the less harried
atmosphere of peace. Surely, then, the decision arrived at in peacetime would

CAN EUROPE'S SECURITY BE "DECOUPLED" FROM AMERICA?
By Andrew J. Pierre
A NEW and contentious concept has seeped into the trans-Atlantic dialogue in
recent times. It has been suggested that the United States may "decouple" itself
from its strategic commitment to Western Europe in the future, or perhaps is in
the process of doing so now. The codification of mutual deterrence in the SALT
agreements of a year ago, combined with the earlier loss of U.S. nuclear
superiority, is seen as having considerably eroded the remaining credibility of
the American nuclear guarantee to Europe. Some go further to find in the
agreements an implicit understanding between the two superpowers that neither
will henceforth initiate the use of nuclear weapons in any circumstances short
of the direct defense of its own territory. And even thoughtful Europeans who
still observe the litany of faith in the nuclear guarantee do so with diminished
conviction and look for opportunities through cooperative European actions to
compensate for a substantial degree of American disengagement.

The issue is, of course, further complicated by growing trans-Atlantic
dissonance on trade and monetary issues, which threatens to poison the
political-security relationship. Increasingly, the economic interests of the
European Economic Community (EEC) and the United States are seen as pointing
more to rivalry than partnership in the future. The emphasis of American leaders
on bilateral relations with the Soviet Union and China confirms for many
Europeans the loosening of the alignments associated with the postwar balance
between East and West. And even the general reassurance in Henry Kissinger's
recent epistle to the Europeans calling for a new Atlantic Charter included a
reference to "radically different strategic conditions," the full implications
of which "have yet to be faced."

That the security relationship between the United States and Europe is in a
process of change has long been clear. This is well understood by the Western
governments involved in the talks on Mutual and Balanced Force Reductions (MBFR)
and at the preparatory phase of the Conference on Security and Coopera-

initiate the use of nuclear weapons for the defense of Western Europe if
ultimately necessary. Now negotiations on this bedrock of security were in
progress and, it was anxiously noted, for the first time the NATO allies were
excluded from a major arms-control undertaking. There was no assurance,
moreover, that the United States and the Soviet Union would not let their

III

During SALT I, the issue of the forward-based systems was temporarily set aside
because of the great difficulties it created. Early in the discussions the
Soviet Union indicated that the negotiations must deal with all strategic
nuclear forces, which it defined as those stationed so as to be capable of
reaching the homeland of the other. According to this definition the FBS,
primarily U.S. nuclear-strike aircraft and land-based missiles in Western Europe
able to reach the Soviet Union, would have to be included. (There are no fully
comparable Soviet FBS capable of reaching the United States.) Because of the
complexities raised by this question and the sensitivity of the Western European
countries, on whose soil most of these systems are based, an impasse was quickly
reached. In May 1971 the two parties agreed to bypass the FBS issue in order to
make progress in other areas of SALT. Now discussions in Geneva earlier this
year have already made it clear that this will be a key problem in the
continuing SALT negotiations.

Let us look at the specific elements of the FBS issue. The United States has
approximately 420 F-4 Phantom II and 70 F-111 fighter-bombers stationed in
Europe that are "dual-capable"—aircraft built to carry both nuclear and
conventional loads—plus about another 75 A-6 and A-7 strike planes on two
aircraft carriers with the Sixth Fleet in the Mediterranean. These figures
exclude non-nuclear U.S. tactical aircraft deployed in Western Europe, four
NATO-committed F-4 squadrons on call in the United States, and the "dual-
capable" aircraft of our allies. The forward-based systems relevant to the
present phase of SALT consist of this group of American aircraft based so as to
be capable of reaching the Soviet Union with nuclear weapons, even though not
all are intended to be used for such a purpose. One of the many complicating
factors is that some U.S. aircraft deployed in South Korea and with the Seventh
Fleet in the Far East are able to reach Soviet territory and could also be
classified as FBS. Moreover, there are Pershing missiles in West Germany with a
450-mile range which could strike into the Ukraine if they were positioned
closer to the border.

military question at hand, because of its bargaining value in negotiations or
because of its potential to create problems between the United States and its

For the West Europeans the FBS have a particularly significant political role.
They are perceived by many political, military and bureaucratic observers as a
symbol of the American commitment to defend Europe as well as being part of the
military strategy for that purpose. The FBS are seen by European governments as
part of the "community property" of NATO. Their presence is taken as ensuring
American involvement in a war in Europe and as a show of intent to the Soviet

defense. They give a higher priority to avoiding the outbreak of hostilities
than to fighting a war on their soil once it has started. Accordingly the West
Europeans see the FBS, like American ground forces in Germany, as part of the
mechanism "coupling" the United States with the defense of Europe. A withdrawal
or reduction of FBS could provoke serious political anxieties unless it were
done in close consultation with the Allies.

V

Alongside the nuclear guarantee, American ground forces in Western Europe remain
a principal element coupling the defense of the Continent with the United
States. These forces are in theory designed to deter and resist Soviet
aggression, should it start, and by their presence to enhance the political
confidence and sense of security of the Allies. To the West Europeans, however,
these forces are above all hostages which guarantee the immediate involvement of
the United States in a war in Europe; the U.S. Seventh Army is seen as the main
link in the escalatory chain which can eventually trigger the U.S. strategic
retaliatory forces. For this reason American efforts in the Kennedy-McNamara
years to increase European conventional-force levels and delay the use of
nuclear weapons received less than total support from European governments.

Pressures to reduce U.S. force levels in Europe are again in resurgence. With
the withdrawal of American troops from Vietnam complete, Senator Mansfield and
members of the Senate Democratic Caucus have suggested heavy cuts in all
overseas forces. It is not yet clear that the all-volunteer armed forces will in
fact induce sufficient volunteers to fill the presently planned thirteen-
division, 800,000-man army. The balance-of-payments situation remains
unsatisfactory in spite of the successive devaluations of the dollar. As the
U.S. defense budget rises, due to the greatly increased costs of manpower and
new weapons systems, the temptation within the defense establishment will be to
look to Europe for any reductions which are unavoidable.

Since Brezhnev's welcome Titlis speech of May 1971 the Nixon administration has
used the imminence of East-West negotiations on force reductions to argue
against and forestall pressures for unilateral reductions. Has it unwittingly
entered a culture-sac?
