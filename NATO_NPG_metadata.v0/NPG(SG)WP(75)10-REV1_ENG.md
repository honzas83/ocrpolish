> [!abstract] 
> # Communicating NATO's Intentions – Warning Messages
> 
> This working paper, prepared by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Staff Group, examines the strategic implications of sending warning messages to the Warsaw Pact before NATO employs nuclear weapons. It builds upon previous studies on Communicating NATO's Intentions (CNI) and addresses whether warnings should be sent before a nuclear use decision or after a decision but before actual implementation. The document analyzes potential Soviet responses, contrasting a 'doctrinal and rigid' approach, which might lead to a wide-scale nuclear response or a pre-emptive attack, against a 'flexible and cautious' approach, where a warning might induce a political solution. It highlights the principal danger that advance warnings could trigger Soviet pre-emption and nuclear attacks, while also noting that they could potentially limit the level of nuclear use. The paper discusses the risks of Soviet deception, propaganda, and military countermeasures, such as the force dispersion to negate NATO's limited nuclear response. It emphasizes the need for careful coordination and consistency within the Alliance to avoid misinterpretation or perceived weakness in resolve. Finally, it addresses the following-on use of nuclear weapons, noting that if an initial warning was ignored, subsequent warnings would be viewed as credible and not a bluff.

COMMUNICATING NATO'S INTENTIONS – WARNING MESSAGES
Draft Report by the Permanent Representatives

I. PURPOSE.
1. This report examines the question whether NATO, in case of conflict, should
send messages of warning to the Warsaw Pact prior to a nuclear weapon use.

II. BACKGROUND
2. At their meeting in Ankara in May, 1975 the NFG Ministers invited(1) the NFG
Permanent Representatives to initiate a study on the implications of
communicating NATO's intentions (CNI) to an enemy, to other countries and to the
public. An initial CNI Study(2), prepared by the NFG Staff Group, was forwarded
to Ministers, together with a Permanent Representatives' report(3) at their
Monterey meeting in June 1975. These documents consider in depth the
communication that NATO might choose to make in support of a decision to use
nuclear weapons selectively. At Monterey, the NFG Ministers took note of these
documents and agreed(4) with the Permanent Representatives' recommendation that
work on the subject should continue.

3. The Permanent Representative's report on the CNI study identified, in

a. whether a warning message should be sent prior to the nuclear use decision or

and

This report addresses the first of those two questions and it should be read in

piece. The question of procedures is considered in a separate report.

III. WARNING MESSAGES
Definitions
4. The CNI Study considered messages which would be sent after a firm nuclear
power decision had been reached (but not specifically making the use dependent
on his reaction to the message. Thus, the information aspects of the message
would predominate, although there would be a warning, explicit or implicit, of
further use. The present paper considers messages where the warning aspect
predominates. This may be in instances where the message is sent before any
specific decision to use nuclear weapons, or where after a nuclear power
decision has been taken, the enemy is warned that it will be implemented unless
he ceases his aggression.

Warsaw Fact Courses of Action

5. The Study on Warsaw Pact Politico-Military Strategy and Military Doctrine for
the Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons(1) says that, "…… there may be two main
courses of action which Soviet leaders may pursue in a crisis: a doctrinal and
rigid one or a more flexible and cautious one. By a doctrinal and rigid approach
is meant that Soviet wartime behaviour would be consistent with their enunciated
doctrine and policy. There is much evidence that supports this possibility,
which would result in widescale nuclear response to NATO initial use, but there
are also a number of factors which suggest that there would be pressures on the
Soviet leadership to limit the conflict and/or to end it through political
means(2). Although this "flexible and cautious" approach is not susceptible of
precise definition, it can be best described for the purposes of this paper as a
range of possible Soviet actions that would be governed by a manifest concern
about the risks of escalation.

Advantages and Disadvantages of Messages of Warning:

6. Both of the assumptions above may be taken as starting points in assessing
the advantages and disadvantages of warning messages. They represent the
opposite poles of Soviet behaviour which in actuality might not be so clearly
definable, and might indeed change from one type to the other during the course
of the conflict. The obvious great advantage of a message of warning

7. Clearly the principal danger in any advance warning is of causing the Soviets
to pre-empt and to initiate nuclear attacks. If Soviet reaction to NATO's
initial use would be widescale nuclear response, consistent with their
enunciated doctrine, NATO's message of warning would do no more than accelerate
an unavoidable progression. The clarification of NATO's intentions in a message
of warning might at least result in Soviet nuclear use at a level below that
otherwise predicated by their doctrine.

8. It mi-it be impossible for alliance political and military authorities to
assess what approach the enemy had decided upon, and his response to NATO's
message of warning might not aid this assessment. A purposely delayed response,
with accompanying request for extended deadlines may, if honoured, cause NATO to
lose valuable time while the conventional battle was going from bad to worse.
The response to the warning message may be purposely ambiguous or deceptive,
possibly leading to disunity among the NATO allies on the nuclear use issue.
Also, the Soviet Union may respond publicly to a secretly delivered warning
message, exploiting the nuclear warning for propaganda purposes and attempting
to crack NATO resolve. In addition, the enemy could take military
countermeasures short of nuclear preemption. For example, dispersion of air and
ground forces may negate the military effect of the NATO limited nuclear
response, forcing NATO into further escalation which otherwise might not have
been required. Finally, use of a warning message could constrain NATO
flexibility in deciding on the timing, scope and location of nuclear use.

The Spectrum of Warning Messages

9. Warning messages might range in time from before or at the outbreak of
hostilities (and thus, presumably, couched in general terms) up to the stage of
a conflict when the nuclear power or powers have reached a firm decision to use
nuclear weapons (in this case, the message would probably be cast in specific
and blunt terms)

We presume that NATO's communications during a period of tension or outbreak of
hostilities would contain at least implicit warnings of the risks of nuclear
escalation inherent in the crisis situation. (Similar warnings would of course
be carried by Major NATO Commander action in relation to the NATO Alert System.)
The need for careful coordination and consistency within the Alliance must be
paramount for all CNI action, and there must be no danger of misinterpretation
either in the direction of exaggerating Alliance defensive intention or of
suggesting any weakness in Alliance resolve. Furthermore, it would be essential
to avoid a situation where the Alliance, in the event of Soviet non-compliance,
did not live up to its warnings. Therefore, it is likely that any message of
warning containing an explicit threat of nuclear weapons use would need to be
closely related to a specific decision by the nuclear power(s) to employ nuclear
weapons.

Warning: Messages and Follow-on Use
10. The foregoing has been written mainly with NATO's initial use of nuclear
weapons in mind. In general, it would also apply, mutatis mutandis, for follow-
on use if it were decided in the Alliance to make any warning further to that
implicit in initial use. However, there would be additional considerations. If a
warning message had been issued before initial use and had been ignored, the
Warsaw Pact would, at least, have learned that NATO did not make warnings as a
bluff. Thus, if NATO made a warning before follow-on use, the Warsaw Pact could
be sure that it would be followed.

Channels for Warning Messages

11. A warning message could be issued as a communiqué by the North Atlantic
Council and disseminated by "all"public means"available to the Allies. By
allowing public knowledge of the warning message sent to the Warsaw Pact, the
Alliance might demonstrate – especially to non-aligned countries – its desire to
hold back from nuclear war. On the other hand, this kind of publicity might not
allow the Alliance to offer to the enemy face-saving de-escalatory options. and,
thus, a secret communication through such diplomatic channels as were available,
or by a nuclear power's hotline, might be preferable. When a warning message is
related to a specific use decision, the time factor may be critical and this
points to the nuclear powers' hotlines as being the most practicable channel.
Moreover, a nuclear power message would carry greatest weight.

Consultation on Warning Messages

12. The CNI Study states(1) that CNI questions should be given active
consideration at all stages of the nuclear consultation process and that, when
first indications are received that NATO might have to resort to the use of
nuclear weapons, the Council/DPC should consult on what measures of this kind
might be appropriate.
