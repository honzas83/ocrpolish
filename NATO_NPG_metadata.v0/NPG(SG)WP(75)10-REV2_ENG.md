> # Communicating NATO's Intentions - Messages of Warning
> 
> This working paper, prepared by the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Staff Group, examines the strategic implications of sending messages of warning to an enemy prior to the use of nuclear weapons. It builds upon previous studies on Communicating NATO's Intentions (CNI) and addresses whether such warnings should be sent before or after a nuclear use decision is reached. The document analyzes potential Soviet responses, contrasting a 'doctrinal and rigid' approach with a 'flexible and cautious' one. It outlines the advantages of warnings, such as inducing the enemy to cease hostilities without nuclear recourse, against the disadvantages, including the risk of triggering a pre-emptive nuclear strike or allowing the enemy to take military countermeasures. The paper also discusses the timing and spectrum of messages, ranging from general warnings at the outbreak of hostilities to blunt, specific threats following a firm decision to use nuclear weapons. Finally, it evaluates communication channels, suggesting that while public communiqués via the North Atlantic Council could demonstrate a desire for peace to non-aligned nations, secret diplomatic channels or nuclear power hotlines may be more effective for face-saving de-escalation and rapid communication.

I. PURPOSE
1. This report examines the question whether NATO, in case of conflict, should
send messages of warning to the enemy prior to a nuclear weapon use.

II. BACKGROUND
2. At their meeting in Ankara in May, 1973 the NFG Ministers invited(1) the NPG
Permanent Representatives to initiate a study on the implications of
communicating NATO's intentions (CNI) to an enemy, to other countries and to the
public. An initial CNI Study(2), prepared by the NFG Staff Group, was forwarded
to Ministers, together with a Permanent Representatives' report(3) at their
Monterey meeting in June 1975. These documents considered in depth the
communication that NATO might choose to make in support of a decision to use
nuclear weapons selectively. At Monterey, the NFG Ministers took note of these
documents and agreed(4) with the Permanent Representatives' recommendation that
work on the subject should continue.

3. The Permanent Representatives' report on the CNI Study identified, in
particular, two questions.

a. whether messages of warning should be sent prior to the nuclear use decision
or whether such messages might be sent after a decision but prior to actual use;
and

b. whether it might be desirable to develop a set of procedures to assist CNI
consultation.

This report addresses the first of those two questions and it should be read in
conjunction with the above-mentioned documents, to which it forms a companion
piece. The question of procedures is /to be/ considered in a separate report.

III. MESSAGES OF WARNING
Definitions
4. The CNI Study(1) considered messages which would be sent after a nuclear
power decision had been reached and not specifically making the use dependent on
the enemy's reaction. Such a message would be essentially one of information and
clarification, although it would also contain a warning, explicit or implicit,
of further use. The present paper considers messages where the warning aspect
predominates. This may be in instances where the message is sent before any
specific decision to use nuclear weapons, or where after a nuclear power
decision has been taken, the enemy is warned that it will be implemented unless
he meets NATO's conditions.

Warsaw Pact Courses of Action

5. The study on Warsaw Pact Politico-military Strategy and Military Doctrine for
the Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons suggests(1) that there may be two main
courses of action which Soviet leaders may pursue in a crisis: a doctrinal and
rigid one or a more flexible and cautious one. By a doctrinal and rigid approach
is meant that Soviet wartilc behaviour would be consistent with their enunciated
doctrine and policy, and there is much evidence that supports this possibility
which would result in widescale nuclear response to NATO initial use. There are,
however, a number of factors which suggest that there would be pressure on the
Soviet leadership to limit the conflict and/or to end it through political
means(2). Although this "flexible and cautious" approach is not susceptible of
precise definition, it can be best described for the purposes of this paper as a
range of possible Soviet actions that would be governed by a manifest concern
about the risks of escalation.

Advantages and Disadvantages of Messages of Warning

6. Both of the assumptions above may be taken as starting points in assessing
the advantages and disadvantages of messages of warning. They represent the
opposite poles of Soviet behaviour which in actuality might not be so clearly
definable, and might indeed change from one type to the other during the course
of a conflict.

7. The obvious great advantage of a message of warning could be that it might
induce the Soviet leadership to take the action desired by the Alliance without
lATO's initial or further recourse to nuclear weapons. This indeed would be the
aim of a message of warning. Should the Soviet approach be rigidly consistent
with their enunciated doctrine, it is unlikely that a message of warning would
have the desired effect and, indeed, it might well trigger a pre-emptive attack
by the enemy. On the other hand, the clarification of NATO's intentions in a
message of warning might result in Soviet nuclear use at a level below that
otherwise predicated by their doctrine. Furthermore, it is possible that a
flexible and cautious Soviet leadership might be swayed by a message of warning.
The message might have the desired effect, for example, if the Soviet leadership
were divided,when a strong warning might tilt the balance towards seeking a
political solution. Even in the event of an enemy decision to cease his advance
only to sit tight on the territory he had seized already, the Alliance might
gain some advantage, if only temporary and partial.

8. Against the possible advantages must be set the possible disadvantages.
Clearly the principal danger in any advance warning is of causing the enemy to
pre-empt and to initiate nuclear attack. But there are other disadvantages. A
purposely delayed response, with accompanying requests for extended deadlines
could be designed to cause NATO to lose valuable time while the conventional
battle

was going from bad to worse. The response to the message of warning might be
purposely ambiguous or deceptive, with the aim of undermining Alliance resolve.
Also, the enemy might respond publicly to a secretly delivered message of
warning and exploit the situation for propaganda purposes. The enemy could also
take military countermeasures short of nuclear pre-emption, such as dispersal,
to negate or lessen the military effects of NATO's threatened use of nuclear
weapons. Finally, use of a message of warning could constrain NATO flexibility
in deciding on the timing, scope and location of nuclear use.

Timing and Spectrum of Messages of Warning

9. Messages of warning might range in time from before or at the outbreak of
hostilities (and thus, presumably, couched in general terms) up to the stage of
a conflict when the nuclear power or powers have reached a firm decision to use
nuclear weapons (in this case, the message would probably be cast in specific
and blunt terms). We presume that NATO's communications during a period of
tension or outbreak of hostilities would contain at least implicit warnings of
the risks of nuclear escalation inherent in the crisis situation (similar
warnings would, of course, be implicit in Major NATO Commanders' actions related
to the NATO Alert System). The need for careful coordination and consistency
within the Alliance must be paramount for all CMI action, and there must be no
danger of misinterpretation either in the direction of exaggerating Alliance

Furthermore, it would be essential to avoid a situation where the Alliance, in

Channels for Messages of Warning

10. A message of warning could be issued as a communiqué by the North Atlantic
Council and disseminated by all public means available to the Allies. By
allowing public knowledge of the message of warning sent to the enemy, the
Alliance might demonstrate - especially to non-aligned countries - its desire to
hold back from nuclear war. On the other hand, this kind of publicity might not
allow the Alliance to offer to the enemy face-saving de-escalatory options, and,
thus, a secret communication through such diplomatic channels as were available,
or by a nuclear power's hotline, might be preferable. Then a message of warning
is related to a specific use decision, the time factor may be critical and this
suggests the nuclear powers' hotlines might well be the most practicable
channel. Moreover, a nuclear power message, on behalf of the Alliance as a
whole, would carry greatest weight.

Consultation on Messages of Warning

11. In view of the aim of a message of warning and the importance of the timing
and nature of such a message, a decision to send a message of warning would
involve interaction of political and military considerations and would be highly
dependent on the actual situation.

Conclusions.

12. This paper has identified advantages and disadvantages of general messages
of warning to be applied prior to a decision on a nuclear weapon use and also of
messages of warning which might be sent after a decision but prior to actual
use. It is concluded that in both cases NATO could only use this approach with
some expectation of success if the decision-making process on the enemy side
with regard to their own mode of operation is still on-going.

13. NAC/DPC consideration of the utility, purpose, and timing of messages of
warning should take place in connection with the on-going nuclear consultation
process.

III. RECOMMENDATIONS

14. It is recommended that Ministers:

(a) take note of this report;

(b) discuss the advantages and disadvantages of messages of warning on the basis
of this report;

(c) agree that consultation regarding messages of warning should take place in
future NATO exercises; and

(d) agree that this paper, along with the preceding CNI document(1) be forwarded
to the D.C.
