> # Nuclear Consultation – CNI Procedures
> 
> This working paper, issued by the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group), outlines the need for specific procedures for Communicating NATO's Intentions (CNI) to an enemy. It defines CNI as any communication concerning the possible or actual use of nuclear weapons by NATO that may be made to an opponent, the public, or third countries. The document distinguishes between communications during peacetime and tension, and those during times of hostilities, categorizing the latter into four distinct periods: before nuclear use is considered, after consideration has started, after a decision to use has been taken, and in parallel with or immediately after execution. It identifies two general types of CNI actions: messages of information to explain the limited character of nuclear use and messages of explicit warning to induce an opponent to change their behavior. The paper emphasizes that any communication regarding nuclear weapons must be subject to consultation among Allies, stressing the importance of simplicity, flexibility, and the ability to adapt to prevailing circumstances. It further notes that messages of explicit warning are ultimate in character and could commit nuclear powers to an affirmative release decision if the others are not heeded. Finally, the document highlights the importance of monitoring enemy reactions to influence subsequent Alliance actions.

NUCLEAR CONSULTATION – CNI PROCEDURES

I. BACKGROUND AND PURPOSE

1. At their meeting in Ankara, NPG Ministers invited(1) the Permanent
Representatives to initiate a study on the implications of communicating NATO's
intentions to use nuclear weapons in the defence of NATO (CNI) to an enemy, to
other countries and to the public, and, on the related procedural matters. Since
communications to the latter two audiences would necessitate broader
consideration in NATO, the NPG has focussed on matters related to CNI to an
enemy(2). The approach in this paper is limited accordingly.

2. The NPG originally agreed(3) that detailed procedures for internal NATO
consultation should be avoided. On the other hand, in the light of their
discussions at recent NPG Ministerial meetings at Monterey(4) and Hamburg(5)
Ministers decided that work on this subject should be continued and agreed that
CNI should be tried out in future NATO Headquarters' exercises. Apart from a
possible requirement for more detailed procedures stemming from this decision,
the Permanent Representatives themselves had raised the question(6) whether it
might be desirable to develop a set of procedures to assist CNI consultation.
The following considerations are intended to assist in a final decision on this
matter.

II. GENERAL DESCRIPTION OF THE TASK

3. The task is to define, on the basis of NPG documents dealing with CNI(1) what
procedures could be followed during general and nuclear consultation to enable
NATO to intensify its signals to an enemy with regard to nuclear defence
preparations or to notify him of the purpose of an actual or immediately
forthcoming use of nuclear weapons as circumstances may require. It should also
be considered if such activities could be related to other initiatives by
political or military authorities during the various phases of general and
nuclear consultation.

III. FRAMEWORK FOR CNI PROCEDURES

4. A CNI action, as part of Crisis Management, can be defined as any
communication concerning the possible or actual use of nuclear weapons by NATO
that may be made by the Alliance or on its behalf to an opponent or enemy, the
public or third countries, at such times when it is deemed necessary and by any
means which may be deemed suitable. In preceding NPG papers, it has been
concluded that several kinds of communication to an enemy could be developed;
depending upon the overall circumstances, the specific timing and NATO's
objectives in making the communication.

5. Peacetime and Tension. In these periods, declaratory actions may range from
routine or occasional diplomatic actions or other policy statements made by
authoritative, qualified and responsible NATO or national political sources

6. **Times of Hostilities.** In cases of aggression on
NATO territory up to major conventional attack, CNI actions
would be closely interrelated with the different stages and
degrees of intensity of NATO consultation on the nuclear
option. Four periods can be distinguished in this respect
and CNI action would need to be tailored accordingly:
*(a)* before any specific use of nuclear weapons has been
taken into consideration (not yet raised);
*(b)* after consideration of using nuclear weapons has
started but no decision to use them has yet been
taken (raised);
*(c)* after a decision to use nuclear weapons has been
taken but the use itself has not yet occurred
*(d)* in parallel with or immediately after the use of
nuclear weapons (executed).

The content of CNI messages would also be determined by the
peculiarities of the developing situation, by the related
objectives which NATO would intend to achieve through
transmission of such CNI messages to an enemy and by
consideration of the risks attendant to each alternative.
All these elements, as well as the pace of the developing
situation are relevant to CNI procedures.

Specifically, the preceding CNI work has dealt with two general types of CNI
actions:

(a) messages of information oriented primarily towards explaining the selective
and limited character of an actual or immediately forthcoming NATO use of
nuclear weapons and warning against the danger of further escalation(1);

These generalisations, although possibly leading to a lack of precision in some

IV. POSSIBLE PROCEDURAL ARRANGEMENTS FOR CNI
8. In line with established principles of the Alliance, any communication
dealing with the possible or actual use of nuclear weapons by NATO should be the
subject of consultation among the Allies. It has been noted (paragraph 5 above)
that in peacetime declaratory policy consists of occasional diplomatic actions
or policy statements made by authoritative NATO or national political sources.
It is obvious that, in this context, specific procedures cannot be established.
On the other hand, procedural arrangements for undertaking CNI actions may
facilitate the consultation process in times of hostilities. Such procedures
should be consistent with the general principle that simplicity, flexibility and
capability to adapt to prevailing circumstances should be the primary

9. As a preceding NPG paper has noted(2), discussion of a general message of

consultation involves broader interests and does not directly fall under the
prerogatives of the NPG, this paper deals only with those procedures that should
apply when the consideration of a possible or an actual use of nuclear weapons
becomes an issue.

10. Consideration should be given to CNI questions at all stages of the nuclear
consultation process. In a developing modern wartime situation, with fast and
large movements and possible immediate and immense effects on both sides, CNI
actions probably would be critically dependent on time and their consideration
should, therefore begin without delay. It is pointed out that while the timing

Hamburg when it was observed without contradiction that failure to follow

in the NATO Headquarters or capitals should immediately be passed to all other
countries, the Council/DPC and Major N.TO Commanders for immediate consultation
on next steps.

NUCLEAR CONSULTATION - CNI PROCEDURES
Messages of Explicit Warning and Messages of Information
I. DEFINITIONS
1. CNI actions within the nuclear consultation process may take the form of
Messages of Explicit Warning (MEW) or Messages of Information (MI).

(a) A Message of Information is oriented towards explaining the selective and
limited character of an actual or immediately forthcoming NATO use of nuclear
weapons and warning against the danger of further escalation. This message may
be sent after a decision to use nuclear weapons has been taken or after the use
has already occurred.

(b) A Message of Explicit Warning is oriented towards inducing an opponent to
take the action desired by the Alliance without N.TO's initial or further use of
nuclear weapons. This message may be sent after consideration of using nuclear
weapons has started, but no decision to use them has yet been taken or after a
decision has been taken but the use itself has not yet occurred.

II. PROCEDURAL /ARRANGEMENTS
2. The basic distinction between consultation and decision-making on message
texts and on finally sending a message should be observed. While a major
objective of every NATO message to an enemy would be the demonstration of
cohesion and solidarity, circumstances and timing considerations might not
always allow for a full consultation on the content of a message.
