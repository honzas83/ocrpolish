> # Study on Communicating NATO's Intention to Use Nuclear Weapons
> 
> This document is a revised study by the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) examining the factors and procedures involved in communicating NATO's intention to employ nuclear weapons in its defense. The study focuses primarily on the communication that NATO might choose to make after reaching a specific decision to use nuclear weapons selectively. It identifies the principal factors that political and military authorities must consider, including the need to convince an enemy of NATO's readiness to escalate and the need to minimize risks of misinterpretation. The document outlines a policy framework based on the Provisional Political Guidelines for the Initial Defensive Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons by NATO, emphasizing the need for messages of explanation and warning to the enemy, as well as communication to the general public and other countries. It also discusses the procedural aspects of implementing a 'Communication of NATO's Intention' (CNI) action, including the selection of channels, communicating agents, and coordination. The study was prepared by the NPG Staff Group with contributions from the International Staff, the International Military Staff, SHAPE, and SACLANT, and it is intended for consideration by the Staff Group at its meeting on July 18, 1974.

Draft Study by the NFG Staff Group

I.   PURPOSE
This Study identifies and examines the principal factors that political and
military authorities in NATO would have to take into account in dealing with the
question of communicating its intention to employ nuclear weapons in its
defence. The Study focuses primarily on the communication that NATO might choose
to make after it has reached a specific decision to use nuclear weapons
selectively. The Study also discusses procedural aspects of handling the
question in NATO.

II.  BACKGROUND
2.  Portions of two reports prepared by the NFG Permanent Representatives for

commented on the question of communicating NATO's intentions. These were a
report(1) containing some preliminary observations on the nuclear aspects of
WINTEX 73, and a report(2) commenting on an illustrative study on demonstrative
use(3) prepared by SHAPE. In the light of their consideration of these reports,
the NFG Ministers invited(4) the Permanent Representatives to initiate a study
on the implications of communicating NATO's intentions to an enemy, to other
countries and to the public, and on related procedural matters.

38. In order to achieve the objective of the initial use of nuclear weapons set

(a) The enemy must be convinced of NATO's readiness to proceed with nuclear

(b) The risks of the enemy misinterpreting the deliberately limited nature of

39. In order that NATO's intention in initiating tactical use of nuclear weapons
can be made clear to the enemy, it will generally be necessary to convey some
message of explanation and warning to him, additional to the message that would
be implicit in the use itself. The form, content and timing of the message would
have to be decided in the light of circumstances. It should reach the enemy in
sufficient time before the initial use of nuclear weapons for him to appreciate
NATO's purpose, and should threaten further escalation if he fails to comply
with the warning. NATO must, however, be aware of the loss of surprise which
would result, and of the danger that the enemy might exploit the explanation and
warning received by reacting in a manner jeopardising NATO's defence posture. If
such an event should occur, NATO should be prepared to escalate further.

40. All political and diplomatic channels available to the NATO governments
should be exploited to communicate the statement to the enemy and to explain
NATO's actions to the rest of the world, and to keep in contact with the enemy
governments in order to learn their reaction without delay and to influence that
reaction to the utmost. In this connection, particular importance must be
attached to the establishment of contacts between the nuclear powers, and the
existing "hot lines" between the capitals are obviously relevant. It must,
however, be kept in mind that, under certain circumstances, public communication
of NATO actions or intentions could have an adverse impact upon the effect of
the warning.

41. All estimates of the situation must take into account the psychological
effects of NATO's use of nuclear weapons and of the reactions of the aggressor,
and the consequent need for suitable measures addressed to the general public
and to the forces.

General Observations on Current NATO Policy
7. Framework for Analysis. Since the foregoing policy guidance forms the
background for the analysis contained in later portions of this Study, it is
appropriate to mention several basic points likely to have a bearing on the
subsequent discussion that should be borne in mind:

(a) The foregoing policy guidance pertains specifically to questions of
communicating NATO's intention(2) that would arise if, in time of hostilities, a
decision is reached to resort to the selective tactical employment of nuclear
weapons. Thus, the focus of this Study is on the substantive and procedural
aspects of CNI questions that would confront political and military authorities
in NATO in the context of a specific use decision.

(b) The foregoing policy guidance may give the impression that a CNI action
would be accomplished by a single message or statement addressed to the enemy.
However, the guidance also recognises the possible need to explain NATO's
actions to NATO's military forces and population, and to the rest of the world.
For the purposes of this Study, therefore, a CNI action is broadly construed to
encompass all of the measures that might be taken to communicate NATO's
intention with reference to a specific occasion of selective tactical employment
of nuclear weapons. In other words, a CNI action is considered to be a
communication package related to a particular use, composed of a number of
mutually supporting messages addressed to different audiences. The scope and
content of this package cannot be precisely determined in advance.

(c) The foregoing policy guidance notes that "it will generally be necessary" to
send a CNI message to the enemy. This is interpreted to mean that current NATO
policy does not obligate NATO to take a CNI action in every case, and that the
question of whether NATO should communicate its intention is an open one. This
Study makes no attempt to define "generally" by predicting the probability of

8. CNI Decisions to be Taken. There are two general kinds of CNI questions that
might confront NATO if, in time of hostilities, a decision is reached to resort
to the selective tactical use of nuclear weapons. First, NATO would have to
consider whether any steps should be taken to explain its intention with respect
to the specific use. If the answer to this query is yes, NATO would then have to
examine a complex of substantive and procedural questions dealing with the
details of carrying out the CNI action.

9. Relationship between CNI Decisions and the Use Decision. As noted earlier,
current policy guidance does not obligate NATO to take a CNI action on every
occasion of selective tactical use of nuclear weapons. Therefore, a decision to
employ nuclear weapons is not in itself a decision to take a CNI action. On the
other hand, a decision to employ nuclear weapons is a pre-requisite for reaching
a decision to "take a CNI action"; in the absence of a decision to employ
nuclear weapons, there is no "use" to explain. Furthermore, it is obvious that
the implementation of a CNI action must be carefully

10. Underlying Factors Affecting CNI Decisions. Before exploring specific
substantive implications of current NATO policy and the related procedural
matters, several considerations of a more general nature deserve mention in view
of their possible effect on CNI decisions:

(a) Support for NATO Strategy. The fundamental objective of the tactical use of
nuclear weapons and the threat of further escalation is, according to the
Provisional Political Guidelines, "to induce the enemy to make the political
decision to cease his attack and withdraw"(1). The agreed NATO strategy of
flexible response /to deter aggression by convincing a potential attacker that
the risks involved are out of all proportion to the objective sought, and that
NATO, in defending itself if aggression does occur, is prepared to escalate the
conflict, if necessary, to a point that would jeopardise even the national
existence of the aggressor. In the event it is attacked, NATO would defend
itself through determined but controlled use of whatever mix of conventional,
tactical nuclear or strategic nuclear forces is needed to bring about the
desired change of mind on the part of the aggressor. There is a

of keeping both the overall situation and the process of escalation under
control(1). A similar emphasis is placed on control of escalation if it should
become necessary for NATO to resort to follow-on tactical use(2). The
contributions-that CNI actions might make toward reinforcing NATO's efforts to

both the nature of, and reasons for, NATO's initial use.

13. Political Considerations. The Provisional Political
Guidelines mention two requirements essential to the achievement of
the objective of initial use (and, by implication, of follow-on
tactical use as well) that have great political significance. These
requirements are that the enemy must be convinced of NATO's readiness
to proceed with nuclear escalation, and that the risks of the enemy
misinterpreting the deliberately limited nature of NATO's initial
use must be kept to a minimum(1). A CNI action might contribute
toward meeting both of these requirements.
14. In this connection, it might be recalled that the CNI
message to the enemy, foreseen in the Provisional Political Guidelines
for either initial or follow-on tactical use, would contain elements
of explanation and of warning. These elements might be examined in
the context of the two requirements mentioned above.

escalation, a CNI action would provide NATO with the means to explain

is inherent in the use per se. By the same token, the warning element of a CNI

does the use itself, that there is a continuing risk of further escalation. This
would contribute to intra-war deterrence by continuing to confront the enemy
with a credible but incalculable risk that NATO might resort to further nuclear
weapon use. It would be important, however, to avoid creating a misapprehension
in the enemy's mind that could be detrimental to NATO's overall objectives,
namely, that NATO's constrained use was an indication of a lack of resolve. If,
for example, the particular use selected by NATO was judged by the enemy to be
timid or harmless, a CNI action might be seen by him to be inflated or a bluff.
Such a CNI action could be fruitless or even counter productive from the
standpoint of convincing the enemy of NATO's readiness to proceed with nuclear
escalation.

16. With respect to minimising the risk of the enemy misinterpreting the
deliberately limited nature of NATO's nuclear weapon use, a CNI action might
also be helpful. Mention has already been made of the possibility that the enemy
could misinterpret NATO's constrained use as a lack of resolve. However, a more
serious risk from the NATO political point of view is the possibility that the
enemy would mistakenly view NATO's limited use as merely a

risk of an enemy over-reaction because it implies that NATO has withheld some of
its potential for use if further escalation becomes necessary. On the other
hand, it could be argued that uncertainties in the enemy's mind concerning the
nature of, and reasons for, the particular tactical use in question might
contribute toward inducing him to make the political decision to cease his
aggression. In such a situation, a CNI action might be ill-advised if it reduced
these uncertainties.

CNI action per se. On the other hand, if no CNI action is taken or if it is
delayed until after the detonation, its value in minimising the risks of the
enemy misinterpreting the deliberately limited nature of NATO's use would seem
to be seriously degraded. As noted earlier, one possible enemy misinterpretation
could be the mistaken view that NATO's limited use was merely a step in an
uncontrolled and uncontrollable escalation process. If this led to a wide-scale
nuclear response by the enemy, the consequences from a NATO military point of
view could be much more severe than the military consequences of loss of
surprise.

20. A similar situation may apply with respect to the danger that the enemy
might exploit the explanation and warning received by reacting in a manner
jeopardising NATO's defence posture. While this risk cannot be denied, a failure
to send a CNI message to the enemy that contributed to the kind of
misinterpretation mentioned above could also result in the enemy reacting in a
manner jeopardising NATO's defence posture, or even overwhelming it.

21. Although a CNI action may be of considerable importance in political terms,
it may not be of the same significance in a military context. It should be
noted, for example, that a CNI action would not be the first or the only
indication to an enemy that NATO may be about to resort to the use of nuclear
weapons. Indeed, in support of its objective to deter aggression, NATO has

situation that exists at the time that a use decision is being made.

25. Selection of Addressees. As mentioned earlier, CNI messages can be addressed

come to the attention of the enemy and the governments of other countries. The
same might be true of a message addressed to other countries, particularly if
they made the information available to their own people. On the other hand,
there could be merit in dealing with the various addressees individually. For
example, such an approach has the advantage of allowing the message content to
be tailored to some extent to fit the particular audience. It also permits some
flexibility in timing, as well as in the various procedural matters that are
discussed later. Finally, it facilitites secret communication with the enemy if
that should be desirable, for example, to reduce face-saving complications that
might arise if a public announcement is made or if other countries become
involved.

NATO's resolve as well as its overall objectives, motivations and commitment to
controlled use of force in its own defence. The message should also reflect the

28. Timing Considerations. The timing aspects of implementing a CNI action can
be viewed from both substantive and procedural perspectives. In the context of
substantive considerations; mention has already been made of the statement in

in sufficient time before the actual use for him to appreciate NATO's
purpose(1). Consideration should also be given to the possibility that the enemy
leaders may need an opportunity to ensure control of escalation on their side.
Timing may also be important in sending CNI messages to other countries if, for
example, NATO wishes to give them the chance to inform their own people or to
use their influence with the enemy on NATO's behalf. On the other hand, the
earlier discussion pointed out that taking a CNI action in advance of actual use
could pose certain political and military risks.

in a way that provides sufficient time for effective exchanges of views on all
facets of the subject as well as for the essential coordination of arrangements
for any implementing steps. At the same time, care must be taken that this
aspect of the consultation process does not unduly delay or complicate
consultation activities leading up to the release decision itself, or interfere
with the effective employment of the nuclear weapons if an affirmative release
decision is reached.

substantive implications of current NATO policy on CNI questions provide a
useful point of reference for the examination of some related procedural

Procedural Aspects of Implementing a CNI Action

39. The aspects of particular interest in this regard concern the channels to be
used for communicating, the agents who would do the communicating, and the
coordination of multiple communications. It can be readily seen that these
aspects are inter-related and that choices with respect to any one are affected
in one way or another by the others. For example, selection of a particular
channel for communicating could, in effect, decide the choice of agent as well.
It should also be apparent that there is probably a great number of possible
combinations that could work effectively under varying circumstances. Since it
is not possible to forecast in any detail the circumstances that will exist when
occasions for communicating arise, the comments that follow should be seen as
points to be considered when choices have to be made, rather than as a
prescription of the procedures that must be used.

the procedural factors that political authorities would need to consider in
choosing specific courses of action in a real situation.

VI. CONCLUDING REMARKS

50. Since the adoption of the strategy of flexibility in response in 1967, NATO
has repeatedly stated in public and in official forums that, if attacked, it
would defend itself at whatever scale might be required, including the use of
nuclear weapons if necessary.

to induce an aggressor to make the political decision to cease his aggression
and withdraw by convincing him through NATO's determined but deliberately
controlled use of force that the risks of continued aggression far outweigh any
gains that might have been foreseen. The mix of conventional, tactical nuclear
and strategic forces maintained in peacetime by NATO gives clear evidence of its
capability to do this; NATO's political consultation activity and military
planning in peacetime testify to the willingness and determination of the
Alliance to follow through with its declared strategy. In particular, the
publicised activity of the Nuclear Plaming Group attests to the serious effort
undertaken within NATO to develop the conceptual and policy underpinning to
sustain a readiness to use nuclear weapons in its defence. Therefore, it is
clear that NATO has already given strong indications to a potential aggressor of
its intention to use nuclear weapons if the need should arise.

to resort to the selective tactical use of nuclear weapons, NATO would also have
