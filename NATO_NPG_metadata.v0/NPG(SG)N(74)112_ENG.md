Schlesinger to achieve 'essential equivalence' with the Soviet Union. THE NEW NUCLEAR DEBATE: SENSE OR NONSENSE?
By Ted Greenwood and Michael L. Nacht
THERE is a widespread and deep-seated dissatisfaction today with many of the
fundamental premises underlying American strategic weapons policy. The
dissatisfaction stems in part from disappointment with the terms of the arms-
control agreements concluded between the United States and the Soviet Union at
the Moscow summit meeting in May 1972. The treaty on the limitation of anti-
ballistic missile systems is sometimes said to provide more than a codification
of the immoral relationship in which the population of each super-power is left
hostage to the strategic nuclear forces of the other. The Interim Agreement on
Strategic Offensive Weapons is faulted for conceding numerical superiority to
the Soviet Union. The inability of political accords to keep pace with
technological innovation, it is argued, is rendering strategic arms-control
agreements obsolete almost before the ink dries. In part, too, the
dissatisfaction stems from the vigor of Soviet strategic weapons programs and
from apparent Soviet intrusiveness at the second round of the strategic arms
limitation talks (SALT II). Other aspects of Soviet policy—their stance during
and subsequent to the 1973 war in the Middle East and their continued rigidity
in dealing with the question of human rights within their own society—while
perhaps logically distinct from strategic issues, nevertheless reinforce a
general skepticism of Russian intentions.

This dissatisfaction has provided the context for a new debate over the size and
structure of the nation's nuclear forces. Origins of this debate may be traced
to statements about the need for increased nuclear flexibility that have
appeared in each President Nixon's State of the World messages since 1970. Last
year's articles in Foreign Affairs by Dr. Fred Iklé, now Director of the U.S.
Arms Control and Disarmament Agency, and Dr. Wolfgang Panofsky, Director of the
Stanford Linear Accelerator Center, provided additional stimulus. Dr. Iklé
questioned the desirability of continued reliance on a policy of deterrence to
prevent nuclear war and expressed the hope that alternatives would be found to
strategic doctrine and weaponry that, in the event of an attack, require a
massive, instantaneous retaliatory strike

The debate claimed increased public attention following two press conferences

release of his Annual Defense Department Report FY 1975. Secretary Schlesinger
called for increased targeting flexibility, more accurate missiles and larger
warheads as well as the development of several new offensive weapon systems.
These include larger intercontinental ballistic missiles (ICBMs) to be launched
from Minuteman silos, a mobile ICBM, the Trident missile and submarine programs,
a smaller missile-launching submarine, air- and sea-launched strategic cruise
missiles, and the B-1 strategic bomber. The Secretary of Defense is clearly
concerned with the recent Soviet deployment of a new, long-range, submarine-
launched ballistic missile and their extensive testing of four improved-accuracy
ICBMs, three of which have been tested with multiple independently targetable
reentry vehicles (MIRVs). He anticipates that the Soviet Union will eventually
be able to deploy many thousands of large and accurate MIRVs that would threaten
the American bombers and fixed land-based missiles. This capability would also
provide the Soviets with an edge in the number of deliverable warheads to add to
their previously acquired advantages in missile launchers and total megatonnage.
He finds it unacceptable for the Soviet Union to be able to threaten major
components of American strategic forces without the United States "being able to
pose a comparable threat." While he does not require complete symmetry between
the two forces, he does insist that all asymmetries should not favor the Soviet
Union. "Essential equivalence" is his stated objective.

Unfortunately, much of the discussion responding to the Administration's
position has failed to clarify the primary issues at stake. Terminology has been
inconsistently and erroneously employed; concepts have remained ambiguous; and
partisan views have tended to dominate analytical discussion. It is appropriate

II

During the period that Robert McNamara was Secretary of Defense, the primary
official justifications for the strategic nuclear forces were assured
destruction and damage limitation, with the former gradually rising in
ascendancy over the latter. In 1966 he stated that the assured destruction
criterion required the maintenance of a force necessary to deter deliberate
nuclear attack upon the United States and its allies by maintaining,
continuously, a highly reliable ability to inflict an unacceptable degree of
damage upon any single aggressor, or combination of aggressors, at any time
during the course of a strategic nuclear exchange, even after absorbing a
surprise first strike.

Secretary McNamara and his staff distinguished carefully between the assured
destruction criterion as a planning device for sizing the force and the doctrine
that was to be followed in the event of war. There was no requirement that
massive nuclear retaliation against industrial centers would automatically
follow any level of nuclear attack against the United States or its allies.
Nevertheless, the increasing emphasis on assured destruction in official
statements focused attention on scenarios involving massive destruction of urban
populations. Other scenarios were relegated to a secondary position and received
less serious consideration.

Use of the strategic forces for other than a massive attack was the subject of
intermittent discussion and debate during the McNamara period. During the early
1960s the strategic contingency plans were altered to include other possible
responses, and Secretary McNamara briefly advocated a targeting doctrine
intended to discourage attacks against cities in the event of nuclear war. But
concern for these matters waned throughout the tenure of the Johnson
administration. Only in recent years have they again come under significant
scrutiny. Various high-level officials of the Nixon administration as well as
the President himself have publicly expressed the need to provide nuclear
options other than the initiation of a massive nuclear attack involving large-
scale civilian casualties.

Three scenarios in particular have received widespread attention: a massive
Soviet attack directed against American strategic

forces; a limited Soviet attack designed to achieve limited political
objectives; and the escalation of a conventional or tactical nuclear war in
Europe. In each of these cases a response other than a massive nuclear attack
against Soviet cities might be desired. In the first instance, the United States
might wish to retaliate by attacking comparable military targets in a manner
that would not encourage the subsequent destruction of population centers. In
the second instance, a limited response might be called for, including perhaps
the destruction of military installations, selected urban areas, dams, power
plants or pipelines. In the third instance, means might be sought to influence
directly the outcome of a European war without encouraging Soviet strikes
against American and European cities. The strategic forces could be used, for
example, in coordinated attacks against communications installations,
transportation facilities, storage depots for nuclear weapons, petroleum
supplies and military hardware in Eastern Europe or Soviet medium- and
intermediate-range ballistic missiles.

In thinking about nuclear war scenarios, two factors need to be taken into
account: the nature of the targets and the magnitude of the attack. Targets may
be categorized as countervalue or countermilitary. Countervalue targets include
urban-industrial areas (commonly referred to as "countercity" targets) and any
non-urban civilian site of economic, political or cultural value.
Countermilitary targets include not only strategic nuclear forces (commonly
referred to as "counterforce" targets) but also the many thousands of other
assets that comprise a nation's war-making capability, including troop
concentrations, airfields, materiel depots, transportation networks and
communications systems. Hardened missile sites and command-and-control
facilities, an important subset of counterforce targets, are referred to as
"hard targets."

Any of these target systems could be attacked at various levels of intensity,
ranging from very restricted, using a few weapons, to very extensive, employing
many thousands of warheads. It is especially useful to distinguish four separate
categories of counterforce attacks, reflecting different political-military
objectives: limited counterforce, that seeks to destroy only a selected portion
of the opponent's strategic forces; substantial counterforce, that permits the
destruction of a larger fraction of the opponent's forces but is not intended to
reduce significantly his ability to

With these distinctions in mind, we can now address the substance of the current
debate about nuclear options. Although participants in this debate have rarely
afforded adequate attention to the views of their opponents, the absence of
empirical evidence makes a complete and systematic examination of all relevant
issues a precondition for reaching a responsible conclusion. It is to such an
examination that we now turn.

III

There are six fundamental issues that dominate the debate concerning the
increase of presidential nuclear options. The first deals primarily with
targeting flexibility and concerns the relationship between increasing the
likelihood of nuclear war and improving its controllability. The second focuses
on the extent to which improving counterforce capability is equivalent to the
pursuit of a disarming first strike capability. The third deals with the linkage
between counterforce capability and the nuclear arms race. The fourth addresses
the feasibility of conducting limited nuclear war. The fifth concerns the effect
of nuclear options on the credibility of American security guarantees to our
allies. The sixth deals with the question of whether advances in Soviet nuclear
flexibility require comparable measures by the United States. These will be
discussed in turn by presenting the arguments on both sides in an order that
reflects the manner in which they have been introduced into the debate.

The first argument addresses the advantages of being able to fight a controlled
nuclear war and the extent to which having such a capability increases the
likelihood of war. Proponents of targeting flexibility contend that since the
possibility of nuclear war cannot be denied, the President must not be limited
to choosing between surrender to an aggressor and touching off a massive
slaughter of civilian populations. They argue that, since there is great
uncertainty about how a nuclear war might start, responses should be available
to deal with a wide range of possibilities. Limiting targeting options to
strikes against civilian populations is said to be immoral, unwise and
unnecessary. If a war begins

on a small scale, there should be military responses that not only refrain from
inviting escalation but also provide incentives against it. Even if war were to
be initiated by a massive counterforce strike resulting in relatively heavy
casualties, an appropriate response must be available that would not
automatically trigger subsequent attacks against population centers. In short,
it is argued that contingency plans are needed to fight a nuclear war at
whatever level and in whatever way is required.

It is not suggested that such plans should be implemented as substitutes for the
pursuit of political and diplomatic efforts toward preventing, limiting or
terminating hostilities. Rather, they are intended to provide credible military
responses if diplomacy fails. In fact, it is sometimes argued that if a
potential aggressor knows that usable military options exist, he will be less
likely to initiate a nuclear war or to resist its termination. Targeting
flexibility is therefore said to strengthen the American deterrent. Indeed, some
would say that it is essential for deterrence to be credible.

On the other side, opponents of targeting flexibility claim that as nuclear war
becomes more manageable, it also becomes more likely. Increasing nuclear options
is therefore viewed as not only undesirable but dangerous. This increased
likelihood of war is said to come about in several ways. The argument is made
that as the use of nuclear weapons becomes more thinkable, it also becomes more
acceptable; the horrors of such weapons would be obscured or forgotten; and the
psychological barriers inhibiting political leaders from employing them would be
weakened. A false confidence might be generated that nuclear war can be
controlled and limited. In a crisis the very existence of a variety of seemingly
clear-cut military contingency plans might suppress the inclination to pursue
elusive and uncertain political initiatives that might otherwise de-fuse the
situation. A nuclear strike might therefore be chosen instead of a diplomatic
initiative. While such a course may seem very unlikely for rational leaders to
adopt, proponents of this view stress that there is no guarantee that
rationality will always prevail, particularly during times of crisis.

The second argument is one that dominated the debate about multiple
independently targetable reentry vehicles from 1969 to 1971. It centers on the
claim that seeking improved counterforce capability is equivalent to working
toward a disarming first strike capability.

improve and the number of available warheads continues to grow, the ability to
destroy an opponent's fixed land-based missile forces can eventually be
achieved.¹ Some view this development, especially when considered in conjunction
with substantial effort to improve antisubmarine warfare, as movement toward the
achievement of a disarming first-strike capability. Others claim that it will be
perceived as such by the Soviets, even if American intentions are otherwise.
Advocates of both views frequently suggest that heightened anxiety over the
vulnerability of land-based missile forces would increase the likelihood of
preemptive nuclear war. Each side might be tempted to fire its missiles before
they could be destroyed.

The claim is also made that improvements in counterforce capability might lead
to the adoption of a launch-on-warning doctrine. While the land-based missiles
are currently felt to be capable of riding out a nuclear attack, in the future
they might be launched early if only a small fraction were expected to survive.
This would increase the probability that nuclear war might begin by accident or
miscalculation. An extreme case of the launch-on-warning doctrine that is
sometimes discussed imagines a system that launches the strategic forces
automatically, without human intervention, upon receipt of electronic signals
from the early-warning satellites or radars, thereby placing the fate of the
world at the mercy of fallible sensors, computers and communications systems.

The equating of improved counterforce with disarming first strike has been
challenged. The claim is made that the redundancy of the strategic forces
precludes even a theoretical ability to destroy all land-based missiles from
constituting a disarming first strike. Those who make this argument frequently
stress that there is no technology, either currently available or foreseeable,
that would significantly reduce the invulnerability of ballistic missile
submarines. Even in the event of an all-out attack, sea-based missiles and the
portion of the bomber force that could avoid destruction by becoming airborne
upon receipt of warning would still be able to inflict overwhelming damage on
the attacker. The conclusion is that improving counterforce

For these reasons it is claimed that improvements in counterforce capability
should not provide an incentive to launch a preemptive attack or to adopt a
launch-on-warning doctrine.

The third argument suggests that the improvement of counterforce capability
would inevitably lead to an expensive and uncontrollable arms race. Given a
counterforce doctrine, it is claimed that the military services can readily
generate requirements for very large numbers of warheads and highly
sophisticated weapons. This is precisely what happened in the early 1960s after
Secretary McNamara endorsed a damage-limiting counterforce strategy, and it was
undoubtedly an important consideration in the abandonment of counterforce
rhetoric. Proponents of this view also foresee that if the United States deploys
highly capable counterforce weapons, the Soviet Union will respond by increasing
its own arsenal. In part this response might derive from a rising level of
anxiety touched off by American activity. In part, too, American actions might
reinforce the position of those in the Soviet government who favor weapons
deployments for other reasons. A variant of this argument suggests that the
large force requirements generated by a counterforce doctrine will exacerbate
problems of verification and asymmetry and thereby inhibit the negotiation of
meaningful limitations or reductions of strategic forces.

Improvements in counterforce-capability are defended against this charge in a
variety of ways. Some concede that an arms race

with the Soviet Union might result from such improvements, but they are willing
to accept this eventuality. They argue that the United States is wealthier and
technically more advanced than the Soviet Union and can almost certainly stay
ahead in such a race. The current problem, as they see it, is for the United
States to keep pace with the continuing Soviet advances in strategic weapons.
Others suggest that technological momentum or bureaucratic and domestic politics
have much more influence on weapons decisions than do actions taken by the other
side. Denying the validity of the action-reaction thesis, they maintain that the
pursuit of improved counterforce capability has little bearing on the strategic
arms race. Still others contend that if only minor improvements are made,
limited to flexible targeting and modest counterforce capability, and if an
image of restraint is projected, the arms race should not be stimulated.

The fourth argument against increasing either targeting flexibility or
counterforce capability claims that limited nuclear warfare is simply not
possible. It is asserted that no nuclear war can be fought cleanly and with few
casualties. For one thing, many military targets are in or near population
centers. Attacks against them would necessarily kill a large number of people.
It is frequently claimed as well that the number of fatalities resulting from
even a low-level attack using strategic weapons would be so large that
escalation into general and strategic warfare involving urban-industrial targets
would be virtually inevitable.

Those in favor of improving nuclear options respond that while it is true that
many people would almost certainly be killed in any nuclear attack and that a
small war might become uncontrollable, there is nothing inevitable about either
escalation or large-scale destruction of populations. It makes a very great
difference whether the number of deaths is measured in thousands, millions, or
hundreds of millions. Contingency plans can and should be designed that would
discourage escalation. The type of weapons available and the manner in which
they are employed are said to influence the number of fatalities and the level
to which a strategic exchange would escalate. Although some proponents of
counterforce targeting favor large nuclear weapons for use against hard targets,
others would prefer reliance on small clean weapons and precision accuracy in
order to minimize the collateral damage resulting from any nuclear exchange.
While the proximity of some military targets to cities

could therefore be made without inflicting enormous numbers of casualties.
Furthermore, it is suggested that the Soviet Union might initiate nuclear war by
means of a limited attack. In such a case the United States should not be the
one to escalate the conflict.

A fifth of issues relates to the continuing credibility of the American nuclear
umbrella. It is argued that the United States must maintain a flexible nuclear
war-fighting posture that could be employed in the defense of its allies.
Otherwise they might perceive a gradual weakening in the American security
guarantee. European countries and Japan might then loosen their economic and
political ties to the United States and seek individual accommodation with the
Soviet Union—a process referred to as "Finlandization." Some even foresee the
emergence of independent German and Japanese nuclear forces as an end result of
this process.

The contrary argument holds that although the credibility of American security
guarantees does depend on the maintenance of rough nuclear parity between the
United States and the Soviet Union, the conduct of diplomacy and economic
affairs tends to dominate alliance relationships. The maintenance of tactical
nuclear weapons and sizable conventional forces in the local theaters and the
linkages these provide to the strategic forces are said to be much more
important than particular targeting plans or levels of counterforce capability.
Our allies' perceptions are relatively insensitive to the detailed structure of
the American strategic forces.

A sixth argument is that the Soviet Union has or will have great targeting
flexibility and counterforce capability in its own strategic forces. The Soviets
now have sufficient warheads for uses other than assured destruction, and their
numbers will continue to grow as MIRVs are deployed. Soviet strategic writers
have consistently advocated a capability to engage in and to win a strategic
nuclear war. With its large missiles, its demonstrated MIRV capability and its
development of improved accuracy technology, the Soviet Union could eventually
have substantial nuclear flexibility. To some American analysts, this prospect
is sufficient justification for the United States to improve its strategic
forces. Others argue that if the United States does not have

comparable options, the deterrent against limited countervalue and counterforce

Opponents of increasing nuclear options claim that possession by the Soviet
Union of a particular capability is insufficient justification for comparable
American actions. This is particularly true of improved counterforce capability
since it is said to be expensive, of little value and a probable stimulant to
the Soviet-American strategic arms competition. These critics argue that the
likelihood of nuclear war would be less if one side rather than both possessed
broad nuclear options.

IV
Neither the arguments usually presented in favor of increased nuclear options
nor those against should be accepted in toto. Targeting flexibility is said to
be either desirable or not; counterforce capability is said to be either
essential or dangerous. But structuring the debate in these absolute terms
obscures the real issues. In the world of policy-making and force-structuring
the important questions are what degree of targeting flexibility is desirable,
how much counterforce capability is needed and what costs are acceptable for
such programs. A formulation is needed that integrates the advantages of
flexibility with those of restraint and which seeks to avoid the major dangers
of both. It would not be a policy of absolutes but one of compromise. It would
take account of both existing capabilities and aspirations to achieve meaningful
arms control.

The most important argument against increasing targeting flexibility is that it
would make nuclear war more acceptable and therefore more likely. Whether or not
this is true is impossible to demonstrate. One's conclusions on the issue must
ultimately depend on personal judgment. While we would not claim that improving
targeting flexibility would have no effect on increasing the likelihood of war,
we would argue that the effect is very small and easily overwhelmed by other
factors, many of which are totally unrelated.

The history of warfare suggests that decisions to initiate hostilities more
frequently than not derive from perceptions and misperceptions of political
will. They are rarely triggered by an increase in the real or perceived
flexibility of available weaponry. Particularly in the nuclear age, details of
military hardware and intricate war plans are unlikely to be crucial in deci-

sions about war and peace between major powers. The uncertainties and risks of
escalation would remain so large that the existence of even great flexibility
should fail to tempt political leaders into a precipitant use of nuclear
weapons. Equally important is the pervasive psychological inhibition against any
use of nuclear weapons. The precedent of almost 30 years of non-use remains a
formidable barrier against future use. The primary firebreak is between
conventional and nuclear weapons. Although there has been no lack of warfare
since 1945 in which nuclear weapons might have been used, the fact is that they
have not been used. Improvements in real or perceived flexibility would not
obscure the nature of this firebreak and consequently would not significantly
increase the likelihood of nuclear war. In fact, by permitting a controlled
response if deterrence fails, the credibility of the deterrent would be enhanced
and the likelihood of nuclear war might be decreased.

The arguments concerning counterforce capability deal with a different set of
issues. If either the United States or the Soviet Union ever developed a
disarming first-strike capability, the strategic balance would be widely
perceived as unstable. Even if it became feasible just to limit damage
significantly by means of a preemptive counterforce attack, there might be an
incentive to initiate a nuclear war in time of crisis. To prevent the other side
from achieving either capability, both countries would surely be prepared to
increase their spending on strategic forces. As Secretary McNamara once pointed
out, the damage limitation problem of one side is the assured destruction
problem of the other.

Neither of these capabilities is even remotely feasible, however. As Secretary
Schlesinger stated in the Annual Defense Department Report FY 1975:

Neither the United States nor the Soviet Union now has a disarming first strike
capability, nor are they in any position to acquire such a capability in the
foreseeable future, since each side has large numbers of strategic offensive
systems that remain untargetable by the other side.

The same is true of significant damage limitation capability. The bombers, sea-
and land-based missiles that would survive even the most devastating attack
would be more than sufficient to inflict overwhelming retaliatory destruction on
the attacker. This follows inextricably from the inherent difficulties in
destroying all three elements of the strategic forces and from the devastating

nature of thermonuclear weapons. It does not even depend on the operational
uncertainties that an attacker must face or the possibility that the retaliatory
force might be launched on warning. There appears to be no improvement in
counterforce capability, antisubmarine warfare or anything else that would
permit either a disarming first strike or significant damage limitation unless
force levels were drastically altered or reduced.

Even if the fixed land-based missiles are in the future perceived to be
vulnerable, there would be no incentive to launch them preemptively. The certain
knowledge that overwhelming destruction could follow such an attack would act as
a deterrent despite such perceptions. Moreover, the ability to launch the
Minuteman force on warning has long existed and will surely be retained. This
option and the uncertainty about whether or not it would be exercised are
important aspects of the deterrent. In no way is this meant to suggest that the
United States should create the sort of automatic system that critics of a
launch-on-warning policy sometimes imagine. The order to launch the force should
and undoubtedly will continue to be the President’s responsibility. There is a
big difference between maintaining an option to launch on warning and installing
a doomsday machine. The logic of the situation, however, may not prevail. Either
Soviet or American political leaders may become anxious about the improved
counterforce capabilities of the other side. Ideological distortion,
bureaucratic arguments and the momentum, emotion and ambiguities of political
relationships have in the past propelled decision-makers to formulate erroneous
linkages between counterforce and first strike. What needs to be stressed,
therefore, are the technological and operational impediments to the achievement
of a disarming first-strike or damage-limiting capability. At the same time, the

Theatre Nuclear Weapons and Europe
LAURENCE MARTIN
The decision to use so-called tactical nuclear weapons is very likely to be the
first form in which NATO would confront the fateful step to nuclear war. Indeed
it is precisely the belief that this step could be more easily taken than the
decision to unleash the strategic forces that accounts for much of the tactical
weapon's central importance in NATO strategy. It is therefore all the more
reprehensible that doctrine for the use of tactical nuclear weapons is ill-
developed and that the forces required have been carelessly designed and
deployed.

This is less surprising when one considers how the present situation evolved.
Nuclear weapons were added to the arsenal in Europe in some degree of
miniaturization became possible and as the period of relative nuclear plenty
emerged in the mid-1950s. Little strategic philosophy lay behind weapons that
were largely a technical spin-off from the effort to build a strategic
stockpile. The now all-important problem of escalation did not arise, because no
one believed that war in Europe could be limited. Instead, such a war would be
accompanied by strategic strikes, and 'tactical' weapons were therefore a
parallel and not a preliminary option. By the early 1960s, however, the emerging
sense of mutual vulnerability, and the consequent imperative to control
escalation, led to a revived interest in conventional defence under the Kennedy
Administration. New attention was given to command and control and some systems,
such as the Day Crockett, that were thought particularly incompatible with the
new emphasis on limitation of war, were withdrawn at an early stage of
deployment. Tactical nuclear weapons fell into dispute with those who governed
American strategy. This helped to inhibit the emergence of a coherent doctrine
for their use and halted the development of new tactical systems specifically

Present Deficiencies

The present situation is highly unsati factory as regards political doctrine,
military strategy, hardware and deployment.

The original idea of using nuclear weapons freely to fight a war in Europe has
been formally abandoned. Today it has become commonplace to say that Europeans
came to regard tactical nuclear weapons as essentially deterrent – an ominous
presence, a warning signal and a bridge to strategic exchanges – while the
United States, seeking ways to postpone the decision for strategic exchanges,
has inclined towards a more prolonged phase of nuclear warfare in Europe.

Geography undoubtedly ensures that particular options would have a different
effect on particular allies. The divergence of interest between the United
States and the Europeans is, however, exaggerated and distorted if expressed as
deterrence versus deterrance, for neither party has ever wanted either strategic
or local warfare, nuclear or conventional. The real debate is over what
constitutes the best deterrent and what would be the least intolerable strategy
to execute if deterrence fails.

So far, however, there has been no full agreement and, while NATO has agreed on
guidelines for initial use of nuclear weapons in 'militarily meaningful'
demonstration, the question of appropriate 'follow-on' use remains bogged down
in study, although military men naturally have plans to employ the weapons in
their possession. The absence of a comprehensive agreed doctrine papers over
differences in allied views, and some derive comfort from the thought that the
vagueness must increase Soviet uncertainty about our intentions. There are other
ways to create uncertainty, however, than being confused in our own minds and
disagreeing among ourselves.

Absence of an agreed and coherent doctrine is only part of NATO’s present
trouble. The stockpile of weapons that has grown up in such an absent-minded
fashion is deficient in many respects. Many of the weapons have high yield and
would pose serious problems of collateral damage if used in great numbers or on
certain types of target. These large yields are related to two other defects in
our present equipment: first, that many of the weapons lack the accuracy that
would permit the use of smaller warheads, and second, that the ability to locate
targets, particularly those that are mobile or in the battlefield area, leaves
much to be desired. This is a problem affecting conventional weapons also, but
where nuclear weapons are concerned there is the obvious but fatality
opportunity to compensate by massive yields. For nuclear weapons the problem is
compounded by an elaborate system of command, communication and control that may
impose delays which, for moving targets, constitute a further source of
inaccuracy. The complex system of command also raises severe doubts as to how
effectively tactical nuclear forces could operate across allied sectors of the
front.

Further cause for concern lies in the vulnerability of existing tactical nuclear
systems, both in storage and in the field. All such forces are, of course, very
vulnerable to nuclear attack, but the advent of precision-guided munitions (PGM)
raises the spectre of future attrition by conventional means. One current remedy
is dispersal, but this raises further problems. Command and control is
considerably complicated, and may in some cases be virtually incompatible with,
dispersal, at least given present technology. The quick reaction alert (QRA)
forces, particularly aircraft, are seriously hindered in exploiting mobility by
the control procedures for their weapons.

Today there is an added source of concern in the fear that nuclear weapons might
be seized by terrorists or dissidents. Although the consequences of such a
seizure would be small compared with those of fighting a war with nuclear
weapons, the danger may be more imminent, and a successful seizure or even an
alarm about security could seriously affect the political context of military
preparations. There is, therefore, a real need to re-examine provision in peace
and war for invulnerability, security and for disabling weapons in harmless
ways. It is very probable that this examination will suggest the strong
desirability of drastically reducing the number of weapons deployed in Europe.

‘Mini-Nukes’

Of all the schools of thought represented in this regrettable esoteric and

'mini-nuke' strategy. The basic premise of this widely publicized strategy is
that mutual vulnerability between the super-powers has decoupled – or, in the
self-interest of the United States, ought to decouple – the American strategic
deterrent from Europe. Consequently, any American nuclear strategy in Europe
that holds out a possibility of escalation to the strategic level is either
incredible or dangerous. Any American nuclear contribution to European security
must therefore abandon the threat of escalation, leading perhaps to strikes on
the Soviet homeland, and confine itself explicitly to defeating an enemy attack
in the field. Fortunately, according to the theory, low-yield, clean, accurate
nuclear weapons can do this with little collateral damage if used in a forward
defence from the very outset of hostilities. Indeed, it is argued that the enemy
can be stopped so close to his starting line that Western Europe would suffer
much less damage than in a mobile, conventional defence. Thus the Europeans can
forget their hitherto vehement dislike for nuclear, or indeed any, 'war-
fighting' emphasis in doctrine. The nuclear weapons involved could be of such
short range (obviously tied closely to the battlefield) that there would be no
confusion with strategic forces, no Soviet misapprehensions of attack on the
homeland, no possibility of any unauthorized deep strikes, and therefore no
danger of unintended escalation. Undoubtedly, the advocates of this denial
strategy have seized on some important truths. First, the objective of denial
rather than punishment deserves emphasis, because it looks to the preservation
of Western Europe and seeks a strategy worth implementing even if deterrence
fails. The more successful a strategy of denial, when implemented, the less NATO
will have to wrestle with the difficult problem of how to liberate overrun areas
and how to terminate a war on satisfactory terms when the enemy holds a good
deal of Western territory. Second, the underlying assumption that the essence of
success in limited nuclear operations is to keep the Soviet Union’s losses below
the level at which the Soviet Union loses incentives for restraint, is one that
all prescriptions must take into account. Third, there is indeed a good deal of
technology available which could be used to improve nuclear weapons. For all its
insights, however, the prescription of early, battlefield, use of 'mini-nukes'
cannot solve NATO’s problem. It cannot pass the tests of military effectiveness,
control of escalation or political acceptability.

As to military effectiveness, it must be admitted at the outset that nobody has
a firm basis on which to predict the course of tactical nuclear warfare. It
should be, however, that battlefield nuclear weapons may be less effective than
their advocates imply. There is doubt about the usefulness of atomic demolition
munitions on much Western European terrain. In many other battlefield weapons,
yields would have to be much larger than the prefix 'mini' implies, partly as a
result of the deficiencies in such attributes as accuracy and target location
and partly because many battlefield targets, such as armoured units, are
surprising hardness. If large yields were adopted, the image of a neat battle
with low collateral damage would be hard to sustain. The alternative of using
very large numbers of small warheads, while still entailing problems of
collateral damage and very possibly proving militarily ineffective, would also
raise serious problems for command and control: either one would disperse
nuclear weapons and devote authority to use them to a degree which many would
find alarming, and which might prove incompatible even with the requirements of
continued military co-ordination, or one would incur delays that would further
negate the utility of the weapons.

Admittedly, technical innovation can probably alleviate some of these problems.
Target acquisition can be improved. A circular error probable measured in tens
of feet would permit a dramatic reduction in warhead yield. Relaxing the
accepted military criterion of 90 per cent to 80 per cent probability of
destroying 30 per cent of an enemy formation would also often allow an order of
magnitude reduction in yield. While there are thus ways in which the use of
nuclear weapons could be made more effective and less damaging, it is by no
means clear, however, that they would suffice to turn the tide. Moreover, we
have as yet not considered the use the enemy may make of nuclear weapons. He
might decide to imitate our own use of battlefield weapons. If he did, it is not
certain that they would favour the defence; one problem is that even if the
attacker must mass his forces, their advance may pose a more difficult target-
acquisition problem than the prearranged, and perhaps more terrain-dictated,
positions of the defender. The current accepted wisdom, however, is that once
nuclear operations had begun the Red Army would conduct them on a massive scale,
with large yields and deep strikes. A NATO posture that had emphasized
battlefield operations in both doctrine and weapons might suggest that the
Soviet Union could do so with impunity, secure in the possession of a better
arsenal for operations far to the rear, and relieved of any anxieties about
further escalation by the decoupling inherent in the battlefield or 'mini-nuke'
strategy. For all these reasons the potential of the forward battlefield
strategy to curb escalation may be less than is claimed.

The single most serious doubt about the forward, 'mini-nuke', strategy concerns
its political acceptability. While the strategy is intended to be one of
restraint, it is also awfully one of early use. Within the assumptions of the
strategy, this is wise, for the effort to slug it out with the enemy on the
battlefield must start while a semblance of organized defence survives, and the
strategy is expressly advocated as a substitute for adequate conventional
forces. It is, in Mr Schlesinger's phrase, a strategy related to 'planned
insufficiency'.

But very early recourse to nuclear weapons is almost certainly not acceptable to
the political leadership of the alliance. A strong conventional response has
been consistently urged on the alliance since the early days of President

demand, more than a tripwire from her allies if she is to remain committed.

This is well recognized. Less acknowledged, but probably equally true, is
European unwillingness to embrace really early use of nuclear weapons as a
strategy for actual implementation. To most Europeans the strategy of using
small nuclear weapons to mount a forward defence without escalation implies a

deterrent aura of nuclear weapons in peacetime, they do not necessarily prefer a

recourse to nuclear weapons. Advocates of early nuclear use may regard these
attitudes as mistaken, but the attitudes have persisted so long in the face of

not easily be changed.

It therefore seems that a substantial conventional capability for what has been
called a 'stalwart defence' remains a necessary condition for healthy European-
American co-operation. A predominantly nuclear emphasis in defence does not only
compel early recourse to nuclear action in the event of major, premeditated
aggression. It also offers few acceptable options for preventing lesser
incidents, perhaps originally unintended, from developing in ways that might
encourage the aggressor to extend his operations or permit him to consolidate
quick gains. Furthermore, because conventional forces offer many obviously
useful options, a conspicuous failure to provide them would communicate
infirmity of purpose to the Soviet Union and breed an insidious self-doubt and
lack of mutual confidence within NATO. From the American point of view,
conventional forces also have the merit that a large part of the burden can be
carried by the Europeans themselves, while for the time being a nuclear strategy
would be primarily American. Blatant European reliance on American efforts for
defence is increasingly unacceptable to the United States Congress and, in the
long run, such dependence would almost certainly prove politically destabilizing
in Europe itself.

Providing adequate conventional forces has always been, and will continue to be,
an uphill task. Nevertheless, there are some encouraging prospects. The quality
of some Western equipment is superior, and reorganization might do much to
increase the teeth available in the initial stages of war. Recent technical
developments in PGM, anti-tank weapons and surface-to-air missiles promise
further benefits. It would be disastrous to fall once more into the typical
Western (and particularly American) fallacy of seeing salvation in a
technological superiority that has always proved transitory, but at present it
really does seem possible that the coming generation of weapons will inherently
favour the

defence. The dialectic of military technology may well upset this expectation,
and perhaps in short order, but for the moment it offers benefits well worth
exploiting. Provision of sufficient strength to create considerable Soviet
uncertainty about victory in conventional operations no longer looks quite
hopeless a task as it did a few years ago.

Complete success, however, is likely to remain elusive. The pressures against
military investment are powerful, and, even if all reasonable force goals were
met, there could be no certainty that Soviet aggression would be deterred or
that NATO would actually be successful in the ultimate test of battle, whatever
the planners’ calculations had suggested. Nuclear weapons also serve, as we have
said, to deter the nuclear forces of the Soviet Union. Thus, while theatre
nuclear weapons are no substitute for conventional forces – which, as we have
argued, possess uniquely valuable characteristics of their own – NATO does need
a nuclear force tailored to the European theatre. This force must match, and
therefore attempt to deter, the introduction of the Soviet Union’s own theatre
nuclear weapons and, if need arises, must be used in a conspicuous, deliberate
fashion, not precipitate but timely, to prevent the enemy from consolidating any
successes which NATO conventional forces cannot prevent or reverse. Such a NATO
nuclear force must attempt both to halt the aggressor’s advance and to issue
such a warning of dire prospects that the combination of momentary defeat and
prospective disaster will impel him to terminate hostilities on terms acceptable
to the West. To be satisfactory, the forces providing such an option to NATO
must be quickly responsive, fully subject to the highest possible level of
political and military control, and impervious to seizure or sabotage in peace
or war.

precipitate action. The other is a firm backing by selected strategic forces. In

Ultimate coupling is the American quid pro quo for European acquiescence in

commitment, however, once the intermediate nuclear strategies are put on a sound
footing and it is made clear that coupling means acceptance of the ultimate

alliance policy with a flat renunciation of coupling in principle: she can

process of escalation.

So far as theatre nuclear forces are concerned, our prescription suggests some
detailed characteristics of the weapons required. (1) They must be invulnerable
to pre-emption by conventional or nuclear attack and also immune to seizure or
sabotage. Taken in combination, these specifications suggest fewer but more
effective weapons, possibly based farther from the scene of potential
operations. If this is to be so, they will have to be highly flexible and
capable of operating across the component national fronts so as to affect any
sector of the battle. (2) While one purpose of the previous specification is to
avoid the need for precipitate action, the weapons must be brought into play in
time to affect the battle. This requires a capability for very quick military
action when the time comes. It also requires a timely political decision to use
the weapons. Because this decision is unlikely to be taken until the military
situation has deteriorated, probably the only acceptable index of this will be
some loss of ground. It is therefore imperative to provide broad and reliable
information on the - state of the battle to political leaders. Present
arrangements for the use of nuclear weapons characteristically envisage requests
from the front, based on the tactical situation, which are approved from above.
This procedure is very time-consuming, and the signal traffic involved may
invite anticipatory enemy action. A whole day may be a conservative estimate of
the delay in processing requests and responses. The only way to cut this
troublesome knot may be to abandon the idea of response to local tactical
exigencies and to let the decision for employment be initiated from above on the
basis of the overall strategic situation. (3) Even though efforts will be made
to be timely, it is clear that in fact the decision to take the nuclear
initiative will come rather late. If the expedient is to be militarily
effective, it will therefore have to be rather substantial when it is taken. In
this way one may hope both to shock and to hurt the enemy. (4) Given present
acquisition capabilities against mobile targets, the fact that the decisions
will come down from the top and in response to requests from the front, and the
need to strike heavily with relatively few weapons while avoiding collateral
damage, particularly in Western Europe, the emphasis must be on interdiction.
The need to reconcile this with a rapid effect on the course of the battle
suggests that interdiction should be moderately close, no more than 200-300km
behind the battle. Such a restriction would accord with the desire to
demonstrate that the action is a limited one intended to affect the situation
within the theatre of operations defined by enemy action. A considerable number
of such targets as airfields, communications, headquarters, supplies, reserve
formations and support weapons should offer themselves and be identifiable
within this area.

distinct, centrally-controlled, nuclear strike forces. By their nature, these

A good deal of work has to be done at once on the technology of control by

so far as actual weapons are concerned, however, much that already exists could

The operations envisaged here could be executed by Lance and by an improved
Pershing, made more accurate, mobile and able to survive. Some of the available
warhead technology able to provide special effects of enhanced or suppressed
radiation or deep cratering could be employed to increase effectiveness and to
serve the purposes of restraint by reducing collateral damage. Aircraft are a
more difficult case. At present, tactical aircraft dedicated to nuclear
missions, including QRA aircraft, are highly vulnerable and, once launched, pose
problems of control inherent in pilot initiative. On the other hand, the same
quality offers a capability against moving targets that may be especially
valuable near the front. A compromise may exist in releasing aircraft from the
nuclear and QRA mission, reducing the total number with nuclear roles – thereby
releasing them for conventional operations – and retaining a small number,
possibly held well to the rear, for special operations against important but
elusive targets.

Last, but very far from least, the United States has a vast resource in her
long-range missile forces, such as the Poseidon, which contain an immense and,
for present strategic purposes, possibly excessive number of warheads, partly
provided to penetrate ballistic missile defences which may never exist. These
missiles carry payloads well suited to major shallow as well as deep
interdiction targets, have adequate accuracy, great survivability and a
'footprint' fully compatible with the mission. It would, therefore, be well
worth considering this system as an answer to some of the specifications laid
down here. Particular thought must be given to the communication problem and to
deciding whether employing a potentially 'strategic' weapon would be a factor
inimical or conducive to the control of escalation.

Like all strategies for the use of theatre nuclear weapons, this one is not
without its failings. Given its relatively massive nature, it should certainly
serve its first purpose of administering a salutary and, one hopes, deterrent
shock. Would it, however, actually halt the enemy’s advance and serve the
purpose of denial? We need a careful re-examination of the effectiveness of
interdiction. There is considerable evidence that, if thorough-going, it can
reasonably quick to affect a battle. Assuming an enemy breakthrough, which is
the contingency in which the covering force is likely to be used, some very
large (and therefore relatively identifiable) targets would be provided by the
logistic and tank forces intended to exploit the enemy success. Clearly,
however, the covering force cannot turn the tide at the front instantly, and
many temporary variables, such as weather, may affect the issue. This suggests
the need to relate the nuclear covering force to proposals for reorganizing
conventional forces. Some conventional forces must be reserved for the phase of
'combined operations' after the commitment of the covering force.

There is in principle, however, no need to rule out the limited use of
battlefield nuclear weapons to stabilize the front, once the nuclear decision is
taken. If this is done, nuclear artillery is a strong candidate for this
mission, given the clear escalation limits implied, the investment that is made
in delivery and fire-control systems for conventional purposes, the accuracy
possible, and the relative portability of the ammunition (which might be held
more securely to the rear than is presently the case). Doubts remain as to how
effective and controllable these weapons would be, but if these can be assured
the objections to battlefield weapons voiced above are objections to the idea of
total reliance and instant use more than to the technique itself.
