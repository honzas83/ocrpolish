> # Draft Report on the ACLANT Follow-on Use Study
> 
> This document is a draft report by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Staff Group regarding the tactical use of nuclear weapons in the Allied Command Atlantic (ACLANT) area. It identifies areas for Ministerial discussion based on the ACLANT Study, which was the final of eight regional studies in Phase I of the Follow-on Use Work Programme. The report discusses the analytical approach used in the study, noting that quantifiable factors often overshadow intangible political and psychological impacts. It emphasizes the importance of timely release of nuclear weapons in maritime environments, where the time between detection and launch is often measured in seconds or minutes. The report argues that military commanders must receive release authorization from political authorities before specific tactical contact is made to ensure military utility. It also examines the release procedures, specifically the ROMEO and SIERRA procedures, and the potential limitations of the the Major NATO Commander's role as the releasing commander in fast-moving defensive engagements at sea.

Military Effectiveness
11. In line with its general approach to the analysis, the Study does not come
to any conclusion about the overall balance of advantage to the task force in
initiating the use of nuclear weapons. The Study does, however, conclude that
there is, in general, a significant increase in the task force's overall
defensive capability if nuclear weapons are used against enemy missiles,
submarines and surface ships. There is also an economy in weapon expenditure
which is particularly important at sea(1). On the other hand, the Study
emphasises that nuclear weapons do not guarantee a complete defence of the task
force and that if the enemy successfully delivers megaton weapons in reply the
task force will suffer severe damage and its arrival at its destination as an
effective fighting force will be in doubt(2). The Study also notes that, if
nuclear operations are initiated by the enemy, there are clear military
advantages if the task force can defend itself with nuclear weapons(3).
Furthermore, the Study suggests that in the situation envisaged if the NATO
force used nuclear weapons first then, given that even one successful enemy
delivery of a warhead of megaton yield could cause serious damage, it would be
essential for the task force commander to have authority for the continuing use
of nuclear weapons in defence(4).

Collateral Effects
14. The Study concludes(1), and SHAPE concurs(2), that collater effects should
not play a significant part in considering NATO's use of nuclear weapons in the
ACLANT area. Radioactive fallout seems likely to be the only hazard to civilian
populations in countries in the general area of the assumed operation, and this
hazard would be encountered only if the Warsaw Pact elected to attack the NATO
forces with megaton weapons burst on the surface(3). In this connection, it
should be observed that surface burst weapons are less effective than airburst
weapons in producing damage on ships(4). Consequently, the Warsaw Pact should
not be under any compulsion on grounds of military effectiveness to use surface
bursts. Parenthetically, it might be noted that current NATO military doctrine
seeks to ensure that NATO nuclear attacks against enemy submarines, aircraft and
ships would be carried out in ways that avoid collateral effects or, where they
are unavoidable, to hold them to a minimum.
