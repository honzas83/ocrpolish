> # Procedural Matters Concerning Communicating NATO's Intention
> 
> This document, an addendum to a working paper from the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group), discusses the procedural aspects of Communicating NATO's Intention (CNI) during a crisis or hostilities. It emphasizes that the process of reaching decisions on CNI questions should be integral to the larger process of consulting on the use of nuclear weapons, with the Council/DPC serving as the normal forum for consultation. The text details the necessary coordination between political authorities and Major NATO Commanders to ensure that military objections are considered and that the timing of communication is synchronized with weapon use. Regarding implementation, it explores various communication channels for reaching the enemy, including direct 'hot lines' such as the Washington-Moscow link, diplomatic missions in neutral capitals, third-party intermediaries like the International Red Cross or the Holy See, and news media. It also addresses communication to NATO's own populations and third countries through bilateral diplomatic channels, the United Nations, and official broadcasts. The goal is to provide a flexible and simple consultation process that can adapt to prevailing circumstances while ensuring speed, reliability, and security in the event of nuclear weapon deployment.

PROCEDURAL MATTERS

Introduction

1. The foregoing analysis of the substantive implications of current NATO policy
on CNI questions provides a useful point of reference for the examination of
some related procedural matters.

2. In view of the "negotiation while fighting" approach that is inherent in the
current NATO strategy of flexibility of response and the need to remain in
communication with the enemy in a developing crisis or in time of hostilities,
it can be expected that NATO members, during the on-going general consultation
process within NATO would be giving consideration to the means by which the
continuing dialogue with the enemy would be conducted. It is reasonable to
assume that this activity would lead over time to the evolution of a basic NATO
pattern for carrying out a dialogue with the other side and to the development
of related procedures for doing so. The same thing can be expected to occur with
respect to meeting the requirements for communicating with other countries and
with the public concerning NATO's purposes and activities. It is within this
context that the procedural questions with regard to communicating NATO's
intention concerning use of nuclear weapons must be viewed. While the ways in
which this specific communication requirement is met could flow from, and be
consistent with, the pattern that had previously developed, this might not
necessarily be the case. The comments on procedural matters in later paragraphs
should be read with this point clearly in mind.

3. The procedures by which NATO members carry out their consultation within NATO
on specific questions of communicating NATO's intention would be consistent
with, and part of, a larger on-going consultation process(1). Since the primary
characteristics of the nuclear consultation process

Procedural Aspects of Consultation within NATO
4. As noted in paragraph 12 of the Study it is useful to view the process of
reaching decisions on CNI questions as an integral part of the larger process of
consulting on proposals or requests for the use of nuclear weapons. Since the
Council/DPC has already been designated as the normal forum for Alliance
consultation on release requests or proposals(2), it would follow that the
Council/DPC would also be the normal forum for consultation on CNI questions.

5. CNI questions should be given active consideration at all stages of the
nuclear consultation process. When first indications are received that NATO
might have to resort to the use of nuclear weapons, the Council/DPC should
consult on what measures of this kind might be appropriate. The inclusion of CNI
questions in the consultation process assumes critical importance when specific
release requests or proposals are being considered. When a request or proposal
for specific use is received, consultation on the CNI aspect should take place
concurrently. There may be situations in which an assessment of the
possibilities of being able to communicate NATO's intention effectively could
have a bearing on the release decision itself. Conversely, the particular
characteristics of the proposed use could have a direct impact on the way in
which NATO would carry out the CNI task. In addition, consultation on the
modalities of delivery of the communication should be carried as far forward as
possible, so that implementing actions can begin without delay if an affirmative
decision is reached. There will also be a need for continuing consultation after
use has occurred in order to assess the reactions.

during all phases of Alliance consultation on CNI questions. Provisions have

Procedural Aspects of Implementing a CNI Action
7. The aspects of particular interest in this regard concern the channels to be
used for communicating, the agents who would do the communicating, and the
coordination of multiple communications. It can be readily seen that these
aspects are inter-related and that choices with respect to any one are affected
in one way or another by the others. For example, selection of a particular
channel for communicating could, in effect, decide the choice of agent as well.
It should also be apparent that there is probably a great number of possible
combinations that could work effectively under varying circumstances.

8. Selection of Channels for Communicating with the Enemy. The Provisional
Political Guidelines state that all political and diplomatic channels available
to the NATO governments should be exploited to communicate the statement of
NATO's intention to the enemy and to the plain NATO's actions to the rest of the
world(1). This is, first of all, an implicit recognition of the fact that NATO,
as an institution, has no direct links with the Warsaw Pact countries
individually or collectively and must therefore rely on the facilities available
to individual members. Moreover, the statement in the Provisional Political
Guidelines reflects the need to be ready to use any of the channels in view of
the probability that a number of them would be used in the

9. A variety of channels could be available to NATO for
communicating with the enemy. One possibility is a "hot line"
between a NATO capital and an enemy capital, such as the
Washington-Moscow link. Another possibility would be to
communicate through NATO and Warsaw Pact, diplomatic missions
that are located in the same neutral capital or at the head-
quarters of an international or regional organisation.
Another way would be to use the services of a third party,
such as a neutral country, the International Red Cross or the
Holy See, to relay the communications through channels that
are not directly accessible to a NATO country. The use of
news media channels, or the use of open broadcast should not
be overlooked. It is obvious that these various channels
differ in terms of speed, reliability, security and
accessibility. Some, if not most, of the alternatives mentioned
would be unsuitable, or at least risky, for communicating
secretly with the enemy. Other channels such as those through
third parties or through NATO-Warsaw Pact links in neutral
countries might be slower and more cumbersome than direct
channels. Finally, the reliability of other channels, such
as the news media, in relaying a NATO CNI message with full
accuracy and completeness may be open to question. On balance
the direct "hot line" linkage would seem to come closest to
satisfying the requirements for speed, reliability and
security. Serious efforts to ensure the continued
availability of this kind of linkage in time of hostilities
would be particularly worthwhile.
10. Selection of Channels to Own Public and Third
Countries. A large number and variety of channels are also
available to NATO for communicating to other countries.
The bilateral diplomatic channels between various NATO
capitals and these other countries would normally be available
unless disrupted unintentionally. Another possibility would
be to make contact with other countries through their
diplomatic missions at the headquarters of international
bodies like the United Nations which has the potential
advantage of facilitating the rapid dissemination of a NATO
CNI message to a large number of addresses. A final method
is to work through the news media.

Selection of Communicating Agent
12. With respect to communicating NATO's intention to the enemy, the distinctive
characteristics of the nuclear consultation process have direct relevance. The
General Guidelines for Consultation Procedure on the Use of Nuclear Weapons
state that "the ultimate decision on the use of a nuclear weapon will always lie
with the nuclear power"(1). This means, in effect, that "an affirmative release
decision would have to be taken by the nuclear power concerned before NATO could
be in a position to send a CNI message. Thus, an affirmative release decision or
indication on the part of the nuclear power concerned marks the point in time at
which the communication process with respect to a particular use can begin.
Since "the time interval between decision and use is likely to be limited," it
would seem advisable, in the absence of over-riding considerations, for the
nuclear power concerned to be the preferred agent to communicate NATO's
intention to the enemy. This step could be taken before during or immediately
after the use in question. In this context, it would be expected that the
nuclear power concerned would seek assistance from other NATO governments if,
for any reason, it was not in effective communication with the enemy.

13. In some situations there would be both the opportunity and the need for a
number of NATO governments, including both non-nuclear and nuclear powers, to be
involved in contacts with other governments to communicate NATO's intention.
This would be advisable in order to demonstrate Alliance cohesion and
determination, to e plot the greater number and variety of channels that would
become available, to share the communication burden, and to take advantage of
special relationships that exist between various NATO governments and other
countries.

14. With respect to communicating NATO's intention to the public, it can be
expected that each NATO government would have primary responsibility for
informing its own people. Many, if not all, would also be involved in providing
assurances and explanations to the public in the rest of the world, including
the population of enemy countries.

Coordination
15. Depending on the situation, the CNI task could involve communicating with a
number of different audiences, the use of a variety of channels, and the
participation of several, if not all, NATO governments. In such cases,
coordination of the various activities involved in implementing a CNI action
would be important to ensure that they are both effective and mutually-
supporting. At a minimum, NATO governments should have a clear understanding of
the thrust of the communication effort, so as to avoid the possible confusion or
misinterpretation that could be generated through the dissemination of
conflicting messages. It could be argued that the expression of the message
should be tailored to its recipient. However this could be dangerous, for even
if the substance of the message were the same, differences in tone and
explicitness might allow different interpretations and the enemy might mistake a
difference of expression for a difference of view.
