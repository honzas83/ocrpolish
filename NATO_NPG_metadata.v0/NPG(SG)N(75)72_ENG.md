> # Communicating NATO's Intentions – Procedures
> 
> This document, a note by the Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group), presents a provisional Working Paper on procedures for Communicating NATO's Intentions (CNI). It emphasizes that internal NATO consultation on CNI questions should avoid detailed, elaborate procedures to remain simple, flexible, and adaptable to prevailing circumstances. The document suggests that Alliance authorities may only require a brief checklist of considerations and actions rather than a complex set of rules. The normal forum for consultation is identified as the Council/DPC, where member governments can express and compare views. Key points for consultation include the desirability, timing, and contents of CNI messages, as well as the assessment of likely enemy reactions and possible actions toward other countries and NATO publics. Additionally, it highlights the importance of Alliance solidarity in CNI actions to induce an enemy to cease attack and withdraw. The document references the CNI Study (NPG/Study/46) and the Permanent Representatives' Report.

COMMUNICATING NATO'S INTENTIONS – PROCEDURES

1. The CNI Study states(1) that since the primary characteristics of the nuclear
consultation process are simplicity, flexibility, and a capability to adapt to
prevailing circumstances, detailed procedures for internal NATO consultation on
CNI questions should be avoided. The related Permanent Representatives' Report
adds that any CNI procedures should have these same primary characteristics. It
may be, therefore, that at a time of nuclear consultation Alliance authorities
would require only a brief checklist of possible CNI considerations and action,
for at such a time an elaborate set of procedures would be confusing and
unwieldy. The economy of the General Guidelines for Consultation Procedure on
the use of Nuclear Weapons may serve as an example in this regard.

2. The General Guidelines state that the normal forum for consultation will be
the Council/DPC, when member governments would be able to express and compare
their views. In regard to CNI – including warning messages – their views may be
sought, or offered, upon the following main points:

a. The desirability of CNI action. The views of the Major NATO Commanders will
be especially relevant here.

b. The timing of CNI action. Except in the case of general warnings it will be
important to establish the time relationship between the message and the related
nuclear use.

c. The contents of the CNI message. Coordination Alliance-wide will be essential
and the form of the message should be clear and incapable of misinterpretation.

d. Assessment of Likely Enemy Reaction.

e. Possible CNI Action to Other Countries

f. Possible CNI Action to NATO publics.

g. Selection of Communication Channels. Although the nuclear powers may have a
key role, other NATO governments should participate when possible.

h. Monitoring Enemy Reactions. This will be essential in relation to all CNI
actions, but especially for warning message.
