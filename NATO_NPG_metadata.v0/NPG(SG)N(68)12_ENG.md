> # ADM Time Factors
> 
> This document, a note by the Secretary of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group), accompanies a paper by Colonel Thomas of the IMS, which analyzes the time factors involved in the selective release process for Atomic-to-Delivery-Medium (ADM) weapons. The process is divided into four phases: Phase I (Corps – Army Group – SHOC Phase), Phase II (SHOC Interval Phase), the NATO Briefing and Consultation Decision Phase (Phase III), and the Release Phase from SHOC to Earliest Detonation Time (Phase IV). The analysis uses data from Exercise FALLER 66 and estimates for steps where data was unavailable or improvements in the ACE SCAR system have been implemented. The document details the specific time estimates for message processing, transmittal, and authentication processes across different levels of command. It concludes that the cumulative time from the original Corps Commander's request to the earliest ready-to-fire time is approximately 22 hours and 54 minutes, including a ten-hour estimate for the consultation phase. The author notes that future exercises like FALLEX 68 are expected to provide a more accurate picture of the current capabilities.

1. My next two slides will consist of an analysis of the time consumed in the
selective release process starting from an ADM release request initiated by a
Corps Commander and ending with the earliest detonation capability.

SLIDE NO.7 ON

2. I have divided the analysis into four phases:
I Corps – Army Group – SHOC Phase
II SHOC Interval Phase
III NATO Briefing and Consultation Decision Phase
IV Release Phase SHOC to Earliest Detonation Time.

3. As you will note, Slide 7 has only the first two phases.

4. On the right hand side of the slide are listed some time factors. The numbers
on the left are those time factors derived from Exercise FALLER 66. In some
instances no numbers appear. This is due either to:

(1) No data reported.
(2) Data available is invalid due to special artificial aspects of the exercise
affecting the times recorded, e.g. an event that could take place only at a
special time regardless of the pace of the exercise progress.

5. On the right are estimates entered in order to finally arrive at a total time
involved in the selective release process which will follow on the next chart.

Phase I

6. Step I. No data available. It is estimated that the time required to process
a message incorporating the Corps Commander’s justification would be 30 minutes.
The time involves only the message processing and transmittal time.

7. Item 2, 3 & 4. No data available. We do know that the same steps involved at
Group level are completed at SHICC level in one hour and thirty two minutes.
Therefore, it is estimated that 2, 3 and 4 at Group level would be the same.

8. Item 5. Average times for this step accorded in Faller 66 were 2 hours and 12
minutes. However, almost all SU-1 messages were sent over common use Teletype
with a FLASH precedence. When 3CARs was tested, however, the same procedures

Phase II

10. Step 1. This step includes decoding, message to message centre,
administrative recording of message, message to operations and finally message
to the Nuclear Operations cell. We have accepted the 42 minutes as valid and
assume that certain speed up measures could be taken to reduce the time.
However, any improvements would probably be measured in just a few minutes. We
would find it difficult here to justify any further refinement of our estimate.

11. Steps 2 and 3. We have accepted the one hour and thirty two minutes
experienced in FALLER 66 as probably normal. The staffing by the NOC preparation
of a recommendation to SACEUR briefing for SACEUR and decision would, it would
seem, consume the time indicated and conceivably more.

12. Step 4. No data available. We have estimated 1½ hours for either the
preparation of a message to NATC with complete justification or the preparation
of a briefing. If SACEUR goes personally to NATC, travel time would have to be
included.

13. As you can see - six hours and fifty-four minutes have now elapsed since the

Phase III

14. We estimate or assume that at least 30 minutes would be taken up in the NATO
briefing of a SACEUR request message or in the briefing by SACEUR or his

consideration of the justification after the briefing might well make this
briefing take much more time.

15. Step 2. No valid data available. We have selected ten hours for this step
without any feeling as to the true time that will be involved. If you think it
should be longer or shorter, add or subtract the time difference you desire.

Phase IV

16. Step 1. when the decision is forwarded to SHOC from NATO it will take,
average estimate, at least 30 minutes to compose staff and receive approval of a
SU-la message. This would be especially true if special instructions of an
unusual or complex nature were included in the release authorization.

17. Step 2. The one hour and fifteen minutes indicated for this step is a
reflection of the best time experienced in FALLEX 66 when the SCAR system was
utilized. Because of the improvements in the ACE SCAR system previously
mentioned we have accepted the best time figure. Only the experiences of FALLEX
68 will give us a better picture of the present capabilities.

18. This step involves message handling through the internal administrative
chain, coding, transmission, decoding and message processing of the SU-1A to the
NOC of the CRC.

19. Step 3. No data available. we estimate ten minutes for the Corps Commander
to re-evaluate his need (this step is necessary since approximately 19 hours
have elapsed since his initial request) and to formulate his decision. The
balance of the time would be taken-up in message transmission and dual
authentication.

20. Step 4. No data available. we assume 20 minutes to pass the message to
Brigade and dual authenticate.

21. Step 5. Same as 4. above.

22. Step 6. In our example (Slide 6) we selected Target 2 for the purpose of
this time analysis. we know from our computations that it will take 2 + 35
minutes from the word go for the earliest detonation time. Disregarding any
troop warning time considerations we add in this time.

23. This brings us to the cumulative time since the original Corps Commanders
request to the earliest ready to fire time of the ADR - 22 + 54. If we strip out
the 70 hours factor we used for the consultation and decision phase of the chain
we are still left with 12 + 54 minutes. Since our time calculations are based
primarily upon estimates, we look forward to the FALLEX 68 which we hope will
give us a clearer picture as to the actual times involved.
