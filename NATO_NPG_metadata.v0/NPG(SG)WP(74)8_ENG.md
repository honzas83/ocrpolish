> # Permanent Representatives' Comments on SHAPE Illustrative Study on Air Defence Use
> 
> This document is a draft report by the Permanent Representatives intended for the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Staff Group to facilitate Ministerial discussion on the SHAPE Illustrative Study on Initial Air Defence Use. The report outlines a hypothetical scenario involving a Warsaw Pact aggression in the Allied Command Europe (ACE) Central Region, where intensive massive attacks against forward defences and air bases lead the Supreme Allied Commander Europe (SACEUR) to propose the use of nuclear weapons against groups of three or more enemy aircraft. The purpose of the illustrative study is to provide examples of the analytical and procedural processes used by Major NATO Commanders to request the selective use of nuclear weapons based on existing political guidelines. The report identifies several areas for Ministerial discussion, including the application of Provisional Political Guidelines for the Initial Defensive Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons by NATO and the military and political implications of such a use. It emphasizes that thees use would be necessary to maintain a favourable air situation and prevent severe damage to nuclear strike forces and storage sites. Additionally, the report discusses the unpredictability of the time, location, and quantity of weapons used in air defence, arguing for the potential need for open-ended authorisation while balancing the need for political control. The document concludes by noting that SACEUR views the use of nuclear weapons over NATO territory as having a low escalatory risk.

I. PURPOSE
This report identifies some possible areas for Ministerial discussion during
their consideration of the SHAPE Illustrative Study – Initial Air Defence
Use(2).

II. BACKGROUND

which focuses on a specific air defence situation in the Allied Command Europe
(ACE) Central Region arising during a hypothetical aggression by the Warsaw Pact
that, from the very beginning, is characterised by intensive massive attacks

discussion of such studies provides a basis for political authorities to become

of decisions that might have to be taken and the possible consequences of such
decisions. The discussion also provides an opportunity for the Ministers, in
their dialogue with the NATO Military Authorities present, to explore ways to

III. POSSIBLE AREAS FOR MINISTERIAL DISCUSSION
Application of the Political Guidelines
6. The SHAPE Study is an illustration of a way in which the Provisional
Political Guidelines for the Initial Defensive Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons
by NATO(1) might be interpreted and applied by SACEUR in the given hypothetical
situation. Since the Provisional Political Guidelines state that the selective
use of nuclear weapons in the air defence mode might be justified, inter alia,
"to prevent a development of the air battle decidedly disadvantageous to NATO"
or "if the aggressor were initiating conventional attacks against the nuclear
strike potential of ACE"(2), we consider that the approach in the SHAPE Study is
compatible with political guidance and is a reasonable example of a way in which
the guidance could be applied in the given hypothetical situat'on.

Implications of the Recommended Use
7. The requested use obviously has both military and political implications. As
was the case in illustrative studies previously addressed by the Ministers, the
SHAPE Study tends to focus on the implications that are primarily military in
character, although some non-military aspects are also mentioned. With respect
to the military aspects, the Study stresses that the use of nuclear weapons to
augment the conventional air defence is necessary to maintain a favourable air
situation and to prevent extensive penetration of defences with

8. One distinctive feature of air defence use is that the time and location of
the proposed use cannot be determined with precision in advance. This is due to
the fact that both these elements would depend almost exclusively on enemy
actions which are beyond NATO's control, as was already noted in one of our
previous reports on illustrative studies(2). This particular feature of the air
defence, common to both conventional and nuclear use, leads to the consideration
of other elements of the proposed use that tend to be unpredictable for the same
reason mentioned above. For example, the number of weapons that might have to be
used cannot be forecast with assurance nor can the rate of use be predicted with
accuracy. The time period during which the enemy attacks might continue is also
uncertain. These factors tend to argue for an open-ended authorisation. On the
other hand, the request in the SHAPE Study mentions that follow-on air defence
use might be necessary which implies that the initial use request has a finite
quality. Although

Implications of Failure to Grant Release
10. In his release request, SACEUR indicates that without the use of nuclear air
defence weapons, NATO is most likely to lose a major portion of ACE nuclear
strike capability and attack air forces in the Central Region. He also points
out that, if Warsaw Pact air superiority is not significantly diminished, NATO's
conventional ground forces cannot restore the integrity and security of ACE, and
extensive Warsaw Pact ground penetration of the Central Region is most
likely(1). In addition to these consequences of non-use, political authorities
should be aware, although no mention of it is made in SACEUR's messages, of the
possibility that later NATO use of nuclear weapons on the battlefield would be
required, with a probable greater risk of escalation and civilian casualties and
collateral damage. Furthermore, the failure of NATO to use nuclear weapons in a
tactical situation that the enemy perceived to be a critical and deteriorating
one from NATO's viewpoint might be interpreted by him as an indication of a lack
of determination and cohesiveness on the part of the Alliance with consequent
possible erosion of credibility of the NATO deterrent strategy.

Communicating NATO Intentions

15. This paragraph might better be drafted when at least the first draft of 'the
study on communicating NATO's intentions has been approved by the Staff Group.
However, some points are tentatively sketched below_7

(a) In the case of air defence, the use of nuclear weapons in such an easily
recognisable mode not accompanied by any other concurrent use of nuclear weapons
might constitute in itself a sufficiently clear communication to the enemy that
the employment is strictly limited. This might made the necessity of an
explanatory message sometimes less cogent from a political point of view.

(b) From a military standpoint, the implications might be somewhat different.
Taking as an example the assessment in the SHAPE Study scenario that future
Warsaw Pact air attacks were likely to be conducted with the employment of up to
650 aircraft, and assuming that the enemy intends to conduct each attack against
a certain target with more than two aircraft (this assumption is not clearly
stated in the Study, but seems to be implicit), the communication that NATO will
use nuclear weapons in the air defence mode might induce the enemy to change the
pattern of his air attacks in a way that might in itself be advantageous to
NATO's air defence. For example, the Warsaw Pact might decide to conduct air
attacks with no more than one or two aircraft to reduce their vulnerability.

and consequently make the target less attractive for NATO. It is obvious that
this is likely to make Warsaw Pact attacks less effective, but it must also be
noted that such a tactic might very well be adopted, in an actual situation,
from the beginning of the hostilities on the mere assumption that NATO could

(c) In the context of the content of the communication, if any, that might be
made in a situation as that depicted in the Study, we note that SACEUR states
that he has no objections to a general communication to the enemy, but at the
same time requests that information concerning the specific mode of employment
not be divulged(1). We interpret this statement as an agreement to inform the
enemy as the intention of NATO to use nuclear weapons "in the air defence mode",
but not mentioning the details of the conditions for the use, e.g., to be
employed against only three or more aircraft. It seems to us that a more general
announcement, for example, the mere mention of the intention to "use nuclear
weapons" without specifying the mode of use, might very well create a wrong
Warsaw Pact perception of NATO's intention, which is exactly what NATO should
try to avoid.
