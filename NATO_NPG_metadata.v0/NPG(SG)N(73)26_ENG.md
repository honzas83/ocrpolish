> # Articles Related to Nuclear Defence
> 
> This document is a notice from the Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) dated March 29, 1973, which provides three annexes containing articles on nuclear defense. Annex A is an extract from 'Western Europe and America in the Seventies' by Ernst H. van der Beugel and Max Kohnstamm, which analyzes the security situation in Europe, the military capabilities and intentions of the Soviet Union, and the role of NATO and the United States. The authors argue that the American strategic nuclear force and a sizable conventional US presence are indispensable for the credibility of the nuclear guarantee in Europe. They highlight the Soviet Union's goal of becoming the dominant power in Europe by weakening the Atlantic Alliance. The text discusses the complexities of the 'Flexible Response' doctrine and the necessity of popular understanding for sustained defense efforts. It also addresses the paradox where the US nuclear commitment is questioned in Europe while the US conventional presence is questioned within the United States. The document explicitly states that the opinions expressed in the articles are those of the authors and do not reflect national positions or the International Staff's endorsement.

Extract from
WESTERN EUROPE AND AMERICA IN THE SEVENTIES
by Ernst H. van der Beugel and Max Kohnstamm
European security cannot be detached from the pattern of global security. We
will, however, concentrate on the security situation in Europe.

Here the basic facts are:
(a) The Soviet Union has achieved military parity with the United States and has
greater military capabilities than ever at its disposal.

(b) Soviet tactics are changing, but there are no grounds for assuming that the
Soviet Union's objectives have changed or will change considerably in this
decade.

(c) NATO has a dual function. Its forces must first deter the Soviet Union from
military adventures and secondly be capable of dealing with possible situations
involving armed conflict.

(d) For the first function the American strategic nuclear force is
indispensable; without a sizable conventional American presence, America's
strategic nuclear force would lose its credibility in Europe and (even more
dangerous!) in Soviet eyes.

(e) There is in the foreseeable future no credible European substitute for the
American strategic nuclear force.

(f) Consequently the question whether the second function could be fulfilled
without American conventional forces is not very relevant – as these forces are
anyhow necessary in order to maintain the credibility of America's nuclear
guarantee.

(g) The European contribution to the NATO effort is insufficient. It should be
increased, if not through higher expenditure, in any case through better

(h) This is essential, not only in order to maintain NATO's capacity to cope
with its two functions, but also for the maintenance of a political and
psychological climate necessary to enable the US Administration to maintain its
nuclear and conventional commitments.

SOVIET MILITARY CAPABILITIES

The Soviet Union has managed simultaneously: to build up a very considerable
force at its Eastern borders; to become within 10 years an efficient and
sophisticated global maritime power; to increase its military power on its
European borders. The necessity to arm against a possible conflict with China
has therefore in no way involved a reduction of Soviet capabilities on (and
under) the high seas and in Europe.

In considering the Soviet capabilities we do not take account of the Warsaw Pact
forces because it is speculative to assess their reliability from the Soviet
Union's standpoint. In this context, however, it is useful to remind ourselves
of the fact that the organisation of the Warsaw Pact cannot be compared to that
of NATO.

The military organisation of Eastern Europe is based on a network of bilateral
agreements between the Soviet Union and the East-European countries. In the
Czechoslovak conflict of 1968, the forces of the East-European countries were
placed directly under the Soviet High Command and the Warsaw Pact structure did
not come into operation.

Any negotiations between NATO and the Warsaw Pact should take into account the
fundamental difference between the two systems.

SOVIET INTENTIONS

Discussion of Soviet capabilities is on a factual basis. In discussing Soviet
intentions we are on less solid ground. It seems to us, however, that four
strategic policy aims can be clearly discerned in Soviet policy toward Western
Europe. The Soviet Union intends:

(1) to avoid a military confrontation with the United States;
(II) to consolidate and legitimise its power in Eastern Europe;
(III) to weaken the Atlantic Alliance, primarily by the removal of the American
presence on the European Continent; and thus

(IV) to become the dominant power in Europe.

Needless to say, both the tactics used in the pursuit of these objectives and
the priorities given to each of them change with the times. There are periods in
which the consolidation of its power in Eastern Europe is accorded priority even
to the point of giving up temporarily attempts to dissolve the Western Alliance.
It is hard to predict what priorities will prevail in which period. For that
reason Max Frankel's statement (New York Times, December 22, 1971) that
"Whatever long-term advantage they seek from a divided Europe and declining
American power there, it is thought to be secondary to the desire to protect
what they have, while the West seems willing to concede it" must be qualified by
pointing out that tactics change but political objectives remain constant. There
is no indication that these objectives are likely to change in a future relevant
to decisions in the seventies.

NATO'S MILITARY ROLE

NATO's strategic concept is difficult to understand and even more difficult to
explain to parliamentary and public opinion. This stems partly from the
intrinsic complexity of a concept which aims both at deterrence (that is,
avoiding conflict) and at survival in a military conflict should deterrence
fail. "Massive retaliation" was not a particularly attractive concept, but it
had at least the advantage of being clear. "Flexible response" is now the only
acceptable military doctrine, but it has the disadvantage of great complexity
compounded by being couched in a military jargon compared to which the language
of the theological disputes of the Middle Ages is a model of simplicity. It
nevertheless remains very important that NATO's military role and its
implications should be explained in a way understandable to the average citizen.
This is not primarily a task for NATO, but for national governments – and here
the record of performance is a bad one. This is a serious weakness because
defense efforts are by definition not the most wildly acclaimed functions of
government. In the long run no meaningful defense effort can be maintained
without a broad basis of popular understanding.

Flexible response requires a major nuclear strategic and tactical arsenal plus a
major conventional element. A substantial change in the total force of NATO and
in the relative strength of its components would create unrest not only within
the alliance but also, strangely enough, in the Soviet Union, because although
the Soviet Union wants to weaken and therefore to change the Western military
posture, major changes on our side tend to make it uneasy and nervous. Military
postures should not be rigid but adaptable to the development of military and
political circumstances. However, one should not lightlyhearted change a posture
that has thus far preserved peace in Europe.

The desire to reduce defense budgets is universal in Western Europe. It is
dangerous to rationalise this desire by the introduction of new strategic
concepts, invented not because the situation has basically changed but because
these concepts provide an excellent pretext for reducing expenditure. The most
recent and fashionable of such new concepts is labelled "crisis management."

THE AMERICAN MILITARY ROLE IN EUROPE

Clichés cannot always be lightly dismissed. It is a truism that 25 years after
World War II, the security of Europe is dependent on the American nuclear
umbrella and on a conventional force of now approximately 300,000 troops. It is
just as much of a truism that the domination of Western Europe's industrial and
human resources by the Soviet Union would be fatal to the security of the United
States.

However, this does not provide Europe with an automatic safeguard. In a
democracy the translation of a fundamental security interest into an adequate
defense posture involves a difficult political process. Most countries,
including the United States, do not go far in their history to find examples of
ties. The existing fundamental interests of the United States can not,
therefore, be viewed in isolation from the European defense effort. A feeling –
rightly or wrongly – that the burden of defense is unequally shared could result
in such strong political pressures for the United States to reduce its
commitments that this idea would prevail over what is and will remain a basic
American interest.

Western Europe and the United States are faced with the following rather
paradoxical situation:

On the one hand, the American nuclear commitment to Western Europe is not
seriously questioned in the U.S. However, in the light of US/Soviet parity, the
credibility of this commitment is being questioned in Europe.

On the other hand, the necessity for a sizable US conventional presence on the
Continent is not seriously questioned in Western Europe. However, the necessity
for this force is seriously questioned in the US.

In order to understand our complicated situation, the following points must be
underlined:

(1) The danger for Western Europe (and the United States!) today is not open
Soviet military aggression but the opportunities which a weakening of the
Alliance would give the Soviet Union to impose its will on Western Europe by
hook and by crook.

(II) As John W. Tuthill put it in a paper prepared for the Atlantic Institute,
"a substantial reduction in NATO conventional forces would induce a reversion to
reliance on almost automatic use of tactical nuclear weapons in the event of a
military action which was interpreted as an act of aggression."

(III) American conventional forces differ from their European counterparts in
the fact that they are composed of troops belonging to the only power in the
West which the Soviet Union considers as really relevant. These forces
demonstrate to the Soviet Union that aggression in Europe means war with the
United States. Favorable results from the German Ostpolitik and of future
East/West negotiations depend on their presence.

(IV) There is no magic in the figure of 300,000. However, NATO has preserved
peace in Europe because those men were there. To quote Tuthill again: "It would
seem reasonable to put the burden of proof on those arguing – with such
persistence – for a substantially lower figure. The issue is

That, then, is the American "quid."
What about the European "quo"?

THE EUROPEAN ROLE

We have stated our conviction that a) there can be no substitute for the
American strategic nuclear force in the foreseeable future and b) the
credibility of this force cannot be dissociated from the presence on the
Continent of sizable US conventional forces. We do not expect much difference of
opinion as to the irreplaceable nature of the US nuclear guarantee. From a
strictly technological or/and economic point of view, the possibility of
creating a European substitute may be arguable.

From the political point of view this seems hardly the case. Without a strong
central authority, empowered to act immediately if circumstances require action,
a nuclear force is useless because it is devoid of credibility. During the
period that concerns us here – this decade – Western Europe will not have such a
central authority. Furthermore, a European nuclear capability could not be built
on the basis of discrimination – neither the German Federal Republic nor its
Western neighbors, however, could accept German co-operation in these matters on
a non-discriminatory basis.

Even if by magic this problem could be solved, the present political climate in
Europe rules out the immense economic effort which would be involved in building
a nuclear strategic deterrent. The idea itself would be extremely divisive and
would split public opinion in our countries right down the middle, making
progress in European integration almost impossible. Again, this long-range
effort would postulate a continuation of the US guarantee – or Soviet
acquiescence – while the effort was being made. Both are unlikely.

However, this leaves open the question whether there should be any nuclear role
in addition to the American one, either for the nations of Western Europe
collectively or for France and Britain, the two countries that at present
possess nuclear capabilities.

These two forces differ, as regards composition and political motivations and
consequently also as regards deployment and relationship to the NATO command
structure. It seems highly improbable that either the French or the British
nuclear force will disappear – at least during the period under consideration.
Recognising that they cannot replace the US nuclear guarantee, there exist many
alternatives for their relationships to the Alliance, between themselves and
with their European allies. We do not believe that the complicated problems
inherent in each of these possible relationships will be solved easily or
quickly.

The difference between French and UK relations with NATO, the US involvement in
the construction of the UK nuclear capability and the hitherto insoluble problem
of decision-making in cases of emergency rule out the possibility of an
integrated, joint Anglo-French force – whatever one may think about its
desirability.

However, there may be possibilities for less far-reaching cooperation between
the two forces. Such cooperation would still pre-suppose the solution of the
problems just mentioned, with the exception of that of final decision-making.

In addition, the very awkward problem of discrimination vis-à-vis the other
West-European nations, and especially the German Federal Republic and Italy,
would require solutions which have yet to be found.

The only way in the near future seems to be careful nurturing of the procedures
which have been successfully initiated in the NATO Nuclear Planning Group,
aiming at better coordination of development and targeting and better
information of the non-nuclear NATO members. All the rest requires time and
prudence; no options, however, should be foreclosed in this slow process. We
therefore share Ian Smart’s conclusion (Adelphi Paper No. 78) concerning the
prospects of Anglo-French nuclear cooperation:

"Caught between the inadequate, the impossible and the incredible, the idea of
Anglo-French nuclear cooperation will still offer obvious attractions. To regard
it as a logical step towards the ultimate creation of a new Europe may not be
unreasonable. But to regard it as an immediate contribution to the cohesion of
the West or as a sure escape from the internal problems of alliance or community
would be obtuse."

For the foreseeable future, the European contribution to NATO and the European
share in the Western defense burden will have to be made primarily in the field
of conventional forces.

IN THE CONVENTIONAL FIELD
Current force levels in Europe are already at a dangerously low point, creating
serious security risks. Will it at least be possible to maintain our forces at
this level? The present political climate, a wrong interpretation of the
character of the present détente, the priorities given to domestic requirements
and a general lack of potential will-power make it unlikely that this question
will be answered in the affirmative. In order to avoid a further weakening of
the Alliance, this trend must be reversed.

It is a dangerous illusion to assume that an increased US defense budget and the
maintenance of the present American commitment to NATO can be combined with a
general tendency on this side of the Atlantic to reduce our efforts – even if
such reductions are more or less intelligently rationalised through the
formulation of new strategic concepts.

The only way to reverse the present trend and to revitalise the European effort
is through greater "Europeanisation" of our present contribution.

For the maintenance of a meaningful European contribution this is a matter of to
be or not to be.

At present, each country performs a national effort as regards land, air and
naval forces. On this basis it is unlikely to maintain our present capabilities
during the seventies, and impossible during the eighties.

Looking at the situation today and trying to cut through the smoke screen formed
by a really unbelievable multitude of committees, working parties and other
forms of national and international bureaucracies, one cannot help being
appalled by the inefficiency and duplication, and the near-total failure of
substantiating the concept of "balanced collective forces" adopted 20 years ago.
What is presented as a joint NATO effort generally turns out to be a shaky array
of national efforts, often inefficient and inadequate in themselves.

This situation must be improved, even though this will require a great deal of
focused political will-power, inventiveness and energy.

The obstacles are daunting. Institutional vested interests, traditions and
national industrial policies must be patiently and stubbornly overcome and
changed. Defense support is the obvious field – training, logistics, and
procurement of arms, leading to what François Duchêne has described as a
European Defense Support Organisation ("Foreign Affairs," October 1971).
Complicated multilateral arrangements with a maximum of countries participating
are certainly not the only road to greater effectiveness and efficiency in the
European defense effort. Sensible arrangements between two or three countries
might in themselves contribute to the solution of some of the most burning
problems.

Only quick and forceful action in this field can prevent a serious weakening of
the European defense effort. This effort is indispensable as part of the total
NATO defense effort and in order to create a climate in which the American
contribution, for which there is no substitute, will be maintained.

Closer European cooperation in defense is indispensable to European security. It
is therefore essential that in future East/West negotiations the way for such
cooperation remains entirely open. If our intention to move in this direction is
not stated clearly now, the Soviet Union may well try to block future
developments of this kind, on the erroneous grounds that European defense
cooperation would endanger détente.

However, European security, involving as it does the maintenance of a credible
American military presence in Europe, is not only determined by our politico-
military situation. The problems inherent in our security are closely linked to
the state of economic relations between Western Europe and the United States and
to the related but wider problem of reshaping the international economic order.

THE WASTEFUL WAYS OF NATO

By Steven L. Canby

Why is NATO outspending the Warsaw Pact – in terms of both men and money – while
buying less security?

This so-called 'people–PEMA paradox' (PEMA being acronynic jargon for
'procurement of equipment and missiles, Array'), which so perplexed the McNamara
analysts, can be resolved by examining the assumptions underlying NATO's
organisation for defence. The Pact, consciously or not, has developed operating
procedures and a force structure appropriate to its strategic requirements in
Europe; NATO, and particularly the American component, has not. Restructuring
NATO could release the resources necessary to implement Flexible Response and
forward defence and remove some difficult political choices. The crucial
questions boil down to whether NATO should opt for highly visible, high initial
combat forces (as the Soviet Union does), or retain low visibility, low initial
combat forces which are sustainable.

At present, I would contend:

(1) American forces are not properly designed to perform their assigned mission,
and many of our allies have inappropriately imitated a dated American military
model.

(2) Attractive options have been effectively foreclosed to decision-makers
because of the costly misdesign of American forces.

(3) Economic and systems analysis of the general purpose forces have focused on
secondary rather than fundamental issues.

The basic assumption underlying American forces is obsolete; that is classic,
unlimited conflict relying upon industrial potential, military expansion and
staying power. This underlying assumption permeates NATO – its strategic
notions, organisation patterns, resource allocations and manpower policies. Such
an encompassing assumption prevents us from recognising and assimilating the
realities imposed by nuclear weapons and new technology.

Broad Considerations
NATO's policy dilemmas - mutual and balanced force reductions, escalation,
tactical nuclear weapons, burden-sharing and the like - stem from the absence of
conventional parity with the Warsaw Pact forces. Conventional parity would
remove or reduce these dilemmas and provide NATO with new and attractive policy
options. NATO's conventional inferiority exists because its strategy, force
posture, and operating practices do not mesh with each other, whereas the Warsaw
Pact's do. Specifically, American and NATO forces are not designed for their
stated strategy, are structured on outdated assumptions, and use unnecessarily
expensive operating practices. Thus the people-PEMA paradox - while NATO's
population (154 percent), GNP (167 percent), military budget (120 percent), and
post-Vietnam military manpower levels (125 percent) are considerably greater
than the Pact's, the Pact nevertheless enjoys conventional superiority.

The NATO military objectives are deterrence and defence. Yet NATO, and the
United States in particular, maintain all-purpose, offensive-oriented,
expeditionary-style forces that do not provide the desired deterrence because of
their low initial combat capability and their destabilising tactical nuclear
posture. A force structure(1) oriented for deterrence and defence can be bought
at much less than present cost - as Soviet forces in East Germany prove.
Adjusted (equal front-line platoon strength, defined as infantry, tank, cavalry,
and anti-tank) peacetime division slices (the division itself plus its share of
non-divisional support personnel) total approximately 42,000 for American forces
in West Germany; but only 18,500 for Soviet forces.

Being poorer in resources, the Warsaw Pact's 'win strategies' are designed to
prevent NATO from developing its much greater military potential. Thus, the
Soviet Union restricted to a quick offensive strategy, and she has organised her
ground forces accordingly. NATO has potential quantitative and qualitative
superiority, yet it would probably lack sufficient time to develop this
potential if war were to break out. If NATO could defend itself initially and
therefore protract a war, the Alliance should ultimately prevail by transforming
its superior resources into a military capability. Provided NATO is concerned
only with deterring and defending itself against the Pact, as stated in declared
policy, then NATO's force posture in the critical centre region should emphasise
the defence and NATO need only to sustain its forces just slightly longer than
Pact's. The crucial point is that unless NATO wants to out-spend Pact in land
forces (the decisive element), NATO must

obtain its greater staying-power by organising properly for defence, but not, as
is currently the case, at the expense of initial combat power. Proper posturing
would give NATO conventional military dominance: a conventional capability at
least as great as the Pact's, and ultimate superiority should a conflict be
prolonged.

Military Strategies

NATO military strategy and planning has been shaped by out-dated concepts and an
exaggerated view of Soviet strength. The effect has been to rule out the idea of
a conventional defence and to emphasise the deterrent effect of nuclear
weapons(2). During the period when an American first-strike was credible, the
nuclear emphasis was cost-effective, despite its questionable premises
concerning the impossibility of conventional defence. Its adverse aspect was its
aggressive appearance to the Soviet Union, which reinforced Soviet deployment of
a countervailing and apparently aggressive (accentuated by the Soviet Union for
political effects) posture aimed at holding Europe hostage to American good
behaviour.

In the past, whether NATO's strategy has been called a trip-wire or flexible
response, its essence has been based on an asymmetrical power relationship with
the Warsaw Pact. NATO deterred the Soviet Union by strategic nuclear weapons and
the Soviet Union deterred the United States by conventional forces and IRBMs
aimed at Western Europe. This equation has now been changed. The Soviet Union
has attained direct deterrence against the United States while still retaining
her European 'hostage' capability. While no-one in the West can say for sure,
the Soviet Union may soon obtain a position of military dominance in Europe. She
has long had conventional superiority and a roughly equal - if less
sophisticated - tactical nuclear capability and is now approaching equality in
strategic nuclear systems. Any strategy NATO develops must cover these three
aspects. For military balance, both sides must be roughly equal in each or
compensate for weakness in one by stressing another, as in the asymmetric system
that has held until recently.

Something New

NATO could conceivably develop new strategies. A return to nuclear strategic
superiority is hypothetically possible, but no sustained American effort that
might provide a permanent advantage appears likely. Therefore, the search for a
new NATO strategy is limited to schemes for doctoring NATO's conventional and
tactical nuclear deficiencies within an over-all strategy of graduated
deterrence. Indeed, a strategy of flexible response without adequate
conventional forces is a potentially dangerous strategy for an alliance. Rather
than serving the cause of thoughtful meditation as believed by some scholars, it
could invite an enemy strategy of lightning invasion and subsequent negotiation
to fracture a disillusioned alliance.(3)

The general difficulty with dependence upon tactical nuclear weapons lies in
crossing the nuclear threshold. The particular difficulty of the 'clean and
small weapon' approach is the implicit assumption that both sides have similar
tactical nuclear postures, which is not now the case. The Soviet Union has
emphasised large-yield weapons with many of them based in the Soviet homeland,
thus virtually precluding the possibility of 'limiting' a tactical nuclear war.
Should the Soviet Union adopt similar clean and small weapons, NATO's original
problem of insufficient combat forces would re-emerge.

As a strategy, flexible response has three problems: the most obvious is the
alleged high cost of providing sufficient conventional forces. This problem is
the result of NATO having bought the wrong type of forces. A second problem is
that the conventional phase of a European war will require mutual restraint. Yet
the current asymmetries in strategic and conventional force postures would
induce instability and possibly escalation to nuclear weapons. The Soviet Union
has organised her conventional forces for a short war by buying forces with a
high 'teeth-to-tail' ratio in order to overrun Western Europe quickly. In war,
therefore, a high possibility would arise that either NATO would opt for
tactical nuclear weapons to stop or slow down the Soviet onslaught, or that the
Soviet Union would pre-empt by moving first. A third problem is that the
Europeans view flexible response as undermining deterrence.

Europeans object to strengthening NATO's conventional defence because they
perceive a number of disadvantages to themselves in such a policy. Most serious,
a strong conventional defence appears to them impractical without very large
peacetime budgetary outlays. They also feel that the incremental increase
continually urged by the United States actually run counter to

their preferred emphasis upon 'declaratory deterrence': they fear that if
conventional forces are available during a crisis, the exercise of strategic
options may be delayed, limiting the conflict to European territory, and
weakening the credibility of deterrence. Finally, Europeans generally associate
conventional defence with either World War II levels of destruction or, given
NATO's conventional weaknesses, tactical nuclear warfare on their territory.

One flaw in this European thesis lies in the assumptions about the destruction
that would be wrought by conventional warfare. A short conventional conflict in
Europe, regardless of its intensity for the combating forces, would be minimally
destructive to the civil populace and economic base. World War II was
destructive because of its duration, the need to destroy the enemy's war-making
industrial capacity, the extensive high explosive bomber and artillery weapons
systems employed, and the scorched earth tactics pursued by retreating
belligerents. None of these conditions would exist for a short war. Such
destruction no longer serves a useful military function and neither side now has
the weapons systems for large-scale conventional destruction.

Another key flaw in European perceptions stems from their cost assumptions. With
appropriate restructuring, NATO could buy a viable conventional defence that
would strengthen declaratory deterrence, since NATO's problem has been caused by
conventional inferiority within the context of strategic superiority. With the
conventional deficiency corrected, there would be no military asymmetry to tempt
the Soviet Union.

The strategic temptation which NATO must resist is to divert resources from the
centre to the flanks and to maritime forces which have little relevance to land
battle in a short war. The propensity to divert resources to non-critical areas
has been justified by clichés (e.g., referring to basic issues on NATO's flanks
in inappropriate tactical language, such as 'out-flanked', 'cut-off', etc.).
NATO's critical region is Western Europe. If Western Europe collapses, control
of the seas and defence of the flanks would be difficult and probably useless.

A final point of importance: the high expenditure for surface and anti-submarine
warfare (ASW) fleets (15 and 13 per cent respectively of total American NATO
costs) are inconsistent with NATO's strategic requirements. The fighting
functions of surface naval craft have become confused with

advertised nuclear capabilities in the confined Mediterranean for combat
purposes has had two adverse results: increasing the vulnerability of expensive
systems and inducing by their presence Soviet naval deployments. Thus, for a
dated nuclear mission, which was always more contrived than realistic, the
United States has fostered - probably irreversibly now that Soviet decisions and
investments have been made - a Soviet naval presence in the Mediterranean and a
mini-arms race in the Middle East.

The Short Versus Long War Problem

For the United States alone, the incremental costs associated with maintaining a
long war capability account for almost half of her total NATO expenditures.
Another quarter is allocated to secondary and contingency missions on the
flanks. Less than 30 per cent of present costs is directly related to fighting a
short war in the crucial central region. I do not mean to suggest that other
capabilities should be eliminated. They have important political functions, and
the possibility of a long war cannot be entirely dismissed. But the point is
clear: the United States is not allocating enough for the primary problem and is
allocating too much for issues of secondary importance.

To state the problem starkly: if NATO continues to ignore its short-run combat
weaknesses, NATO's long-run capabilities could be rendered useless by the Pact's
initial onslaught. A military force structure for a short war can usually also
fight a long war if necessary, provided long lead time items are hedged and
economic resources are available. But a military force that is structured for a
long war (and consequently weak in short war capabilities) may not survive to
marshal its potentially superior power.

Much more is involved than calculating the simple, non-recurring cost of
stockpiles. The entire structure of NATO's land and air forces and its maritime
strategies are involved. NATO's land forces are now backed up by resources
necessary only for a long war; its air forces are oriented to air superiority
and long-range supply interdiction primarily for long-run purposes; and its ASW
forces are useful only in a long war.

A Qualitative Example: Individual versus Unit Replacement

The most important operational difference between NATO and the Pact, from which
other more specific differences emanate, is the replacement system for wartime
combat losses. NATO emphasises individual replacement; the Pact, unit
replacement. NATO's combat units are built on the assumption that units ought to
be big enough to absorb substantial casualties and still function. Soviet units
are designed for maximum shock, are incapable of sustaining serious losses and
must periodically be replaced and refitted in toto. While the topic of
replacement systems may seem overly technical, significant organisational
implications follow from this choice.

Of the two systems, unit replacement is the more appropriate for tactical
nuclear conflicts, short conventional wars, and the focused
penetration/exploitation tactics of blitzkrieg-style conflicts. Individual
replacement is relatively attractive in protracted infantry wars where the
combat focus is diffused among all units and low casualty rates permit
replacement to be absorbed in experienced units. Individual replacement systems
are appropriate only under the assumptions of World War II, Korea and Vietnam:
sustained conflict and a tactical philosophy allocating effort (and supplies)
more or less equally across the front.

The replacement system also directly affects reinforcement schedules and the
size and type of war reserve stocks. NATO has large manpower and equipment
reserves, including a reported 5,000 tanks in war reserve stocks. But with an
individual replacement system, NATO's manpower and equipment reserves must
essentially stand by until losses in existing units create a vacancy or
replacement need. The Pact, on the other hand, organises their reserve manpower
as 'fillers' for a large number of extant peace-time divisions, most of which
are kept at low strength levels. As a result, the Pact has a large number of
organised divisions, which can potentially swamp NATO because their build-up can
occur more rapidly. Thus, the unit replacement system also partly accounts for
the Pact's mobilisation and reinforcement advantage.

Other Examples

While the replacement system probably represents the greatest single example of
potential savings through analysis of operating practices, innumerable other
conceptual blocks exist that are not so interconnected with strategy and force
structure. Many of these hang-ups result from the military's unwillingness to
realise that it lives in a world of opportunity costs. Perceptions held by
senior commanders, based on earlier experiences as company and battalion
commanders, have caused military decision-makers to opt for costly options at
the unit level which detract from overall system-wide effectiveness. For
instance, the 'equal mobility' thesis (that supporting units should have cross-
country mobility comparable with tanks) causes a preference for tracks over
wheels for combat and combat support vehicles and complex tactical trucks over
rugged commercial designs. Another example is the notion of 'the tank as the
best anti-tank weapon,' which has inhibited the exploitation of new anti-tank
and armoured vehicular technology. Still another is the 'flexibility of
artillery' thesis, which over-looks the cost and rate-of-fire benefits of
mortars and multiple rocket-launchers. Analysis of these and other doctrinally
entrenched practices could produce major potential savings. It should be noted
that the Russians economise by generally using these more rationalised
procedures. The Europeans in general fall between the extremes.

Although few NATO students have recognised it, NATO's military problem is
primarily structural. The search for greater uniformity and standardisation
among NATO members is one example of the failure to recognise this problem; it
sounds logical but proves erroneous in practice, and would lead only to the
universal adoption of an inappropriate American model, and a considerable
increase in support personnel at the expense of already scarce combat units.

There are limitations to the analytic tool kit of the systems analysts, and they
must be recognised. Systems analysis has not done enough analysing of the entire
system, relating the details of its functioning to the larger question of its
basic purpose. Systems analysts have relied upon brute empiricism, trade-offs at
the margin and resource imputs when they should have been looking at the quality
of the military institution itself. Questioning the system to be analysed is the
sine qua non of good systems analysis.

Such analysis, applied to NATO today, would show clearly that its structure is
ill-designed for rational missions in the 1970s, either stated or unstated.
Regardless of the outcome of larger issues that may be resolved at the summit
concerning military forces in Europe, the time is long overdue for a basic
restructuring of NATO from the inside out.

It's no yawing matter that Europe could find itself naked between the
superpowers in the 1980s. It needs to start thinking now about keeping its
nuclear forces alive

Sometimes the best thing a submarine can do is to come to the surface, when the
navigating down below gets too murky. Two things have happened this month which
make it sensible to bring the matter of Britain's nuclear striking force, and
the submarines that carry it, out into the daylight. One of these things could
make the next decision Britain has to take a good deal more complicated; the
other could make it easier.

The good news is France's election, and the possibility that as a result of the
swing it showed away from the old nationalist right wing of the gaullist party
the next French defence minister, when the new cabinet is announced next week,
will be someone other than M. Debré. The departure of M. Debré would at least
remove the most obsessively nationalistic and narrowly anti-American member of
the cabinet, and it might ease the way towards a new relationship between France
and the United States that could eventually change the pattern of nuclear power
within the western alliance.

The bad news, so far as it affects this particular problem, comes from the
resumption last week in Geneva of the second round of strategic arms limitation
talks between America and Russia. It seems clear that one of the Russians' aims
in the argument about missile strengths that could keep Salt-2 in session until
1977 is to make it as difficult as possible for the Americans to help their
European allies to keep their nuclear weapons up to date. The Russians will be
asking the United States to compensate them for any such help it may give to the
Europeans by allowing the Soviet submarine missile force to be that much bigger
than the American one.

The effect of this on Britain is to make it necessary for the British to have
some pretty clear ideas about what they want to do when their present Polaris
force starts to get out of date; and to make sure that both the United States
and France know what these ideas are.

There is no need just yet to take any actual decision about the next lot of
weapons, or even to decide whether there will have to be a next lot. If the
calculations of the International Institute for Strategic Studies are correct,
the present British force of four submarines can get a fair number of its
warheads through the anti-missile defences the first round of Salt negotiations
allows the Russians to have around Moscow and one other area of the Soviet
Union. The rest of the Soviet Union, of course, is an open target. That is
enough for the British deterrent to remain effective. The difficulty will come
if by about 1977 the second round of Salt turns out in such a way that the
Russians are able to build more anti-missile defences; or if, which is less
likely, the Russians improve their ability to track and destroy the Polaris
submarines to the point where the submarines might not be able to fire their
weapons at all. There is also the problem that, after the middle 1980s, the
hulls of these submarines will be getting dangerously old.

Two and a half choices
There are two and a half different decisions that Britain could take. The half-
decision is to improve the existing Polaris force by mirving its missiles: that
is, by fitting them with what the jargon cells multiplo independently targetable
re-entry vehicles, which would increase the number of warheads the Russian
defences would have to stop, and would make it unlikely that any improvement in
these defences could stop them all. That would cope with the danger of a higher
Soviet anti-missile net. But it would not increase the range of the missiles
that carry the warheads, and so it would not give the British submarines the
extra sea-room they may need to prevent the Russians tracking them down. It
would not change the fact that the submarines themselves will eventually be
unable to keep the water out. And since Britain gets the Polaris missiles from
the Americans (although it makes its own warheads to go into them), and since
the Americans are moving on from the Polaris to a better missile, the cost of
buying the necessary spaces from the United States may rise sharply.

The first real alternative is to invite France to join Britain in looking for
the successor to Polaris. The trouble here is that, for some years to come,
France will have nothing to put into the co-operative pool. By insisting on
building their nuclear force without American help, the French have produced a
missile with a much shorter range than Polaris, and at about twice the cost of
the British deterrent. Until they have closed the technological gap, if they
ever do, a joint British-French effort is unlikely to be much more efficient
than the British one alone. And the French experience shows that any British-
French attempt to build a new weapon without American help is going to be
enormously expensive. Of course, it would make very good sense for Britain and
France together to accept American help, and the going of M. Debré could remove
one of the obstacles to that; but in the end this is something that only the
French and the Americans can work out between them.

The other possibility is for Britain to make use of the technological
trailblazing the Americans have already done: with France, if possible, without
it if necessary. The assumption most people have made is that this would mean
buying Poseidon, the new missile the Americans are now fitting into most of
their submarines. But for Britain's purposes Poseidon is not all that much

Even if Britain does not have to take its decision yet, there are three
principles that should be clear in the mind as it starts the business of keeping

cost would justify a failure to try to keep Britain's modest but sufficient
nuclear power effective through the 1980s; there is nothing in the conduct of

to the French that the latter is as bad for them as it is for Britain. The
third, which is really for Mr. Nixon to think about, is that an American refusal
to meet a request for help from western Europe, because it slightly complicated
the Americans' own negotiations with Russia, would be the mark of so profound a
change in American priorities that it would inevitably affect the future of the
alliance.
