> # Farewell Comments to the NPG Staff Group
> 
> Colonel Robert M. Krone of the United States Air Force, serving as the Chief of the Nuclear Policy Section and SHAPE Representative to the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Staff Group, delivers farewell remarks on February 11, 1974. He reflects on his 34 months of duty, noting his participation in Ministerial meetings in Mittenwald, Germany, and consultations in Brussels, Copenhagen, London, Ankara, and The Hague. Colonel Krone emphasizes the importance of the NPG as a successful international consensus-building group and suggests it should serve as a model for other political-military consultative processes within and outside of NATO. He highlights the agreement between NATO's military and political sides regarding nuclear policymaking and asserts that the success of the Alliance depends significantly on how nuclear issues are handled within the NPG forum. Finally, he expresses gratitude to the Chairman of the NPG Staff Group and the International Staff, specifically mentioning representatives from Greece, Italy, Portugal, Turkey, the United Kingdom, and the United States.

FAREWELL COMMENTS TO THE NPG STAFF GROUP
by
COLONEL ROBERT M. KRONE, USAF
Mr. Chairman, Gentlemen,
On the assumption that you will offer me equal time during the cocktail hour
that follows I would like to divide my comments into two parts – saving the
informal ones for later, and thus not delaying the movement of the Group to the
bar, and expressing only a few reflections here in my last formal session of the
Staff Group to you, to the International Staff, to the distinguished
representatives of the twelve NPG nations here today, to my SHAPE, SACLANT and
IMS colleagues, and to those here today from the excellent engineering and
translation staffs.

I am leaving SHAPE after 34 months of duty as Chief of the Nuclear Policy
Section and SHAPE REP to the NPG Staff Group. Two weeks after I arrived I
attended my first Ministerial meeting at Mittenwald, Germany in May of 1971.
Since then it has been my pleasure and honor to be a part of SACEUR's team for
NPG meetings in Brussels, Copenhagen, London, Ankara, and The Hague where I have
enjoyed the lavish hospitality of the Governments of your countries, as well as
been able to participate in one of the truly unique consultation processes of
the world.

The NPG has meant more to me than just a job. I came to SHAPE from the
University of California where I had completed the course work, but not the
dissertation, for a degree in political science. It was a natural for me to use
the NPG as my dissertation subject and with the encouragement and assistance of
our Chairman I worked the official and classified NPG problem in the daytime and
researched and wrote the unclassified dissertation at night for the first year I
was at SHAPE. That was professionally very beneficial because it completely
immersed me in the NPG for my waking hours – which were quite long in those
days.

Because of that experience, and my continued association with the NPG, I have a
strong personal feeling about the NPG, the people involved in it, and the work
it produces. It may be the most successful international consensus building
group anywhere in the world today and, in my view, should be studied as a
possible model for other political-military consultative processes – inside and
outside of NATO.

I have enjoyed the confidence of the excellent SHAPE Staff and Command Group and
have gained immeasurably from my associations there as a result of SHAPE's work
with the NPG. I consider that there is agreement and understanding between
NATO's military and political sides concerning the ultimate goals of NATO
nuclear policymaking within the NPG. The resolution of what used to be called
"the nuclear sharing dilemma" in NATO, continues to hinge on the consultative
process within the NPG. I don't believe that it is an exaggeration to say that
the success of the Alliance depends, to a considerable degree, on how the
nuclear issues are handled within the forum of the NPG.

Finally, Mr. Chairman, I would like to pay a special tribute to you and to your
small, but highly capable, International Staff. I feel that Colonel
Dimitroulopoulos of Greece; Colonel Azzari of Italy; Mr. Meira of Portugal; and
Mr. Sungur of Turkey; Mr. Beaumont of the United Kingdom; and Mr. Woodworth of
the United States, who have all just recently joined the Staff Group, must share
the feeling that I had in my first few months of 1971, and the feeling that I'm
confident is shared by all the other representatives here today – as well as by
those many others who have previously served with you in the Staff Group and
gone on to other responsibilities – that the extremely sensitive and difficult
position of Chairman of the NPG Staff Group could not have been handled in a
more professional, unbiased, or diplomatic manner.

Gentlemen, it has been a distinct privilege to represent SACEUR and to share the
NPG experience with each of you in this group and I wish you all continued
success in your future work.

Thank you, Mr. Chairman.
