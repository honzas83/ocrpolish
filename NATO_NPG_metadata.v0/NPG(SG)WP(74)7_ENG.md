> # Communicating NATO's Intention to Use Nuclear Weapons
> 
> This document is a draft study by the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Staff Group examining the factors and procedural aspects of communicating the North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) intention to employ nuclear weapons in its defense. The study identifies various factors that political authorities must consider when deciding whether and how to communicate such intentions to an enemy, other countries, and the general public. It references the Provisional Political Guidelines for the Initial Defensive Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons by NATO, emphasizing the need to convince an enemy of NATO's readiness for escalation while minimizing the risk of misinterpretation. The document outlines a framework for analysis including political motivations, military objectives, and psychological implications. It compares the use of ultimatums versus informative messages and discusses the timing and potential disadvantages of communicating intentions, such as the loss of surprise. The study also addresses procedural matters, including the selection of communication channels and agents, and the process of consultation within NATO. The draft was prepared by the NPG Staff Group with contributions from the International Staff, the International Military Staff, SHAPE, and SACLANT, following a request from the NPG Ministers in May 1973.

I. PURPOSE
This Study identifies and examines various factors that political authorities in
NATO would have to take into account in dealing with the question of
communicating its intention to employ nuclear weapons in its defence. The Study
also discusses procedural aspects of handling the question in NATO.

General Observations on Current NATO Policy
6. It should be pointed out that the message foreseen in the Provisional
Political Guidelines for either initial or follow-on use would contain elements
of explanation and of warning. These elements might be equated to the two
requirements that are stated in paragraph 38 of the Guidelines (quoted above).
Explanation is necessary to contribute toward convincing the enemy that NATO's
intended nuclear use is controlled, selective, and limited in scope and
duration. This, in turn, should help to reduce the risk that the enemy might
over-react. At the same time, however, the warning element is essential to
reinforce intra-war deterrence, that is, to continue to confront the enemy with
a credible but incalculable risk of further escalation. In this context, it
would be important to avoid at least two potential misapprehensions from being
established in the enemy's mind that could be detrimental to NATO's overall
objectives, namely, that NATO's constrained use was an indication of a lack of
resolve, or that NATO's use was the first step in an uncontrolled and
uncontrollable escalation process, to which a wide-scale nuclear response to
pre-empt NATO's remaining capability would seem to be the only rational
reaction. Consequently, the question of whether to communicate NATO's intention
and the question of how to do this if an affirmative decision is made must be
seen as part of a process of balancing the continuing need for maintaining a
consistent frame of incalculable intra-war deterrence in the enemy's mind
through the undefined threat of further escalation against the requirement of
preventing an enemy over-reaction.

7. In addition to reflecting a general requirement to communicate with an enemy
regarding NATO's intention in connection with tactical use of nuclear weapons,
the Provisional Political Guidelines also point to the likely need to inform
other countries and the public(1). The possible requirement to communicate with
each of these audiences raises important questions of coordination that must not
be overlooked.

8. The interactions between the processes on the NATO side to communicate the
rationale behind the intended use of nuclear weapons and the processes on the
Warsaw Pact side of learning to perceive and to adjust its assessments
accordingly are complex phenomena. It is not unreasonable to ask whether the net
advantage to NATO of communicating its intention effectively is worth the effort
and the risks involved. There can be little doubt that NATO might find itself
confronted with a need to try to answer this question and there are several
reasons to suggest that the effort would be worthwhile.

9. Support for NATO Strategy. The fundamental objective of the tactical use of
nuclear weapons and the threat of further escalation is, according to the
Provisional Political Guidelines, "to induce the enemy to make the political
decision to cease his attack and withdraw"(2). The agreed NATO strategy of

IV. ANALYSIS OF SUBSTANTIVE IMPLICATIONS OF CURRENT POLICY

Methodology

13. Although, in the light of the foregoing comments, there might seem to be no
real problem in attempting to answer such general questions as whether or why an
effort should be made to communicate NATO's intention to use nuclear weapons,
several uncertainties arise once we enter the field of practical applicability.
Although such questions as "when?", "when to whom?", "what?" (content) and "what
side-effects?" (potential disadvantages from misperception), are all
interrelated and inseparable in reality, for analytical purposes it is necessary
to treat them separately. For the same reason, an artificial distinction is
drawn between the discussion of substantive factors and the examination of
procedural aspects. It should be clearly understood that they would have to be
viewed together as a coherent complex of considerations when and if the question
of communicating NATO's intention actually arises as part of NATO's consultation
and decision-making process.

Motivations and Objectives.

14. Before addressing the problems of content, timing and addressees of messages
to announce the use of nuclear weapons, it is perhaps useful to recall in
somewhat greater detail the political and military motivations for use and for
communication and to mention also some of the psychological implications. This
is done to expand on-the-general-implications of NATO strategy and to prepare
the ground for considering such questions as "what", "when", "to whom", and
"what side effects".

15. Political Motivations. Apart from showing NATO's resolve and ultimately
inducing the enemy to cease his attack, the selective defensive use of nuclear
weapons could also contribute to NATO's internal cohesion if brought about after
intensive consultation. Both motivational aspects, internal cohesion and
external demonstration of resolve, can be interdependent political factors. The
fundamental objective of NATO's use and any related communication would be to
end the aggression and to preserve or restore the integrity and the security of
the NATO area. In particular, NATO's communication endeavours could contribute
to establishing the conditions that would permit the enemy to make the political
decisions we desire.

16. Military Objectives. The NATO Military Authorities have stated and
commented(1) on various military objectives of NATO's eventual defensive use of
nuclear weapons: destruction, surprise, canalisation and delay of enemy forces.
Specific military objectives of the use of nuclear by NATO would be to avoid the
loss of own capabilities (attrition, particularly of nuclear means) and to avoid
the loss of critical areas.

17. Psychological Implications. Psychologically motivated features of NATO's
political will to communicate would be to inform in advance (thus giving credit
to the belief in mutual confidence in constrained escalation), to demonstrate an
understanding of the enemy situation and to implicitly express an expectation of
reciprocal

18. Other Implications. Last but not least, the framework for politico-military
action is shaped also by moral and legal implications. While it may be difficult
to define the degree to which NATO is morally obligated to communicate in war
with an enemy before nuclear weapons are used, a strong moral obligation might
be felt to exist with regard to informing audiences such as other countries or
the public. A somewhat similar situation may prevail with respect to legal
implications. For example, some provisions of the United Nations Charter, to
which all NATO members have adhered, might be interpreted to impose a legal
commitment with regard to communicating NATO's intention(1). Other multilateral
or bilateral treaties or agreements(2) might also be construed to carry an
implicit legal obligation to protect the integrity and continuity of mutual
communication measures during hostilities.

20. On the other hand, an obvious advantage of communicating secretly with the
enemy alone before any other steps are taken would be that the enemy would not
necessarily be put under time pressure, as is the case once a public
announcement has been made or other countries have been involved. Similarly,
face-saving considerations may not weigh as heavily if secret communication is
employed. Before exploring these options in greater detail, it may be useful at
this point to draw a distinction between two different kinds of messages that
could be used to convey NATO's intention: the ultimatum and the informative
message. An ultimatum would be used to convey a NATO threat to resort to
tactical use of nuclear weapons if the enemy falls to comply with a NATO-demand.
Whether NATO actually carries out the threatened use would be dependent on the
enemy response. In contrast, the informative message would be used in a
situation in which NATO had already taken a specific decision to

employ nuclear weapons and wanted to ensure that the purposes of its intended

21. Since an ultimatum is designed specifically to bring about a particular
enemy response, it is clear that it must be addressed directly to the enemy. In
this context, a question may arise whether it should be sent openly or secretly.
If sent secretly, should the fact that an ultimatum has been delivered be made
known to other countries and the public immediately, after an interval of time,
or not at all? If an enemy fails to comply with the NATO demand contained in the
ultimatum and NATO finds it necessary to carry out the threatened use, what
steps, if any, should be taken to communicate NATO's intention? The answers to
these questions would bear directly on substantive aspects such as message
content, methods of communication and timing considerations. Procedural aspects
of the consultation process might also be affected. While similar considerations
may arise in connection with informative messages, it is advisable to compare
some of the inherent features of the ultimatum and the informative message in
somewhat greater depth.

Ultimatum and Informative Message: Comparison
22. General Comment. In making this comparison, one should first note two facts.
First: while an ultimatum threatening specific use of nuclear weapons is
logically employed only in a wartime context and in relation to a particular
response which is demanded from the enemy, greater latitude exists with respect
to the NATO SECRET - 17 -

informative message. In fact, it might be argued that a good part of the thrust
of any informative message communicating NATO's intention would have already
been made apparent to the enemy, other countries and the public on various
earlier occasions: at present, in peacetime (declaratory policy); in crisis
(declaratory policy plus general warning); in the conventional phase of
hostilities (declaratory policy plus general warning); and just prior to or at
the time of selective tactical use (explanation and warning).

Second: whenever a specific communication is made before an actual use, one
consequence is obvious, namely that one of the principal elements of deterrence
- confronting an enemy with incalculable risks - is somewhat undercut by the
mere fact that the intended use is being very clearly and carefully defined.
Nevertheless, the fundamental purpose of deterrence, and its effectiveness in an
intra-war aspect, need not be affected adversely if the loss in incalculability
is offset by a gain in credibility, that is, by a gain in calculable "unbearable
risk" expectation on the part of the enemy.

23. It might be suggested at this stage that a deterrence policy that rested
mainly on the principle of incalculability might run into problems with regard
to the credibility of NATO's war announcements on the use of nuclear weapons and
might therefore have to resort to the ultimatum approach. On the other hand, a
deterrence policy based on a policy of calculable risk expectations on the enemy
side (declaratory policy) might imply the advantage of allowing a more easily
understandable and therefore a more credible

informative communication build-up with a potential enemy and with third
countries. Repercussions with respect to the public reaction are not evaluated

and the informative message would depend largely on the situation which prevails
when the decision will have to be taken, it is clear that the full advantage of
informative communication would only emerge if the decision to embark on such an
approach is made as early as possible.

24. Current NATO Guidance. It should be mentioned at this point that NATO policy
as contained in the Provisional Guidelines so far does not go into any detail
with respect to these matters. The Provisional Guidelines give no particular
guidance with regard to message content to other countries or to the public.
They state(1) that the message to an enemy should threaten further escalation if
the enemy fails to comply with the warning, a formulation which seems to suggest
a combination between actual informative credibility-oriented and prospective
ultimatum incalculability-oriented elements of content. This combined approach
may stem from the assumption that the purpose of deterrence can best be served
if both its elements, incalculability of mid-term expectation and calculability
of immediate action were to be stressed alike. In view of the earlier
discussions, a more differentiated approach seems to be advisable and feasible.

25. How can a distinction be drawn, in the light of earlier findings, between
the contents of the ultimatum and the informative message? While the drafters of
an ultimatum might have to think more in terms of blurred perceptions on the
enemy's side with regard to NATO's intention, the drafters of an informative
message to the enemy would be more inclined to start from the assumption that
decision-makers on the enemy side perceive a high correlation between what NATO
has communicated and what it has done so far. Although the actual content of
either of these message types would be greatly affected by the volume and
character of preceding communications between both sides and on the political
and military situation at the time when the decisions on message contents will
have to be made, some remarks with regard to the general distinctions can be
made at this time.

26. General Features of the Informative Message to the Enemy.
Although the volume, the duration, the originator(s) and the content of the
communication flow which would precede the informative message cannot be
estimated in advance, it seems to be advisable that the informative message be
clearly linked with earlier traffic so that the continuity of action on NATO's
side becomes obvious to the enemy. A demonstration that the risk levels taken by
the Alliance are carefully calculated and that the enemy's options are
intelligently perceived by NATO is likewise advisable. Furthermore, the analyses
of former crises suggest that it would be unwise to drive the enemy into the
direction of only one alternative, but

with regard to NATO's own political and military expectations and alternatives.
This could demonstrate NATO's intention and resolve to continue to pursue a
policy directed towards the termination of hostilities under acceptable
conditions.

27. General Features of the Ultimatum. In the same way as the informative
message, the ultimatum cannot ignore the content expressed in, and cannot
underestimate the climate produced by, preceding communications. However, the
threat in the ultimatum has to retain a degree of incalculability, if it is to
be effective. It should be recognised, therefore, that the ultimatum, by its
very nature, tends to create an atmosphere in the enemy's mind that may
encourage him to believe that his alternatives are narrowed down to one: pre-
emption. Great care must be taken to avoid this consequence. For this and other
reasons, the contents of the ultimatum should cover, to the greatest possible
extent, the same points that were mentioned above in connection with informative
message content. In addition, however, the ultimatum should be very explicit
with respect to the enemy response that is required to cause NATO to refrain
from the threatened use.

collateral damage would be indispensable ingredients of this kind of informative
message.

Situations Degrading the Communication of NATO's Intention.

32. It is conceivable that NATO could be the victim of a massive pre-emptive
nuclear attack by the Warsaw Fact which would be likely to provoke a general
nuclear response. Under these circumstances, it is very likely that there would
be no time to communicate NATO's intention to the enemy. Moreover, there would
seem to be little reason to attempt to do so in the context of showing NATO
resolve or gaining control over escalation. At that point, even the achievement
of the basic objective of NATO strategy to induce the enemy to make the
political decision to cease his attack and withdraw is called into question.
Having said this, however, there may still be persuasive reasons for NATO to
communicate its intention to other countries and the public.

33. Even in the absence of a massive pre-emptive attack by the Warsaw Pact,
circumstances could arise that would degrade NATO's capability or opportunity to
communicate its intention. This might be the case, for example, in a very fast-
moving situation where the time interval between a decision to use nuclear
weapons and their actual use is short, or in advanced stages of a conflict where
particular occasions for follow-on use might follow each other rather closely in
time. In these circumstances, major reliance would have to be placed on the hope
that an enemy would perceive such use as being consistent with a pattern of
determined but controlled defence already established by NATO. It would be
advisable, nevertheless, to communicate NATO's intentions after the fact,
particularly to other countries and to the public.

Potential Disadvantages of Communicating NATO's Intention.

34. Although the Provisional Political Guidelines stress those factors that
argue in favour of communicating NATO's intention, they also acknowledge the
potential risks that are involved. The discussion in the foregoing paragraphs
has alluded briefly to these risks as well, but it would be useful to examine
them somewhat more closely.

35. Potential Military Disadvantages. A major area of concern lies/the military
sphere. The Provisional Political Guidelines warn that communicating NATO's
intention in advance could result in loss of surprise and could raise the danger
that the enemy might exploit the explanation and warning received by reacting in
a manner jeopardising NATO's defence posture(1). For discussion purposes, two
general kinds of enemy reaction might be considered: specific defensive measures
designed to frustrate the particular use that NATO had in mind, or a wide-scale
pre-emptive move designed to escalate beyond the level of NATO's intended use.
Both of these reactions would be serious from a NATO military point of view, but
the wide-scale pre-emptive move would obviously pose the greater threat against
NATO's defence posture. Consequently, it is reasonable to ask to what extent
communicating NATO's intention in itself increases the likelihood that an enemy
would take either of the two actions mentioned.

have the political disadvantage of openly straining NATO cohesion. However, even
in the absence of such a message, the same strain is likely to be evident when
the use itself occurs. In some circumstances, advance communication of NATO's
intention to other countries could have the political drawback of allowing time
for protests and counter-vailing pressures to build up. Finally, there is the
psychological danger that advance information to the public could, either
spontaneously or under intentional stimulation, generate unnecessary panic.

Timing Considerations
39. The timing aspects of communicating NATO's intention can be viewed from

order to give enemy leaders the opportunity to give information and guidance to
subordinates that would forestall enemy over-reaction. Similarly, informative
messages to other countries and to the public should be disseminated far enough
in advance of the time of intended use to permit the information to be
understood and accepted and to allow for appropriate protective measures to be
completed.

50. The aspects of particular interest in this regard concern the channels to be
used for communicating, the agents who would do the communicating, and the

in one way or another by the others. For example, selection of a particular
channel for communicating could, in effect, decide the choice of agent as well.
It should also be apparent that there is probably a great number of possible
combinations that could work effectively under varying circumstances. Since it
is not possible to forecast in any detail the circumstances that will exist when
occasions for communicating arise, the comments that follow should be seen as
points to be considered when choices have to be made, rather than as a
prescription of the procedures that must be used.

VI. CONCLUDING REMARKS
61. Since the adoption of the strategy of flexibility in response in 1967, NATO
has repeatedly stated in public and in official forums that, if attacked, it
would defend itself at whatever scale might be required, including the use of
nuclear weapons if necessary. NATO has also widely publicised the objective of
its defensive effort, namely, to induce an aggressor to make the political
decision to cease his aggression and withdraw by convincing him through NATO's
determined but deliberately controlled use of force that the risks of continued
aggression far outweigh any gains that might have been foreseen. The mix of
conventional, tactical nuclear and strategic forces maintained in peacetime by
NATO gives visible evidence of its capability to do this; NATO's political
consultation activity and military planning in peacetime testify to the
willingness and determination of the Alliance to follow through with its
declared

to a potential aggressor of its intention to use nuclear weapons if the need
should arise.

do so in order to reinforce the evidence of NATO's resolve but at the same time
to reduce the risk of enemy over-reaction as a result of failure to appreciate
the controlled, limited character of NATO's nuclear effort. On the other hand,

Political authorities should also recognise the importance of beginning their
consideration of the communication question early, in parallel with their
consultation on the nuclear weapon use itself. Finally, they should understand
that a timely decision on the communication question would be essential so that
the complex but necessary coordination arrangements can be worked out in time to
permit the communication effort to be implemented effectively and without delay,
in the event that the nuclear power concerned approves the release request.
