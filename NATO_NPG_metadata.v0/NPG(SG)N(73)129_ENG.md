> # Articles Related to Nuclear Defence
> 
> The document consists of a cover note signed by H.B. Seim from the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) dated December 12, 1972, and the first part of an article by Colonel René David titled 'La Bombe à Neutrons: Mythe ou Réalité'. The cover note specifies that the opinions expressed in the attached articles are those of the authors and do not represent national positions or the endorsement of the International Staff. The articles included are 'La Bombe à Neutrons: Mythe ou Réalité' by Colonel René David, 'Over the Threshold' by William Beecher, and 'Smart Weapons: A Coming Revolution' by Colonel John T. Burke. Colonel René David's article discusses the technical and tactical aspects of the neutron bomb, arguing that it could allow Western Europe to overcome tactical military inferiority without the massive destruction and residual radioactivity associated with traditional nuclear weapons. He explores the ideal tactical weapon as one that is anti-force rather than anti-city, focusing on high instant neutron radiation with minimal blast and heat effects. The text examines the historical context of neutron bomb research in the United States and the Soviet Union, and the context of the NATO flexible response strategy.

LA BOMBE A NEUTRONS: MYTHE OU REALITE
par le Colonel René David
Revenant périodiquement à l'actualité, mystérieuse quant à son existence,
paradoxale dans ses effets, la bombe à neutrons semble appar- tenir au domaine
de la science-fiction. En fait cette arme poten- tielle, « nec plus ultra » de
la technique, pourrait occuper dans l'arsenal nucléaire une place à part : elle
offre en effet la perspective du pro- gression éventuel d'une stratégie de
dissuasion par une stratégie d'em- ploi. Mais, si elle n'est pas inconcevable,
est-elle réalisable...?

ÉTUDIE dès 1951 par les Soviétiques, considérée comme résolu « sur le papier »
en 1959 par Teller, le problème de la bombe à neutrons a suscité aux États-Unis
jusqu'en 1963 de nombreuses polémiques, puis semble avoir été mis en sommeil.
Les difficultés tech- niques, l'inutilité de son emploi dans le cadre de la
stratégie améri- caine alors en vigueur, ou le souci politique d'éviter la
multiplication des nations à capacité thermuncléaire ont pu motiver, à l'époque,
le freinage des études en cours. Toutefois, depuis un peu plus d'un an, les
armes à rayonnement, dont l'intérêt réside dans la possibilité de prolonger une
stratégie de dissuasion par une stratégie d'emploi, retien- dent à nouveau
directement ou indirectement l'attention des experts militaires. Les raisons de
ce reventement peuvent être attribuées aux profonds changements intervenus au
cours des dernières années dans le rapport des forces en présence : crise de
l'O.T.A.N., parité de l'U.R.S.S. et des États-Unis dans le domaine des armes
stratégiques, éventualité d'une réduction des forces américaines en Europe.
Destinée à un emploi tactique, utilisable seulement dans des limites de
puissance très faibles, la bombe à neutrons est néanmoins consi- dérée comme
devant entraîner un bouleversement stratégique com- parable à celui
qu'occasionnait la bombe H. Or, si les articles traitant du sujet (cf. entre
autres « On tactical nuclear war », Revue Militaire Générale, janvier 1971)
développent largement l'aspect tactique des bombes à neutrons, ils laissent par
contre dans l'ombre les phénomènes physiques, pourtant essentiels à la
compréhension des raisons qui militeront en faveur de la mise au point de ces
armes nouvelles. Il est

ASPECT TACTIQUE
La bombe à neutrons, selon certains analystes américains, permet de pallier
l'inferiorité militaire tactique de l'Europe occidentale inhérente à
l'insuffisance de ses effectifs et à l'exiguïté du théâtre d'opérations
européen, sans présenter les inconvenants des armes nucléaires existantes.

Ces inconvenants sont bien connus :
— discontinuité qualitative, liée essentiellement aux phénomènes radioactifs
résiduels qui entraînent un risque d'irréversibilité du conflit ;

— discontinuité quantitative dans l'énergie unitaire (W ≈ 10 kt), qui en rend
l'emploi délicat et impose le choix d'objectifs suffisamment importants ;

— difficulté d'emploi en cas d'imbrication des dispositifs adverses et amis,
situation que recherchera évidemment l'agresseur ;

— probabilité élevée de destructions considérables, eu égard au caractère
fortement urbanisé de l'Europe occidentale ;

— importance des délais de mise en œuvre, conséquence de l'autorisation d'emploi
par un échelon de commandement élevé. Cette servitude est particulièrement
contraignante car, contrairement à l'action stratégique, programmée de longue
date, l'action tactique devra être menée en fonction du contexte opérationnel et
risque donc de s'écart er notamment des thémes habituellement étudiés dans les
Ecoles de guerre.

L'absence de données opérationnelles ne permettant pas de définir une doctrine
d'emploi précise, la dotation a priori des armées en armes nucléaires tactiques
revient à attribuer à celles-ci un rôle comparable à celui des armes
stratégiques : dissuader l'agresseur de lancer des attaques conventionnelles
massives. Ainsi, la menace nucléaire tactique, en obligeant l'adversaire à
diluer ses forces, apporte une certaine protection contre le procédé du « fait
accompli » qui exige une manœuvre puissante et rapide. Par contre, dès que l'on
en vient à l'emploi effectif, on peut considérer que la dissuasion, sur le plan
tactique comme sur le plan stratégique, à échou.

On comprend donc, surtout depuis l'adoption de la stratégie dite de la «
flexible response », que les États-Unis aient limité l'utilisation éventuelle
des armes nucléaires tactiques aux cas de nécessité absolue.

Pour obtenir la mise hors combat des troupes ennemies, principal objectif de la

occupants ; le rayon de neutralisation d'une arme coincidant donc généralement

surpissance des armes nucléaires paiera alors indépendamment de la contamination
radioactive, d'un taux de destruction élevé du territoire envahi.

L'arme tactique idéale, qui se devrait d'être antiforce et non antici, serait
ainsi caractérisée par un rayon d'efficacité antipersonnel adaptable à
l'objectif à traiter, quelle que soit son importance, et par un rayon de
destructions matérielles nul.

Une solution consiste à mettre au point une arme nucléaire présentant des
caractéristiques bien particulières :

— majeure partie de l'énergie libérée sous forme d'une émission neutronique
instantanée à grand rayon d'efficacité antipersonnel (1);

— effets de souffle et de chaleur inférieurs à ceux d'une arme à explosif
classique de puissance équivalente ;

— effets de radioactivité résiduelle éliminés ou tout au moins fortement réduits
;

— rayon létal adaptable aux objectifs tactiques du champ de bataille
(déploiement de chars, retranchements en zones urbanisées ou boisées, etc.), aux
objectifs des armées ennemies (dépôts, chaînes logistiques) situés en territoire
ami et, éventuellement, à des objectifs navals ;

— un rayon de dégâts matériels très supérieur, à poids égal, à celui d'une arme
à explosif classique, par conversion de la manifestation neutronique en effets
mécaniques (attaque d'un objectif très « dur », création d'obstacles par minage)
;

(1) La protection fournie par un blindage ou un bâtiment courant est faible.
Dans une agglomération, seules les populations civiles réfugiées dans les caves
ou les abris atomiques seraient protégées efficacement.

ASPECT TECHNIQUE
La seule façon d'obtenir des neutrons utilisables sur le plan militaire est de
provoquer des réactions nucléaires explosives. Mais, dans les armes à fission ou
à fusion de type classique, l'émission neutronique ne constitue qu'un sous-
produit des explosions, la majeure partie de l'énergie libérée apparaissant sous
forme de rayonnement X ultérieurement converti en souffle et chaleur par
interaction avec l'atmosphère. Cette limitation tient au fait que le neutron est
l'agent essentiel des réactions nucléaires utilisées ; dans les armes à fission,
il permet au processus de réaction en chaîne de se développer ; dans les armes à
fusion de grande puissance, il permet la formation du tritium (trop cher pour
être directement incorporé à l'engin) nécessaire à la réaction exoénergétique à
partir du lithium, suivant le schéma :

n + Li → a + T (u : particule d'hélium; T : tritium)
Moteur indispensable à l'entretien des réactions, le neutron ne peut donc à la
fois être utilisé « in situ » et être disponible à l'extérieur ; en fait seule
une très faible partie de l'énergie totale (quelques %) d'une explosion
nucléaire se manifeste sous forme de rayonnement neutronique. Par contre, il en
va tout autrement si, dans un engin H, on utilise directement du tritium. En
effet, la réaction élémentaire (2) L'activation induite des matériaux de l'arme

Efficacité d'une arme à rayonnement
Le rayonnement instantané émis par une explosion nucléaire comprend :
— un rayonnement gamma primaire, dont la majeure partie est absorbée par
l'enveloppe de l'engin ;

— un rayonnement neutronique, interagissant relativement peu avec l'enveloppe ;
— un rayonnement gamma secondaire, provenant principalement de l'interaction des
neutrons avec l'atmosphère (diffusion inélastique et capture radiatives).

SENSIBILITÉ DE LA CIBLE
Les effets des rayonnements sur l'homme sont dus à l'ionisation produite dans
les tissus vivants, ionisation qui définit la dose d'irradiation reçue, exprimée
en rads (5). La dose létale, c'est-à-dire entraînant 50 % de décès dans les
trois semaines suivant l'irradiation, est d'500 rads. On retiendra comme critère
d'efficacité tactique la dose d = 5000 rads, correspondant à la mise hors de
combat, dans un délai d'une demi-heure, des effectifs irradiés. L'électronique
et surtout la matière fissile, milieu amplificateur naturel, sont également très
sensibles aux rayonnements. L'exploitation de cette vulnérabilité semble être
envisagée en défense antismits, bien qu'il soit possible, dans une certaine
mesure, de protéger les ogives nucléaires de ces effets par des techniques de
durcissement.

OVER THE THRESHOLD

by William Beecher

The Nixon Administration is considering deploying smaller, "cleaner" tactical
nuclear weapons in Western Europe, heralding new strategies for NATO defenses
against the Eastern bloc forces.

The disclosure came in a recent interview with Defense Secretary Melvin R. Laird
who, shortly before President Nixon's summit meetings in Moscow, declared that
expected mutual limitations on strategic weapons would of necessity place higher
importance on tactical nuclear weapons and conventional land, air and sea forces
assigned to defend America's allies in Europe.

Other Defense and State Department senior officials explained that a freeze of
the current strategic balance in which a long-range missile strike by one side
could lead to mutual annihilation, tends to undermine the pledge during the
Eisenhower Administration that the United States was prepared to respond to an
overwhelming Soviet conventional assault on Europe by sending strategic bombers
and missiles against Russia. In the 1950s, when the pledge was voiced, the
United States had a commanding lead in strategic weapons.

In today's much changed situation, with more nearly equal strategic arsenals,
many Administration officials stress that the two other elements of European
defense—conventional forces and tactical nuclear weapons—must be strengthened
somehow to increase their credibility to deter war.

One tack gaining momentum within the government, but not yet adopted as official
policy, calls for smaller, "cleaner" tactical nuclear weapons and new formations
and doctrines to strictly control their use.

Knowledgable sources say that labs working for the Atomic Energy Commission and
the Pentagon are actively pursuing technology that would permit a decision to
field, within a few years, much improved tactical weapons designed to continue
damage to the immediate target area.

"That's probably what you have to do," Secretary Laird said in an interview.
"You have to move in that direction." But he made it clear that no decision has
yet been made to deploy a new generation of such weapons.

For nearly a decade a debate has been under way, principally behind closed
doors, over whether the United States should stay with, or

fundamentally transform, the stockpile of some 7,200 relatively large and
"dirty" tactical nuclear weapons deployed in Western Europe. These are less
refined than current technology permits, producing relatively large amounts of
radioactive fallout in relation to their blast yield.

These weapons are classed as tactical not because all are smaller than strategic
weapons, but because they are intended for use in the battlefield area rather
than against targets in the enemy's homeland.

While some weapons are relatively small, such as artillery shells and atomic
demolition munitions (ADM) that are said to have an explosive force equivalent
to less than 1,000 tons of TNT, some are quite a bit larger. For example, bombs
to be carried by F-4 and FB-111 fighter-bombers in Europe are said to scale at
least 100 kilotons each, equivalent to 100,000 tons of TNT.

That's five times the explosive force of the atomic weapons that devastated
Hiroshima and Nagasaki and roughly twice the force of each of the 10 to 14
warheads carried by a Poseidon submarine-launched strategic missile.

American tactical nuclear weapons range mostly from about 10 kilotons to
hundreds of kilotons. A relatively few ADMs and artillery shells have an
explosive force somewhat less than one kiloton.

To some extent, as older weapons that have been in the stockpile a long time are
torn down and reworked, more efficient technology permits lower yields.

The old weapons contain large amounts of ory (enriched uranium-235). As the
weapons are reworked, much of this material is rechanneled for use in power
reactors. Warheads of newer design contain less ory, or plutonium, or a mixture
of the two.

But weapons experts say it should be possible to reduce certain tactical nuclear
weapons to a punch equivalent to 20 to 50 tons of TNT.

Depending on the employment objective, the weapons can be designed in different
ways.

'SMART' WEAPONS: A COMING REVOLUTION IN TACTICS

by Colonel John T. Burke

It is sometime in the next few years, somewhere in western Europe. Warsaw Pact
forces initiated a full-scale offensive two days ago. Your infantry battalions
is hit by a massive armor attack. TOW and Dragon antitank missiles stalled the
first wave, but now a second formation of 50 tanks or more appears about 2,500
meters out. The approved solution? You call for artillery!

Even certified Redlegs will concede that artillery isn't too hot against tanks.
But this isn't 1972, and those shells now demolishing enemy armor with pinpoint
precision aren't your common garden-variety ordnance. They are "smart rounds"
that lock onto a signal from the target and zero in on it like a homing pigeon
to its roost.

As is well known, some types of smart weapons are a proven capability, not a
technical dream. Air Force smart bombs are striking targets in Vietnam with
surgical precision, a single bomb doing the job of hundreds and doing it better.
Army weapons using similar technology will perhaps fulfill our impossible dream:
A capability for discrete, single-shot destruction of a variety of targets at
extended ranges with a family of weapon systems. If so, what we have is perhaps
not simply a "product improvement" but a new tactical ballgame.

What makes weapons "smart"? The answer is technology known as "terminal homing"
in which projectiles are given a "homing instinct." Two principles are involved:
First, every object has a set of characteristics known as its "signature." A
tank engine, for instance, puts out heat and electro-magnetic energy. Radar and
radios, by definition, put out an electrical signal. Like human handwriting,
each kind of signature is different and betrays its owner. The second part of
the equation is electronic sensors which can "read" the signature—know what and
where it is. Put the sensor on the nose of a projectile, connect it to a
guidance mechanism that steers the projectile and you have terminal homing—a
smart round that zeroes in on its prey with uncanny accuracy.

What if the target is "dead," lacking an energy signature? It still usually
reflects less light than its background—the sky or surrounding earth—and this
optical contrast is itself a signature.

What if the signature is so ill-defined that the sensor can't read it? Then the
target is given what's called an "induced signature." The principal means is the
concentrated light energy of a laser beam. Using a laser gun (or "designator"),
you "paint" the target with laser energy, which reflects off the target and can
be seen by the sensor.

Terminal homing is much less dependent than conventional systems on the accuracy
of the initial trajectory. It is sufficient to put the projectile in some
general area (or "basket") above the target, from which the sensor and internal
guidance mechanism take over. Imagine the implications for our old problem of
ballistic dispersion that scatters artillery rounds around instead of on the
target!

Also of great importance is the "launch-and-leave" aspect of terminal homing. If
there is no requirement to track the projectile from the launcher, the launcher
itself can depart the scene immediately, evading counter-fire. A "fire-and-
forget" capability would be even better. Once on a given trajectory, the
projectile wouldn't need any external designation—you could literally forget it.

explanation. Suffice it to say that on the horizon are techniques for almost
infinite combinations of sensors, seekers, guidance mechanisms and ways of
packaging these to get precise, discrete target destruction.

In the area of weapons design, terminal homing seems to call for greater
emphasis on rockets with light, relatively crude, inexpensive launchers. Tube
artillery—or perhaps more sophisticated rocket launchers—would then deliver the
big punch against critical, longer-range targets. Shades of the old Honest John
missile! Only now it would not be "free flight," and would not depend on a
nuclear warhead for effectiveness.

The laser-designated round has been stressed largely because the technology is
already at hand. The ultimate would be projectiles that lock on the target's
natural signature or put their own signal on the target, thus eliminating the
external designator. Remember the mice who decided their best defense was a
warning bell on their enemy? This naturally provoked a key issue: Who would bell
the cat? Similarly, designators could be extremely vulnerable—particularly if
our opponent's equipment has built-in sensors. Hence, the logic of self-
contained terminal homing systems.

What infantry badly needs is, first, a quick-kill capability against massed
tanks and other hard targets and then, something much better than existing
mortars to neutralize opposing infantry. Take the artillery picture I sketched
and apply it, in miniature, to the mortar family. Mortars, too, are highly
erratic and certainly not very effective against tanks or other hard targets,
particularly moving ones. Smart mortars would be quite another story.

Of course, it would take a hefty high-explosive warhead to hurt a tank and the
kinetic energy needed to penetrate armor requires much more velocity than that
of a mortar round. But this will not stop the ordnance engineer. He might give
the projectile a shaped-charge warhead, like the tanker’s high-explosive
antitank round. Or perhaps he will find a way to accelerate the round in its
downward trajectory.

Mortars pose other technical problems, like getting the launch velocity required
for extended range from small tubes. Again, this seems to support the case for a
family of rocket systems. The relationship between infantry and artillery
systems and their roles should make for interesting debate.

Tank-killing techniques took a giant step forward with the TOW and Dragon
antitank systems. These, to be sure, are themselves "high IQ" weapons and will
not doubt be valuable members of our family of smart systems for years to come.

But TOW and Dragon lack the "education" of terminal homing systems. They require
launcher-to-target operator guidance and are restricted in range by the limits
of their guidance systems. They cannot throw projectiles from indirect positions
and are more vulnerable to counterfire than indirect fire systems.

With smart weapons, infantry's organic firepower will be enormously increased.
Operations will be much less dependent than now upon artillery and armor support
for success. Fortified positions, the nemesis of the foot soldier, will be
neutralized with surgical precision. Infantry staying power will be vastly
increased—with obvious implications for airborne, airborne and amphibious
operations. What is more, infantry mobility can be maintained and even enhanced.
Our light, crude, smart weapon launchers can be back-packed or put on light
vehicles. Ammunition requirements, even with more firepower, are greatly
reduced.

Smart weapons may well alter infantry doctrine. It is infantry's job, for
example, to take and hold terrain. This will always have to be done—but what
extent? We need dominant terrain for observation to permit forward momentum of
the attack and to deny the same advantages to the enemy. The pinpoint precision
of educated ordnance will neutralize terrain as never before possible. Moreover,
masking terrain is no barrier to long range, accurate, indirect fire systems. Of
course, we must still find the target and a laser designator, if we use it,
requires a clear line of sight to the target. Still, the smart commander will
use his smart weapons, not infantry lives, whenever circumstances permit.

Obviously, any high-technology nation can develop smart weapons, so we might
have what’s called a “symmetrical” battlefield. If so, massed infantry on both
sides will be extremely vulnerable, even when shielded by a terrain mask.
Dispersion would be more critical than ever and the infantry engagement might
well become a battle of small teams, each with its own armored carrier, smart
weapons and supporting riflemen. This dovetails with the “arms room concept,” in
which infantrymen carry with them a variety of weapons which they are cross-
trained to use under appropriate circumstances.

Regardless of Army capabilities, we will continue to depend greatly upon Air

Good gunners insist that if someone will find the target, they can hit it! They
frequently can not, of course, but the point is nevertheless well taken. Take
counter-battery, for instance. Enemy artillery pounds us for days. We do
everything possible to knock it out without success. Why? Because we cannot find
it and if we do it moves.

Better acquisition is the key to putting ordnance—smart or otherwise—on the
target. We can get it with advanced terminal-homing technology, including smart
weapons with the capability to acquire their own target. They can, that is, if
we know the general target area. This refers to the highly educated rounds that
lock on the target’s signature with their own, self-contained system. When the
enemy attacks there will be plenty of easily acquired short range targets. But
our objective is to hit them early, back in their assembly areas. Also, we want
precise counterbattery fire against artillery. This is why exploiting the full
range of terminal homing possibilities is well worth the effort.

The very nature of terminal homing suggests numerous countermeasures. It is no
news, for instance, that the electromagnetic spectrum is subject to jamming and
"spooking" with decoy signals. But countermeasures are never infallible, and
more often than not can not themselves be countered using the right technology
and tactics. For instance, the use of chaff to frustrate air defense radar was
common as early as World War II, but it stopped neither bombing nor the use of
radar. As an example of what might be possible in the future, suppose that enemy
tanks are equipped with sensors so they know when they are being painted with
laser energy. How do they react?

By firing at the designator. But with what accuracy when moving? What if the
designator is a decoy, and when the tank reacts it’s painted again from another
direction? All this will likely slow the momentum of the enemy’s attack and
waste his ammunition.

Our attitude should be based on the following: Countermeasures are effective
only if you have them on time—we must get ahead of the curve and stay there;
countermeasures never stop a promising technology, though they frequently change
its direction; really effective countermeasures may not be possible; and
countermeasures may force a major change in enemy tactics, to our advantage.

Smart projectiles require sensitive, precise seekers and guidance mechanisms,
and therefore won’t come cheap. It’s much too early to mark the price tag, but
let us assume that with mass production we can get the price down to about
$2,000 to $5,000 per projectile. Are they worth it?

Surely they are if they do the job they’re supposed to. What counts are not
absolute cost, but cost relative to some other way of getting the same battle
effect, that is, “cost effectiveness.” Cited earlier was a figure of 300 rounds
of conventional artillery ammunition to get one enemy casualty and an estimated
1,200 rounds for a kill. At $100 per round, that is $30,000. If you want to
ensure kills, it is $120,000. But even this is not the whole cost. All that
ammunition must be moved, stored, maintained and secured. Imagine the additional
cost of all this, not to mention the drain on troops and other resources.

A direct comparison of smart ordnance and today’s ammunition costs is
simplistic. We don’t know what realistic trade-offs can be made. Of vital
importance is the fact that with smart weapons we can do what we could not do
before, like kill tanks with artillery. Intuitively, it is reasonable to
conclude that smart weapons will be well worth the cost. What will count is the
imagination and efficiency with which we apply rapidly advancing technology.

Conventional ammunition is a heavy, bulky, logistical nightmare. At the height
of the Vietnam war, according to one report, the figure was 10 million ton-miles
of ammunition transport per year! Security of ammunition stockage was a dreadful
problem in Vietnam. Imagine the problem when we're faced with a sophisticated
enemy.

Smart weapons should demand only a fraction of this effort. I say "should"
because there is a catch: What if we're in a two-sided smart projectile battle
and save ammunition while suffering increased equipment losses? We will just
have to devise tactics that produce enemy attrition without comparable losses on
our side.

Of vital importance is logistic flexibility. We cannot maintain major forces
overseas, and in the event of conflict in Europe or elsewhere, be hamstrung by
logistical problems. Imagine the obstacles to intercontinental movement of major
forces and their support, particularly heavy equipment and ammunition, when we
are faced with sophisticated enemy air and sea power. The benefits of smart
weapons in this respect are clear. Equally evident is the advantage in quick
response to local contingency operations.

There are two ways to kill a gnat: Hit it with a sledgehammer or stick it with a
pin. He won't hold still for the pin, so we use the hammer—and knock a hole in
the wall at the same time. The smart projectile's capacity for really selective
destruction has far more than tactical implications. Both our national ethic and
the logic of warfare demand that the violence of war be limited to our
opponent's military machine and support system. Yet, mass destruction has
frequently been the only alternative. Smart weapons are thus enormously
attractive from the political and psychological viewpoint and this alone
justifies their development.

This clearly applies to low-intensity conflict but not so apparent are the
effects of urbanization. Look at Western Europe which is rapidly becoming one
great megalopolis. Densely populated cities become barriers in the path of war.
The alternative to massive, unwanted destruction is accordingly.

terminal homing, with its genius for discrete destruction.

Consider nuclear weapons. That they would be employed only under extreme
circumstances is an article of faith. Even with small-yield weapons there might
be delivery errors resulting in unacceptable collateral damage. But what if we
mate very low-yield weapons with terminal homing, and can eliminate—or at least
tightly control—radiation fallout? Will not this eventually tend to blur the
distinction between conventional and nuclear effects?

The more things change, you might think, the more they stay the same. Small
units, dispersion, mobility, flexibility—it does all sound familiar and it
should. The philosophy I've suggested is precisely what we have aimed at for
years. It was driven earlier by our appraisal of nuclear effects and more
recently by the checkerboard pattern of Vietnam counter-insurgency operations.
Smart weaponry may blur the distinction between low- and high-intensity
conflicts. If so, we can develop a multi-purpose package of doctrine, tactics
and weaponry that gives us a true flexible response instrument.

The skeptic will view the precision projectile as being at best a "product
improvement" and at worst, one more exotic and expensive toy. His pessimism is
understandable. Our closets are full of useless gadgets that were going to
change the face of war.

But the evidence favors a contrary viewpoint: That we are entering an era of
precision weaponry, one calling for dramatic changes in equipment, organization,
tactics and even strategy. Whether or not we see the potential, our opponents
surely will. So would any nation with adequate technology but limited resources,
which desperately seeks a shortcut to combat power.

This technological symmetry does not mean stalemate. One side or the other will
gain the advantage in technology or tactics, or both. Victory is the prize of
the visionary who grasps the full significance of new weapons and uses them
accordingly.
