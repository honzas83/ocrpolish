> # Permanent Representatives' Comments on SHAPE Illustrative Study on Air Defence Use
> 
> This document is a draft report by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) regarding the tactical use of nuclear weapons for air defence in a hypothetical Warsaw Pact aggression scenario. The report identifies areas for Ministerial discussion based on a SHAPE Illustrative Study, which focuses on a specific air defence situation in the Allied Command Europe (ACE) Central Region. The scenario describes a hypothetical aggression by the Warsaw Pact involving heavy air attacks against NATO forward defences and air bases. In this scenario, the Supreme Allied Commander Europe (SACEUR) proposes the use of nuclear weapons against groups of three or more enemy aircraft to prevent the substantial degradation of NATO's conventional air defence capability. The report emphasizes that the illustrative studies are intended to provide examples of the analytical and procedural processes used by Major NATO Commanders to request the selective use of nuclear weapons based on existing political guidelines. It further discusses the military and political implications of such use, including the potential for deliberate escalation to deter the enemy. The document also highlights the unpredictability of air defence use, noting that the number of weapons and the time period of use cannot be forecast with precision. Finally, it suggests that Ministers discuss ways to ensure political control over the use of nuclear weapons in these unpredictable circumstances.
> 
> ## Categories/Topics
> 
> #Doctrine-and-Strategy/Flexible-Response — The document discusses the 'deliberate escalation' and the 'selective use of nuclear weapons' in a hypothetical air defence scenario, which are core tenets of the Flexible Response strategy (MC 14/3) to raise the nuclear threshold and avoid immediate total war.
> #Internal-Alliance-Dynamics/Command-and-Control-Authority — The text explicitly focuses on the 'analytical and procedural process' for a Major NATO Commander (SACEUR) to 'request' the use of nuclear weapons, as well as the 'necessary political control' over the release of such weapons.

I. PURPOSE
This report identifies some possible areas for Ministerial discussion during
their consideration of the SHAPE Illustrative Study - Initial Air Defence
Use(2).

II. BACKGROUND
2. At their meeting in Ankara on 15th and 16th May, 1973, the Ministers of the
Nuclear Planning Group(NPG), with a view toward promoting the useful dialogue
between political authorities and the NATO Military Authorities, invited the
Chairman of the Military Committee to arrange for the Major NATO Commanders to
prepare additional illustrative studies for Ministerial consideration dealing,
for example, with tactical use of nuclear air defence(3). In response to the
Ministerial request, SHAPE prepared a study which focuses on a specific air
defence situation in the Allied Command Europe (ACE) Central Region. It arises
during a hypothetical aggression by the Warsaw Pact that, from the very
beginning, is characterised by heavy air attacks throughout the Central Region
against forward defences,

III. POSSIBLE AREAS FOR MINISTERIAL DISCUSSION

Application of the Political Guidelines

6. The SHAPE Study is an illustration of a way in which the Provisional
Political Guidelines for the Initial Defensive Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons
by NATO(1) might be interpreted and applied by SACEUR in the given hypothetical
situation. Since the Provisional Political Guidelines state that the selective
use of nuclear weapons in the air defence mode might be justified, inter alia,
"to prevent a development of the air battle decidedly disadvantageous to NATO"
or "if the aggressor were initiating conventional attacks against the nuclear
strike potential of ACE"(2), we consider that the approach in the SHAPE Study is
compatible with political guidance and is a reasonable example of a way in which
the guidance could be applied in the given hypothetical situation.

was the case in illustrative studies previously addressed by Ministers, the
SHAPE Study tends to focus on the implications that are primarily military in

to the military aspects, the Study stresses that the use of nuclear weapons to
augment the conventional air defence is necessary to maintain a favourable air

8. In common with certain other measures of a defensive nature, e.g. anti-

actions which are beyond NATO's control. This particular feature of the air

reason mentioned above. For example, the number of weapons that might have to be

Implications of Failure to Grant Release
10. In his release request, SACEUR indicates that without the use of nuclear air
defence weapons, NATO is most likely to lose a major portion of ACE nuclear
strike capability and attack air forces in the Central Region. He also points
out that, if Warsaw Pact air superiority is not significantly diminished, NATO's
conventional ground forces cannot restore the integrity and security of ACE, and
extensive Warsaw Pact ground penetration of the Central Region is most
likely(1). In addition to these consequences of non-use, political authorities
should be aware, although no mention of it is made in SACEUR's messages, of the
possibility that later NATO use of nuclear weapons on the battlefield would be
required, with a probable greater risk of escalation and civilian casualties and
collateral damage. Furthermore, the failure of NATO to use nuclear weapons in a
tactical situation that the enemy perceived to be a critical and deteriorating
one from NATO's viewpoint might be interpreted by him as an indication of a lack
of determination and cohesiveness on the part of the Alliance with consequent
possible erosion of credibility of the NATO deterrent strategy.

13. The rapidity with which the situation described in the SHAPE Study evolves
after the enemy starts his attack presents a time problem which might well be
common to any kind of air defence by the use of nuclear weapons and gives this

Communicating NATO's Intention
15. If the hypothetical situation illustrated in the Study materialises and NATO
is confronted with a request for selective employment of nuclear weapons for air
defence purposes, the matter of communicating NATO's intention will also arise.
As the recent Study(1) on this subject observes, political authorities might
have to deal with two general kinds of CNI questions. First, NATO would have to
consider whether any steps should be taken to explain its intention with respect
to the specific use. If the answer to this question is yes, NATO would then need
to examine a number of substantive and procedural questions concerning the
details of carrying out the CNI action(2). Although the course of action that
NATO chooses to follow would be based on a mix of political and military
considerations that can only be assessed in the context of the situation that
exists when NATO is considering the specific release request(3), some comment of
a general nature may be appropriate.

16. With respect to the question of whether NATO should communicate its
intention, a primary factor, from both political and military points of view,
would be the extent to which a CNI action might be expected to reduce the
possibility of enemy misinterpretation of the deliberately limited nature of
NATO's initial use. A major
