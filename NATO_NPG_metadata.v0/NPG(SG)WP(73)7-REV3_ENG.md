> # Consultation Procedures and Facilities
> 
> This working paper, prepared by the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Staff Group, examines selected aspects of procedures and facilities for political consultation in time of war concerning the use of nuclear weapons. It aims to strengthen the consultation process by making recommendations for specific actions and proposing future NPG work. The document emphasizes that the effectiveness of nuclear consultation depends on rapid, secure, and reliable information exchange, primarily through message traffic. It addresses the content of selective release messages, suggesting that plain language be used to reduce misunderstandings and delays, particularly for political authorities. The report also discusses the handling and identification of nuclear consultation messages, noting that the use of the NATO-wide Communications System has generally improved speed and reliability. However, it identifies delays experienced during Exercise WINTEX 73, attributing them to the initial failure to use the NATO-wide Communications System for release requests. Consequently, the Defence Planning Committee (DPC) has agreed that the NATO-wide Communications System should be used for all critical nuclear consultation traffic pending the implementation of the NATO Integrated Communications System (NICS).

I. PURPOSE

This report examines selected aspects of procedures and facilities to be used by
the Alliance for political consultation in time of war on questions of using
nuclear weapons, makes recommendations for some specific actions to strengthen
this consultation process and develops proposals for future NPG work in this
context.

II. BACKGROUND

2. In fulfilment of one of its basic responsibilities(1), the Nuclear Planning
Group has, since its inception, played an important rôle in developing
improvements in the machinery for carrying out agreed methods of consultation
with respect to actual or potential use of nuclear weapons in defence of NATO.
As part of this process, a number of questions dealing with consultation
procedures and facilities have, at the direction of the Ministers, been under
active investigation: the content of selective release messages and
identification and handling of messages related to nuclear consultation(2) and
classification and precedence of selective release messages(3). These matters
are discussed in the following paragraphs.

III. DISCUSSION

3. The effectiveness of the nuclear consultation process is critically dependent
on the ability of participants in that process to exchange information and views
in a rapid, secure and reliable way. Since a major part of this information
exchange will be in the form of message traffic, it is important to ensure that
the content of the messages is clear and unambiguous, that the messages are
handled rapidly, and that they are easily identi- fiable to responsible
officials as being relevant to the nuclear consultation process.

Content of Selective Release Messages

4. Message Substance. Past discussions within the NPG concerning the content of
selective release messages have tended to focus on the kinds and substance of
the information to be included in such messages. This emphasis is reflected, for
example, in the Provisional Political Guidelines of the Initial Defensive
Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons by NATO which, among other things, lists(1) the
specific kinds of information needed by political authorities in consulting on
requests for release of nuclear weapons. Much of this information would be
included in the release messages. Since there appears to be general agreement on
the kinds of information that selective release messages should contain, recent
efforts have been devoted primarily to the clarification of details. There is a
need to continue this

11. Classification. A previous report stated(1) that under existing guidance and
procedures certain nuclear release messages (e.g., WHISKEY, SIERRA or ROMEO)
originated by Major NATO Commanders are likely to contain ATOMAL information and
would therefore have to carry an ATOMAL marking in addition to any other
assigned security classification, such as NATO SECRET or COSMIC TOP SECRET. On
that occasion, some of the implications flowing from the ATOMAL classification
requirement were explored, with particular reference to limitations that might
be imposed on the scope of consultation within the Alliance and the timeliness
of such consultation. With respect to the scope of political consultation among
Alliance members, for example, it might be difficult or even impossible for
governments not cleared by the United States to receive and handle ATOMAL
information to participate effectively in the nuclear consultation process if
that process must of necessity use a nuclear release message bearing an ATOMAL
classification as its focal point. Regarding the impact on timeliness, the
processing and handling of messages carrying an ATOMAL classification is more
complicated and time-consuming than that required for other classified, but non-
ATOMAL, traffic. This was demonstrated as recently as WINTEX 73(2).

warrants highest priority attention.

Further Work
16. This report and earlier ones(1) have discussed several separate but related
aspects of the nuclear consultation process, all of which should now be
consolidated into a single, cohesive package that would contain information and
guidance on the details of the agreed procedures that would govern such
consultation. This consolidation effort would be consistent with, and similar
to, work now being carried out in NATO under the overall coordination of the
Council Operations and Exercise Committee in the field of NATO crisis management
procedures and arrangements(2). The compilation of nuclear consultation
procedures could, when completed, form a contribution to the crisis management
work now in progress under the aegis of the Council Operations and Exercise
Committee. These detailed procedures would, of course, support, and be
compatible with, approved policy guidance on nuclear consultation procedures(3).

Content of Selective Release Messages
3. Political consultation in time of war on specific proposals for the use of
nuclear weapons during the period prior to any general nuclear exchange will be
based in large measure on the particular messages that request the release of

Further Action

7. In addition to the matters pertaining to the nuclear consultation process
that we have mentioned in this report, we recall that we have covered a number
of other aspects in earlier reports to the Ministers(1). It appears to us that
these various elements should now be consolidated into a single, cohesive
package that would contain information and guidance on the details of the
procedural aspects of the nuclear consultation process. We note that this step
would be consistent with, and similar to, work now being carried out in NATO
under the overall coordination of the Council Operations and Exercise Committee
in the field of NATO crisis management procedures and arrangements(2). Subject
to Ministerial concurrence, we propose therefore to undertake the development of
a compilation of nuclear consultation procedures that will incorporate the
diverse elements mentioned above. The package could, when completed, form a
contribution to the crisis management work being carried out under the aegis of
the Council Operations and Exercise Committee. The detailed procedures contained
in the compilation would, of course, support, and be compatible with, approved
policy guidance on nuclear consultation procedures(3).

RECOMMENDATIONS
8. In the light of the foregoing, we recommend that the Ministers:
(a) take note of this report and, in particular, of the actions that have been
taken with respect to the content of selective release messages addressed to
political authorities and the handling and identification of nuclear
consultation traffic;

(b) agree that, for the purposes of political consultation on nuclear release
request messages, the present system for message precedence is workable and that
there is no requirement for introduction of a higher precedence for nuclear
consultation traffic; and

(c) invite the Permanent Representatives to develop a compilation of nuclear
consultation procedures.
