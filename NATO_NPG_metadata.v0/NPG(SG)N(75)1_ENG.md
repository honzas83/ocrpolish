> # Note by the Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group)
> 
> Following a request from the United States Secretary of Defence for NATO political views on military assessments concerning the nuclear weapons deployment posture in Europe, the Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) issued this note. The document outlines a framework for the Staff Group to evaluate the political implications of military assessments, specifically focusing on the number of nuclear warheads in the stockpile, force mix, and the role of Quick Reaction Alert (QRA) forces. It details considerations for the stockpile size, including the credibility of deterrence, political control of escalation, and theinvulnerability of forces. Additionally, it addresses the balance between conventional and nuclear forces and the role of non-nuclear countries in the NATO deterrent through QRA forces. The note also emphasizes the importance of enhancing the security of nuclear weapons within the Allied Command Europe (ACE) to improve overall deterrence value. The process is coordinated by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group to ensure a full evaluation and report on the political implications before a February deadline.

I. BACKGROUND
The United States Secretary of Defence, in his letter to the Secretary General
of 5th November, 1974, requested(1) NATO political views on certain military
assessments to be provided by the NATO Military Authorities in connection with
the Congressional request for a re-evaluation of the nuclear weapons deployment
posture in Europe. NATO views were desired on such issues as number of weapons
in the stockpile, enhanced security, force mix and CRA (Quick Reaction Alert
forces).

2. The Secretary General, by his PO/74/158, 4th December, 1974, informed the IPG
Permanent Representatives that the International Staff had been instructed to be
ready to start work on the preparations of a draft assessment of the political
implications of the issues mentioned in the letter by the United States
Secretary of Defence. It was also foreseen that work on the political
implications would begin as soon as the military assessment was made available.

3. At the NPG Ministerial meeting on 10th December, 1974, Ministers concurred
with the Secretary General's suggestion that Permanent Representatives of the
Nuclear Planning Group be requested(2) to monitor the overall process of
consultation and co-ordination required for a full evaluation and proposed
report on the political implications, taking into consideration the military
assessment to be made available by the NATO Military Authorities. The NPG Staff
Group, in its session on 16th December, 1974, agreed that work should proceed
with the Staff Group providing the working level. Staff Group members from
Canada, the Netherlands, Norway and Turkey, whose countries had not been
involved in the 10th December IPG Ministerial meeting expressed the agreement of
their capitals to proceed as suggested by the NPG Ministers.

4. In order to meet the deadline of early February a number of preliminary
considerations should be examined by Staff Group members in order to be prepared
to start work as soon as possible after receipt of the military assessments.
This notice offers a checklist as a contribution to an initial discussion in the
Staff Group in anticipation of preparing the necessary reports.

II. 'ARFAS' OF POSSIBLE POLITICAL IMPLICATIONS

5. In keeping with the 'Four main subjects in the United States Secretary of
Defence, letter of 5th November, 1974, the following matters, among others, may
be fruitful items for discussion by the Staff Group in the meeting on 6th
January, 1975.

A. Possible Adjustments to the Number of Nuclear Warheads as the Stockpile is
modernized

6. In his remarks regarding the Nunn Amendment the United States Secretary of
Defence mentioned that, since the present United States stockpile in Europe had
been established in a different political and strategic environment, it should
now be re-evaluated. He also said in this context that the size of the present
stockpile had been developed primarily on the basis of political rather than
military considerations. Therefore, in attempting to develop a conceptual
rationale for the stockpile the following should be considered:

(a) credibility of deterrence in the age of rough strategic parity;

(b) means for restoring deterrence after the outbreak of a conflict;

(c) political control of escalation;

(d) relationship between the task to deny an enemy access to NATO territory and
the task to extend own use into enemy territory in furtherance of the primary
objective of conflict termination;

(e) degree of invulnerability of own forces as an inducement to an opponent to
renounce a limited first strike capability;

(f) range of selective employment options (to include substitution of central
strategic systems in lieu of some existing non-central systems).

All these and probably a number of other considerations would affect the
rationale for the size of the nuclear stockpile in Europe as well as the
rationale for solutions to the other problems raised by the United States
Secretary of Defence.

B. Possible Adjustments to Force Mix

7. Force mix could be 'treated' according to various meanings: mix between
conventional and nuclear forces, mix between central strategic and non-central
systems, and mix within non-central systems in Europe. All three considerations
would be affected by the rationale outlined in the preceding paragraph.

8. Force mix is also a function of the amount of new technology to be applied.
Application of modern technology itself is inexorably linked to the rationale
for the stockpile.

C. Re-examination of the Role and Composition of the GRA Forces

9. The existing GRA forces in Europe represent a measure by which non-nuclear
countries can contribute to the NATO deterrent. The nuclear weapons provided for
these forces are part of the United States stockpile and therefore there might
be a reciprocal impact between modernization of weapons and the role and
composition of GRA forces.

10. In connection with the related considerations as outlined in paragraph 6
above Staff Group members may wish to prepare themselves to discuss the
following points among others:

- Role of GRA forces.

- Non-nuclear countries' participation.

- Psychological effects within countries.

- Visible evidence of readiness versus vulnerability, including possible enemy
pre-emption.

- Possible Soviet perceptions of NATO's GRAs as an offensive posture.

- GRA role of central strategic systems.

- Military effectiveness of GRA forces in relation to their role.

- GRA and weapon modernization.

- GRA as European force factor.

D. Possible Relocation and Consolidation of Weapons within ACF from Certain
Sites as a Means of Enhancing Security

11. Enhanced security of nuclear weapons might be considered as a most
substantive matter at the present time. Although it is difficult to balance
security against deployment necessities and deterrent value, this problem
deserves particular attention. Furthermore, there can be little doubt that
improved security would enhance the reliability and thereby the overall
deterrence value.
