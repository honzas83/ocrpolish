> # Military Committee Function in the Nuclear Consultation Process
> 
> This document is a revised draft report by the Permanent Representatives reviewing the role and function of the NATO Military Committee in the process of Alliance consultation regarding the possible use of nuclear weapons. The report outlines the purpose and background of the following review, noting that the Ministers in Ankara in May 1973 had identified uncertainties regarding the Military Committee's involvement in the process, particularly concerning specific requests for nuclear weapon use. It emphasizes that while the ultimate decision to release nuclear weapons rests with the nuclear power, the Alliance aims for the greatest possible degree of consultation compatible with time constraints. The document discusses the principles governing the nuclear consultation process, referencing the General Guidelines for Consultation Procedure on the Use of Nuclear Weapons adopted by the Defence Planning Committee in 1969. It highlights the need for rapid expression of national views to be communicated to the nuclear powers to ensure these views can influence the release decision. The report further clarifies that the Military Committee's role is to advise the North Atlantic Council and the Defence Planning Committee on the use of military power, without interposing itself between Major NATO Commanders and political authorities. Finally, it suggests that in times of tension or hostilities, the Military Committee would likely operate at the Permanent Representative level rather than having national Chiefs of Staff in continuing session at NATO Headquarters.

III. DISCUSSION
Principles Governing the Nuclear Consultation Process
3. The primary complexities surrounding the nuclear consultation process arise
from the fact that certain of its features differ in a very fundamental way from
the usual approach used in general Alliance consultation. These differences
concern the manner in which the Alliance consults on specific requests or
proposals for nuclear weapons use. The basic guidance governing the procedures
to be used within the Alliance in consulting in time of tension or hostilities
on questions of nuclear weapon use is contained in the General Guidelines for
Consultation Procedure on the Use of Nuclear Weapons(2). These Consultation
Guidelines were adopted by the Defence Planning Committee in 1969 on the
recommendation of the NPG and the Nuclear Defence Affairs Committee in the light
of intensive consideration of the subject in the NPG during the first three
years of its existence(3). They also reflect a significant body of opinion and
experience developed within the Alliance over a period of years prior to the
creation of the NPG.

The Consultation Guidelines provide the benchmark against which the rôles played
by the various participants, including the Military Committee are to be
assessed.

4. The Consultation Guidelines state that nuclear consultation would fit into
the framework of a procedure of general consultation between member countries
which is already operating(1). Accordingly, the bulk of the activity associated
with the nuclear consultation process follows very closely the pattern of
general consultation(2). Nevertheless, the Guidelines point out that nuclear
consultation has its own particular characteristics(3), which are reflected in
the treatment given to individual country views on specific requests or
proposals for use of nuclear weapons.

5. On the question of country views, the Consultation Guidelines point out that,
although the ultimate decision on the use of a nuclear weapon will always lie
with the nuclear power, it is essential that there should be the greatest
possible degree of Alliance consultation compatible with the time available and
the relevant circumstances, before any ultimate decision is taken(4).
Consequently, a general aim of the nuclear consultation process is "to permit
the rapid expression of views of member governments on any request or proposal
for the use of nuclear weapons"(5). These views will be communicated to the
nuclear power(s) concerned by the

7. Closely linked with the need to preserve the clear identity of individual
government views on a specific request or proposal for nuclear weapon use is the
requirement to do everything possible to ensure that these views reach the
nuclear power(s) concerned in time to have an influence on the release decision.
This decision is time-sensitive, since the political, psychological and military
impact of the proposed use can be critically dependent on the timing of that
use. Much of the considerable effort that the Alliance has devoted toward
ensuring that suitable facilities and procedures are available for the nuclear
consultation process is designed to permit the rapid expression of views of
member governments(1).

responsibilities, the Military Committee role in wartime includes "advising the

12. In its Report, the Military Committee mentions the possibility of a need to
reconcile the points of view of the Major NATO Commanders during the critical
period just prior to and after the start of preliminary consultation on the
release of nuclear weapons(1). To be consistent with the previously-cited
description of the Military Committee's specific task in connection with nuclear
consultation(2), we think that the process of reconciliation must be achieved in
a way that does not interpose the Military Committee between the Major NATO
Commanders and the appropriate political authorities with respect to the
authorisation for release of nuclear weapons.

Concluding Remarks

13. In the light of the foregoing discussion, we conclude that the function of
the Military Committee in the nuclear consultation process is essentially the
same as its rôle in general consultation, with one notable exception. As is the
case with the Council/DPC(3), the Military Committee in Permanent Session, in
carrying out its activities in support of the nuclear consultation process, must
avoid actions that might make it difficult or impossible to identify clearly the
views of each member government.

14. In carrying out its functions, the Military Committee
in Permanent Session would need to take fully into account the
great emphasis that the Council/DPC would place on the "rapid
expression of views of member governments on any request or proposal
for the use of nuclear weapons"(1). During the process of
consulting in time of tension or hostilities, particular attention
must be given to ensuring the smooth and effective coordination of
the Military Committee's activities with those of the Council/DPC.
In keeping with the stress in the Consultation Guidelines on
avoiding elaborate or inflexible procedures(2), we believe that
such coordination should be worked out in a pragmatic way as
circumstances dictate during the course of consultation and that
no specific arrangements should be prescribed in advance.
