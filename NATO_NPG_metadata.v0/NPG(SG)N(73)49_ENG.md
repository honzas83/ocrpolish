> # Articles Related to Nuclear Defence
> 
> This document, issued by the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) on June 13, 1973, serves as a cover note from the Chairman, H.B. Seim, distributing two articles for the consideration of Staff Group members. Annex A, written by Neville Brown, discusses the instruments of nuclear deterrence, contrasting the 'flexible response' doctrine of the United States and the 'reflex retaliation' approach. The author explores the historical context of firepower, the distinction between tactical and strategic nuclear weapons, and the influence of Soviet military thought, specifically the reference to Clausewitz and the nature of war. The text analyzes the North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) nuclear shield and the Atlantic Alliance, the perceived limitations of flexible response when facing the Soviet Union's perspective on war. It further examines the Soviet Union's potential for 'overkilling' Western Europe through Medium and Intermediate Range Ballistic Missiles and the strategic implications of these a-symmetric capabilities. The Chairman notes that the opinions expressed in the articles are those of the authors and do not represent national positions or the official endorsement of the International Staff.

THE INSTRUMENTS OF NUCLEAR DETERRENCE
(1) Reflex retaliation or flexible response?
N O discussion of deterrence under modern conditions can be meaningful unless it
is informed by a deep awareness of the scale and character of the revolution
that came over military affairs in August 1945. Throughout history firepower had
been so scarce a use all their personal drive, intuition, and intellect to
ensure its economic application at the decisive point and moment. Then a sudden
flash in the Hiroshima morning sky transformed it into a superabundant resource
which Great Powers fear to employ save in tiny fractions. The high-light of
Napoleon's 1812 campaign against Russia was the Battle of Borodino at which his
armies expended munitions weighing in aggregate some forty tons. The British
fired off 50,000 tons of high explosive in the vast bombardment which heralded
the great Somme offensive of 1916. Authoritative estimates show around six
million tons to have been expended by all the combatants in all the years of the
Second World War. But another total war would probably involve the release of
explosive energy equivalent to tens of billions of tons of TNT and, in addition,
give rise to very widespread special nuclear effects.

Initially the implications of the new state of affairs were subjected to little
academic analysis. However, in the decade or so after the advent of
thermonuclear warheads (alias hydrogen bombs) in 1953 a vigorous effort was made
to resolve conceptually the contradiction between the ultimate futility of
warfare under modern conditions and the manifold tendencies towards widespread
armed conflict endemic in the world. The doctrines of nuclear deterrence thereby
evolved separate into two broad categories, each of them very relevant to
European defence. One concerns what are euphemistically described as tactical
nuclear weapons—i.e. those designed for use along or relatively close to any
sector where a limited war was in progress. The other involves recurse to
strategic weapons; and by "strategic" in this context is meant suitable for
dispatch against targets thousands of miles the other side of the line of
confrontation. So far as the nuclear shield or umbrella over the Atlantic
Alliance is concerned, the distinction normally made is that whereas strategic
delivery vehicles are able to reach the USSR tactical ones cannot. Nevertheless,
the two categories are prone to overlap as witness some of the more recent
Soviet moves in the Strategic Arms Limitation Talks.¹

Towards the end of the decade or so seminal thought triggered off by the first
hydrogen bombs two main theories of strategic deterrence emerged: the epicentre
of one was Washington and of the other Paris. Each rested on premises and
perceptions of the USA and France respectively. But both proceeded from their
premises with pure and almost faultless logic. And both reflected concern about
the way in which the USSR was moving inexorably towards a state of nuclear
parity with the USA.

The American body of doctrine came to be known in the Kennedy-McNamara era as
"flexible response", this term usually being employed in a broader or perhaps
looser sense than it currently is by NATO. In outline the argument ran thus: "It
would be foolish to threaten an all-out nuclear attack in response to some local
challenge by an adversary—e.g. the USSR in 1970—possessed of an ultimate
capacity for overwhelming destruction under any conceivable circumstances. So
what is required is to be able to keep every crisis firmly under central
political and military direction in order to modulate all counter-moves in
accordance with enemy offensive action and the progress or otherwise of
diplomatic bargaining."

maybe tactical nuclear weapons. But in some situations there could be an actual
or threatened initiation of what was entitled "limited strategic war", by which
was meant the slow motion or selective release of strategic weapons. In the
"total counterforce" option the United States was in a position for a few years
early in the 1960s to exercise against the USSR, the object would have been
effectively to disarm the opposition. More generally, however, the aim borne in
mind was not so much to reduce the adversary's ability to aggress as to
undermine his will to do so. Intimations were given to the effect that whichever
of America's allies were most involved could expect to be brought in some way or
other into any crisis deliberations.

But it has always been acknowledged that no country could ever carry through any
strategy of flexible response against a major opponent unless its own strategic
force was both large and sophisticated. For one thing it has to be constantly
ready and able to deliver quite intolerable retaliation in the event of some
massive act of escalation by the other protagonist. For another the subtle but
sustained employment of strategic weapons would depend on a substantial quantity
and variety being available to start with.

Herein lay the rationale for the doubts entertained by some leading members of
the Kennedy administration about the value of the Alliance of the relatively
small independent strategic deterrents being built by Britain and France. These
doubts were made bluntly explicit by Mr McNamara in the course of his Ann Arbor
speech of June 1962 when he contended that "… limited nuclear capabilities,
operating independently, are dangerous, expensive, prone to obsolescence, and
lacking in credibility as a deterrent".

Unfortunately, however, the philosophy of flexible response is not without its
limitations, a cardinal one being a tacit presupposition that the Russians think
along at least broadly similar lines. For it is not at all certain whether they
do or ever would. One influence at work in the Kremlin is the continental
tradition that warfare is inherently too incoherent for either close analysis or
tight control. Thus, whereas American and British military perceptions have
stemmed in the main from a long succession of limited operations overseas, the
Russians and, for instance, the Germans share long experience of mass
mobilisation, followed by savage and often chaotic general war across their own
territory. So, although it was an English bard who devised the metaphor of
ignorant armies clashing by night, the image of war conjured up by this phrase
is akin to the Prussian view as expressed so well by Clausewitz. For if there is
one message which comes across above all others in the deliberate literary
convolutions of this pioneer of European strategic thought, it is that armed
violence is inherently too rough and clumsy consistently to lend itself to
precise control.

The pertinence of Clausewitz in this regard is enhanced by the direct bearing
his work has had on the formulation of Soviet perspectives on war. He came from
the same Hegelian background as Marx. Lenin made detailed notes on him and
occasionally alluded to them. Tolstoy, a contemporary of Clausewitz, shared his
distrust of the whole notion of command and control. Indeed, he even managed to
persuade himself it was invalid because it was an extension of the philosophy of
freewill, a confusion which is betrayed time and time again in the pages of War
and Peace.

So it comes as no great surprise to find that the overt Russian reaction to the
burgeoning American interest in flexible response and, more specifically, in
limited strategic war was to dismiss it as not only unreal but also unethical.
Yet the fact remains it is the USSR which has been most disposed to talk of
selective strategic attacks during times of actual tension. Reference has
already been made to the overt, though perhaps inconsequential, threat of
strikes against London and Paris made by Marshal Bulganin during the Suez
crisis. And in 1960 the Soviet leadership threw down two more challenges of
similar form and maybe greater substance. During the crisis over the U-2
reconnaissance flights in May, Marshal Malinovsky warned of instant retaliation
against whatever bases any further such flights were dispatched from.² Then in
July Mr Khruschev himself spoke of rocket reprisals in the event of an American
attack on Cuba.

What is most indicative from the European standpoint, however, is not this
random spread of statements from the Khruschev era but the echelons of six or
seven hundred Medium and Intermediate Range Ballistic Missiles and several
hundred strategic medium bombers located in Western Russia. For herein lies a
potential for "overkilling" by a very wide margin the whole

urban civilisation of Western Europe. Alternatively it is manifestly suitable
for use in a piece-meal fashion if so required, perhaps as a counterweight to
NATO’s tactical nuclear superiority. Furthermore, although it is the expansion
of missile strength at intercontinental level which has been accorded highest
priority in recent years, there is no doubt that the Russians are still
determined to retain massive strategic strike among their counter-European
options as well. Proof of this can be seen in the replacement of the Tupolev-16
Badger medium bomber by the supersonic Tupolev-22 Blinder which is now a third
complete;³ and also in the continued development of an IRBM suitable for
mounting on the tracked launcher-erector vehicle known to NATO as Scamp. Being
solid-fuelled this weapon will represent a substantial advance on the existing
MRBM and IRBM force.

Given the ever-vaster scale and complexity of the military nuclear power of both
the USSR and the USA, many commentators have dismissed the British and French
strategic forces as too small to be significant. Characteristically the official
rejoinder has been split out much more precisely and explicitly in Paris than
ever has in London. And at the heart of orthodox French thinking on this matter
lies the continentalist notion that nuclear war could never be an all-or-nothing
(tout ou rien) encounter. Therefore what really matters is not a meaningless
range of theoretical strategic options but the possession of sovereignty
authority over the deterrent force in question. Moreover, men like the late
Charles de Gaulle and now Michel Debré, the present Defence Minister, have
stressed that such sovereignty is inherently national and so indivisible.
Accordingly it is, in their view, and even insidious to talk of multinational
participation in, or mutual integration, for, the direction of nuclear policy.
As Debré has put it: always there will be the integrated, who follow, and the
integrator who commands".⁴

From which the inference has been drawn that the substitution of the balance of
power … for the United States monopoly in this field has transformed the general
conditions of Western defence.⁵ What is meant is that the advent of nuclear
parity between the Superpowers cannot but make the USA increasingly reluctant to
employ nuclear weapons in defence of Europe. lest she thereby invoke a savage
onslaught

Conversely the French deterrent force is regarded as a shield for neighbouring
territories because it would automatically come into full play as nuclear
strikes upon any of those territories spread over the French border. The entire
argument was, in fact, neatly encapsulated by Georges Pompidou in 1964 when he
claimed the defence of Europe is “… physically and geographically inseparable
from that of France which is not the case with forces outside the European
continent even if allied”.⁶

But a weakness has lately appeared in this matrix of neo-Cartesian logic. This
is that the French authorities themselves no longer insist it embraces the whole
truth. For the retirement of President de Gaulle in April 1969 and the tragic
death in an air crash the previous year of General Allier, his chief of staff of
the armed forces, paved the way for a reappraisal. In an address delivered at
the Institut des Hautes Études de Défense Nationale in March 1969 General
Fournet, the new chief of staff, specifically rejected the tout ou rien approach
in favour of one in which tactical nuclear weapons might initially be used
independently of the strategic deterrent, thereby raising the threshold at which
the latter would be instantly and totally committed. One might add that by
introducing this qualification General Fournet moved close to the compromise
position the British, in their habitual fashion, had adopted between the years.

In a sense, of course, the European and American strategic forces stand in a
paradoxical or inverse relationship one to another, at least so far as the
protection of Europe is concerned. For the small but indigenous European
deterrents would appear at their most credible if a saturation Russian attack
was in the offing, the point being that they would then be liable to come almost
automatically into play in reflex retaliation against the wholesale destruction
of their own home region. The much bigger and more sophisticated American
strategic umbrella, on the other hand, would at its most convincing in the face
of more subtle and low-level threats. But the crux of the matter is surely this:
any theory of deterrence intended for comprehensive application to contemporary
Europe should take due account of the complementarity inherent in this contrast.
In other words it should incorporate both the Washington

and the Paris concepts. For, in the last analysis, nobody really knows what
course a nuclear confrontation might take once it had started. Nor is the case
for retaining and, indeed, consolidating this combination purely an exercise in
strategic theory. At the political level, too, it can be well sustained. Take,
for example, the widespread hope that Western Europe may eventually become what
the late President Kennedy used to call a "twin pillar" of the Atlantic
Alliance, a pillar both eager and able to relieve the USA of some of her
international burdens. Obviously the satisfactory operation of the bipolar
system would depend on a continual, and sometimes abrasive, interaction between
each pillar. Yet surely no attempt ever made by Western Europe, either openly or
covertly, to influence radically the thought or action of any American
administration on any crucial issue would be likely to be decisive unless the
sanction of dissociation from the USA could be invoked in the ultimate. Nor is
it easy to imagine how Western Europe might sustain the will fully to involve
itself in Alliance affairs in the absence of this fundamental option. No such
sanction could be credible, however, unless the Western Europeans already
possessed a foundation on which to build up their own defence, nuclear as well
as conventional, in the event of a profound rift with the Americans
precipitating a general withdrawal by the latter from this side of the ocean.
Fortunately any cleavage as serious as this appears improbable. Even so, it is
not absolutely inconceivable and Western European nations, like others, must
base their security arrangements on attitudes of reasoned pessimism. All the
same some cogent arguments have frequently been deployed against the
construction of a fully independent European strategic deterrent in any
situation which is at all likely actually to obtain in the foreseeable future.
Not the least is that such an initiative would almost certainly be read by the
Americans as an invitation entirely to relinquish their military commitment to
Europe at an early date. Not least among the adverse consequences which would
stem from that is the loss of a uniquely valuable body of experience in such
exotic branches of technology as nuclear warhead design, space reconnaissance
and battlefield communications. Then again, naval deterrence in the North
Atlantic is undoubtedly easier, both materially and psychologically, so long as
this ocean lies at the centre of a collective security framework. Furthermore,
American manpower is still a key element in local deterrence on land,
particularly in Germany. Nor is this by any means the total picture. For all
these strictly military considerations would have to be evaluated against the
background of confusion in politics and perhaps other aspects of national life
which a gratuitous rupture of the Atlantic Alliance could engender in all the
countries concerned. Hardly less disconcerting are some difficulties inherent in
the concept of a fully-fledged European strategic force. Presumably "Europe"
will henceforward mean in this context all the full members of the enlarged
Common Market, an entity which would clearly be unable to offer Greece and
Turkey as firm a guarantee as NATO does at present. Nor could it easily replace
the present implicit extension of the American nuclear umbrella to cover West
Berlin. Indeed, no such deterrent would be able satisfactorily to shield the EEC
area proper against every conceivable kind of nuclear blackmail or attack unless
it contained warheads and delivery systems which were both numerous and varied.
Yet the creation from the existing narrow base of a deterrent of an entirely
adequate size and quality under all conceivable circumstances could well take
one at least eight to ten years and cost some one or two billion pounds. What is
more, it could easily generate nuclear command and control problems at least as
perplexing as any NATO has ever had to face. A key factor in this complex of
problems are the constraints on nuclear access still operating in respect of the
Federal Republic, constraints which derive from memories of German aggression
and from the continued biscuit of the country as a whole by the Iron Curtain.
Not that even this issue is quite so straightforward as is sometimes assumed.
Already the Bundeswehr operates short and medium range tactical nuclear delivery
vehicles and so participates directly in the control of the warheads they would
be fitted with in the event of a nuclear conflict. On the other hand, during the
1954 revision of the Brussels Treaty of 1948 which established WEU Bonn formally
undertook "…not to manufacture atomic, biological, chemical, and certain other
weapons in West Germany…". Likewise

it is still all but universally assumed that a physical involvement on the part
of West Germany in strategic nuclear weapons would cause an adverse Soviet bloc
reaction a good deal fiercer than those induced by other moves to accord her an
improved status with the Western alliance. Besides, on 28th November 1969 Willi
Brandt's new government signed the UN nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty of 1968,
although it did simultaneously emphasise that ratification would have to wait
upon a detailed agreement on the coordination under the terms of the NPT of the
control functions of Euratom and the International Atomic Energy Agency. The
reservation still stands.

Not that this aspect of the German question should be allowed to overshadow the
more general importance of the NPT. Its essential purpose is spelt out most
clearly in Article I, this being “… not in any way to assist, encourage, or
induce any non-nuclear-weapon State to manufacture or otherwise acquire nuclear
weapons or other nuclear explosive devices, or control over such weapons or
explosive devices”. Every state in Europe bar Albania, France, Portugal, and
Spain has signed by the end of 1969. Now that 40 states across the world have
both signed and ratified, this treaty is operative. Therefore to all intents and
purposes the only indigenous nuclear forces permissible henceforward in Europe
will be the existing ones—i.e. those of Britain and France. And, in principle,
this will remain true at least until such time as the EEC achieves a federal or
confederal status.

Yet this is not the whole story for the simple reason that, in legal terms
alone, the NPT will constitute a very tentative arrangement even after all the
arrangements for monitoring have been worked out. As Article VI makes clear, no
signatory will remain morally bound by the treaty unless it leads on to more
general disarmament. Besides which, any government can withdraw from it at a
mere three months’ notice “… if it decides that extraordinary events, related to
the subject-matter of this Treaty, have jeopardised the supreme interest of its
country” (Article X). So in Europe, as everywhere else, the battle against
nuclear dissemination is by no means won as yet.

Weight is lent to this conclusion by some omnious technological trends. The
know-how for the gas centrifuge, a compact and so relatively cheap way of
enriching uranium, has started to spread through Europe. At present the sole aim
is to consume the metal thus refined in civil reactors. But this could pave the
way for the much higher enrichment needed for military purposes.

Nor is this the most immediate peril in this area. In the course of the
consumption of natural or enriched uranium in these reactors, a fissile isotope
not found in nature—Plutonium 239—is formed as a by-product.6 And whereas this
has only a limited industrial value, it is eminently suitable for the
fabrication of nuclear warheads. Worse still, it can be comparatively easily
separated by chemical means.

The scale and imminence of the resultant hazard can be illustrated by reference
to one of the smaller European states. For even the small research reactor
supplied to Yugoslavia by the USSR about a dozen years ago yields enough
plutonium to make a Nagasaki-type bomb every four years; and a distinguished
Yugoslav physicist has alleged that this is precisely what a then powerful
faction inside the government had in mind.7 Be that as it may, the ultimate
possibilities of proliferation are especially horrendous in a region as
industrialised as Europe. By 1980 the present members of Euratom alone will
probably have power reactors churning out enough to make not less than 1,000
such warheads a year. A most sophisticated anti-proliferation strategy will have
to be devised to manage this situation both within Europe and throughout the
world at large.

In my view, a decisive longer-term argument for the preservation of an adequate
collective security system in Western Europe lies herein. For were this system
to disappear various states in the region might acquire national deterrents at
least ostensibly able to threaten a simple “bee-sting” kind of reflex
retaliation in the event of a Russian nuclear attack. Nor should we be concerned
in this connection only with Atlantic Alliance members. There are several
countries8 which are today interposed between NATO and a Soviet military
presence and which therefore would grow acutely apprehensive in the event of a
collapse of the existing regional balance.

(2) Anglo-French nuclear collaboration?

In principle, technological evolution could affect the efficacy of a strategic
deterrent in a whole variety of ways. Nevertheless, it does seem improbable
that, in the course of the next 10 to 15 years, this factor will do very much
either to undermine or to reinforce the American guarantee to NATO Europe. For,
while the intercontinental nuclear deadliness may gradually get more complex, it
is unlikely to be broken.

More specifically, nobody seriously expects it to include an Anti-Ballistic
Missile screen on European soil. For, although the Nuclear Planning Group did
discuss this when meeting at The Hague in April 1968, it dismissed the idea on
grounds of cost-effectiveness and also for other reasons.

Perhaps the only other possibility to note in this connection is that eventually
some NATO tactical nuclear forces on the continent of Europe may re-equip with
ground-to-ground missile systems sufficiently large and versatile to have an
incidental and marginal but none the less significant capacity for strategic
delivery against the USSR. much as some strike aircraft do today. But when or if
this change might take place or what impact it might have is a matter of pure
speculation.

In this age of intercontinental deterrence European nations cannot realistically
hope ever to regain the incremental assurance once inherent in being able to
offer vital support facilities for American strategic forces. Back in the 1950s
the airfields made available to medium strategic bombers and reconnaissance
aircraft by countries like Britain and Spain were a key element in the overall
nuclear ascendency of the United States. But today she has vast numbers of
missiles and rockets, as well as plenty of

reconnaissance satellites, in or operating out of her own home territory. True
some Polaris boats of the United States Navy habitually cruising in European
waters find it convenient to operate from anchorages in Scotland and Spain. In
addition, sundry arrangements continue for coordinated surveillance all round
the Atlantic Basin, the most noteworthy in this particular connection being the
two Ballistic Missile Early Warning Stations in North Greenland and England
respectively. Yet none of these Alliance links can sensibly be regarded as
either actually or potentially indispensable to the USA's capacity to wage a
global war.

However, her status as a guarantor could be affected in a negative sense, by
social and political changes. Ever since the foundation of NATO the Russians
have behaved as though they believed America's psychological commitment to the
territorial integrity of the other members was absolute, whatever anybody else
thought. Yet a further growth of introspection within the United States could
induce a fresh assessment by them, especially if it followed defeat in Indo-
China and ushered in major troop withdrawals from Germany. On the other hand,
there are several quite feasible adjustments whereby any such disposition within
the Kremlin might be nipped in the bud. One is to make the overall nuclear
deterrent look more convincing by making the tactical nuclear element within it
more secure, a possibility that will be further examined below. Perhaps, too,
the concept of mixed-manning could be revived in a modest way. There may also
still be scope for increasing allied participation in American crisis management
and continuity planning.

Then there is the question of whether whatever uncertainties may develop in the
minds of either adversaries or allies in regard to American reactions might not
be offset in part by consolidating the British and French strategic deterrents
by some process of merger. A flurry

of interest in this subject did, in fact, occur during the presidential election
in France subsequent to the retirement of Charles de Gaulle in 1969. Thus, Alain
Poher, the President of the Senate who stood as the candidate of the Centre,
deplored the expense of a national deterrent that remained little more than a
"panoyle of prototypes". His view was that this force would be better deployed
within an Atlantic Alliance so reconstructed as to give Europe parity with the
USA. Meanwhile, Georges Pompidou himself contended that "the future of a common
European defence in the nuclear field lies in an agreement between France and
Britain". And soon after the election —on 27th June to be precise—the new
Premier, Jacques Chaban-Delmas, reiterated this interest in cooperation with
Britain in respect of nuclear arms. He commented that France's effort could
thereby "cease to be national and become European".

Nor was this the first time the matter had been broached at top political level.
On the contrary, Edward Heath and Franz-Josef Strauss had several times called
for this bilateral integration in the hope that it could provide the foundation
of the military aspect of a "European core" within the Atlantic Alliance. Not
that the idea was then or is now received with universal enthusiasm. Michel
Debré, French Minister of Defence since 1969, is blatantly scornful of the very
thought that nuclear autonomy might ever be divisible. Meanwhile most Social
Democratic opinion in Europe, especially in Britain and West Germany, is either
indifferent or hostile. Accordingly this possibility has deliberately been
excluded from the negotiations about EEC enlargement. Conceivably, however, it
did figure tentatively in the Heath-Pompidou summit talks last May. And, whether
it did or not, there is no doubt that some influential people in both London and
Paris are carefully keeping their long-term options open. Crucial to any scheme
that involved exchanges of nuclear and related data (i.e. anything beyond some
loose provision for joint operation in wartime) is whether France might be
permitted, or induced, to share Britain's special nuclear relationship with the
United States or whether, instead, this should be either pared down or
terminated. As is well known, this relationship first flourished during World
War Two. Thus the Roosevelt-Churchill Hyde Park agreement of 1944 provided for
full collaboration between the American and British governments in developing
fissile material for civil and military purposes after the cessation of
hostilities. Disregarding this, however, Congress in 1946 passed an Atomic
Energy Act (otherwise known as the MacMahon Act) which stipulated the United
States conduct "no exchange of information with other nations" of a sort which
might enable them to apply nuclear energy to any military ends. But then, in
1951, the qualification was introduced that data on reactor construction might
be passed if the Atomic Energy Commission's members were unanimous that as a
result "the common defence and security would be substantially promoted". Three
years later legislative constraints on the release of allies information about
nuclear weapons effects were removed, a relaxation manifestly intended to assist
the introduction of tactical nuclear weapons v-thin NATO. Finally, in 1958, the
MacMahon Act was more radically modified. What the 1958 Act of Congress did was
to authorise arrangements for the supply of fissile materials plus a range of
detailed information on nuclear weapon "design, development, and fabrication
capacity," to allied countries which had already "substantial progress" in these

What also needs to be remembered, however, is that in March 1959 the Eisenhower

due course France’s emergent force de dissuasion would benefit from the new
provisions. Indeed, that July Mr Dulles did promise help in the construction of

dogged opposition in Congress.

NO FIRST USE OF NUCLEAR WEAPONS

By Richard H. Ullman

In his first speech to the General Assembly, the leader of Peking's delegation
to the United Nations, Chiao Kuan-hua, stated on behalf of his government: "I
once again solemnly declare that at no time and under no circumstances will
China be the first to use nuclear weapons." And he continued: "If the United
States and the Soviet Union really and truly want disarmament, they should
commit themselves not to be the first to use nuclear weapons. This is not
something difficult to do."

Chiao's remarks, and their prominence in the first official statement by a
representative of the People's Republic of China before the United Nations, came
as no surprise: the declaration that China would not be the first to use nuclear
weapons has been a staple of Peking's foreign policy, constantly reiterated,
ever since the first Chinese nuclear explosion in October 1964. From a Chinese
point of view, such assurances make eminent good sense. In their first years,
the few Chinese nuclear weapons and their manufacturing facilities offered a
tempting target for a preemptive enemy. Indeed, it was more than once rumored
first that the United States and then that the Soviet Union was planning such an
attack. Even today a first strike could very largely eliminate China's nuclear
capabilities. In far as a solemn declaration foreswearing first use tends to
make it morally more difficult for others to use nuclear weapons against China,
the P.R.C. continues to be well served by it. Moreover, it is also self-evident
that, given the marked disparities between the Chinese nuclear force as it will
be for the rest of the 1970s and the forces possessed by the United States and
the Soviet Union, first use by China against either "nuclear superpower" (a term
the Chinese use only derogatorily) would be suicidal.

Now that Peking is at last represented at the United Nations, it is likely that
the theme of no first use of nuclear weapons will be heard with increased
frequency and from increasing numbers of voices among the non-nuclear powers,
fearful as they are that some day, in some perhaps unforseable circumstance,
nuclear weapons might be used against them. The United States and the Soviet
Union will undoubtedly be under more pressure to declare

"No use" carries with it the implication that the more possession of nuclear
weapons is wrong, and that existing stockpiles should be destroyed. "No first
use," however, has very different practical implications and probable
psychological consequences. It contains no built-in presumption toward the
phasing out of nuclear stockpiles. In the absence of dependable international
guarantees, a nuclear power undertaking not to initiate the use of nuclear
weapons would retain its ability to retaliate fully in response to the use of
nuclear weapons by others. It would, in short, give up nothing of its ability to
deter nuclear attacks. Many factors have contributed to the nuclear truce which
the world has observed since the bomb that destroyed Nagasaki in August 1945,
but not least—and perhaps most—among them has been the knowledge that the United
States and the Soviet Union (and lately, perhaps, other nuclear powers as well)
are able to absorb a devastating nuclear attack and yet respond in a manner that
will devastate the attacker. Under a "no-first-use" obligation, no matter what

As regards Asia, even more than Europe, U.S. military doctrine has been to
regard nuclear weapons as a cheap and effective barricade against communist
"hordes." Asia, and not Europe, has been the scene of the only U.S. threats to
use nuclear weapons —to induce the Chinese Communists to accept a ceasefire in
Korea in 1953 and to forestall their invasion of the Nationalist-held offshore
islands in 1958. Yet while the doctrine remains the same, reality is changing.
The domestic outcry which greeted rumors—almost certainly unfounded—that the
Johnson administration was planning to use nuclear weapons, if necessary, to
lift the siege of Khe Sanh in 1968, led to official statements that the use of
such weapons in the Southeast Asian war was out of the question. At the same
time, there is a heightened general awareness of the racist undertones of a
military doctrine which had implied a greater readiness to use nuclear weapons
against Asians than against Europeans. These sensitivities, combined with our
new relationship with China and a widespread realization that we had wildly
overestimated the likelihood of Chinese military expansionism, make it
increasingly improbable that we would ever initiate the use of nuclear weapons
in any foreseeable Asian contingency.

V

A U.S. commitment to "no first use" might come through agreement with one or
more other governments, or it might be unilateral. In addition, it might be
general or specific (i.e. confined to a specified geographic region) in its
application. A logical first such bilateral agreement would be between the
United States and China. Each would pledge not to initiate the use of nuclear
weapons against the territory or the armed forces of the other. Such a bilateral
agreement would perhaps be easier to negotiate than one that is multilateral. In
addition, it might be easier to get agreement to it within the U.S. government
than a consensus on a wider commitment. An agreement including the Soviet Union
might be postponed until agreement had been reached on other matters affecting
European security, such as mutual and balanced force reductions. On the other
hand, a bilateral U.S.-China agreement would necessarily leave the parties free
to initiate the use of nuclear weapons against third countries, which would
clearly be undesirable. (It can be argued, however, that China has already
assumed unilaterally a general "no-first-use" commitment which would not be
reduced except by explicit reservations.)

multilateral, rather than bilateral—agreement. An international agreement would
be seen both in the United States and abroad as another measure of arms control,
a follow-on to the test-ban, the nonproliferation treaty, SALT and the treaties
banning nuclear weapons from Latin America, Antarctica, the seabeds and outer

Yet, desirable though "no first use" by international agreement might seem,

unilateral declaration, as the Chinese have done. Within the United States, a
unilateral declaration would meet less resistance from the foreign affairs and
military bureaucracies and the Congress, because its wording would not need to
be negotiated with other governments and therefore could incorporate any
shadings or reservations—such as a statement of conditions which would make the
declaration void—which might be regarded as necessary on political grounds.

Moreover, of all the various measures for arms control already in effect or
foreseeable, "no first use" is the least dependent for its efficacy on
international agreement. The purpose of a "no-first-use" commitment is to
decrease the likelihood that parties to a conflict will resort to the use of
nuclear weapons. Even if only one party (call it nation A) has made such a
commitment, the behavior of its opponent (nation B) will also be affected. A's
"no-first-use" declaration will give B at least some assurance that A will not
strike with nuclear weapons. B will therefore have less of an incentive to use
nuclear weapons to strike preemptively at A's nuclear forces in order to
minimize the damage that they might inflict. Indeed, B will have a positive
incentive not to use nuclear weapons, for their use would free A from its "no-
first-use" commitment, and invite nuclear retaliation. Thus, a "no-first-use"
commitment undertaken unilaterally by only one party to a conflict will still
serve to widen the fire-break between conventional and nuclear weapons for the
conflict as a whole. And unilateral "no-first-use" declarations will encourage
other governments to do the same: the more that do so, the wider the fire-break
becomes.

the consequences for individual nations are exactly the same, for "no first use"
does not depend for enforcement upon international mechanisms. Instead, it is
self-enforcing: once a nation violates its pledge of "no first use," others are
thereby released from their pledges, subjecting the violator to the prospect of
nuclear retaliation—a prospect which would be a powerful deterrent against
violation. This simplicity
