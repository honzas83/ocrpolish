> # Communicating NATO's Intentions - Warning Messages
> 
> This working paper, prepared by the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Staff Group, examines the strategic implications of NATO issuing warning messages to an enemy prior to the use of nuclear weapons. The document outlines a draft report by the Permanent Representatives on the problem of whether NATO should initiate warnings before nuclear weapon use in the event of a conflict. It references previous studies on Communicating NATO's Intentions (CNI) and notes that it addresses the first of two key questions identified in the CNI Study. The paper distinguishes between messages of information and messages of warning, noting that the warning aspect predominates when a message is sent before a specific decision to use nuclear weapons has been reached or to threaten an enemy to force a withdrawal. The Analysis considers two potential Soviet responses: a 'doctrinal and rigid' approach, where warnings might be trigger a pre-emptive nuclear attack, and a 'flexible and cautious' approach, where a warning might tilt the balance toward a political solution. The document concludes that while warnings may have some advantage in avoiding conflict escalation, the principal danger is the risk of causing the Soviets to initiate tactical nuclear warfare.

COMMUNICATING NATO'S INTENTIONS - WARNING MESSAGES
Draft Report by the Permanent Representatives

I. PURPOSE
1. This report examines some problems in connection with the question whether
NATO, in case of conflict, should initiate warnings prior to a nuclear weapon
use.

II. BACKGROUND
2. At their meeting in Ankara in May, 1973 the NPG Ministers invited(1) the NPG
Permanent Representatives to initiate a study on the implications of
communicating NATO's intentions (CNI) to an enemy, to other countries and to the
public. An initial CNI Study(2), prepared by the NFG Staff Group, was forwarded
to Ministers, togther with a Permanent Representatives' report(3) at their
Monterey meeting in June 1975. These documents have considered in depth the
communication that NATO might choose to make in support of a decision to use
nuclear weapons selectively. The NPG Ministers agreed(4) that work on the
subject should continue.

3. The Permanent Representatives' report on the CNI Study identified in
particular two questions:

a. whether a warning message should be sent prior to the nuclear use decision or
whether such a message might be sent after a decision but prior to actual use;
and

b. whether it might be desirable to develop a set of procedures to assist CNI
consultation.

This report addresses the first of those two questions. It should be read in
conjunction with the above-mentioned documents to which it forms a companion
piece. The question of procedures remains under consideration.

III. WARNING MESSAGES
Definitions
4. There is no absolute distinction between messages of warning and messages of
information. Any communication to the enemy concerning the possible or actual
use of nuclear weapons is bound to comprise both aspects. The CNI Study
considered messages which would be sent after a firm nuclear power decision had
been reached (but not specifically to seek to cause the enemy to ease his
aggression and withdraw by making the use dependent on his reaction to the
message). Thus, the information aspects of the message would predominate,
although there would be a warning, explicit or implicit, of further use.

5. The present paper considers messages where the warning aspect predominates
either because the message is sent before a specific decision to use nuclear
weapons has been reached or, a nuclear power decision having been made, to
threaten an enemy with the use which will be withheld if he responds correctly
to the message.

6. The Study on Warsaw Pact Politico-Military Strategy and Military Doctrine for
the Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons(1) says that, "…… there may be two main
courses of action which Soviet leaders may pursue in a crisis: a doctrinal and
rigid one or a more flexible and cautious one. While much of the evidence is in
favour of the former option (which would point to widescale nuclear response to
NATO initial use), there are a number of factors which suggest that there would
be some pressures on the Soviet leadership to limit the conflict and/or to end
it through political means."(2) The latter Soviet attitude was used as a
starting assumption for the study's analysis of the possible Soviet response to
NATO initial use on the grounds that, if the Soviets followed the other
approach, analysis was unnecessary. Both assumptions can be taken as starting
points in assessing the advantages and disadvantages of warning messages. They
represent the opposite poles of Soviet behaviour, which, in actuality, might not
be so clearly definable and might, indeed, change from one type to the other
during the course of the conflict.

(2) DPC/D(74)18, Annex, Volume I, paragraph 6

7. Doctrinal and Rigid Approach. By a doctrinal and rigid approach is meant that
Soviet wartime behaviour would be consistent with their enunciated doctrine and
policy. In this case it is hard to see that the warning aspects of a message
would have any great effect on an enemy which was already prepared for a full-
scale nuclear war. Indeed, it might well trigger a Soviet pre-emptive nuclear
attack. On the other hand, the information aspects of a message might have an
important function. The Military Intelligence estimation is that "if the Soviet
leaders became convinced that NATO was about to deliver strategic nuclear
attacks against them, they would almost certainly order pre-emptive nuclear
attacks."(1) Thus, it is possible that a message informing the Soviets that NATO
was about to make limited tactical use of nuclear weapons might, at least
temporarily, stay the hand of the Soviet leadership. But it is unlikely that,
with this starting assumption, a warning message could make much difference in
the long run because, even if he ignored it, his reaction to NATO's initial use
would surely be the pre-emptive nuclear attack that he failed to make in
response to the warning message.

8. Flexible and Cautious Approach. The term "flexible and cautious" approach is
not susceptible of precise definition as it encompasses a range of possible
Soviet attitudes to the war. The Warsaw Pact Study should be consulted for an
extensive description. For the purposes of this paper, it can best be
illustrated by a range of Soviet perceptions as follows:

a. NATO would not have the nerve to go nuclear and that NATO Europe could,
therefore, be overrun by conventional means alone; or

b. NATO might well make limited tactical use of nuclear weapons (but, if not,
case a. above) but accepting this risk for limited territorial gains, with a
ceasefire after NATO initial use; or

c. there would be a limited tactical nuclear exchange after which NATO
resistance would collapse.

The question that poses itself is whether, having committed themselves to a war,
the Soviet leadership would be swayed by a warning message. This question cannot
be answered in advance. All that can be said is that the warning may have the
desired effect; for instance, if the Soviet leadership was divided, a strong
warning might tilt the balance towards seeking a political solution. Thus, there
may be advantage, but the possible advantage may even then only be partial.
Thus, in case b., he may cease his advance, but, in keeping with his
calculations, sit tight on the territory he had gained; NATO might, therefore,
still be obliged to use nuclear weapons. Against these possible advantages must
be set the possible disadvantages. Clearly, the principal danger is of causing
the Soviets to initiate tactical nuclear warfare. This must be considered
unlikely in cases a. and b. above but possible (or even, since the enemy would
have calculated for a limited tactical nuclear exchange, probable) in case c.
