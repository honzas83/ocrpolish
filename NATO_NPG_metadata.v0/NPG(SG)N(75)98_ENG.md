> # Articles Related to Nuclear Defence
> 
> On November 19, 1975, the Vice-Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) issued a notice containing three articles on modern weapon developments for the interest of Staff Group members. The first article, written by Richard Burt, examines the impact of cruise missiles on arms control and the Strategic Arms Limitation Talks (SALT), arguing that the blurring of lines between strategic and tactical weaponry may undermine traditional deterrence. The second article, from The Economist, discusses the ongoing negotiations between the United States and the Soviet Union regarding SALT, highlighting the political pressures and technical disputes over multiple independently-targetable re-entry vehicles (MIRVs) and the Back-fire bomber. The third article, authored by Col. Barney Oldfield, focuses on the Lance missile system. The document emphasizes that the opinions expressed in the attached articles are those of the authors and do not necessarily reflect official positions or the endorsement of the International Staff.

ANNEX A to NPG(Staff Group)N(75)98

ARMS CONTROL AND THE CRUISE MISSILE: RECONCILING A DIFFICULT EQUATION
by Richard Burt

Debates in the United States over new strategic weapon programs have fallen into
a wearisome routine. When the Pentagon approaches Congress for funds for the Air
Force's B-1 bomber or the Navy's Trident submarine, dedicated arms controllers
swarm over Capitol Hill and warn of the dangers of "destabilizing" the U.S.
Soviet strategic balance by "fueling the arms race." The defense community, for
its part, talks in similar knee-jerk fashion of "maintaining the technological
initiative" and acquiring "bargaining chips" for arms control negotiations.

It is particularly noteworthy, then, when a new piece of hardware fails to
elicit such a swift response and leaves both groups uncertain and somewhat
baffled over its impact on the strategic balance and its long-term implications
for arms control. This is precisely the state of affairs with the strategic
cruise missile program.

Now under joint development by the Air Force and the Navy, the cruise missiles
are missiles, low-altitude rockets that by the early 1980s will reportedly be
able to deliver nuclear warheads over ranges exceeding 2,000 miles with extreme
accuracy: less than 15 feet in length and in credibility weight, they could be
launched from the wings of bombers, out of submarine torpedo tubes, from the
decks of ships or from launchers on the ground. Because of the adaptability of
cruise missiles, observers have been quick to point out

The writer is assistant director of the International Institute for Strategic
Studies in London.

that they pose serious monitoring problems to U.S.-Soviet efforts to place
mutual ceilings on weaponry at the Strategic Arms Limitation Talks (SALT).
Indeed, the question of whether to include cruise missiles in a SALT arrangement
(and how to count them if they are included) is one of the last unresolved
issues that stands in the way of a new agreement, a question that appears to
have been at the heart of the dispute that ultimately led to James Schlesinger's
dismissal last week. But what many analysts appear to have failed to recognize
is that the central problem is not what SALT will do to the cruise missile, but
what weaponry

like the cruise missile will ultimately do to SALT.

The cruise missile is not simply another dubious addition to the American stable
of nuclear arms, but the most visible forunner of a new generation of weapons
technology that promises to alter fundamentally how we think about deterrence
and defense in the nuclear age.

Negotiators must grapple with the fact that cruise missiles can be fired from a
variety of launchers, but a greater problem is that these weapons can be fired
at so many different targets by so many different nations. Unlike
intercontinental-range ballistic missiles, whose deployment is now regulated by
SALT, cruise missiles will be able to perform both strategic and shorter-range
tactical missions with equal efficiency. Armed with conventional warheads, they
could have the accuracy to undertake numerous missions. For instance, as anti-
ship missiles, they might replace far-more-expensive carrier aircraft. Yet the
same missiles could be quickly adapted to deliver nuclear weapons, and deployed
aboard ships in the North Atlantic or on mobile launchers in Central Europe.
They could carry out strategic strikes on the Soviet Union. A tactical weapon
one minute would essentially become a strategic weapon the next.

But an even more profound development is perhaps on the horizon. With missile
accuracy approaching fantastic limits, there is the possibility that
conventionally-armed cruise missiles might be able to engage in strategic
strikes against the Soviet Union. Precision guidance and improved conventional
munitions would thus introduce a new era of "strategic conventional" wars — the
destruction of strategic targets in the Soviet Union (or the United States)
without crossing the nuclear threshold.

In attempting to come to grips with the significance of this development, it is
important to emphasize how strategic warfare became synonymous with nuclear
warfare in the postwar era. Deterrence of strategic attack rested on the mutual
fear of using nuclear weapons. But with the advent of strategic conventional
weaponry, this simple equation is undermined.

For arms control the implications appear ominous. In many ways, the ground rules
for discussion at SALT reflect the technological and political realities of a
vanishing period, when strategic arms could be more easily distinguished from
other categories of weaponry and when they were only possessed in great numbers
by the two superpowers.

But now criteria for distinguishing between strategic and tactical weaponry are
rapidly disappearing. And as strategic conventional war becomes feasible, there
is little reason to believe that other nations, especially the NATO allies in
Europe, will resist the temptation of arming themselves with weapons that can
strike the Soviet homeland but do not bear the onus of triggering a nuclear
threshold.

How should we approach the task of salvaging arms control in the face of new
weapons technology like the cruise missile? Initially, a more thorough
appreciation of the problems and prospects raised by the blurring of nuclear and
conventional weapons is required. A major premise in arms control thinking has
been placed on "raising the nuclear threshold." Now there is a possibility that
the United States could engage in non-nuclear strikes against the Soviet Union,
following a large-scale attack on Europe. But how would this capability affect
prospects for escalation? The caution bred from fear that characterizes the
existing superpower military relationship could be dissipated by a new set of
strategic conventional options. While perhaps raising the nuclear threshold,
weapons like the cruise missile could lower the overall inhibition to use force.

Until more complete answers are available, it will not be possible to fashion a
more coherent strategy for arms control. But it is clear that continuing along
the road paved by SALT won't work. While strategic and tactical roles can be
differentiated it may soon be no longer possible to identify weapons that
perform these separate missions. Moreover, as distinctions between nuclear and
non-nuclear weapons become fuzzier, so will political distinctions between
nuclear and non-nuclear powers.

If the Soviet Union and the United States are finally successful in ironing out
the details of a new 10-year agreement at SALT, there will be strong tendency to
argue that strategic problems have been relegated to the back-burner of
superpower relations. This must be resisted, for the real challenges lie ahead.

Source: The Washington Post, Sunday, November 9, 1975.

THE TORPEDO POINTING AT SALT

The drive to make a new missile agreement between the United States and Russia
is approaching make-or-break point. As usual, the real dealing is being done by
the men at the top rather than by the two countries' official negotiators at the
strategic arms limitation talks (Salt) in Geneva. The latest move was some new
American proposals transmitted to Mr Gromyko during his visit to New York in
September. The Americans are hoping to get the Soviet answer soon; perhaps when
President Ford and Mr Kissinger are in Europe in mid-November.

There is a hurry. Mr Brezhnev wants to get a new Salt agreement signed before
his party congress in February, and the Americans want to get it done before Mr
Brezhnev retires, which may not be long after that. If it isn't done then, they
reason, his successor will want to take a look at the whole immensely
complicated negotiating package, which could take until the American
presidential election in November. Not only would Mr Ford like to point with
pride to a new Salt deal during the election campaign, but if he should be
unseated his successor too would want to go back to square one if nothing had
been fixed up by then. The present interim agreement could then expire in
October, 1977, its terminal date, before there was a new agreement to replace
it.

Things have been moving. The Russians have made a major concession over one of
the three main outstanding issues, the matter of multiple independently-
targetable re-entry vehicles (Mirvs). They have agreed that any kind of missile
ever tested with a Mirv should be counted as being mirrored, and therefore
subject to the limit of 1,320 mirrored missiles for each side. If this happens,
American reconnaissance satellites can check reasonably well which kind of
missile is which, and count them. A second bone of contention, whether to count
the supersonic Russian Back-fire bomber in the total of 2,400 delivery vehicles
each side will be allowed to keep, can probably be dealt with too—perhaps by
asking the Russians not to build the airborne tankers that could make the
Backfires able to fly to America and back, and checking in five years' time to
see whether they have done as they said.

It is the third issue—the new cruise missiles the Americans are developing—that
seems to be causing the most trouble. The Russians would like to ban all these
weapons except the short-range ones. The Americans seem to be willing to apply
some limits, but to cruise missiles that can cover very long ranges; they also
want “short-range” to mean a lot farther than the 360 miles that was the first
Russian proposal.

The word “cruise” is used for slow—subsonic or barely supersonic—missiles that
are guided all the way to the target (as distinct from a ballistic missile,
which is aimed like a projectile by the initial blast-off), and breathe air into
a jet engine instead of being rocket-driven. It was a cruise missile that began
the missile age when the first German V-1 “buzz bomb” hit London on June 13,
1944. The ballistic missiles then jumped ahead. But now a series of
technological developments have combined to bring a long-range cruise missile on
to the scene for the 1980s:

● Fuel. Until fairly recently an aircraft missile had to be pretty big to carry
the fuel it needed to fly any distance, plus the weight of its warhead and the
guidance equipment it needed. But with recent advances in high-energy fuel
chemistry (and jet-engine economy) it is now possible to get two to three times
as many miles from a cubic foot of fuel as was possible only a few years ago.

● Explosives. New munitions, some still under development, can burrow deep into
the ground or through concrete before exploding. The results of the tests are
highly classified, but figures like 15 feet of concrete, 30 feet of hard soil or
150 feet of wet soil are probably not far off. Explosions at these depths cause
enormous craters. New conventional explosives can pack much more punch into the
same size than an old-fashioned 1,000-pound TNT bomb. These two factors
together—aided by this weapon's extraordinary accuracy—make it not unreasonable
to think of destroying missile silos, or airfields, with a non-nuclear missile.

● Miniaturisation. Not only have nuclear weapons been miniaturised, but —at
least as important for the cruise missiles—so have computers. The numbers are
enough to blow the mind. Here is one: it is likely that the cruise missile
computer of the 1980s will contain 100m bits of information in each cubic
centimetre of electronic memory.

● Accuracy. The computer is a major element in the exquisite accuracy of the
cruise missile. It seems that by 1980 cruise missiles, after 2,000 miles of
flight, will land within 30 feet of their targets. The inertial navigation
system will take them most of the way, by sensing all accelerations and
decelerations and comparing them with what should be happening to keep the
missiles on track (the computer will have this memorised in a few of its cubic
centimetres), and then making corrections by sending signals to the missile's
wings and rudder and elevators. But in spite of being able to measure shifts in
the centre of gravity within .00000005 of a centimetre, the inertial system can
make mistakes—and these tend to reinforce one another unless put right.

One way of putting them right is called digital map matching. A map is prepared
of a strip of land along the missile’s intended track, and then reduced to
numbers indicating the altitude at the centre of each square of, say, 10 metres.
At such tiny intervals the contours not only mark hills but would show such
things as water towers, office buildings and light houses. The missile "reads"
the contour of the track it is flying by a radar or laser altimeter (to an
accuracy of 5–10 centimetres vertically and about 15 centimetres horizontally);
its computer compares this with the ideal track, and corrects as necessary. The
image of the target itself can be stored by using altitude contours only or by a
combination of this with other features such as the reflection pattern of light,
or infra-red radiation. The missile can recognise the target as if it were a man
entering his own house.

So there it is: the modern cruise missile, all in a cylinder about 14–20 feet
long and 21 inches thick. It can be launched from the air, or from surface
ships, or shot from under water, whence it pops to the surface, lights its own
fire and goes merly on its map-watching way, purring along at around 500 miles
an hour as low as maybe 100 feet (to get under the radars), for 1,500 miles or
more. And it’s all on the drawing boards and in the labs; the tests are due to
start in 1977; some missiles should be operational at least by 1980.

And if all this weren’t enough, it will be cheap, too, as modern weapons go.
Probably about £250,000 each at today’s prices. With this price and the new
explosives, it needn’t necessarily have a nuclear warhead to earn its keep, and
can be a valuable addition to the arsenal for conventional war. Which is one of
the reasons it is so troublesome for Salt: you can’t tell what kind of war-head
it has even if you see it—or hundreds of them—coming. And an even bigger problem
is that it is impossible to check the absence of a weapon that fits into a
21-inch cylinder six yards long and can be launched from anywhere. The range a
cruise missile is capable of—which the Russians would like to limit—is equally
impossible to verify; a small reduction in warhead size (which could be made in
a few minutes) could make room for enough fuel for several hundred extra miles.

Any questions? A few. Does this mean that a lot of aeroplanes designed to attack
ground targets are now going to be looking for other work, because cruise
missiles can do their work for them? Yes. Can you call the thing back once it is
launched—unlike a ballistic missile—or change its target? Yes. Is the thing
invincible? No. You can shoot it down like any subsonic aeroplane, but it will
be hard because it is so small and flies so low. And it may come by the dozens
or hundreds. Is it really the greatest thing since sliced bread? No, but it
compares favourably with the average mousetrap.

Source: The Economist, November 1, 1975.

THE LANCE Missile System has a gross weight of 2,833 pounds, and is only
22-inches in diameter, with an overall length of 20 feet and six inches.
Constructed of thick aluminum, a maximum ruggedness is made possible by its
sealed modular design, its simplicity, the ease of its maintenance and high
reliability. Centrally mounted booster and sustainer engines, along with two
liquid propellants – Red Fuming Nitric Acid and Unsymmetrical Dimethylhydrazine
– make up the propulsion system which is pre-packaged in the LANCE body.

LANCE has an uncomplicated inertial guidance system which contains all the
sensing, the electrical power and electronic computation equipment which is
necessary to control the missile. With hermetically sealed, solid-state sub-
assemblies, this aids the replacement of needed parts. It’s a fast climber to
its maximum operational altitude of 150,000 feet, a thrust of 46,450 pounds
zooming it up there with but six seconds of “burn time”. Takeoff to target at
most extreme ranges takes less than 200 seconds. The primary launcher is mounted
on a modified M-113 type vehicle and it has a crew of eight men. There is a
third dimensional adaptation kit which can convert the primary launcher into a
lightweight, tow-able configuration for use in special environments such as
helicopter, or aircraft airlift, and it can swim deep inland waterways, and
functions un-dismayed in all manner of weather and terrain conditions.

A companion vehicle to the self-propelled launcher is the loader-transporter
which can carry two complete LANCE missiles and can be used in both the loading
and warhead-mating actions.

The Michigan Division of LTV Aerospace Corporation is the prime contractor for
the LANCE Missile Systems, and this Division whose primary product is the LANCE,
was founded more than a decade ago at the 2,000,000 square foot old Michigan
Army Missile Plant in Sterling Heights, a Detroit suburb. The employee force
exceeds 1,400 people, and it’s here that the missile’s tankage is manufactured
and final LANCE assembly completed. The Division also has a LANCE test and
support activity at the White Sands Missile Range, New Mexico. The first U.S.
Army LANCE Battalion – 1st Battalion, 12th field Artillery, was activated at
Fort Sill, Oklahoma in June of 1972, and in their very first firing of the LANCE
that November, six LANCES were used and all six banged right into the targets.
There are other U.S. Army LANCE battalions deployed in Europe with the 7th
Army.)

Considered a remarkably innovative approach to anti-tank warfare, the LANCE is a
veritable bee-ve of devastation when it arrives in the vicinity of any armored
vehicle massing or concentration area. The LANCE can spit forth from nine to 15
terminally guided, thermal homing sub-missiles which need only engine radiation
or exhaust emissions as a come-on to bring them in for their lethal workmanship.
These sub-missiles weigh about 30 pounds, of which a 15-pound shaped charge is a
part. Each of the sub-missiles is six inches in diameter, and 30 inches long.
Testing of these items was carried out intensively and with great success during
1974.
