> # Articles Related to Nuclear Defence
> 
> On January 22, 1974, the North Atlantic Council's Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) issued a notice containing three articles on nuclear defense. The first article, written by Marc Geneste, discusses the 'nuclear mythology' and the defense of Europe, specifically critiquing the 'myth of escalation' and the Flexible Response strategy. Geneste argues that the reliance on Flexible Response may have undermined the credibility of deterrence in Europe by creating a window for conventional warfare. He also touches upon the the Strategic Air Command's role and the impact of the Strategic Arms Limitation Talks (SALT) on nuclear arsenals. The second article, by W.S. Bennett, R.R. Sandoval, and R.G. Shrefler, is titled 'A Credible Nuclear-emphasis Defense for NATO'. The third article, by Dennis M. Gormley, is titled 'NATO's Tactical Nuclear Option'. The document emphasizes that the opinions expressed in the articles are those of the authors and do not represent official national positions or the endorsement of the International Staff.
>

>

> #Doctrine-and-Strategy/Flexible-Response — The document contains a detailed critique of the 'Flexible Response' strategy, mentioning the Kennedy and McNamara administrations' attempt to create a 'firewall' at the tactical nuclear level to avoid general cataclysm.

L'ESCALADE

De toutes les idées aussi sommaires que pérémissives attachées à l'arme
nucléaire, le mythe de l'escalade est le plus ancien, le plus salutaire et le
plus dangereux pour l'Europe.

Il est, en effet, à la base de la stratégie de dissuasion. C'est le spectre de
l'holocauste nucléaire général qui hante les esprits, dès qu'il est question de
la moindre affrontement entre puissances nucléaires : la guerre, pense-t-on, ne
saurait être limitée ni géographiquement, ni en violence. Dès lors, Russes et
Américains s'entendent plus ou moins tacitement pour éviter les contacts
directs; les querelles se règlent, jusqu'ici, entre valets périphériques. Après
la Corée, le Vietnam, pendant dix ans, a été une saisissante illustration de
cette salutaire retenue. Des milliers de B 52 ont déversé des millions de tonnes
de bombes pour couper les lignes de communication entre le nord et le sud
Vietnam, alors que les cargos soviétiques, jusqu'au blocs de Haiphong, amenaient

lequel, tachant d'acharnement en suisse, sans succès, les aviateurs américains.
Combien d'Américains ont-ils payé de leur vie cette singulière « éthique » de
l'âge nucléaire ?

Pour étranger qu'elle soit, le règlement du jeu est pourtant capital pour le
maintien de la paix. Des positions avancées de l'un ou l'autre camp, aussi
indéfendables militairement que Berlin et Cuba, doivent leur maintien réciproque

aussi intouchables (on peut l'espérer) par les Soviétiques qui pouvaient l'être

rempart contre une pression venue de l'est, car, en fond de tableau, se
profilait le spectre de Hiroshima et de l'énormité des stocks du Stratégic Air
Command (SAC). La vraie dissuasion, c'était cela...

Malheureusement, la perspective effrayante de l'escalade assortie de calculs
largement publiés de millions de morts qu'elle provoquerait en URSS, et par voie
de réciprocité, aux USA, devait immédiatement entraîner, dans l'opinion US, une
réaction bien compréhensible, mais redoutable pour le jeu de la Dissuasion en
Europe. Dès 1960, pour calmer les alarmes, l'administration Kennedy et son
secrétaire à la Défense MacNamarra, ont cru pouvoir tirer un pare feu dans
l'incendie, s'il s'en produisait un, au niveau inférieur à l'emploi tactique des
armes nucléaires, pour éviter le risque du cataclysmes général. Ce faisant, ils
acceptaient l'idée d'un engagement classique en Europe, possible avant le
recours à l'arme nouvelle, au-delà duquel plus aucune limitation n'était,

Autrement dit, pour apaiser les craintes de l'opinion US terrorisée par le mythe
de l'escalade, ils sapaient purement et simplement son effet le plus positif :
les bases de la dissuasion en Europe. Pour éviter que l'ombre de Hiroshima ne

camp...

Dès lors que la puissante Amérique, paralysée par la hantise de l'escalade, «
retient ses coups » en Corée et en Indochine au prix de son prestige et de
milliers de ses soldats (et bien que les risques d'extension du conflit y
fussent bien moindres), comment ne pas réduire en Europe de mauvais calculs de
l'adversaire, témoin de l'irrésolution officiellement proclamée par la Flexible
Response?

Tout cela était vrai il y a dix ans, en un temps où le Stratégic Air Command,
écrasant par rapport à l'adversaire, laissait malgré tout planer une menace
telle que sa faible crédibilité pour la défense de l'Europe restait compensée
par son excès de puissance. Aujourd'hui, les SALTS viennent d'interférer, par
traité, une part au sommet de l'échelle de la violence, certes discutable sur le
plan physique mais psychologiquement ressentie comme une véritable

serviraient plus qu'à leur neutralisation réciproque. Le SAC, sorti du jeu,

Alors il restera à l'Europe de la charge écrasante de rétablir par ses propres

organisant sa propre défense. Aide-toi, l'Amérique t'aidera.

Mais un autre mythe vient compliquer la tâche : la non prolifération, née, elle-
même du mythe de l'escalade.

LA NON-PROLIFERATION

Dès la première explosion nucléaire expérimentale, le 16 juillet 1945 à
Alamogordo, les témoins de l'événement évoquent l'apprentissage sornier et
quelques jours plus tard, Hiroshima troubloit profondément la conscience des
Américains qui se sentent responsables du déchaînement de forces surnaturelles,
inquiétantes pour l'avenir de l'humanité.

Plus prosaïquement, certains responsables y viennent, sans doute, la fin, à très
court terme, de l'inviolabilité du continent américain... Superbe générosité ou
froid calcul, ou les deux, le plan Baruch proposait un an après, l'abandon de
l'Amérique de son arme décisive sur l'autel de la paix du monde, la destruction
des stocks et le contrôle des Nations unies sur cette extraordinaire forme
d'énergie, aussi terrifiante que prometteuse. Les Russes entrent le projet et,
avec lui, la seule chance sérieuse qu'ait jamais eu la

« non-prolifération » d'une arme nouvelle : tentatives où la Papauté elle-même
avait échoué, naguère, lorsque le Vatican interdit la prolifération des
arbalètes, puis des arquebus. Doit-on en tenir rigueur à Staline? Oui, en

probablement sauver le monde d’un nouveau conflit que ses ambitions auraient

classique » serait rapidement devenu « nucléaire », car aucune force morale, ni
même aucun traité n’auraient pu empêcher les Américains en guerre de
reconstruire en quelques mois les engins qui avaient abattu le Japon et sauvé la
vie de centaines de milliers de leurs soldats.

On voit bien ainsi qu’en principe du moins, la « non-prolifération » n’est qu’un
mythe. Les armes nucléaires ont déjà proliféré — potentiellement — dans toutes
les Nations du monde civilisé, en fonction de l’évolution scientifique qui ne se
conforme pas de frontières. Et les nations évoluées, signataires du traité de
non-prolifération ont fait un geste de bonne volonté (assorti d’un espoir plus
prosaïque d’économies financières immédiates sur l’autel des grands principes
économiques et d’une bonne volonté des signataires, qui s’agit d’un pari
optimiste et hasardeux sur l’avenir de la paix, qu’une évolution défavorable de
la situation internationale leur ferait amèrement regretter, car il ne suffit
pas de connaître les principes scientifiques de ce nouvel armement, il y faut
tous les délais de mise au point, d’essais, de déploiement. Des années, entre le
potentiel scientifique de base et sa concrétisation militaire; des années qui
s’allongent sans cesse et deviennent irrattrabables; entre la science de base,
connue de tous, et la technologie des armements nucléaires modernes, dont les
progrès galopants impliquent un effort permanent à ceux qui sont entrés dans la
course, pour pouvoir y rester. Considérant qu’ils ont purement et simplement
sabordé leur défense.

En Europe, seules les deux plus vieilles puissances, instruites peut-être par
leur longue histoire, l’Angleterre et la France, n’ont pas voulu faire une
impasse aussi redoutable, baser leur sécurité sur des arcs et des flèches
illusoires ou se réfugier totalement, comme les autres, sous un parapluie
nucléaire américain percé depuis dix ans par la Flexible Response, et maintenant
fermé, ou presque, par les SALT. L’Allemagne, nucléaire émasculée, n’est pas
dans la course.

Il ne reste à l’Europe que les arsenaux nucléaires français et britanniques
appuyés, pour le moment, sur les seules ressources économiques de ces deux
Nations, bien minces pour l’ampleur de l’effort à prévoir, si l’évolution
politique ne dispense pas rapidement l’Europe de bâtir sa défense.

La responsabilité de l’Amérique est écrasante et, a posteriori, pourrait être
dramatique. Sitôt l’échec du plan Baruch, soit démarre l’effort nucléaire
soviétique, dont la première bombe thermonucléaire, de la première bombe

La « dose » représentée par les seules Forces stratégiques européennes actuelles
risque d'être fort insuffisante, car les conséquences d'une escalade aux
extrêmes, base de la discussion, deviennent calculables, et le nombre d'otages

trajectoires renseignées par satellites, peut éliminer d'une seule bordée les
moyens fixes de l'attaque, aérodromes et silos; les fusées des sous-marins,
jusqu'alors invulnérables, rencontreront le barrage des anti-missiles sur

hermétique à 100 %, l'évacuation préalable des villes, les abris, la défense

risque est apprécié par l'adversaire, et qui peut dire, à sa place, le prix
qu'il consent à payer? Dès l'instant qu'il devient calculable, le risque perd
beaucoup de son pouvoir inhibiteur. Ce n'est plus du poker.

En pratique, il est clair, aujourd'hui, que la capture par l'Armée rouge de
l'Europe occidentale, réduite à ses seuls moyens, coûterait infiniment moins
cher à l'Union soviétique que la deuxième guerre mondiale. L'Europe va bien
quelques villes; moyennant des précautions élémentaires, les reprisailles
massives franco-britanniques casseraient beau coup de vitres, mais probablement
moins de gens que l'on ne croit. L'adversaire pourrait même se payer le luxe de
ne pas riposter à notre unique salve anti-cité, pour prendre l'objectif intact.
En ce sens, l'atome stratégique n'a plus rien d'égalisant », au contraire, dès
qu'il perd son pouvoir inhibiteur.

Il est donc tout à fait capital, pour la sécurité de l'Europe par le maintien de
la Paix, de conserver un lien solide entre les facteurs d'inhibition : la masse
des stocks stratégiques américains, aussi incertains soient-ils, et les forces
stratégiques européennes, dont la faiblesse est compensée par la crédibilité. Il
faut donc, à tout prix, conserver l'Alliance, dont la solidarité nucléaire
stratégique, si mince et contestable soit-elle, rend à nouveau le risque in
calculable pour l'adversaire. La dose d'anesthésie doit être massive... Et l'on
comprend ainsi les limites et l'extrême danger des paradoxes, inspirés par le
mythe du « pouvoir égalisateur », qui ont conduit à des affirmations du genre «
à l'ère atomique, les Alliances n'ont plus de sens » — raisonnements qui ne
résistent guère à l'examen, mais égarant trop facilement l'opinion.

DISSUASION ET DEFENSE
A première vue, en effet, l'inhibition nucléaire, due à l'escalade aux extrêmes
est capable de prolonger la paix, par la terreur. Dès lors, à quoi peuvent bien
servir des systèmes de forces dits « classiques » extrêmement coûteux,
apparemment périmés, si personne ne peut plus songer à attaquer qui que ce soit
? Ne doit-on pas porter tout l'effort sur ces moyens de préserver la Paix, en
sacrifiant ceux nécessaires pour « gagner la guerre »; pas de demi-mesures, « si
tu prends mes billes, je te tue »; tout ou rien : les représailles massives, la
solution des problèmes militaires par le massacre des civils, le chantage au
suicide collectif. Inutile de refaire ici le procès de ce système : ce qui est
exagéré ne compte pas et sa faiblesse tient à son énormité. Les Forces
stratégiques sont aussi nécessaires pour l'inhibition des velléités guerrières,
encore faut-il que l'échelle de la violence comporte quelques barreaux. Sinon
elles ne sont pas crédibles a priori. L'Armée rouge à Strasbourg déclencherait-
elle la catastrophe général anti-cité et le suicide de la France? Il ne suffit
pas de le proclamer; il faut en convaincre l'adversaire... Reddition ou
holocauste. Rather red than dead; ce n'est pas une bouteille. C'est le choix
laissé par une stratégie de dissuasion, basée uniquement sur les Forces
stratégiques, si la dissuasion échoue. Or, elle peut échouer, et de diverses
manières qui, d'ailleurs, se combinent :

— la première est l'insuffisance de la dose d'anesthésique, on l'a vu ci-dessus,
si les Forces stratégiques sont trop faibles pour infirmer des dommages surtout
du domaine calculable et acceptable, ou si elles sont trop peu crédibles ;

— la deuxième est encore plus inquiétante : la dissuasion est basée sur un état
d'esprit, l'anxiété des dirigeants sur les conséquences d'une action
particulière; elle dépend de leur rationalité; mais, s'ils sont normalement
maîtres de leurs décisions dans l'environ-

THE NUCLEAR MYTH AND EUROPEAN DEFENCE
The course of world events would doubtless have been very different had the
birth of the nuclear age not been marked by the terror of Hiroshima. Should we
now exercise the diabolical aura surrounding the atom or should we maintain the
instinctive fear which is the very basis of dissuasion? Moreover, will that
dissuasion endure long enough for Europe to save itself the cost of a genuine
defence system?

The escalation myth on which is based a strategy of dissuasion is at once
salutary and dangerous for Europe. The Russians and Americans have a more or
less tacit understanding to avoid any direct confrontation. In order to
eliminate the terrifying prospect of escalation, Kennedy and McNamara accepted
the idea of a classic engagement in Europe—to delay recourse to nuclear
weapons—and pronounced the strategy of "flexible response". This would have been

A few months after Hiroshima, in July 1943, the Baruch Plan proposed the
abandonment of all nuclear stockpiles on the altar of peace, but it was ignored
by Moscow. This however was the only real chance of preventing a proliferation
of the new weapon. But on the other hand, was it not the existence of US stocks
which presented a new conflict between the West and the Soviets? This clearly
shows that "non proliferation" is just a myth. And the developed nations that
signed the treaty of July 1st, 1968 giving up the idea of their own nuclear
weapons are likely to regret it—they have simply sabotaged their own defence.
Europe's only defence is now the French and British weapons. But in the face of
the Soviet potential, with US allies paralysed by the fear of escalation and
European allies eliminated by non proliferation, what could France and Britain
do towards European dissuasion and their own defence if such discussion were to
fail?

Another curious factor in the nuclear myth is the notorious "equalizing power of
the atom". If the atom does indeed aestheticize the will-to-aggression, it in no
way equalizes the forces. The present European forces are likely to be
hopelessly inadequate, since their effectiveness lessons in keeping with the
rise in new factors in defence: counter-battery, anti-missile missiles, and
passive defence. It is essential therefore that Europe preserve a link between
its own strategic forces and those of the US. The North Atlantic alliance must
be maintained at all costs. The French and British nuclear forces must be
strengthened so that the potential threat they represent in themselves must to a
possible aggressor will exceed the value of the undertaking.

Indeed dissuasion does not dispense with defence, that is to say with the
balance of forces. In itself the strategy of dissuasion (by massive reprisals)
offers Europe only one alternative: capitulation or death. However, the strategy
may fail—first if the forces of dissuasion are inadequate, and second, in the
event that the Red Army launch an irrational aggression. Clearly therefore
European defence needs a classical potential and it is necessary to reestablish
a balance of forces, especially in tactical nuclear weaponry. Such a balance
could be achieved only if Europe had at its disposal the thousands of NATO's
tactical nuclear weapons. Nor neither Great Britain, paralysed as it is by the
non proliferation treaty, nor the French forces could suffice to defend the
continent.

Disarmament is desired by everyone. But it is wrong to say it must begin by the
abandonment of the most dangerous weapons. The fact has been insufficiently
stressed that the last localised conflicts (Korea, Vietnam, Israel) have broken
out in countries that had no nuclear weapons. Strategic nuclear forces, able
only to destroy, cannot in themselves constitute a defence system—they must be
backed by conventional forces. What they can do however is ensure territorial
defence. This means that the reduction of conventional weapons (especially
mechanized divisions) would in no way lessen the defence potential and it could
be achieved efficiently under shelter of the shield of strategic dissuasion and
of tactical nuclear defence. The MBFR negotiations for the reduction of forces
in Europe should be conducted in this light.

A CREDIBLE NUCLEAR-EMPHASIS DEFENSE FOR NATO
by W.S. Bennett, R.R. Sandoval and R.G. Shreffler
THIS is a proposal of long-term goals toward which changes
in NATO's defense posture should be directed. Its primary
aim is to increase the effectiveness of NATO defense
against Warsaw Pact aggression and to make that increase
manifest and credible to both NATO and Warsaw Pact
decision-makers. Negotiation of the issues dividing East
and West in Europe could then be approached by the NATO
governments, secure in the knowledge that the West's
military posture was adequate to repel any invasion.
An effective defense threatens nothing but the ambitions
of an adventurous aggressor. If a similar posture were
acquired by the Warsaw Pact, no harm would be done to
NATO's defense. Instead, the parties would enjoy a mutual
state of confident deterrence, or stalemate – a situation
even more stable than that now existing between the U.S.
and USSR strategic forces.
The primary aim of making NATO's defense effective, and
therefore credible, cannot be achieved without careful
consideration of the prevailing political, military, economic
and technical conditions affecting both NATO and the
Warsaw Pact. The necessary changes must be planned and
accomplished deliberately to avoid disturbing the
foundations on which the North Atlantic alliance is built.
In particular, positive attitudes toward the existing NATO
political and military posture must not be weakened during
the conversion to an effective defensive posture. Finally, it
is assumed that the configuration of a credible NATO defense
must result in a decreased economic burden.

the Soviet Union has acquired intercontinental nuclear

Our Perspective of Nuclear Weapons

The numerous complex parameters required to define nuclear weapons and the
infinite set of political-military situations their existence influences make
them difficult to classify. Are they defensive, retaliatory, or both? Confusion
is especially evident in this period when the United States is obliged to
formulate its world position accurately.

For example, the 7,000 U.S. weapons in Europe would be regarded by many as a
stockpile of defensive nuclear weapons. One would do better, however, to take
the opposite view and acknowledge that, although the NATO General Strike Plan
(GSP) is directed against military targets, its execution with NATO's present
high-yield weapons would cause widespread collateral damage in Warsaw Pact
homelands. This situation arises because we usually consider the GSP in the same
context as the U.S. SIOP — i.e., as a deterrent only. But because the Warsaw
Pact perceives the GSP to be coercive (or even pre-emptive) in intent, weapons
assigned to the plan cannot be regarded as relating solely to the defense of
NATO. Only those weapons put to use directly on the battlefield can be properly
described as defensive.

U.S. Courses of Action with Respect to NATO
Before addressing the NATO defense problem, it is appropriate to consider a
range of future U.S. military linkages with Western Europe. At the risk of
oversimplification, one might consider four courses of action: (1) Complete
removal of U.S. military forces from Europe as quickly as possible. (2) Complete
removal of U.S. military forces from Europe according to a schedule set by the
U.S. government. (3) Retention of present U.S. military forces in Europe
indefinitely into the future. (4) Retention of a reduced U.S. military presence
in Europe indefinitely into the future.

If any of the first three courses is pursued, there is no need to develop a
rationale to support refurbishing stockpiles of nuclear weapons or redesigning
the U.S. contribution to the military force. The action required to follow the
fourth course is less clear. U.S. international responsibilities and domestic
considerations should induce us to choose the fourth course, but drastic
revisions of the U.S. force will be necessary to make this a feasible course to
follow. Associated revisions of the NATO force as a whole would likewise be
mandatory to ensure the military compatibility of its components. Our proposal
addresses this problem.

U.S. political decision-makers can hardly choose intelligently unless they are
given carefully evaluated military options from which to select. In February
1971, President Nixon documented his frustration in developing these options:

The basic problem was not technical or esoteric. It was an absolute necessity to
devise a sensible posture of defense we can plausibly ask our people to support.
Many voters, legislators, and officials in Western countries have raised
questions about the continuing burden of defense budgets—not because they did
not see the need for security, but because they did not see a clear rationale
for the forces proposed. Our armies are not ends in themselves, or merely tokens
of a commitment. They have a function to perform: to aid in deterrence and to
defend if deterrence fails.¹

Defensive Capability and Deterrence

There is a great deal of discussion and confusion over whether and how
deterrence is associated with a war-fighting capability, which we prefer to call
a defensive capability. The following argument is presented for consideration:
First, we hypothesize that deterrence is a function — possibly some product — of
how an adversary views the military potential of your force and your willingness
to use that force. It is not hard to associate this definition of deterrence
with a conventional or a defensive nuclear force. For either force, one
associates military potential with defensive capability, and the close
connection is understandable if you assume willingness to commit the nuclear
force. We maintain that the U.S. President would encounter great reluctance, and
therefore great difficulty, in releasing defensive nuclear forces in NATO as
long as the NATO stockpile contains so many high-yield, indiscriminate weapons.
The present NATO force accordingly has little or no real deterrent value beyond
that attributed to the uncertainty of its use.

The situation is not so clear with respect to strategic forces. The military
potential of the U.S. or the Soviet strategic force can be placed near infinity
on any scale. Even as willingness to use the force approaches zero, deterrence
can be large, or at least indeterminate. Not only is the military potential
enormous, but it has an impersonal and unreal character — a far cry from the
concrete features that define a conventional or defensive nuclear force. U.S.
strategic forces, however, constitute the only significant deterrent we have in
Europe — a deterrent conceived before the advent of strategic parity.

Escalation of a Nuclear War in Europe

There is a puzzling ambivalence in current attitudes toward escalation. On the
one hand, it has been contended that any use of nuclear weapons introduces a
qualitative change in the nature of the battlefield. Allegedly, one side or the
other will find this change intolerable and attempt to redress it by widening
the conflict in some fashion that will initiate an irreversible escalatory
process ending inevitably in the intercontinental strategic exchange, an
untimely denouement. On the other hand, once the introduction of nuclear weapons
is postulated, escalation is viewed by the same people as the only credible

the intercontinental strategic exchange may well come to pass. But if our
security needs are to be truly met, it is essential that a war fought with
nuclear weapons should not escalate out of hand. Any employment doctrine must be
fashioned to this end.

The NATO Force and Its Interactions with the Threat
The character of the NATO force has varied little since shortly after its
origin. Briefly, NATO relies on a large conventional army, air force and navy;
the protection of the U.S. SIOP, if necessary; and nuclear weapons deployed in
the European theater, both as an ill-defined backdrop of the conventional force
and as a coercive deterrent, deliberately implying a trigger to the SIOP.

This combination of forces would seem unrealistic in light of the previous
discussion and the following points in particular:

(1) The inflexibility of the current NATO force causes in addressing problems
that arise in debates on SALT, MBFR and other security matters.

(2) The lucrative targets offered to Warsaw Pact nuclear weapons by a
conventionally deployed NATO force.

(3) The consideration of nuclear weapon use in extremis, particularly if the
conventional force has insufficient time to mobilize.

(4) The difficulty concerning Presidential release of nuclear weapons in the
theater, where the stockpile and delivery systems include threats to the
territory of the Soviet Union.

(5) The absence of suitable military doctrine for using nuclear weapons in a
purely defensive war.

(6) The fundamental difficulty of warping a conventional-force structure to meet
the demands of nuclear war and the associated difficulty of structuring a
sensible stockpile of defensive nuclear weapons.

(7) The probable Soviet intention to expand Moscow's influence without a major
war — certainly without a strategic nuclear exchange.

NATO should be extremely cautious in considering any change in its military

NATO'S TACTICAL NUCLEAR OPTION: PAST, PRESENT AND FUTURE
by Dennis M. Gormley
Beyond their value as a deterrent to war, how should our tactical nuclear
weapons in Europe be used to counter specific Warsaw Pact military threats?

WITH the above key question in his February message on "US Foreign Policy for
the 1970s," President Nixon renewed interest in an examination of the value of
tactical nuclear weapons in the defense of NATO. A year later, the President
added weight to the interpretation that NATO's strategy of flexible response
included the initial use of nuclear weapons, by stating: "… having a full range
of options does not mean we will necessarily limit our response to the level or
intensity chosen by an enemy." It thus seems clear that current NATO policy
includes a tactical nuclear option that may even be employed in response to a
Warsaw Pact nonnuclear attack.

The purpose here is to examine the military and political role and viability of
NATO's tactical nuclear option. The article includes certain proposals for
changes in the Alliance's military forces. In the course of examining NATO's
tactical nuclear option, primary attention will focus on the following
questions:

• Does our huge stockpile of tactical nuclear weapons in Western Europe
represent an effective military force that would minimize collateral (civilian)
damage, if employed?

• Can greater reliance on the use of tactical nuclear weapons compensate for
reductions in conventional NATO ground forces?

• Can NATO fight a controlled "tactical" nuclear war in Europe without
precipitating escalation to more destructive levels of nuclear conflict?

• Can NATO defend conventionally against supposedly superior Warsaw Pact ground
forces?

A full and frank discussion of these and other pertinent questions concerning
the future American military commitment to NATO is imperative given the dynamic
nature of the current political, military and technological environment.
Congressional pressures to reduce US ground forces in Europe are not expected to
subside. Preliminary NATO-Warsaw Pact negotiations on mutual and balanced force
reductions (MBFR) have begun. And the military services face hard decisions on
the next generation of complex and costly weapons systems—decisions that have
critical strategy implications for NATO. Central to the resolution of these
critical issues is the role tactical nuclear weapons should play in NATO's
defense of Western Europe.

Reliance on Low-Yield Nuclear Weapons: Its Limitations

Proponents of greater reliance on a new discriminate class of tactical nuclear
weapons tend to look upon the problem of escalation as one that can be
scientifically calculated through the use of certain analytical tools,
including, for example, systems analysis, game theory and simulation. 25 An
elaborate model of decisionmaker rationality is constructed in which an attempt
is made to show how prospects for certain types of goal-attainment are
calculable. This model of decisionmaker rationality represents, in effect, an
assumption that, if policymakers will only make correct (that is, rational)
choices, they can avoid the problem of escalation. As long as the conflict is
being fought cooperatively, it is implied, negotiated settlement follows as a
rational consequence.

However, it is entirely rational for the side that momentarily perceives it is
losing to act uncooperatively. Nuclear escalation is not necessarily an
irrational act for the side that perceives, however wrongly, that it is losing
and will not or cannot countenance the effects of an expected loss. Moreover,
the essential ambiguity of the military act tends seriously to erode each side's
ability to deduce the other's intentions, despite efforts to make intentions
explicit. The irresistible urge to assume the worst and avoid the error of
underestimating an enemy's intentions is likely to force each side into
seriously considering a strike at the other's retaliatory capability,
particularly in view of the enormous advantages surprise affords the attacker.
Can NATO safely assume that the Soviets would perceive its immediate defensive
use of nuclear weapons, albeit discriminate and measured, as merely an act of
defensive restraint? Would the Soviets disregard the source of energy of NATO's
low-yield nuclear weapons and view them as nothing more than conventional
explosives in convenient packages?

Interestingly enough, the belief that the Soviets would have an incentive to
fight a limited tactical nuclear conflict in Europe is predicated on another
erroneous assumption—namely, that the Soviets show little interest in
conventional war and, as such, have trained, organized and equipped Warsaw Pact
forces primarily for tactical nuclear war. 20 It is quite true that Warsaw Pact
ground forces were structured during the early 1960s to optimize their
effectiveness for operations in concert with nuclear forces. Essentially, this
strategy was the product of Premier Khrushchev's fascination with Soviet
Strategic Rocket Forces and his belief that deterrence could be bought cheaper

units. Consequently, Khrushchev significantly reduced the number of artillery
pieces and logistic supplies to improve mobility and reduce vulnerability.
Presumably, nuclear strikes were to perform the breakthrough function in
offensive operations. After Khrushchev's ouster, however, and particularly since
the mid 1960s, unclassified Soviet military articles have expressed an

The Conventional Force Balance Sheet

If greater dependence on low-yield nuclear weapons will not compensate for the
risks of nuclear escalation and reduce the risks of nuclear escalation, what
role, then, should tactical nuclear weapons play in the defense of Western
Europe? And is the current stockpile of nuclear weapons in Europe responsive to
this role? Before coming to these questions, it seems logical, first, to examine
briefly the conventional force balance sheet in Central Europe31 with a view
toward defining the future role of America's conventional force commitment to
NATO.

There is—and, indeed, there will continue to be—widespread disagreement on the
existing balance of nonnuclear forces in Central Europe. The pessimists,
consisting mainly of those who call for greater reliance on tactical nuclear
weapons, suggest that NATO can do nothing to redress a serious inferiority in
conventional capabilities vis-à-vis the Warsaw Pact. Spuriously, some even use
the analogy that, since we could scarcely defend the Republic of Vietnam where
we had vast air and firepower superiority, it is futile to expect to defend
conventionally in Europe.32 In contrast, the optimists hold that the nonnuclear
balance is maintainable even at substantially reduced levels of direct American
support to NATO.33

It is safe to say that the extremists on both sides are far off the mark. Both
start their analyses with a set of very leading assumptions and follow by
selecting figures and scenarios which best tend to support their argument. They
make calculating the military balance in Europe look easy, when, in fact, it is
an extremely complex task. In measuring the balance of forces in Europe, should
one consider only immediately available forces or the total capabilities of both
major combatants? What kind of conflict is most likely to occur in Europe—a
massive deliberate surprise attack, a slowly developing crisis, or a minor
border incident? How does one calculate the intangible factors of leadership,
morale, discipline and organization? Suffice it to say that these uncertainties
and intangibles, combined with the intrinsic conservatism of military planners,
must make it difficult for both American and Soviet military experts to write a
successful scenario of conventional operations with any degree of confidence.

The quantitative balance sheet shows that NATO does not suffer an enormous
disadvantage relative to Pact forces in Central Europe.34 NATO is credited with
approximately two-thirds the manpower, two-fifths the tanks, and one-half the
tactical air forces of the Warsaw Pact. When qualitative factors are considered,
the balance becomes much closer. For example, even though the Warsaw Pact has 60
percent more tanks, NATO's essentially defensive role and 50 percent superiority
in antitank weapons tends to redress this imbalance. The greater lethality in
NATO's conventional artillery and its greater logistic capability to sustain
higher rates of fire partially offset a numerical inferiority with Pact
artillery. And, as far as combat-ready forces are concerned, NATO has as many
fighter ground attack type aircraft as the Warsaw Pact. In overall resources,
NATO countries have more men under arms, and more money is spent on defense than
in the Pact countries. NATO also has greater civilian manpower and industrial
resources. While these overall resource factors would have little immediate
impact on a conflict situation in Central Europe, the Soviet military planner
must weigh their long-term effect.

Any fair analysis of the NATO-Warsaw Pact conventional balance should also
include a consideration of relative mobilization and reinforcement.35

and target location capabilities. There is no need, however, to spend lavishly
on the development of pure fusion weapons. While there is little doubt that such
an achievement would represent an important technological feat, it is unlikely
that the availability of "neutron bombs" would have any great military or
political value beyond our current low-yield nuclear technology.

Some, of course, will argue that ongoing Soviet-American negotiations, both for
mutual and balanced force reductions in Europe and the limitation of strategic
arms, 44 preclude consideration of a phaseout of theater nuclear weapons. To be
sure, negotiators must examine closely all proposals that could possibly lead to
mutual arms and force limitations or reductions. For example, MBFR negotiators
should consider a mixed package arrangement in which each side reduced the
capability that the other considered the most threatening—NATO tactical nuclear
weapons for Warsaw Pact tanks perhaps. We should not, however, underestimate the
