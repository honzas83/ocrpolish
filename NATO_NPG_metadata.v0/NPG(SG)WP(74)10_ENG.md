> # Draft Permanent Representatives' Report on the CNI Study
> 
> This document is a draft report by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) regarding a study on communicating NATO's intention to use nuclear weapons, referred to as the CNI Study. The report, authored by H.B. Seim, recommends that the NPG Ministers forward the CNI Study and accompanying comments to the Defence Planning Committee (DPC) via the Nuclear Defence Affairs Committee (NDAC). The CNI Study examines the substantive and procedural factors that NATO political and military authorities must consider when communicating intentions to an enemy, other countries, and the public to reduce the risk of escalation resulting from misinterpretation. The report emphasizes that communicating NATO's intention (CNI) should be part of a continuous series of political and military actions and must be consistent with NATO's deterrent policy to maintain credibility. It also notes that internal stresses within NATO could be aggravated if dissenting members are forced into CNI actions. Furthermore, the report highlights that CNI actions may be a necessity in cases of demonstrative nuclear use against no target to ensure intentions are made clear to the enemy.

I. PURPOSE

Political Implications

5. It appears from the CNI Study(1) that the main purpose served by CNI actions
is to reduce the risk of escalation resulting from a misinterpretation by the
enemy of the nature of NATO's use of nuclear weapons. This is clearly stated in
the Provisional Political Guidelines(2) and, in more general terms, is noted in
our Report(3) on 'the Study on Warsaw Pact Politico-Military Strategy and
Military Doctrine for the Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons(4). It is also obvious
that CNI actions should not be regarded as isolated steps, but as part of a
continuing series of political and military actions that very likely would have
been taken and would be underway from the very beginning of the crisis. In this
context, we agree with the Study(5) that the content of CNI messages should be
in harmony with such actions to avoid any possible loss of credibility, in the
enemy's eyes, of NATO's deterrent policy. In the same vein, we underline the
fact that CNI actions related to a specific use of nuclear weapons might be a
means to reinforce the evidence, implicit in the use, of NATO's resolve to
defend itself(6). We note, however, that if the decision to use nuclear weapons
did not reflect a broadly-based NATO consensus, internal stresses within NATO
could be aggravated if attempts were made to involve dissenting members in CNI
implementing actions, such as communicating NATO's intention to other countries
and to the public.

6. The Provisional Political Guidelines state(1) that the fundamental objective
of initial use will always be political and that demonstrative use would be
designed primarily to have psychological effect. They also point out(2) that, in
the case of demonstrative use against no 'target, it is particularly important
that NATO's intentions are made abundantly clear to the enemy. Without
attempting to pre-empt decisions that should be taken only at the time the
situation arises, we believe that, though not explicitly mentioned in the Study,
such a case is one of those in which the implementation of CNI actions might
very well be viewed as a necessity. While in general the observations made in
the CNI Study are pertinent to demonstrative use, we do not rule out the
possibility that, in such cases, the thrust of the CNI message might have to be
directed primarily toward convincing the enemy of the firm determination of NATO
to defend itself by whatever means may be needed, including the use of nuclear
weapons. In other words, the warning element of the CNI message may deserve
primary, if not exclusive, emphasis.

**Military Implications**
8. In dealing with the military aspects, the CNI Study recognises that CNI
actions could result in loss of surprise(1). However, the Study analyses a
number of factors that, under certain circumstances, could overshadow the
adverse element of loss of surprise in favour of other political and military
advantages of CNI. We consider this analysis very lucid and sound, and agree
with the Study conclusion that the consequences of possible loss of tactical
surprise should be carefully evaluated, at the time the need arises, against the
contribution of CNI actions toward minimising the risk of enemy over-
reaction(2).

**Procedural Aspects**
9. In agreement with the Study(5), we emphasise that the procedures for
consulting within NATO on CNI questions should be consistent with the General
Guidelines for Consultation Procedures(4) and have the same characteristics of
simplicity, flexibility and capability to adapt to prevailing circumstances.
Detailed procedures for internal NATO consultation on CNI questions should be
avoided. We are in agreement with the CNI Study's observation that the process
of reaching decisions on CNI questions should be viewed as an integral part of
the larger process of consulting on proposals or requests for nuclear weapon
use(5). We also concur that the Council/DPC should be the normal forum for
consultation on this subject(6).

Concluding Remarks
10. Noting that specific decisions with regard to CNI actions can only be taken
in the light of the circumstances that exist at the time that nuclear weapon use
is under consideration, the CNI Study expresses the view that it is nevertheless
essential that political authorities familiarise themselves now with the
substantive and procedural factors that would have to be taken into account in
dealing with the CNI question in time of hostilities(1). We concur with this
observation and suggest that this subject be covered, when feasible, during the
play of political authorities in future exercises, such as HILEX and WINTEX.
Moreover, the Nuclear Planning Group will continue to give active attention to
the CNI question during its work in the future.

IV. RECOMMENDATIONS
11. It is recommended that the DPC:
(a) note the CNI Study attached hereto and the related comments in this Report;
and

(b) agree that the CNI Study and the comments in this Report should be taken
into account in NATO peacetime planning and exercise play, and in decision-
making in time of hostilities.
