> [!abstract] 
> # Nuclear Consultation Procedures
> 
> This document, prepared by the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Staff Group, consolidates existing guidance on procedures for political consultation within the North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) when the use of nuclear weapons becomes an issue. It serves as an aid to political authorities and is intended for incorporation into the NATO Crisis Management Manual. The text emphasizes that while the ultimate decision to use nuclear weapons rests with the nuclear power, the greatest possible degree of Atlantic consultation is essential. It outlines the roles of the Council and the Defense and Political Committee (DPC), and highlights the importance of the views of countries most affected, such as those providing warheads or those whose territory is used for delivery. The document details the process for action initiated by a member government, including the submission of politico-military assessments and formal requests for nuclear use. It specifies that such requests must be sent to the Council/DPC, capitals, and Major NATO Commanders (MNCs) using FLASH precedence and a specific label. The process is instytucially designed to ensure rapid communication and secure information exchange between political authorities and military commanders during times of tension or war.

NUCLEAR PLANNING GROUP (STAFF GROUP)

DRAFT COVER NOTE FOR COMPIATION

In fulfillment of one of its basic responsibilities(1), the Nuclear Planning
Group has, since its inception, played an important rôle in developing
improvements in the machinery for carrying out agreed methods of consultation
with respect to the use of nuclear weapons in defence of NATO. as part of this
process, a number of related issues have been covered in various DFC and NPG
documents, and procedures for nuclear consultation established.

2. The proposition for NFG Permanent Representatives(2) to develop a compilation
of nuclear consultation procedures incorporating the diverse elements contained
in these various documents was approved by NPG Ministers in November 1973(3).

3. While work on the compilation of procedures was under way, two other
procedural aspects of the nuclear consultation process were being considered:
"Country Request" and "Communicating NATO's Intention to Use Nuclear Weapons
(CNI)". It was agreed that the completion of the compilation document should be
deferred until these two additional issues were approved by Ministers, thus
allowing their inclusion in the document. This now having been accomplished, the
NPG Staff Group has prepared a draft compilation document which is attached for
your consideration.

4. This document does not supersede any of the previously approved policy
documents and reflects the principles laid down in the General Guidelines for
Consultation Procedure on the Use of Nuclear Weapons(4). Its primary aim is to
provide an aid to those political authorities involved in the nuclear
consultation process. For this reason, the compilation document, when approved
will be incorporated in the NATO Crisis Management Manual which is now under
preparation by the Council Operations and Exercise Committee for approval by the
DFC. To avoid repetition, it does not cover those arrangements and procedures
common to both general and nuclear consultation, which will be dealt with
elsewhere in that manual.

PART I
NUCLEAR CONSULTATION PROCEDURES

PURPOSE
1. This document consolidates existing guidance on procedures for political
consultation within the Alliance at times when the possible use of nuclear
weapons becomes an issue.

SCOPE
2. The guidance herein is concerned with the procedural aspects of nuclear
consultation, which is part of a broader consultation or crisis management
process. It is necessary to stress the special features of the nuclear
consultation process, as set out in the General Guidelines for Consultation
Procedure on the Use of Nuclear Weapons(1). This compilation draws on the
General Guidelines, as well as a number of other approved policy documents;
Major NATO Commanders' Nuclear Weapons Release Procedures (NWRP)(2) also apply
for that part which concerns the submission of warnings or requests to political
authorities. These documents remain the definitive texts and are not superseded
by the compilation. To provide detailed guidance, the compilation has in some
places re-ordered and amplified the basic texts, whilst remaining consistent
with them. A list of policy documents dealing with political consultation is
attached at Annex A. Some definitions are contained in Annex B.

GENERAL
3. The principles of consultation on the use of nuclear weapons, accepted in
Athens in 1962, outline in general terms the possibilities for Alliance
consultation under three different assumptions concerning the scale of Warsaw
Pact attack(3). The General Guidelines, which include the Athens decision, note
that the ultimate decision on the use of a nuclear weapon will always lie with
the nuclear power, but that it is essential that there should be the greatest
possible degree of Atlantic consultation compatible with the time available and
the relevant circumstances before any final decision is taken. This consultation
is an essential element of collective planning and co-ordination. The normal
forum for consultation will be the Council/DPC, where member governments would
be able to express and compare their views. Recording NFG Ministers' decision in
the Hague in 1968,"

the General Guidelines note that special weight should be given to the views of
the NATO countries most affected, i.e.:

- the country on or from whose territory nuclear weapons would be employed;

- the country or countries providing the nuclear warheads;

- the country or countries providing or Manning the contemplated means of
delivery.

4. The general aim of all consultation procedure are to permit the rapid
expression of member governments views on any request or proposal for the use of
nuclear weapons in the defence of NATO, and to indicate that the Alliance as a
whole is concerned. Any request for the use of nuclear weapons in the defence of
NATO either from a member government or from a Major NATO Commander and any
proposal for the use of nuclear weapons in defence of NATO by a nuclear power
should be communicated to the governments and the Council/DfC.

5. The effectiveness of the nuclear consultation process is critically dependent
on the amount of available information and on the ability of the participants in
that process to exchange information and views in a rapid, secure and reliable
way. Nuclear consultation would take place against the background of the flow of
information which is envisaged in the process of general consultation during
peacetime and which is expected to increase in time of tension, crisis and
hostilities. In time of war, political authorities will need to be kept fully
informed of developments that may foreshadow the use of nuclear weapons. This
requirement will extend from the early stages of a conflict, when only very
general indications are likely to be available, through later stages when
specific release requests may be made.

ACTION INITIATED BY A MEMBER GOVERNMENT

6. In line with the General Guidelines, a NATO member government may, in a
politico-military assessment of the conflict situation, raise the issue of the
possible need for use of nuclear weapons or may make a definite request. To
allow modification in the light of actual circumstances, procedures related to
such initiatives have been kept as simple and flexible as possible(1).

Assessments
7. politico-military assessments prepared by member governments in time of
hostilities could properly contain national views on a possible need for the use
of nuclear weapons in defence of the Alliance. These assessments need not
conform to any rigid format, but they should contain sufficient information to
assist member governments and the Major NATO Commanders concerned in preparing
their views. The address on a national assessment of this kind will be AIG 6014,
which includes all political and military authorities concerned with the
consultation process. The precedence should be FLASH, while the classification
would be determined by the originator. The consultation process should start as
soon as a country assessment dealing with the possible need for the use of
nuclear weapons is received at NATO Headquarters and in capitals. To assist
nuclear consultation, the MNC(s) concerned should provide, as soon as possible,
their views on the national assessment to the Council/DFC and capitals.

Request for Use
8. If a NATO country decides to make a request for the use of nuclear weapons,
it should send a message to the Council/DFC, capitals and MNCs making the
request in as much detail as possible, and including all information which may
help in carrying out the consultation process. This information should state, in
particular, the politico-military objectives of the requested use and also the
possible consequences of non-use. The consultation process should begin as soon
as a country request is received at NATO Headquarters and in capitals.

9. It is essential for Country Request messages to be addressed to the MNCs at
the same time as to the political authorities, since the implementation of a
nuclear release decision is the responsibility of the MNCs alone. The use of AIG
6014 will satisfy such necessity. The messages prepared by the requesting
country should carry FLASH precedence and a clear identification (NUCLEAR
CONSULTATION MESSAGE label). The internal distribution and handling of these
messages should be in accordance with present procedures for nuclear
consultation messages.

10. Upon receipt of a Country Request, the MNC(s) concerned should provide, as
soon as possible, to the Council/DFC and capitals a military assessment of the
request including, where appropriate, recommended courses of action to satisfy
the purposes of the requested use. The MNC should particularly seek

ACTION INITIATED BY A MAJOR NATO COMMANDER
11. Major NATO Commanders have established common or similar Nuclear Weapons
Release Procedures (NWRF)(2). In implementing such procedures, the first
concrete action under- taken by a Major NATO Commander would be the transmission
of one of the following messages(3):

(a) Early Notification Message (SACEUR only – not formally part of his WillP
referenced in footnote (2));

(b) WILLY Message (warning message);
(c) ROMEO Message (actual selective release request message);
(d) SIERRA Message (preconditioned selective release message).
These messages may be originated by the Major NATO Commander on his own
initiative or as a follow-up action to similar messages (as those under (b), (c)
and (d)) received by him from a subordinate commander.

12. Early Notification Messages are part of SACEUR's separate procedures, by
which he provides early notification to national capitals and to the Council/DFC
when consideration is being given to the possible use of nuclear weapons in
defence of ACE.

HANDLING OF MESSAGES
13. Nuclear Consultation Messages would normally be sent over the NATO-wide
network. If, for any reason, it appears that delivery to any of the addresses
might be delayed or impossible on the NATO-wide network, it is expected that one
or the other of the NATO-wide switching centres would refile the messages on
another circuit, such as the HICOM network, for transmission to the relevant
addressses. Similar action would be taken by each country to ensure that the
receiving terminal will make immediate distribution of the messages to those
national officials and agencies requiring the information and cleared to receive
it.

COUNCIL/DFC CONSULTATION

16. To assist countries to gain as clear an understanding as possible of the
implications of any request or other messages concerning the possible use of
nuclear weapons, the Council/DFC would be able, during consultation, to call
upon members of the Military Committee for advice. However, such Military
Committee activities should be carried out in a way that preserves the basic
objective of the nuclear consultation process, namely, the timely presentation
of clearly-identified government views in the Council/DFC and capitals(1). If
necessary, the Council/DFC and Military Committee could meet in joint session
and assistance from Representatives of the Major NATO Commanders would usually
be available at NATO Headquarters. Major NATO Commanders are not expected to be
present.

17. Using the NATO-wide network or national facilities, the Representatives on
the Council/DFC and on the Military Committee would be able to send to their
respective capitals any additional information concerning the Selective Release
Messages and their implications. National views would, in turn, be relayed to
other member governments through the Council/DFC as the normal forum for
consultation. Depending on the circumstances, other channels may also be used
for the communication of views and information between capitals.

18. The national views expressed in the consultation process would be
communicated by the Secretary General to the nuclear power(s) concerned by the
most expeditious means available(1) for consideration in reaching a decision
whether or not to release the weapons for the proposed use. It can be expected
that the nuclear power(s) would also be kept informed by its( their) own
representative(s) on the evolution of the consultation process in the
Council/DIC and the national views expressed therein. In reaching a decision,
the nuclear power(s) would give special weight to the views of the NATO
countries most directly affected by the requested use(2). Major NATO Commanders
must also be kept up-to-date on developments in the political consultation
process.

19. When the consultation process concerns a Country request, actual Release
Request (ROMEO) or preconditioned release request (SIERRA) messages, such a
process must culminate with the decision of the nuclear power(s) concerning the
release. The nuclear power(s) would convey its( their) decision to the Allied
governments, to the Council/DFC and the Major NATO Commanders by the most
expeditious means. The possible reception of Warning (WHISKEY) messages in
advance of the ROMEO or SIERRA messages would not affect this procedure.

20. Though it is not formally part of the nuclear consultation process, the
Major NATO Commander concerned would inform the Council/DFC and member
governments by a BLUE DOT ECHO message(3) when the released weapons had been
used and what results were achieved. The nuclear power concerned would notify
the Major NATO Commanders, the Council/DFC and capitals if for any reason it
decided that a release authorisation should be withdrawn or cancelled.
Similarly, if for any reason the Major NATO Commander concerned were unable to
use a weapon that had been released to him, he would so inform the Council/DFC
and capitals by a BLUE DOT SEVEN message(4).

(a) A jossuare of Information is oriented towards explaining the selective and
limited charicter of an actual or immediately forthcoming NATO use of nuclear
weapons and warning against the danger of further escalation. Such a message may
be sent before implementation of a decision to use nuclear weapons, or in
parallel with, or immediately after such implementation.

(b) A Message of Explicit Warning is oriented towards inducing an opponent to
take the action desired by the Alliance without NATO's initial or further use of
nuclear weapons. This message may be sent after consultation on a specific use
of nuclear weapons has started, but before a decision has been taken, or after a
decision has been taken but before the use itself has occurred.

22. The basic distinction between consultation and decision-making on message
texts and on finally sending a message should be observed. While a major
objective of every NATO message to an enemy would be the demonstration of
cohesion and solidarity, circumstances and timing considerations might not
always allow for a full consultation on the content of a message.

COUNCIL/DFC COMMUNICATION TO THE NUCLEAR POWER(3)
27. The views expressed, through their Representatives, by member governments
during the consultation concerning a specific request for use of nuclear weapons
would be communicated to the nuclear power(s) concerned by the Secretary General
(as Chairman of the Council/DFC) by the most expeditious means available. This
communication, which may consist of more than one message as consultation
proceeds, should reach the nuclear power(s) in time to enable it(then) to make a
decision within the time limits for effective use as set in the message of the
requesting country or MNC.

28. The Secretary General's message is not bound to any specific format. It may
refer to one or more requests for use and should identify the national views of
each country, either singly or by group of countries. In particular, the
national views of those countries which, as defined in the General
Guidelines(1), would be the most affected by the requested use, should be
clearly identified.
