> # Draft Report on the ACLANT Follow-on Use Study
> 
> This document is a draft report by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) regarding the tactical use of nuclear weapons in the Allied Command Atlantic (ACLANT) area. It identifies possible areas for Ministerial discussion based on the ACLANT Study, which was the last of eight regional studies in Phase I of the Follow-on Use Work Programme. The report discusses the analytical approach used in the study, noting that quantifiable factors often overshadow intangible political and psychological impacts. It emphasizes the importance of timely release of nuclear weapons in fast-moving maritime engagements, arguing that military commanders may need to initiate release requests to political authorities before specific tactical contact is made. The report also examines the SIERRA and ROMEO release procedures, specifically addressing whether a Major NATO Commander could grant authority to an executing commander at sea before tactical contact. The study was prepared by an ad hoc team including representatives from Canada, the Netherlands, Norway, the United Kingdom, and the United States, with assistance from the Supreme Allied Commander Atlantic (SACLANT) and the International Military Staff.

10. It should be noted parenthetically that political authorities always retain

Military Effectiveness

13. The Study concludes that there is, in general, a significant increase in the
task force's overall defensive capability if nuclear weapons are used against
enemy missiles, submarines and surface ships. There is also an economy in weapon
expenditure in some cases, which is particularly important at sea(1). On the
other hand, the Study underlines that nuclear weapons do not guarantee a
complete defence of the task force and, if the enemy successfully delivers
megaton weapons in reply, the task force will suffer severe damage and its
arrival at its destination as an effective force will be in doubt(2). Although
the Study does not address this point specifically, it appears quite probable
that, if NATO continues to rely only on conventional defence in the given
scenario, the enemy would be able to accomplish the same thing with conventional
attacks alone. Therefore, it is suggested that the possibility (not a certainty)
that the enemy could penetrate and override a combined nuclear-conventional NATO
defence should not in itself be the controlling factor in reaching a decision in
NATO whether or not to release nuclear weapons for follow-on use.

Collateral Effects
16. The Study concludes(1), and SHAPE concurs(2), that collateral effects should
not play a significant part in considering NATO's use of nuclear weapons in the
ACLANT area. Radioactive fallout seems likely to be the only hazard to civilian
populations in countries in the general area of the assumed operation, and this
hazard would be encountered only if the Warsaw Pact elected to attack the NATO
forces with megaton weapons burst on the surface(3). In this connection, it
should be observed that surface burst weapons are less effective than airburst
weapons in producing damage on ships(4). Consequently, the Warsaw Pact should
not be under any compulsion on grounds of military effectiveness to use surface
bursts. Parenthetically, it might be noted that current NATO military doctrine
seeks to ensure that NATO nuclear attacks against enemy submarines, aircraft and
ships would be carried out in ways that avoid collateral effects or, where they
are unavoidable, to hold them to a minimum.
