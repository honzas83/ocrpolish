> [!abstract] 
> # Communicating NATO's Intentions
> 
> The Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Staff Group outlines a plan to initiate a study on the implications and procedural matters associated with communicating NATO's intentions to an enemy, other countries, and the public. Following an unproductive canvass of NPG countries, the work will now proceed under the collective aegis of the NPG Permanent Representatives. The study is intended to cover both substantive questions, such as the factors political authorities must consider during a crisis when deciding whether, when, and to whom to communicate intentions, and procedural matters, including the arrangements for consultation within the Alliance. The document distinguishes between communicating intentions after a decision to employ nuclear weapons for tactical purposes and issuing an ultimatum. It also suggests that the study explore additional procedural arrangements to facilitate consultation and identify appropriate channels, spokesmen, and timing for communications. The Chairman emphasizes the the need to coordinate this work with other NATO activities, such as the Crisis Management Group (COEC), the Political Committee, and the NATO Press Service. The document references several previous guidelines and studies, including Provisional Political Guidelines and General Consultation Guidelines, as background material for the Staff Group members to review before their meeting on October 8, 1973.

COMMUNICATING NATO'S INTENTIONS
Note by the Chairman
Staff Group members will recall that the NPG Ministers at their last meeting
invited the Permanent Representatives to initiate a study on the implications of
communicating NATO's intentions to an enemy, to other countries, and to the
public and on the related procedural matters(1). As a first step toward carrying
out this task, a canvass of NPG countries was made to determine whether one or
more would wish to undertake the study. The results of this canvass were
unproductive and it appears that the work should therefore go forward under the
collective aegis of the NPG Permanent Representatives. Accordingly, it is
intended that the Staff Group make an initial exploration of this subject at its
next meeting on Monday, 8th October.

3. In addition to reviewing the background material, Staff Group members may
wish to prepare themselves to discuss the following points:

(a) General Thrust of the Study. The Ministerial request mentions implications
and related procedural matters. It would seem to follow from this that the study
should cover substantive questions as well as the mechanics of the consultation
process itself. With respect to substantive questions, the study should perhaps
at a minimum examine the various factors that political authorities would have
to consider during an actual crisis in dealing with the question of
communicating NATO's intentions to an enemy, to other countries and to the
public. Concerning procedural matters, the study should probably explore
arrangements for consulting within the Alliance during a crisis on the question
of communicating, as well as the procedures that might be employed for carrying
out the communications if the Alliance should decide to do so. These two
possible study elements are discussed in further detail below.

(b) Substantive Questions. Using approved DPC guidance (see paragraphs 2 (a) and
(b) above) as a point of departure, the study could attempt to identify the
various factors that political authorities might want to take into account in
deciding at time of crisis whether, when and to whom NATO's intentions should be
communicated. The study could also discuss, as appropriate, the possible
favourable or adverse implications of the various courses of action open to NATO
from different points of view (military, political, psychological, moral, legal,
etc.). In this context, the study may wish to make a distinction between
communicating NATO's intention after a decision has already been made to employ
nuclear weapons for tactical purposes (for example, to inform others of the
specific objectives of our use and to reassure them that it is a selective
application of nuclear force with carefully controlled effects) and an ultimatum
(a communication of a threat by NATO to use nuclear weapons if the enemy fails
to conform to a NATO demand).

(c) Procedural Aspects. In the context of existing consultation procedures, the
study could explore what additional specific procedural arrangements, if any,
could be introduced to facilitate consultation among Alliance members in time of
crisis on the question of communicating NATO's intentions. At a minimum, the
existing guidance on consultation procedures might be amplified to ensure that
this aspect of consultation is clearly recognised. The study could also discuss
procedures that might have to be brought into play if the Alliance decides to
communicate its intentions, covering such aspects as the channels that might be
open to NATO, the spokesmen or agencies that might act on NATO's behalf for
making the communications, the timing of multiple communications, and
coordination with related activities (e.g., release decisions).

(d) Study Results. The question of whether the study should reach any
conclusions on substantive matters, or whether any policy recommendations should
be made, probably cannot be answered at the moment, but they should be kept in
mind as the work progresses. With regard to procedures, however, the study could
include proposals for clarifying or strengthening the existing guidance.

(e) Relationship with Other Activities. The question of communicating NATO's
intentions in time of crisis concerning use of nuclear weapons is undoubtedly of
interest to other activities within NATO, some of whom may already be involved
in working out arrangements concerning communication of NATO views and positions
on other matters during an actual crisis. It would seem to be both useful and
necessary to ensure that the Staff Group's work on this study is coordinated
with the related work of other activities, such as COEC, the Political
Committee, NATO Press Service, etc.

(Signed) H.B. SEIM.
