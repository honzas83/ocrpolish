> # Operational Concepts and Doctrine Currently Governing NATO Employment of Nuclear Weapons for Tactical Purposes
> 
> This draft report by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) provides a detailed review of operational concepts and doctrine governing the tactical employment of nuclear weapons within the North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO). The document discusses the reports from the Supreme Headquarters Allied Command Europe (SHAPE) and the Military Committee, discussing how these concepts are evolved and dynamic to remain responsive to political and military developments. It emphasizes that these doctrines are intended to provide direction for planning without restricting the flexibility of subordinate commanders. The report further examines the specific military considerations, such as the speed and distance capabilities of delivery methods, and the targeting of fixed targets. It also addresses the authority to request nuclear weapon release, noting that while the Supreme Allied Commander Europe (SACEUR) takes governmental views into account, governments maintain the option to make requests directly to the Council or the Defence Planning Committee (DPC). Additionally, the report mentions the the use of Atomic Demolition Munitions (ADMs) and the importance of updating concepts to account for changes in the threat or NATO's posture. The overall goal is to assist Ministers in investigating unresolved problems related to tactical nuclear defence.

III. DISCUSSION
General Comment

3. The SHAPE Report provides information on the operational concepts and
doctrine which govern current planning for tactical employment of nuclear
weapons within Allied Command Europe (ACE), and which would, in time of war,
apply to ACE operations as well. The Report defines such terms as operational
concepts, operational doctrine, tactical purposes and tactical use; identifies
the broad political guidance on which the ACE operational concepts and doctrine
are based; outlines the general and specific military considerations that are
relevant to the development of such concepts and doctrine; and describes these
operational concepts and doctrine in considerable detail. In the Report, SHAPE
emphasises(3) the dynamic nature of the concepts and doctrine, pointing out that
they have evolved over a period of years and that they are kept under continuing
review in order to be responsive to political and military developments.

Specific Comments
7.   Nuclear Weapon Characteristics.   With regard to general military
considerations that are relevant to the development of operational concepts and
doctrine, the SHAPE Report lists(1) a number of ways in which nuclear weapons
differ significantly from previous munitions. One of the differences mentioned
is the speed and distance capabilities of delivery methods. This could be
construed to mean that nuclear weapons are delivered more rapidly to greater
ranges than conventional munitions or, alternatively, that improvements in
delivery methods have been confined to nuclear weapons alone. There are, of
course, some systems exclusively dedicated to the delivery of nuclear weapons,
such as PERSHING or POSEIDON, that have speed and distance capabilities far
greater than delivery methods used before nuclear weapons were available. On the
other hand, many of

8. Authority to request nuclear weapon release. The SHAPE Report states(1) that
SACEUR's decision to request nuclear weapon release could be based, among other
things, on requests of governments. It is, of course, clear that governments
always have full freedom to suggest to SACEUR that he make a nuclear weapon
release request. It is equally clear that SACEUR would not only have the right
to take such governmental views into account in reaching his decision but would
be expected to do so. On the other hand, mention of 'this possibility' in the
SHAPE Report should not be viewed as any infringement on the option of a
government to make a nuclear weapon release request direct to the Council/DPC,
as provided for in the General Guidelines for Consultation Procedure on the Use
of Nuclear Weapons(2).

9. Targeting. Reference is made(1) in the SHAPE Report to the broad area in
which fixed targets have been identified and to the possibility that some of
these fixed targets might be struck with nuclear weapons for tactical purposes.
In the light of existing guidance pertaining to tactical employment of nuclear
weapons, it is evident that not all fixed targets within the broad geographical
area mentioned in the SHAPE Report would necessarily be suitable targets.

10. Atomic Demolition Munitions. Although atomic demolition munitions (ADMs) are
mentioned(2) in an indirect way in the SHAPE Report, they are not covered(3)
specifically in the examination of the tactical employment of nuclear weapons in
the land battle. This is understandable, since ADMs are nuclear devices normally
used only for demolition purposes and are therefore quite different from nuclear
weapons that are used to attack enemy forces on the battlefield. We think this
distinction between ADMs and battlefield nuclear weapons is a valid one, and
note that it is reflected in the separate issuance of Special Political
Guidelines for the Possible Use of Atomic Demolition Munitions(4). On the other
hand, we believe that the operational concepts and doctrine outlined in the
