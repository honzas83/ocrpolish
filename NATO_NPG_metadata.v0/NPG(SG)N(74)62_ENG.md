> # U.S. Statement on Nuclear Weapon Development
> 
> This document consists of a { "//": "Note by the Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) accompanying an extract from a statement made by Ambassador Joseph Martin, the U.S. Delegate to the Conference of the Committee on Disarmament (CCD) in Geneva on May 23, 1974. The statement addresses concerns raised by Swedish delegates, Mrs. Myrdal and Mrs. Thorsson, regarding newspaper reports of the development of 'mini-nukes' or low-yield tactical nuclear weapons. Ambassador Martin clarifies that the term 'mini-nukes' is misleading and that the United States has not achieved a qualitative breakthrough in tactical nuclear weapons, but has instead been engaged in a gradual process of moderately upgrading its stockpile. He emphasizes that the United States Government has no intention of treating tactical nuclear systems as interchangeable with conventional arms, thereby preserving the 'firebreak' between nuclear and non-nuclear weapons. Furthermore, he states that the United States remains open to a Comprehensive Test Ban (CTB) provided other nations abide by the same restrictions. Finally, he confirms that agreements between the United States and the Soviet Union on the prevention of nuclear war, as well as Security Council Resolution 255 (1968), apply to the use of tactical nuclear weapons.

EXTRACT FROM A STATEMENT BY THE US DELEGATE TO
THE CONFERENCE OF THE COMMITTEE ON DISARMAMENT (CCD),
GENEVA, 23rd MAY, 1974

I wish to turn now to a subject raised last year by the former leader of the
Swedish Delegation, Mrs. Myrdal, and brought up again by Mrs. Myrdal's
distinguished successor, Mrs. Thorsson, at our 63rd Plenary Meeting. In her
statement last August 9, the Swedish Representative made reference to newspaper
accounts of the development of a new generation of tactical nuclear weapons, so-
called "mini-nukes", which, in her words, "would blur the present distinction
between conventional and nuclear weapons." Such a development, she said, would
drastically aggravate the nuclear threat against non-nuclear states and "affect
the very premises on which adherence to the NPT is raised." I should like to
explain why such fears are wholly unwarranted.

The term "mini-nukes" is misleading in two important respects. First, the
coinage of this new catchword in itself conveys the false impression that we are
talking about a radically new and futuristic family of weapons. Second, the
diminutive element of the term "mini-nuke" falsely suggests some miniature
nuclear device which can be handled and used in the same manner as conventional
weapons. I would like to correct both of these misimpressions.

We are not now, nor have we been in recent years, at the brink of some
qualitative breakthrough in tactical nuclear weapons development. Instead, as
Secretary of Defense Schlesinger has stated, we have been engaged over many
years in a gradual process of moderately upgrading our tactical nuclear
stockpile. There is nothing new about the existence of tactical nuclear weapons
of very low explosive yields. Indeed, it is public knowledge that nuclear
weapons with explosive yields measured in the sub-kiloton range were introduced
in the U.S. inventory many years ago. No decisions have been made to produce or
deploy any new systems.

However, the main issue raised by the Swedish Representative is not whether or
when any particular improvements in the tactical nuclear stockpile will be made,
but rather how any such improvements would affect our objective of preventing
the outbreak of warfare, particularly nuclear war. In deciding questions of
possible modifications in the U.S. tactical nuclear weapons stockpile, we are
governed by the objective of reducing the risk of nuclear war. That is to say,
any improvements in our tactical nuclear weapons stockpile must make deterrence
more effective and thereby reduce the likelihood of nuclear warfare of any kind.

In response to speculation that further development of low-yield tactical
nuclear weapons would blur the present distinction between conventional and
nuclear weapons, I wish to state categorically that the U.S. Government has no
intention whatever to treat such tactical systems as interchangeable with
conventional arms. We fully appreciate that the distinction, or "firebreak,"
between nuclear and non-nuclear arms is a major factor in preventing nuclear
warfare, and we will not act to erode this distinction. The very special
command-and-control and safety arrangements that apply to nuclear weapons in
general have of course always applied to small-yield nuclear weapons as well.

I think that, in making these comments, I have answered two of the questions
posed last August by the former leader of the Swedish Delegation, specifically
her questions dealing with the development and deployment of sub-kiloton nuclear
weapons. In regard to her question whether development of such tactical weapons
would be an obstacle to a CTB /'Comprehensive Test Ban', members of the
Committee will recall that, in response to a similar question by Sweden in 1972,
I stated that the U.S. stands ready to give up the advantages derived from
nuclear weapon testing if we can be assured that others are abiding by the same
restrictions (CCD/PV. 580). That answer applies to the testing of any nuclear
weapons, including tactical ones.

Finally, Mrs. Myrdal asked last August whether the agreement between the U.S.
and U.S.S.R. on the prevention of nuclear war would apply to wars in which only
"mini-nukes" were used, and also whether the U.S. interprets Security Council
Resolution 255 (1968), concerning security assurances, as applying to nuclear
aggression in which only "mini-nukes" were used. I have explained that my
Government will continue to treat all tactical nuclear weapons in the same
manner. Therefore, I can answer both of these questions with an unequivocal
"yes".
