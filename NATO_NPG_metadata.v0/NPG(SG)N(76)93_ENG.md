> # Soviet Nuclear Doctrine
> 
> This document provides a NATO intelligence assessment of the Soviet Union's nuclear strategy, detailing their approach to both strategic and theatre nuclear warfare. The International Military Staff (IMS) prepared the statement based on agreed NATO intelligence to inform the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Staff Group and their Permanent Representatives. The assessment concludes that while the Soviet Union no longer views a general war as nuclear from the outset, they assume nuclear weapons could be used at any time during a conflict. Their theatre nuclear strategy involves an initial mass strike on all fronts, rejecting a strategy of graduated response. Regarding strategic nuclear war, the Strategic Rocket Troops (SRT) are the primary offensive threat, targeting government control centers, military, and industrial targets in North America and NATO Europe. The doctrine also emphasizes pre-emption in the event they believe a nuclear attack is imminent. Theatre nuclear war involves mass strikes using air forces and Short-Range Ballistic Missiles (SSM) to create corridors through NATO's defenses for attacking divisions. The document further notes that the Soviet Union believes conflict in a general war would continue even after a strategic nuclear exchange.

SOVIET NUCLEAR DOCTRINE
(prepared from NATO agreed intelligence by the Intelligence Division, IMS)

**GENERAL**
In the early 1960s the Soviet Union evidently felt that any general war with the
West would be nuclear from the outset. For some years now, the possibility of
conventional operations in the early stages of war is also envisaged; however
all Soviet doctrine is based on the assumption that nuclear war could occur at
any time during a conflict, but is perhaps less likely at the beginning of it.
In such a case, when nuclear weapons are employed, the Soviet concept seems
generally to call for an initial mass theatre strike, simultaneously, if
possible, on all Fronts of the Theatre. The doctrine apparently does not
subscribe to a strategy of graduated response; however, in recent years,
military strategists have shown interest in flexible employment of nuclear
weapons for theatre operations. In response to a NATO first use of nuclear
weapons, the risk of an abrupt massive escalation would be great; though it is
believed that the Soviets would at least initially limit their response to the
theatre concerned.

The Soviets have apparently concluded that a "bolt out of the blue"
intercontinental surprise attack is not a presently workable option. However,
their doctrine emphasises the desirability of pre-emption; and, if they were to
conclude that an enemy was about to deliver either a strategic or a theatre
nuclear attack on them, they would be likely to pre-empt massively in kind.

**STRATEGIC NUCLEAR WAR**
The missiles of the Strategic Rocket Troops (SRT) (ICBMs, IRBMs and MRBMs) are
the major element of the strategic offensive threat. Their mission is believed
to be to destroy government control centres, and military, urban and industrial
targets. They are supplemented by a large and growing SLBM force, and the Long
Range Aviation (DA) bomber force. Most of the ICBM and SLBM force would probably
be targeted against North America; while the IRBM and MRBM force and some of the
older SLBMs would conduct a massive nuclear attack throughout NATO Europe. These
initial ballistic missile strikes would be followed by DA, Frontal Aviation (FA)
and, if available, Naval Aviation (AVMF). At sea, NATO carrier strike forces
would be prime targets for submarines, AVMF aircraft and some DA bombers.

Soviet doctrine envisages that conflict in a general war would continue beyond
the strategic nuclear exchange.

**THEATRE NUCLEAR WAR**
Soviet general purpose forces are equipped to engage in a whole spectrum of
theatre nuclear options. As already noted, the concept seems to be for a mass
theatre strike. The number of weapons employed by one front in such a strike can
run into several hundreds, of which over half might be delivered by air forces
and the remainder by Front, Army and Divisional SSМ acting to a co-ordinated
plan. Targets of high priority will be NATO nuclear facilities and weapons
systems, including airfields, command and control, major air defence systems and
reserve formations likely to influence the battle; but probably as many as half
of the strikes would be devoted to creating corridors through NATO's main
defences, along which the attacking divisions will travel. Subsequent to the
initial nuclear strike, divisions and armies may be free to employ a limited
number of nuclear weapons in support of local operations; but the launching of
Front second echelon armies may be the occasion for a second Front mass strike.
