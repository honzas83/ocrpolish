> # Articles on Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons
> 
> On December 21, 1971, the Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) issued a notice distributing two articles for the interest of Staff Group members. The first article, titled "Alliance, Parity and Nuclear Defense" by Marc Geneste, argues that Western defense strategy for the 1970s must be based on a clear vision of strategy and unity of strategic doctrine. Geneste emphasizes that the Communist threat, specifically the military capabilities of the Soviet Union and the revolutionary militancy of China, remains the primary driver of Western solidarity. He discusses the concept of 'parity' and 'sufficiency' in the context of Strategic Arms Limitation Talks (SALT) and argues that a quantitative stalemate in strategic nuclear stockpiles would neutralize strategic deterrents. The author posits that a credible nuclear alliance, combined with a counterforce strategy, would limit the Soviet Union's ability to blackmail or coerce Europe. The document also touches upon the division of labor within the alliance, specifically regarding the maintenance of parity in the Intercontinental Ballistic Missile (ICBM) field by the United States. The second article mentioned in the notice is "NATO in Theory and Practice" by J.T. Trotman, although the full text of that article is not provided in the first part of the document.

ALLIANCE, PARITY
AND NUCLEAR DEFENSE
by Marc Geneste

1. GENERAL

Any change in the Western defense system for the 70's
must stem on a clear vision of strategy. The general concept
should be sufficiently simple to be easily understood and
accepted by all the members of the Western Alliance. Unity
of strategic Doctrine is the absolute prerequisite for any
"division of labor" concept, of any improvement of our present
system : the newly contemplated construction must have a
backbone. Since WWII and for the foreseeable future, the
Communist threat to our social system has been and will remain
the principal, if not the only cement of Western solidarity,
because the western societies are torn apart by the sharp
economic competitions inherent to the capitalist world.
The "Political integration, which is so frequently quoted as the
"natural wave of the future," might not be so natural after
all, under present conditions of economic warfare. Individual
GNPs cannot at the same time easily face the exhausting
American (and other) challenge and the Russian military threat.
Incidentally, this might be the reason why the Soviet, who
dread political integration in the West, are so much interested
in a detente....

Pending some necessary armistice among allies in their
internal economic squabbles, the formidable and growing military
might of the Soviet Union, coupled with (and excited by) the
Chinese revolutionary militancy are still there to counter the
many centrifuge trends in the West.

Although opinions differ as to the exact nature of the
Communist threat in the 70's - some argue that the Soviet
empire is on the brink of disaster from internal pressures,
that the only long range of the Kremlin bosses is to stay in
power, that China is Russia's main headache, that the Soviet
are seriously interested in Disarmament, etc. - one bare
fact of life remains undisputed : the fantastic Communist
military capabilities are here to stay as the common problem
of our western societies, whatever might appear to be the
Communist intentions. Defense postures, today like
yesterday, must stem on physical capabilities rather than
on hypothetical "intentions" which are subject to change,
especially in a time of internal political instability.

At least, this is the area where a common agreement does exist in the West.
Let's then try to build, from this firm basis, the common solution to a common
problem, which must be, to begin with, a common strategic approach for our
common Defense against a common military threat. This must be the backbone of
any further construction.

2. ALLIANCE, COUNTERFORCE AND PARITY

First of all, some equivocal attitudes about the meaning of "Alliance" should be
dissipated. The oldest, and simplest definition of that term is by far the best
: alliance means that any aggression of any kind against any ally will be
regarded and treated as being an individual attack against all.

If we transfer this age old basic truth in the modern military equation, we
might draw immediately interesting conclusions, due to the fact that modern
weapons are built more for deterrence, or for political pressures than for
practical use; for psychological rather than physical action : this is the first
cardinal difference between the conventional and nuclear military eras. (The
other main differences are the enormity of destructions and the limitation of
available stockpiles of ammunitions that we shall discuss below.)

For instance, everyone agrees today that the long range arsenals of ICBMs,
SLBMS, MRBMS and the like, piling up rockets and megatons year after year, are
not likely to be used in a massive and surprise attack, in an offensive manner :
this would be sheer insanity, i.e. suicide in view of automatic retaliation. It
does not serve any military purpose, the object of war being control rather than
blind and useless destruction. Their main purpose is to prevent the opponent
from using them, either physically, which would be absurd, or psychologically in
coercive manner, which has been experienced in Suez. Their political value is
rather defensive in nature, as long as no clear cut advantage in numbers or
quantity appears to shift decisively the strategic balance. Furthermore, since
their physical use is conceivable only at the end of an escalating process – and
not at the beginning of a war – their awesome threat is extremely important to
prevent escalation. Their enormous weight tends to crush the development of
local crisis rather than to extend the blazes. Viet Nam, Suez, Cuba and the
Middle East are a vivid illustration of this principle. This is why the
maintenance of overall Strategic Balance is so important for Peace in this
world, and why the idea of parity – or "sufficiency," (which is the subjective
appreciation of parity) has progressively crept up to the international
headlines and opened the way to SALT type negotiations.

To be sure, on a purely operational ground, "parity" is almost impossible to
demonstrate. Even if all the variables could be taken into account, such as
civil defenses, vulnerabilities, morale of populations, reliability and accuracy
of weapons systems, etc. which cannot be exactly proved and safely controlled,
there would still remain this part of the military Act which permitted Napoleon
and the other great Captains to win wars with quantitatively inferior forces.
"Parity" of hardware does not mean military equality; like in the computer
business, software is extremely important. But it is not measurable in
peacetime, and we must satisfy ourselves with what we have : the capability to
evaluate, approximately, the reciprocal weight of military hardware, through
satellite and other modern intelligence means. However insufficient, this
"parity" concept is better than nothing, if it permits to freeze the reciprocal
deployment of weapons systems at a certain level, and stops the current Arms
race in the Strategic field, to begin with.

Psychologically, such a quantitative stalemate would amount to a complete
neutralization of Strategic Deterrents, not only between the two superpowers,
but – and this is the main point – between any superpower and its opponent
allies.

Under a controlled and reliable system of "parity" between the USSR and the US,
coupled with a counterforce strategy and an Alliance reaffirmation on very
simple terms as pointed out above, it is easy to see that the Soviet long range
bombardment apparatus could not be used in any coercive manner against European
allies : this is due to the fundamental difference between conventional and
nuclear "combat power," on the one hand, and to the recent developments which
have led to the concept of counterforce strategy, on the other hand.
Conventional Combat power has the capability of several successive uses : the
same Divisions can make several campaigns. Nuclear weapons, improperly labeled
"combat power" since they are firepower only, are lost as soon as committed.

Under the quantitative freezing of strategic Nuclear stockpiles, the USSR
obviously cannot threaten successfully or separately Europe and the US with the
full weight of their nuclear might; they cannot blackmail and coerce Europe with
their vastly superior stockpiles (assuming that their rocketry is technically
capable of targeting both sides of the Atlantic) provided the credibility of the
nuclear Alliance is unquestionable in European minds.

Moreover, the Counterforce Strategy in the field of long range Bombardment, that
the development of target acquisition devices, abus, MIRVs etc., has rendered
possible in recent years, the comeback of Defense in the strategic equation,
should help alleviating European fears about their local inferiority in front of
the overall Soviet strategic might.

To take a very simplistic example, let's assume that the strategic stockpiles of
the two Superpowers are frozen at, say, 2000 warheads each. Would the Soviets
expand, or threaten to expand 1,000 warheads against Europe, face the
destruction of the remaining 1,000 from US Counterforce strikes, and stay naked
in front of the 1,000 remaining US warheads? What would be the credibility of
such treats, if the Alliance is credible? Of course, things are more complex,
but on the overall, such a gamble from the Soviet side would be so risky that,
under Alliance and "Parity" their blackmailing power is limited to that part of
their arsenal which is not pinned down by its US counterpart. Finally, under
Nuclear Alliance, SALT type parity, and Counterforce the only real problem for
Europe in its local Strategic Bombardment field seems to boil down to the MRBM
and short range bombers deployment.

In this field, one can see the possible "division of labor" in the new alliance
and roughly speaking :

– parity in the ICBM field to be negotiated and maintained by the US;
– parity in the medium range field – which appears tactical for the US, but is
strategic for Europe, to be achieved (in the long run), and negotiated by the
European.

Economically and technically, the combined strength of European nations should
be more than enough to cope with their part of the Soviet strategic threat.

250 million European plagued with the economic challenge of 450 million
capitalists (American plus their fellow European) and the military threat of 200
million Russians cannot finance by themselves an "all azimuths" multi capable
Defense as easily as Sen. Mansfield appears to think (whatever might do the 700
million Chinese, not so friendly to Europe after all). But in the military field
alone, where the Soviet totalitarian system is capable to invest in military
hardware much more than we do, they certainly could participate a fair share in
the overall needs of a common Western strategy, if there was one! In the
strategic field, the 700 (outmoded?) Soviet rockets which are the European share
of the overall Soviet threat do not appear to be an impossible challenge for the
European GNPs and technology.

3. THE "TRIGGER" STORY

How about the highly controversial trigger problem? Should it continue to
confuse the whole issue? To day, three Western Allies have got the trigger : the
US, Britain and France, instead of one at the beginning of NATO. It is often
argued that no "individual" triggers should exist, and all the US policy during
the last decades stuck to the principle that the trigger should remain unique
and – naturally – in US hands, and even on US shores.

As long as the monopoly of the Promethean fire was American, there was no
practical problem, although the stubborn keeping of Washington of the tactical
"key" finally casted logical doubts on the extension to Europe of the US made
umbrella. Then, when France and Britain climbed on the nuclear chessboard, in
spite of the MacMahon barrage, outcries were heard : a general nuclear war could
be started, and the US destroyed, because some irresponsible Ally was now able
to open the Pandora's box. The nuclear allies were sometimes considered like
children toying with matches in a dump of high explosives. To day, because of
the trigger problem, it is said that no European Nuclear Defense is thinkable
before total European political integration. All this fuss is kid stuff. This is
not serious.

For who would seriously believe that France or Britain are so crazy as to drop a
bomb for fun on Warsaw, Prague or Moscow? Would they, that lightly, start the
stupid and suicidal game of strategic bombardment?

As we have seen above, strategic bombardment might occur only at the end of an
escalation process; that clearly means that the Pandora box will have been
opened at a tactical level – which we shall discuss below – the two European
strategic triggers are here to deter the Russian from using physically or
psychologically their anti-European strategic system, and they add their weight
to deter escalation of local European crisis. As seen from Moscow, under
Alliance and parity, don't they add considerable credibility to the faraway
Washington trigger for the local Nuclear Defense of Europe? Does not this gain
in credibility, for Deterrence or anti-blackmail purposes, more than compensate
for the theoretical risk of irresponsible French or British action?

The same holds true for the two European triggers. After all there is one on the
Continent, and one across the Channel. Although the British are striving to join
the Common Market, the Channel might remain in the back of their

4. TACTICAL ALLIANCE?

Assuming the final success of SALT, the general freeze
and correlative neutralization of strategic arsenals, Soviet,
American and European would focus the needs of Western Defense,
down to the tactical level. The reciprocal acceptance of
"parity" at the upper level of the scale of violence amounts,
practically, to the tacit admission that thermonuclear warfare
cannot serve any political purpose : a truth that sheer common
sense has discovered long ago. The problem of peacemaking
through Deterrence (pending Disarmament) becomes the physical
capability to block any military action which, unlike absurd
and blind strategic bombing, might still harvest political dividends.
Deterrence, which is, in the strategic field, the daughter of retaliation
with firepower only, becomes on the battlefield a product of Defensive
potential which integrates the two traditional variables of combat : movement
and firepower. The puzzle is much more complex : the problem of "parity",
for instance, already difficult to solve in the strategic field, becomes almost
impossible. Any evaluation of military "parity" in terms of hardware (Divisions,
firepower etc.) would certainly be false and dangerous. Even if numerical
parity was possible and controllable, there would still remain the other
variable

that only actual battle can evaluate : military tactics, which recently
permitted

Von Manstein, Guderian and co to win a spectacular land battle under an
apparent "parity" in the number of tanks. To say the least, Deterrence
in this field becomes the problem of military professionalism, much more than
in the nuclear Strategic area, where no wartime experience, and the likeliness
of

psychological use only, has silenced the military experts and left the game
mainly to civilian amateur's imagination.

Short of a satisfactory vision of "parity" in the tactical field (which is the
field of true "counterforce").

How then could we find that sense of security without which no confidence is
possible to continue the Arms Control process, already begun with SALT in the
strategic zone, Detente, Ostpolitik, Disarmament and the like? Is there no hope
to solve the problem, for instance, of Mutual Balanced Forces Reductions, in
view of the dissimetry between the USSR mobilization system, and the West's
inability to bring up manpower in due time? Does not the newcomer in the modern
military equations, the Atom, bring into the tactical picture the same
inhibition to attack it has already brought in the Strategic field, which has
led the two nuclear giants, the US and USSR, to give up, admit parity and
stalemate, open SALT, and relegate strategic arsenals in a purely negative role?

Of course, the Atom does bring a revolution of the same magnitude. Would at long
last the politician accept to hear the advice of the modest and silent
professionnels of military tactics?

Let's assume, once more, that the absurdity of Strategic Nuclear Bombardement
has led to the neutralization through "parity" of strategic arsenals, and the
abandonment of this scale of violence as an instrument of policy. Let's the
priest, pacifists and even "Hippies" continue their commendable fight against
violence in general (which will remain a fight against human nature, for a long
time to come). Pending their final victory, which is not yet in sight, the
problem of peace or "deterrence" is the control of escalation of violence. It is
a "crisis management" affair. Somewhere along the escalating line, which leads
from local squabbles (say between Arabs and Jews, or among Czechs) to
thermomuclear exchanges, a firebreak must be found.

The tactical Atom provides the obvious technical answer to build this necessary
firebreak.

Tactical nuclear warfare is a deterrent to tactical war as powerful as Strategic
Nuclear Warfare has been, and will be for Douhet-type Strategic war.

The reason is finally simple : everyone knows that the two components of land
warfare, firepower and movement, complement each other; their interplay is
maneuver, that is to say military tactics. When firepower takes an advantage
over movement, defensive action has precedence over offense. This was the case
in WWI, when the movement of foot soldiers and cavalry was stopped by the
machine gun and Artillery barrages. The German Army had its back broken in front
of the impassable Verdun trenches.

Then the tanks and airplanes of WWII gave the advantage to modern movement
versus a firepower which had not changed significantly since WWI. The result was
the German "Blitzkrieg."

To day, the means of movement (tanks and airplanes) have not changed
significantly since WWI, whereas firepower, thanks to the Atom, has been
multiplied tenthousandfold. The result is that Defense (tactical nuclear
Defense) has got such a crushing advantage, for a long time to come, over land
attack that any ground offensive seems to be outdated and hopeless, like would
be a cavalry charge against machine guns.

In short, the tactical Atom can render military ground offensive on the
battlefield, as absurd physically and meaningless politically as the Strategic
Atom did with Douhet-type strategic strikes.

Now, it is up to the politician to draw the conclusions of the revolution
brought by the technicians, and up to the tacticians to refine their doctrines
to adapt them to this revolution. Assuredly, Wilhelm II would not have started
WWI if his military experts had anticipated Verdun, and later the French would
have had invested in Armored Divisions rather than in the Maginot Line, if the
changing precedence of the two components of maneuver had been clearly
established before the actual 1940 try. Such a foresight was difficult before
WWI and WWII. To day, the problem is crystal clear, and however, the political
and doctrinal conclusions have not been drawn. The reason is that some American
"fire-breakers" (mainly politicians or young self-appointed "strategists") have
consistently outlawed the use of Tactical Atom, as being the certain detonator
of General Nuclear War. In other words, any use of Atomic Demolition Munitions
or artillery shell would immediately and automatically start ICBS and blow up
the planet. With such a philosophy, the fate of New York could not be left in
the hands of an Army major with his "Davy Crockett" and not even in the hands of
Gen Eisenhower, Norstad or other C in C in Europe. Needless to say, control of
tactical nuclear weapons could even less be given to such crackpots as the
French, the German, or even the British....

This mistake approach has led to the present political confusion in the Alliance
and to the worst doctrinal mess ever observed in military history : the NATO
armies had the manpower calculated to fight only a nuclear war against the
Soviet mobilization potential. But, they were required to start the war
conventionally, then, "if things went wrong," to change horses in the middle of
the stream. Their tactics had to be dual capable, that is to say ; gunpowder or
bows and arrows. They could not be so sure after all to use, in

Practically, this view of the overall Alliance strategy, based on the virtue of

Europe to take care of this part of the Soviet arsenal targeted against Europe.

The trigger problem is not important. With some US technological help, this
might be achieved by the two European allies which are not tied by the NPT for
nuclear warheads and possible participation of other Allies for the rest of the
strategic hardware. For the Allies who have not the strategic trigger and
individual retaliatory capabilities (assuming they still feel in the back of
their minds, the need for additional national assurance against the very remote
strategic threat) some BMD deployment might be offered : beside the physical
Defense provided (which is not worse than "Defense through Retaliation"), this
would translate the unequivocal US nuclear commitment to the Defense of Europe,
because ABM warheads must react before getting the political "green light" from
Washington.

*

Then, there is still the tactical problem, where the trigger affair is
important, as well as "integration". If, rejecting once and for all the
firebreaker's taboos, the Allies admit that the only Defense feasible in front
of the Soviet mobilization potential is nuclear firepower, they must consider
the Atom as an integral part of their land combat power, cease the artificial
distinction (purely technical) between "conventional warfare" and "nuclear
warfare" and satisfy themselves with definition of "strategic warfare" and
"tactical warfare." After all, one TNT bomb dropped on Moscow is a strategic
weapon, one megaton bomb on the Iron Curtain is a tactical weapon. There are no
strategic or tactical weapon, there are only strategic or tactical targets.
Given a modern combat power debarrassed from old taboos, the military should be
authorized to use their tool without past inhibitions : this means, absolutely,
that the tactical trigger is in the hands of the Alliance Commander in chief at
least for the defensive use of its nuclear firepower.

Here again, the formidable defensive value of the Atom does permit a distinction
which was unthinkable at a time when firepower had not such a crushing advantage
over ground movement (over offense). It is to day conceivable to crush surface
movement and offense without bombing the distant rears, to limit the use of
firepower on a band of terrain within one's own territory. The geographical
escalation process can be left to the aggressor, without ending the initial
capacity to defend. Practically, a successful ground attack would depend on the
total success of a first NATO UNCLASSIFIED - 10 -

Technician and tactician should now concentrate on this problem. The answer does
exist, but, naturally, this is a domain where "integration" of doctrine is
absolutely mandatory for all the Allies : the tactical game is very complex and
the rules must be commonly defined and accepted by all the prospective
participants. Tactical Nuclear Doctrine is, in fact, the only domain which
requires the total "integration" of Allied land forces in peacetime : national
Armies can join the Allied Command in time of crisis, as they did in recent
wars; the "integration" of command in peacetime is not absolutely vital : the
only thing they just can't do is to play a different game. They have to learn it
long before, and it has to be "common game."

Pending political developments which might, some day, lead to political and
military "integrations" in the Western World, unity of military doctrine is a
must of our present time. This is both feasible and necessary. It would be
absurd, it might be criminal to subordinate unity of military doctrine to the
acceptance of political Unity of Command in peacetime. Tactics and politics
should not be confounded.

To be more specific, assuming a common nuclear tactical doctrine, how then could
we imagine a "division of labor" between the Allies? The tactical key must be in
the hands of the C in C of the Alliance, as we have seen above. But, to remain
realistic, due to the considerable emotion likely to be raised by such a move,
especially in US public opinion fashioned by 20 years of "firebreaker's"
philosophy, all the appearances of "civilian control" on the trigger have to be
clearly maintained. In other words, a set of conditions for nuclear engagement
should be established : for instance, no first use; in other border; and other
conditions for progressive release; in other words, a "predelegation system"
which would satisfy the politician without hampering the military from doing his
job. The only vital point is to assure the military that he can count on his
weapons, for his defensive plans : (the civilian interdiction to waste a nuclear
weapon on a lost patrol should not bother him too much...). The common tactical
doctrine implies a common agreement among Allies on predelegation to the C in C.
In other words, the strategic triggers remain in national hands, and the
tactical trigger is predelegated to the common soldier for the common cause,
with a set of rules established by the politician to control escalation.

With a clear cut tactical doctrine and a clear cut solution to the trigger
problem, the main past roadblocks to Allied "divisions of labor" would be
removed, in Europe at least. The repartition of wartime missions, the stationing
and the number of troops, even the financial sharing, compared to the political
puzzles involved in the trigger affair, appear to be technicalities, although
one can expect a good deal of discussion on administrative details. If the
Allies were to agree on a common Nuclear Defense Strategy and Nuclear Defense
tactics, a new Alliance could arise out of the past troubles of NATO, and solve
the ominous problem of the Soviet military threat on our future.

5. NUCLEAR DEFENSE AND ARMS CONTROL
Assuming that a new Alliance system based on a common Nuclear Strategy would
block all the avenues of war approach, the predominance of Defense (through
Retaliation in the Strategic field, and through the impossibility of Offense on
the battle-field) might give at the present time an opportunity to go ahead in
Arms Control and possibly Disarmament. This opportunity might not last for ever,
because Offense some day (not yet in sight) could regain its old military value
and political

meaning, following technical improvements in the means of movement (flying
tanks, etc.) currently trailing far behind firepower.

To day, and for a long time to come, Nuclear Defense provides the necessary
military and psychological shield behind which such risky political undertakings
as SALT, Detente, Ostpolitik, MBR can be safely pursued.

Due to the fundamental difference between conventional power, which is necessary
for Offense, and nuclear firepower, which by itself alone cannot assure
"control" but only destruction, that is to say tactical Defense, it seems now
possible to reduce the number of Divisions without diminishing the Defensive
potential. That way, Mutual Balanced Reductions of Offensive Forces (Divisions)
could be achieved while the tactical Defensive power is increased. The Magnot
and Siegfried lines were no threat in themselves : the threat was the Panzer-
divisionen.

To day, one can imagine some kinds of Maginot and Siegfried highly effective
"nuclear lines" to cover the reciprocal reductions of Mechanized or Armored
Divisions in Europe.

In other words, both sides could give up their costly manpower and Offense
capabilities without endangering their Defensive postures, and demonstrate their
good will without the former risks inherent to reductions of manpower when
"Attack was the best Defense." This again is a product of the Tactical Atom, the
result of the current disproportion between Firepower and Ground movement. The
clear vision of this professional truth of modern warfare might give to the
politician an unique opportunity : try to reverse the trends of the Arms Race,
refine Arms Control Agreements, and eventually go ahead with Disarmament, while
keeping their powder dry. This is possible, provided the "powder" is nuclear
powder, which alone can render meaningless all military adventures.

Then the irony of our Times drives us to a strange conclusion : the nuclear
weapons should not be the first to be sacrificed on the altar of Disarmament,
they should be the last... The Atom, which has been the "Holy terror" of our
age, would then be the guardian of Peace while we work at a better world.

NATO IN THEORY AND PRACTICE
by J.T. Trotman
The gulf between theory and practice is wide in almost all human affairs, but
particularly so where NATO and nuclear strategy is concerned. In this area an
extraordinary abundance of theoretical analysis has been undertaken, but there
have fortunately been only very indirect opportunities to put the theories to
practical tests.

The two doctrines upon which most attention has been concentrated are massive
retaliation (as evolved by President Eisenhower and Secretary of State John
Foster Dulles), and flexible response (as eventually promoted by President
Kennedy and his advisors). The former is now scorned as scarcely credible, while
the latter has the full support of the strategic establishment. We hope to
suggest in this paper that there is more to be said for massive retaliation than
is sometimes realised, that there are greater difficulties with flexible
response than sometimes admitted, and that in fact, whatever the rhetoric, the
result is an uneasy amalgam of both with a practical reliance upon the massive
nuclear threat but a theoretical base in the convolutions of flexible response.

The changes that have taken place in NATO's strategic doctrines over the years
can be interpreted in terms of changing perceptions of the relationships between
conventional warfare, tactical nuclear warfare and strategic nuclear warfare. At
the time of the signature of the North Atlantic Treaty in 1949 it was clearly
politically impossible to suggest that Soviet conventional strength should be
matched by the Western powers; in any case the United States had the atomic
bomb, and the important thing was to ensure the commitment of these nuclear
forces to the defence of Europe, and to convince the potential enemy that they
would be used as strategic weapons in the event of any attack. This concept of
the relationship between conventional and strategic nuclear warfare – a concept
which totally subordinated the employment (or even the existence) of
conventional forces to the strategic use of nuclear weapons – was to represent
the formal rhetoric of NATO until 1967.

However, almost as soon as the Treaty was signed, the Soviet detonation of a
nuclear device broke the United States' nuclear monopoly. A year later, some
doubt was cast on the deterrent value of nuclear weapons by the invasion of
South Korea; here, it

---

# Page 17

Page 2 of ANNEX B to NPG(Staff Group)N(71)136
was discovered that there was no alternative to the hasty mobilisation of
conventional forces. In theory, the lessons of these events were not lost on the
North Atlantic Council: in 1952 it approved completely unrealistic conventional
force goals of 96 divisions. The new Eisenhower Administration looked at the
potential costs of these forces, and immediately decided to match force with
rhetoric and to reaffirm the policy of almost complete dependence upon nuclear
weapons. Two new phrases were coined – 'massive retaliation' and
'brinkmanship'(1) – and, somewhat illogically, American forces in Europe were
equipped with tactical nuclear weapons. Later in the same year the North
Atlantic Council took formal notice of these developments, and authorised NATO
commanders to base their plans on prompt – and first – use of nuclear weapons.
Field Marshal Montgomery, at the time Deputy Supreme Allied Commander, put the
point bluntly. He said, 'I want to make it clear that we at SHAPE are basing all
our operational planning on using atomic and thermonuclear weapons in our own
defence. With us it is no longer: "They may probably be used." It is very
definitely: "They will be used if we are attacked."(2) In 1957 the North
Atlantic Council somewhat belatedly took advantage of the opportunity offered by
this doctrine to approve a set of considerably reduced conventional force goals
– 30 combat-ready divisions in the Central Region, and very few reserves. Thus,
by the end of the 1950s, nuclear response was the basis of NATO strategy. Both
strategic and tactical weapons were available, but how they would be controlled
and what the effects of their use might be were all unresolved riddles: the
point on which all members of the Alliance agreed was that nuclear weapons were
essential both on military and (indirectly) on economic grounds.

As early as 1946 there had been a dissenting voice. One scholar – Bernard Brodie
– had suggested that the important thing about nuclear weapons was not to use
them.(3) And by the second half of the 1950s – a time of intellectual ferment in
the field of strategic theory – he had been joined by Kaufmann, Osgood,
Kissinger, Wohlstetter, Morgenstern and others, who all suggested that massive
retaliation was an almost incredible doctrine.(4) Finally, it fell to Herman
Kahn to attempt to make nuclear weapons seem truly operational by suggesting
that a controlled nuclear strategy should be possible by which would 'coerce'
the enemy, rather than defeat or destroy him.(5)

To cut a long and complex story short, all these threads came together in the
Kennedy Administration at the beginning of the 1960s. The year before he took
office as President, Kennedy had said, '... We have been preparing to fight the
one kind of war we least want to fight and are least likely to fight. We

---

# Page 18

Page 3 of ANNEX B to NPG(Staff Group)N(71)136
have been driving ourselves into a corner where the only choice is all or
nothing at all, world devastation or submission – a choice that necessarily
causes us to hesitate on the brink and leaves the initiative in the hands of our
enemies.(6) This 'all-or-nothing' thesis (coupled with some colourful phrases
such as 'pre-emptive surrender' as a description of Western doctrine) led to a
deceptively simple argument: if strategic nuclear weapons were too big to use,
we must have something smaller; maybe we should have nuclear weapons of all
sizes, and use them flexibly, as may be required to halt the enemy. Thus was
born – in the United States at least – the appealing doctrine of flexible
response, and in particular its concentration on nuclear weapons.

The doctrine of flexible response (and the accompanying concept of graduated
deterrence) can be articulated in a variety of ways. One of the latest is from
President Nixon's 1971 report to the Congress on foreign policy. Here he says,
with reference to the doctrine of strategic sufficiency, '... We must insure
that we have the forces and procedures that provide us with alternatives
appropriate to the nature and level of the provocation. This means having the
plans and command and control capabilities necessary to enable us to select and
carry out the appropriate response without necessarily having to resort to mass
destruction.'(7) Apart from the obvious need to have the forces, as well as the
plans and the command and control structures, this is a reasonable statement of
the current view. In NATO terms, it is sometimes abbreviated to the rather
ambiguous assertion that enemy attacks should be met with the necessary level of
force. This raises some awkward practical questions. Is the necessary level of
force that required to halt the enemy or to defeat him? And what does defeat
mean in this context? And if we merely halt him, do we also attempt to roll him
back? No one would argue that outcomes of wars can be precisely clear before
decisions are taken to fight them, but it is reasonable to suppose that some
effort should be made to elucidate such central issues before defining a
strategic doctrine. In any case, the convenient theory of flexible response was
adopted by NATO during the mid-1960s, and formally endorsed in 1967.

The difficulties of the strategy of massive retaliation are now fairly clear to
the West, if not to the Soviet leaders. The main problem arises from what is
known as 'credibility' (really 'unbelievability'). The basic question can be
phrased in a variety of terms, but all are variants of the 'better Red than
dead?' problem. If this is too blunt, the question may be put as follows: Will
the Soviet leaders be persuaded that the West will be prepared to use its
strategic nuclear weapons,

---

# Page 19

Page 4 of ANNEX B to NPG(Staff Group)N(71)136
especially if the Soviet Union itself possesses means of retaliating for the
retaliation? The answer to this question is that they will probably not be so
persuaded, but they cannot be certain. However, the arguments that flow from
this answer are of almost mediaeval complexity. Are the Soviet leaders more or
less likely to be persuaded that the West means what it says about massive
retaliation if the West has other options? If there is no alternative to mutual
destruction (the 'all-or-nothing' thesis), they may find the Western posture
incredible. On the other hand, if other options do exist (in accordance with the
doctrine of flexible response), they will certainly feel that the West will
explore them first, so that in a sense the strategic deterrent is even less
credible at the initial stage. Will it be any more credible at a later stage,
after other options have failed, than it would have been if the other options
had not existed? Perhaps; but perhaps not. In other words, the doctrine of
flexible response does not solve the 'all-or-nothing' dilemma; it merely
relieves it to a dimmer and even more uncertain background.

Maybe such arguments as these are the crux of deterrence; maybe, as is often
suggested, mutual deterrence is not simple, but a complex dynamic structure of
national political policies and objectives, military postures and capabilities,
real and declaratory expressions of determination, estimates and perceptions of
the opponent's capabilities, intentions and determination, and levels of
confidence in these estimates, and so on. It must be admitted, however, that
there is something to be said for simplicity when the stakes are high; whatever
the complexities of massive retaliation, the doctrine of flexible response is
yet more convoluted.

However, this may be, the least that can be said about massive retaliation is
that it appears to have worked. It applied during the Stalin era of the greatest
Soviet intrasignance, and, if their rhetoric is to be believed, was no means
incredible to the Soviet leaders' perceptions. For many years, they refused to
be convinced by the arguments for flexible response, and doggedly stuck to the
view that a nuclear war is a nuclear war is a nuclear war; it is not impossible,
in retrospect, that their seemingly stubborn attitude represented an essentially
practical view of the realities of the military confrontation between themselves
and the West.

Flexible response, in spite of its obvious appeal and sophistication, also has
its problems. The first and perhaps the most important arises from what may be
called 'truncated flexibility.' It is inherent in all articulations of the
doctrine that there is a kind of spectrum of violence, through which we and the
enemy will cautiously proceed. In terms of United States policy this spectrum

---

# Page 20

Page 5 of ANNEX B To NPG(Staff Group)N(71)136
is very broad – from political agitation, through insurgency, guerrilla warfare,
sub-theatre conventional warfare (whatever that is), theatre conventional
warfare and theatre nuclear warfare to strategic nuclear warfare; (8) NATO is of
course only concerned with theatre warfare, but it has an obvious interest, to
say the least, in a strategic exchange. If the doctrine is to operate as
envisaged, it is probably essential that both sides are able to progress at
relatively equal speeds through the whole of the relevant part of the spectrum –
and especially through the conventional part of the spectrum. In other words,
the doctrine implies reasonable equality in conventional capabilities. The side
that is deficient in any one of these elements – and particularly the
conventional element – may find itself facing practical decisions that will tend
to invalidate the theory.

The difficulty for NATO has been to arrange for this full range of capabilities
– and thus flexibility – in the absence of the political and social will to
deploy adequate conventional forces. The problem has been solved, temporarily
and probably only intellectually, by distinguishing between tactical and
strategic nuclear weapons, and suggesting that, though strategic weapons are
incredible, tactical weapons are a reasonable substitute for conventional
forces. As has been indicated there is considerable doubt about this in terms of
concept; in terms of tactics there is even more doubt, as was pointed out as
long ago as 1952. (9) The argument is fairly obvious: front-line divisions will
be destroyed by the tactical nuclear exchange; NATO has few reserves and will be
unable to form a new front to force the enemy to concentrate his reserve
divisions when they in turn attack; without concentrations there are few targets
for tactical nuclear weapons.

It is not the intention of this paper to overemphasise the problems and
difficulties that attach to the doctrine of flexible response, but certain
elements of the accepted wisdom relating to the doctrine are open to question.
It is argued, for example, that with careful control tactical nuclear weapons
can be so employed that collateral damage (a euphemism for 'unintentional' or
'useless' damage) and civilian casualties are minimal. It is also suggested,
especially by Robert M. Lawrence(10) that very small yield, carefully-targeted
weapons with a minimum of fall-out would solve many of the problems associated
with the employment of tactical nuclear weapons. There may be something in these
suggestions, if only on the basis that attempts to lower collateral damage and
civilian casualties may indicate to the other side that we are serious in our
strategic concept, but it is hard to carry the practical argument very far. The
first problem arises from the

---

# Page 21

Page 6 of ANNEX B to NPG(Staff Group)N(71)136
so-called 'fog of battle.' Whatever our intentions when the battle begins, and
however carefully we select our weapons and target our strikes, the development
of the battle will inevitably degrade communications and capabilities for target
acquisition. But the pressures to use nuclear weapons will not lessen; rather
they will increase if the battle goes against us. In these circumstances, our
commanders must compensate for loss of information by 'opening the field of
vision' and blanketing larger areas. The second difficulty is that we shall be
defending Western Europe against a Soviet advance, and thus many of our nuclear
weapons will be used on NATO territory. This will be especially likely if the
Soviet planners are shrewd and successful in disguising their intentions, so
that political inhibitions develop within the alliance about the excalatory
effects of attacking targets on Soviet (or perhaps even satellite) territory. It
is in fact by no means impossible that NATO control procedures may in the event
impose such delays on the use of tactical nuclear weapons as to render them
militarily pointless; if this happens, an even larger part of our spectrum of
flexible response will have been invalidated.

Further, the enemy will retain the option of using tactical nuclear weapons
himself to assist his advance. Soviet tactical nuclear capabilities are
generally greater than ours, they can be reinforced readily, and many of the
delivery capabilities are located in the Soviet Union itself and may thus be
immune from attack, especially in the early stages of a battle. The Soviet
leaders may have fewer inhibitions about 'blanket' use than the NATO Council,
and, if they do use nuclear weapons, collateral damage and civilian casualties
on our side are likely to be large. This is partly because, in contrast to our
large numbers of short-range, relatively low-yield systems, the Soviet Union has
emphasised higher-yield, mobile tactical missiles useful for terrain or area
targets.(11) Indeed, Alain Enthoven and K. Wayne Smith argue in their study of
the United States defence programme in the 1960s that '...the Soviet force
structure raises doubt about Soviet capability to fight a limited tactical
nuclear war, much less one in which collateral damage and civilian casualties
are kept to low levels....Limited wars with one side using discrete fire
techniques and the other using terrain-fire are likely to be notoriously one-
sided in favour of the latter.... In short, the Soviets have neither the
organisation nor the force structure for a limited nuclear war fought
exclusively against military targets in an engaged battle zone.'(12)(15)

A further important consideration arises from the so-called 'firebreak.' It is
simple to see the difference between conventional war and nuclear war, even
tactical nuclear war. There is no logical reason for this, and no reason why
small-yield nuclear weapons should not be considered as merely

---

# Page 22

Page 7 of ANNEX B to NPG(Staff Group)N(71)136

explosives in convenient packages, but it is a fact that they are not, and it is
possible to argue that any doctrine (such as flexible response) which tends to
blur this distinction is somewhat undesirable.

Perhaps arising from a sense of this distinction is the view that our use of
tactical weapons for the defence of the NATO area may cause the Soviet leaders
to halt their operations and reappraise their position. Having observed NATO's
determination and willingness to take the risks inherent in escalating to
tactical nuclear use, they may pause, reconsider their strategy and decision to
attack, and quietly withdraw, or at least offer to negotiate.

This is a very appealing concept indeed, especially in a politico-military
context. It provides a fundamentally 'peace-loving' rationale for our deployment
of the tactical nuclear weapons that enable us to avoid facing the costs of
conventional defence. Rather unfortunately it is open to serious doubt.

In the first place, it is based on an assumption that the Soviet leaders would
undertake military operations in the most sensitive of all areas – operations
the results of which would be hazardous in the extreme – without having
considered their prospects of success in all conceivable sets of circumstances.
Or, if we assume that they have considered all possibilities, it means that we
believe the Soviet leaders will be prepared to attack, but also be prepared to
withdraw at the first sign that NATO is resisting with nuclear weapons. Such a
withdrawal would have an enormous impact on the world-wide credibility of Soviet
postures and intentions, and it is surely more reasonable to suppose that a
scenario in which the Soviet leaders were thus prepared to withdraw would be a
scenario in which they would have been deterred in the first place. In other
words, their willingness to commence serious military operations in the NATO
area is evidence of their willingness to face the prospect of escalation, and
certainly to face NATO's tactical use of nuclear weapons, which must appear to
them to be a very likely stage of escalation. (In passing, it may be noted that
there is no evidence in Soviet strategic literature to suggest a tactic of
probing by military aggression in Europe and withdrawal in the face of
escalation. The Cuban crisis which is sometimes cited as an example of Soviet
willingness to withdraw in the face of determination is not relevant to a
possible offensive in Europe. The Cuban situation could be (and was) represented
as a movement into the enemy's heartland that led to important concessions on
the part of the United States. No such rationalisation would be possible after a
serious attack in Europe).

Finally, it can be argued that the existence on both sides of allegedly secure
second-strike capabilities, which theoretically make immediate massive strategic
action unnecessary, should add credibility to the prospect of protracted theatre
war at lower levels of violence. In practice, however, there may be justifiable
doubt about this thesis. Whatever assurances may or may not appear to exist
about the security of the strategic capabilities, there are bound to be
considerable pressures to use these weapons fairly rapidly to avoid even a
minimal risk of their loss or partial destruction in an enemy strike.

How, it may be asked, in the face of all these circumstances, are we prepared to
contemplate the use of tactical nuclear weapons in the NATO area? The answer is
simple, though rarely expressed. We are prepared to use tactical nuclear weapons
because we feel that the strategic nuclear deterrent – the original threat of
massive retaliation – will deter the enemy from replying in kind. Thus the
allegedly incredible threat of strategic retaliation is just as relevant to a
tactical nuclear war as to a conventional one, and it is hard to see why it
becomes more credible in the former circumstances.

In a sense tactical nuclear weapons are a complicating factor in the strategy
and doctrine, but this is not the whole of the argument; it certainly does not
prove that tactical nuclear weapons have no place in our posture. In fact, there
are a number of reasons for retaining them in Europe. The first is, of course,
that they are there, and it would be hard to withdraw them. Secondly, they make
a limited contribution to the deterrent, in the general sense that the Soviet
planners might be less willing to attack if confronted with tactical weapons
than if they did not exist, and in the tactical sense that the enemy must take
their existence into account in his planning, because the massed forces required
for conventional attack are precisely the forces that invite a nuclear strike.
Thirdly, it has been pointed out(14) that tactical nuclear weapons may deter the
initial use of such weapons by the Soviet Union, and force them to rely on their
conventional forces; this factor is only relevant, however, if our conventional
forces are of adequate strength. Finally, there is the question of hedging one's
bets. All these matters of doctrine and employment are matters of theory, and it
is as well not to discard options without compelling reasons.

In spite of the fact that there is no ready alternative to the present
situation, it is as well to be clear what that situation is. It would appear
that, for a variety of reasons, many of them unrelated to the essential military
problem, NATO has adopted a doctrine – flexible response – which the Alliance
has neither the

---

# Page 24

Page 9 of ANNEX B to NPG(Staff Group)N(71)136
economic nor the social will to implement in its fullest sense. To make the
doctrine credible we need additional conventional forces plus, preferably, an
agreement to reduce and limit tactical nuclear weapons. Thus we could have a
credible conventional deterrent backed by massive strategic nuclear forces;
tactical nuclear weapons would be relegated to their right place in the
spectrum, as a minor addition to the deterrent in certain circumstances, but
with no important operational role. At present, as far as one can see, all our
efforts are directed towards fewer (rather than more) conventional weapons, and
towards limiting strategic (rather than tactical) nuclear weapons.

There is a further related possibility – the development of conventional forces
that are less costly in manpower, if not in money. There are of course problems
about the duel between equipment-intensive forces and manpower-intensive forces,
but some developments on these lines are in progress, in the form of novel
weapons such as the German Pandora (an area anti-tank or possibly anti-personnel
weapon), Medusa (an air-droppable or rocket-delivered mine) and Dragon Seed (an
advanced cluster bomb).(15) All of these appear to be steps on the way to
incorporating in conventional weapons some of the area effectiveness of nuclear
devices. In addition the United States is said to be developing a variety of
sophisticated devices, which have as one of their objects increasing the
effectiveness of conventional forces and weapons, and thus reducing the
requirement for manpower.(16) It is perhaps doubtful how far these and similar
developments can be stretched, and probably our best hope lies in the eventual
appreciation that talks about limiting tactical nuclear weapons are as important
as talks aimed at the limitation of strategic nuclear weapons. In the event,
TALI may be even more complex than SALT, and last considerably longer.
