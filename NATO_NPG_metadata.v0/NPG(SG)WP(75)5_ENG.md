> # Report on Phase II of the Work Programme on Follow-on Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons
> 
> This document is a draft report by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) regarding the second phase of a work programme analyzing the tactical use of nuclear weapons following an initial deployment. The report identifies policy issues for Ministerial discussion based on an ad hoc Study Team report on Phase II of the Work Programme on Follow-on Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons. It outlines the three-phase structure of the work programme: Phase I focused on basic data inputs, Phase II on comparative analysis and synthesis, and Phase III on the formulation of political guidelines. The document highlights limitations of Phase I, including a focus on military aspects in restricted areas without examining politico-military interactions and the consideration of only existing NATO weapon systems. It also notes that Phase I assumptions often assumed the worst possible military situation, such as failed initial use and inferior conventional forces. The report emphasizes the duality between military effectiveness and political objectives, noting that follow-on use must send an unambiguous signal to the adversary to cease aggression. The draft report was submitted by the Chairman, R.L. Shelar, to the Staff Group of the Nuclear Planning Group for consideration at their meeting on April 7, 1975.

FOLLOW-ON USE WORK PROGRAMME - PHASE II
Draft Report by the Permanent Representatives
I. PURPOSE
1. This report identifies possible policy issues for Ministerial discussion in
connection with consideration of the ad hoc Study Team report on Phase II of the
Work Programme on Follow-on Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons(1) and makes
suggestions for the implementation of Phase III.

II. BACKGROUND
2. In November 1969, the Nuclear Planning Group approved "Provisional Political
Guidelines for the Initial Defensive Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons by NATO(2)
as policy guidance for national authorities and as a directive to NATO Military
Authorities. At the same meeting, Ministers recognised the need for a study of
follow-on tactical use of nuclear weapons, to examine the phases of tactical use
subsequent to initial use by NATO, and to explore NATO responses to an initial
use of nuclear weapons for tactical purposes by the Warsaw Pact. Rather than
attempt this task in a single comprehensive study, they agreed to proposals put
forward by the Permanent Representatives, which defined(3) the objectives,
general assumptions and broad structure of an overall work programme to be
carried out in a series of interrelated steps.

3. The work programme seeks to provide, inter alia, through its exploration of
the implications and interactions of follow-on tactical use of nuclear weapons,
a basis for the Ministers to develop policy proposals, as appropriate, for
submission to the Defence Planning Committee. In this context, the NPG agreed
that the Follow-on Use Work Programme would consist of three major components:

Politico-Military Implications of Follow-on Use
8. One of the generalised conclusions of the Phase II Study Team report is that
if the enemy responds to NATO follow-on use "at the same or a higher level ……
NATO …… could find itself ultimately worse off in that the balance of
conventional military advantage would shift further in favour of the enemy"(1).
This conclusion, as all other conclusions reached in the Phase II Study Team
Report, was controlled by the assumptions mentioned in paragraphs 5(a) and 6
above that were made in construing the scenario for follow-on use in the Phase I
studies. It appears that these studies, in selecting the number of nuclear
weapons to use, leaned towards rather low levels of such use, e.g., those which
could cope with very limited specific local military situations. From the
analysis of this limited use, the above-mentioned conclusion logically followed;
however, higher degrees of follow-on use, both with respect to quantity and
expanded geographical area, were not examined. Obviously, higher level of use
would have very sensitive political implications. Nevertheless, we think that
political decisions to implement options which are less restricted, i.e.,
involving wider and/or more intensive use of nuclear weapons

9. Military Effectiveness and Political Objectives. The duality between military
effectiveness and political objectives is recognised by the Phase II Study Team
Report which states that(1) "the consequences of military action have to be
considered in relation not just to specific military goals but above all to the
ultimate political objective of persuading the enemy to cease his aggression and
withdraw". Since this duality is a basic element guiding any decision to use
nuclear weapons, it may give rise to a number of other considerations. In making
the case that the military objectives of follow-on use will in almost every case
be overtaken by political aims, the Phase II Study Team Report points out(2)
that follow-on use must bring to the adversary an unambiguous and convincing
signal, stronger than in initial use, of the risk he will face if he does not
cease his aggression. In view of its more escalatory character, this use would
have primary political purposes.

the same considerations apply to all. While it can be generalised that there is
a distinction between initial use and follow-on use, it is observed that
successive follow-on uses may very well have clear distinctive characteristics,

14. According to the U.S. Secretary of Defense, the importance of theatre
nuclear, along with that of conventional forces, in the era of strategic
equivalence and Vladivostok, has increased(1). If this is also to mean that the
deterrent value of theatre nuclear weapons has likewise increased, it is
worthwhile to recall some basic principles of NATO's strategy of flexibility in
response which deal with deterrence. While it is clear that political
implementation of this strategy has to be carefully evaluated when the situation
arises, it is also indispensable that the response be such that NATO's overall
objective (the preservation or restoration of the integrity and security of the
NATO area) is achieved. Flexibility thus refers to the degree of selectivity in
the forms of NATO reactions as well as to the related process through which such
reactions are implemented. It not only requires the ability to cope with various
military types of aggression, but also implies that NATO should rely on various
degrees of political resolution to be implemented in a crisis on the basis of
the political-military situation. Such a strategy, when emphasising forward
defence, encompasses a denial military concept as well as the political
necessity to implement this strategy in a crisis management mode(2).

15. It is obvious from the Phase II Study Team Report that the NPG approach to
the theatre nuclear weapon issues (initial, follow-on use) is primarily oriented
towards the political implementation of NATO strategy. On the other hand, the
basic NATO military strategy paper, MC 14/3, provides the Alliance with
sufficient conceptual flexibility in so far as options with regard to the forms
of NATO reaction are concerned. Furthermore, it is our understanding that the
Major NATO Commanders are currently increasing the range of their selective
employment options, thus providing increased flexibility in response.

16. With these thoughts in mind, an even broader consideration might be valuable
at this point. The Phase II Study Team Report points out that basically three
negative political assumptions must be made for NATO to arrive at the necessity
for follow-on use: deterrence failed, conventional defence is inadequate, and
initial nuclear use did not convince the enemy to cease his aggression and
withdraw(1). In such a situation, the Report suggests that NATO might need to
provide more escalatory evidence of its resolution(2) to include also use in an
extended geographical area(3). The Phase II Study Team Report thus reveals that,
if the Alliance at this point chooses to interpret flexibility merely as a chain
of politically acceptable successive single steps of reaction, NATO could not
live up to its basic defence objective in the nuclear age, i.e., maintenance or
restoration of the integrity and security of NATO areas. We have already
commented in part on this issue in paragraphs 9 and 10 above. We would like to
add here that if such an attitude should emerge, it would be as a result of the
NPG approach to the problem.
