> # Implementation of Special ADM Guidelines
> 
> This draft report by the Nuclear Planning Group Permanent Representatives evaluates the current status of implementation of the Special Political Guidelines for the Possible Use of Atomic Demolition Munitions (ADMs). It summarizes and comments on the progress made by NATO Military Authorities in implementing these guidelines, based on a Military Committee report. The document outlines the planning for the employment of ADMs, noting that while planning in areas of sole responsibility for Major NATO Commanders has been implemented, work continues in areas shared with national authorities. It highlights that target folders have been prepared for possible ADM use in the Northern, Central, and Southern regions of Allied Command Europe (ACE). The report also addresses national constraints, noting that Italy and Greece have no particular constraints beyond the guidelines, while Turkey is re-evaluating the utility of ADMs on its territory. The document further discusses the uncertainties regarding national constraints, advance preparations, and the rapid movement and emplacement of ADMs that limit the scope of possible planning. It emphasizes that NATO Military Authorities have refined their planning to the maximum extent possible within these limits, such as reducing the number of locations earmarked for ADMs in the Northern Region of ACE.

I. PURPOSE
This report summarises and comments on the current status of implementation of
the Special Political Guidelines for the Possible Use of Atomic Demolition
Munitions(1) and certain other measures related to atomic demolition munitions
(ADMs).

III. DISCUSSION
5. The Special ADM Guidelines state that the employment of ADMs is one of the
options available to NATO for defensive tactical use of nuclear weapons. The
Guidelines direct that the NATO Military Authorities, in conjunction with the
national authorities concerned, should take appropriate steps to ensure that
this option can be exercised(1). In this context, the MC Report summarises the
status of implementation of the Special ADM Guidelines under four broad
headings: planning for the employment of ADMs, national constraints, advance
preparation, and release procedures. Within this framework, certain specific
questions, such as training of non-US ADM teams and transportation of ADMs in
non-US helicopters, are also treated. The most recent MC Report, like earlier
similar reports, is based on information provided by the Supreme Allied
Commander, Europe (SACEUR) and Ministries of Defence of countries concerned.

Planning for the Employment of ADMs
6. The Special ADM Guidelines stipulate that the NATO Military Authorities
should develop plans for the use of ADMs, either singly or on a package basis,
as appropriate, to complement conventional defensive obstacle systems where
terrain features are suitable and where timely use of ADMs may be critical to
the defence. The Guidelines caution, however, that overall military plans should
not be predicated on an assured release of ADMs(2).

7. The MC Report reinforces, in substance, the opinion we reached in an earlier
document(1) that the planning aspects of the Special ADM Guidelines have been
implemented in those fields for which the Major NATO Commanders have sole
responsibility but that work is still continuing in those fields where Major
NATO Commanders share the implementing task with national authorities. The
Military Committee reported(2) in 1971, for example, that the existing plans of
the NATO Military Commands had been amended as necessary to conform to the
Guidelines. Their most recent Report indicates(3) in general terms that planning
by SACEUR and his subordinate commanders for possible employment of ADMs
continues to be actively pursued. For example, target folders have been prepared
for possible locations where ADMs could be used in support of conventional
defensive systems in the three regions (Northern, Central and Southern) of
Allied Command Europe (ACE). This planning has been carried out in collaboration
with national military authorities, except in those cases where it is precluded
as a matter of national policy.

8. Having said this, we recognise that uncertainties still exist that impose
limits on the extent to which the NATO Military Authorities can plan for the
effective employment of ADMs. There are, for example, unresolved questions
pertaining to national constraints, to advance preparations, and to arrangements
for rapid movement and emplacement of ADMs, that directly affect the

Advance Preparations
13. The Special ADM Guidelines state(1) that the NATO Military Authorities
should, subject to agreement by the host country, make arrangements to improve
ADM effectiveness, to improve reaction times, and to minimise where possible the
risk of fallout from ADMs through such actions as prechambering (2) in peacetime
or site preparation(2) in times of tension and initial hostilities. The
feasibility of implementing these two actions has been pursued by SHAPE with the
countries concerned.

14. German authorities have indicated(3) that national policy precludes
prechambering on German territory. Consequently, arrangements have been made for
expediting the preparation of ADM sites in periods of tension or initial
hostilities, whenever such work might be authorised by the German authorities.
In this connection, the Military Committee considers there is a need for further
research, by nations, into methods of constructing burial shafts in the minimum
possible time(4). We concur.

(1) Special ADM Guidelines, paragraph 8
(2) Prechambering is the deliberate preparation of burial shafts in peacetime.
The usual objective of prechambering is to facilitate rapid emplacement of ADMs
to a depth to optimise crater dimensions and to minimise fallout. Site
preparation (through hasty construction of burial shafts) seeks to achieve the
same objective. Since such construction would not be undertaken until
hostilities commence or until specified alert stages are declared, site
preparation measures may be less effective than prechambering.

Rapid Movement of ADMs

21. The Special ADM Guidelines state(1) that the NATO Military Authorities
should, subject to agreement by the host country, make arrangements to improve
ADM effectiveness, to improve reaction times, and to minimise where possible the
risk of fallout from ADMS through, among other things, planning for the
expeditious movement of ADMS from storage sites to the vicinity of potential
targets and for their rapid emplacement. The Guidelines also mention(2) that
increasing the availability of faster means of transportation, such as
helicopters, would contribute to the capability for expeditious movement. At the
present time, only US helicopters may be used to carry ADMS prior to a release
decision, although current US regulations permit the use of non-US aircraft,
including helicopters, to be employed for transportation of US nuclear weapons
after R-hour or selective release of the weapons concerned. Since it has been

Conclusion

23. A general survey of the status of implementation of the Special ADM
Guidelines and other related measures during the period covering almost three
years since the Guidelines were approved by the Defence Planning Committee leads
to the conclusion that progress has been impressive in some respects and
disappointing in others. Delays in implementing the Special ADM Guidelines have
been attributable in most cases to uncertainties regarding national positions
and decisions. The countries concerned should be urged to resolve these
uncertainties. Where this cannot be done, the NATO Military Authorities should
be encouraged to develop contingency plans and other alternative measures.
