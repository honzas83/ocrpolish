> # Draft Report on the ACLANT Follow-on Use Study
> 
> This draft report by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) identifies areas for Ministerial discussion concerning the Study of Follow-on Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons in the Allied Command Atlantic (ACLANT) area. The ACLANT Study is the final of eight regional studies in Phase I of the Follow-on Use Work Programme, prepared by a team including representatives from Canada, the Netherlands, Norway, the United Kingdom, and the United States. The report examines the tactical use of nuclear weapons to defend amphibious task forces in the Atlantic against submarine, surface, and air attacks from the Warsaw Pact, while also analyzing the vulnerability of NATO forces to enemy nuclear attacks. A significant focus is placed on the timely release of nuclear weapons, emphasizing that the short duration of naval engagements requires release requests to be initiated before specific tactical contact is made. The document discusses the release procedures, specifically the ROMEO (selective release request) and SIERRA (pre-conditioned authority) procedures, and the notes that the Major NATO Commander's current procedures may hinder effective use in fast-moving engagements. It further suggests that subordinate commanders may request weapons be made available prior to tactical contact to ensure immediate availability. Political authorities retain the option to specify conditions for release authorization, which must be balanced against military effectiveness. The report concludes that nuclear weapons must be immediately available for defense in the event of an enemy nuclear initiation or response.

In practical terms, however, it would probably be difficult in an actual
situation to distinguish among these aspects, or to predict the precise ways in
which they might materialise. For example, it is highly improbable that a
defending commander would be able to determine, in every case with absolute
certainty, the precise nature (conventional or nuclear, initial or counter-
attack) of an incoming enemy attack before the weapons detonate. Furthermore, it
is entirely conceivable that successful NATO defence against an enemy submarine
threat, for instance, could provoke an enemy counter-attack with air-delivered
weapons. In the light of such considerations and if it is concluded that the use
of nuclear weapons to defend the force is warranted, political authorities may
very well find themselves confronted with the necessity of making a release
authorisation in terms broad enough to meet the spectrum of potential threats.

Early Use by NATO
13. The Study concludes that, if nuclear weapons were to be used in defence of
the task force, from a military point of view they should be used early on
before the task force's defences were eroded(1). This would be particularly
important in terms of the force's capability to meet an enemy nuclear response.
The Study points out, however, that it may be difficult for political
authorities to decide to use nuclear weapons in a situation where it has not yet
become evident that conventional defeat faces NATO forces, even though the
nuclear threshold has already been crossed and although it could be argued that
the degree of escalatory risk is less with the use of nuclear weapons at sea(2).
In this connection, it may be worthwhile recalling that the Provisional
Political Guidelines for Initial Use conclude that the timing of such use must
allow for any subsequent Warsaw Pact reaction and for possible NATO follow-on
use while NATO's conventional forces are still capable of taking advantage of
the effects of using nuclear weapons. These Guidelines also note that timely
initial use on a limited scale may avoid the need for more widespread and
damaging use later in the conflict(3).

It seems reasonable to expect that similar considerations could apply in
addressing questions of follow-on use. At a minimum, the objectives of
preserving flexibility in the face of uncertain and unpredictable developments
in a possible future conflict and of maintaining the integrity of NATO's
deterrent posture argue against making a stipulation now that NATO's
conventional capabilities have to be exhausted before follow-on use would be
authorised. As a related matter, it might be observed that follow-on tactical
use of nuclear weapons is, like initial use, designed to complement conventional
operations and not to supplement them.

Collateral Effects
14. The Study concludes that collateral effects should not play a significant
part in considering NATO's use of nuclear weapons in the ACLANT area.
Radioactive fallout seems likely to be the only hazard to civilian populations
in countries in the general area of the assumed operation, and this hazard would
be encountered only if the Warsaw Pact elected to attack the NATO forces with
megaton weapons burst on the surface(1). Parenthetically, it might be noted that
current NATO military doctrine provides for NATO nuclear attacks against enemy
submarines, aircraft and ships to be carried out in ways that effectively
localise the collateral effects.

15.  Regarding Warsaw Pact surface bursts, the Study estimates that, with an
effective wind of about 20 knots, fallout from a 5 MT (megaton) weapon could be
lethal to about 10% of the people without any shelter at a distance of about 150
miles and could cause minor discomfort (nausea and vomiting) for about 10% of
the people without any shelter at a distance of about 170 miles. For a 1 MT
surface burst, these distances would be about 75 miles and 95 miles
respectively.  If people were warned and remained in shelters, the distances at
which these effects would be experienced would be considerably reduced, in most
cases by more than half(1). In this connection, it should be noted that surface
burst weapons are less effective than airburst weapons in producing damage on
ships(2) and the Warsaw Pact is not under any compulsion on grounds of military
effectiveness to use surface bursts.
