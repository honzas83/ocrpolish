> [!abstract] 
> # Selective Release in the Policy on the Containment
> 
> This document, a note by the Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Staff Group, outlines a series of questions intended to facilitate the continuation of informal discussions regarding selective release procedures for the follow-on tactical use of nuclear weapons. The Chairman, J. B. Lem, emphasizes that these questions reflect issues raised during a Staff Group meeting on March 9, 1970, and an aim is to ensure that SACEUR's selective release procedures conform to the general guidelines for consultation approved by the Defence Policy Committee (DPC) in December 1969. The questions specifically address the communication channels and formats SACEUR expects to use to notify governments and the Council/DPC of requests for nuclear weapon use. Furthermore, the document explores whether national authorities would prefer to maintain control through successive selective releases or grant a single blanket release for tactical purposes. It also considers the risk of decision-makers being overtaxed by the volume and complexity of case-by-case requests and suggests exploring alternative ways to be present issues to facilitate timely judgements. Finally, it addresses the interdependence of separate requests, such as air defence and battlefield use, and how such interdependence should be communicated to decision-makers.

QUESTIONS ON SELECTIVE RELEASE PROCEDURES RELATED TO FOLLOW-ON USE

1. with respect to SACEUR's selective release procedures, what steps have been
take or are contemplated to insure that the procedures conform to the general
guidelines for consultation procedure on the use of nuclear weapons approved in
December 1969 by the DPC (DFC/D(69)59, 20th November 1969)?

2. Specifically, do the procedures insure that "any request for the use of
nuclear weapons in the defence of NATO... from a Major NATO Commander / SACEUR
in this instance? ... should be communicated immediately to the governments and
the Council/DPC?" How?

3. In connection with the foregoing, the guidelines for consultation procedure
stipulate that "the Major NATC Commanders ... will use the most expeditious
means of communication at their disposal...". What are the channels and formats
SACEUR expects to use to communicate with the governments? with the Council/DPC?

4. Is it anticipated that SACEUR may send information to governments that
differs (not as detailed, different in format, not as frequent, etc.) from the
information he provides to representatives of these governments in the
Council/DPC?

5. Does SACEUR anticipate sending to governments and the Council/DPC messages of
each of the DFC categories?

6. with respect to broader aspects of selective release problems related to
follow-on use, is it envisaged that political (national) authorities will wish
to retain a measure of control through successive selective releases? Or, in
contrast to the above approach, is it contemplated that national authorities
would be disposed to make a single blanket release (after the release for
initial use) that would authorize the NATC Military Authorities to use nuclear
weapons for tactical purposes on a selective basis as the military authorities
see fit?

7. If it is envisaged that follow-on use will be subject to a measure of control
through : successive selective releases by national authorities, must all
requests be considered on a separate, case-by-case basis?

8. If so, is there a risk that the capacity of the decision-maker to make timely
and effective judgements will be saturated and then exceeded as the number and
complexity of the requests grow?

9. Can the risk of overtaxing the capacity of the decision-maker be reduced by
treating follow-on requests on bases other than case-by-case consideration? In
what other ways can issues be presented to the decision-maker to facilitate the
reaching of timely and effective judgements on his part?

10. If national authorities may be required to address successive selective
release requests, should they deal with them in the order in which they arrived
– first received, first considered?

11. Would the inclusion in a request of the "time-limit within which a political
decision will be required" (vide para 105(g), DFC/D(65)58) provide a valid
indicator of relative priority? Of relative importance to the conduct of the
battle? If so, should national authorities deal with requests on the basis of
the time-limit indicator – shortest time for decision, first considered?

12. Are certain separate requests likely to be interdependent, e.g., a later one
reinforcing an earlier one; air defence use and battlefield use; b: field use on
adjacent areas? If so, how is this interdependence to be made clear to decision-
makers?
