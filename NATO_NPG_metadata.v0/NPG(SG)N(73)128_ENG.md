INTERNATIONAL DEFENSE DIGEST
USSR
MIRV-TESTING
The USSR has flight tested multiple independently targetable re-entry vehicle
(MIRV) warheads on 3 ICBMs during the course of the summer, and in early
September detonated the largest underground nuclear device so far detected by
the US.

The United States Atomic Energy Commission said the underground test took place
at Novaya Zemlya in the Arctic and it had a yield of three to six megatons. (The
largest US underground test to date was the 5-megaton Camkin burst at Amchitka
in the Aleutians in 1971 to validate the Spartan warhead design for the
Safeguard anti-ballistic missile system.)

Pentagon officials thought that the Novaya Zemlya test might involve a new
warhead for the six-MIRV version of the SS-9 Scarp heavy ballistic missile,
which carries the US intelligence designation SSX-18. In earlier disclosures
that the new version of the liquid-propellant Scarp would be able to carry at
least six MIRV warheads, Defense Secretary James Schlesinger said they would
have a yield "in the order of one megaton."

The Soviets have also tested MIRV warheads on the SSX-17, a new version of the
SS-11 Savage solid-propellant ICBM, and on a third missile designated the SSX-19
thought to be in competition with the four warhead SSX-17 to replace the
existing force of SS-11 missiles. No such tests have yet been observed on the
SSX-16, an outgrowth of the SS-13 solid-propellant ICBM, or on the Soviet
Union's new 4,000-mile SSN-8 submarine-launched ballistic missile, but Pentagon
officials see no reason why the Soviets will not extend the MIRV technique to
these weapons "in due course."

The Soviet MIRV tests, unlike some previous Russian advances, caused no surprise
in Washington. If anything, they came as something of an anti-climax, since the
Pentagon had been warning of an imminent Soviet MIRV breakthrough since the
first US MIRV was tested about five years ago. In the spring of this year,
however, the US did obtain definite evidence that the

Russians were on the verge of a true MIRV capability. US surveillance radar
tracked Soviet missiles in flight tests which exhibited the characteristics of
onboard computer commands, including the types of steering commands during
"cruise" which the "bus" must execute in order to place each warhead in its
separate trajectory.

There is relatively little concern over the MIRV capability for the SSX-17,
since that is a Minuteman-scaled weapon. The US Minuteman III accommodates three
MIRV warheads of about 200 kilotons in yield, and the combination of modest
yield and ample CEP (circular error probability) means that it cannot serve as a
first-strike or counter-force weapon. What does set off concern in Pentagon
strategic quarters is the prospect of half a dozen high-yield MIRVs aboard the
SSX-18, since the yield-accuracy combination of those warheads is thought most
likely to have counter-force capability against the 1,054 land-based underground
missiles the US is permitted under the SALT I interim agreement on offensive
weapons. This agreement permits the Soviet Union about 50% more land-based
missiles than the US in terms of numbers, but the Russian force has a "throw-
weight" about four times greater than the US missile force.

Current US intelligence estimates anticipate that the Russians will need at
least two years of development and flight testing before they can begin to
deploy operational MIRV warheads. It is also thought that the total number of
Soviet MIRVs operationally deployed on land- and submarine-based missiles will
not equal the US total until about 1979.

US RESPONSE
The Defense Department has countered the news of the first successful test of a
Soviet MIRV warhead with the suggestion that the United States is contemplating
a reversal of its historical policy against development of "hard target" nuclear
warheads.

At the press conference during which he announced that the Soviets have recently
begun flight testing of as many as six MIRVs on new range missiles, Secretary of
Defense James Schlesinger clearly implied that he favours development of
extremely accurate warheads able to destroy reinforced targets such as missile
silos and underground command posts.

Development of such weapons has traditionally been rejected by US defense
strategists for fear it would accelerate the arms race by suggesting that the US
was creating a first strike capability. But in the light of the new Soviet MIRV
successes, Schlesinger has apparently decided that a hard target capability is
necessary to maintain nuclear parity.

At the same time, defense strategists believe that hard target weapons could be
employed in a limited nuclear exchange and thus would avoid attacks on
population centers, which has been the core of the "assured destruction"
strategy the US has followed for the last decade but which has met with
disfavour in the Nixon Administration.

This emerging new nuclear strategy would basically parallel Soviet nuclear
philosophy and thus might eventually result in a situation where nuclear war
could be waged without destruction of civilian populations, but it is not
expected to slow the arms race. Schlesinger, in fact, has predicted that
prospects for arms control have now "deteriorated sadly."
