> # Articles Related to Nuclear Defence
> 
> This document is a notice from the NATO Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) dated January 8, 1974, distributing three articles on nuclear defense. The first article, 'Can NATO Fashion a New Strategy?', by R.C. Richardson, discusses the likelihood of United States force reductions in the NATO area and the need for a new, credible and cost-effective NATO strategy. Richardson argues that the current strategy of conventional, flexible response is no longer credible due to force levels. He proposes a set of boundary conditions for planning a new strategy, including weapons technology, public attitudes, and political trends, with a target implementation date around 1977. The other two articles included in the the document are 'The Realities of Tactical Nuclear Warfare' by James H. Polk and 'A Strategic Concept for the Defense of Europe' by Wynred Joshua. The notice emphasizes that the opinions expressed in the articles are those of the authors and not official national positions or endorsed by the International Staff.

CAN NATO FASHION A NEW STRATEGY?
by R.C. Richardson
SOME U.S. force reductions in the NATO area seem likely within the next few
years. This is not to argue that they are desirable, only to recognize that the
signs point in this direction. The shift in priorities from defense to domestic
programs, the unfavorable U.S. trade balance, the détente environment and
related force reduction conferences, and the growing public feeling that by now
Europe should be able to defend itself will continue to build up pressures for a
U.S. withdrawal. The question is less "if" than "when."

The present NATO strategy is one of conventional, flexible response. The force
levels required to carry it out are at, or below, the level of credibility for
this particular strategy. Some claim that so long as these levels are
maintained, our NATO commands could do a credible job of defending against most
plausible threats. No one claims this to be possible with even less force than
is now available. To do so would be to admit that NATO had too many troops for
its present strategy – and all responsible NATO leaders argue the contrary.

If a force reduction is inevitable, for whatever reason, what happens to the
security of Europe? No one proposes to dismantle NATO or abandon any pretense at
maintaining as effective defenses as practical, at whatever force levels the
member nations agree to support. This being the case, the problem is to devise
and "sell" a new NATO strategy that can be credible and effective at a lower
level of force and cost. Is this feasible?

Some may argue that it may be possible to provide an adequate defense for Europe
with fewer forces, and at less cost, this would have been done long ago. This is
not necessarily so. Different strategies carry different price tags and force
levels. In the strategic arena, massive retaliation is cheaper than
counterforce. It requires far less sophisticated weapon systems to carry out. In
the early 1950’s NATO turned to a strategy emphasizing tactical nuclear weapons
when it became apparent that the force requirements for a purely conventional
defense of Europe could not be met.

The choice of strategy can affect both cost and force requirements in any plan
or area, but these are not the only considerations in determining what the
strategy should be. Political acceptability, past military experience, arms
control considerations – such as peaceful space or nonprotection – and vested
interests, all have an impact on the selection process. Technology also plays a
key role, for new weapons are what really make new strategies possible.

There was a time when soldiers decided the strategy required to accomplish the
goals set by political leaders, the nation provided the means – or if it was
unable to do so, the goals were changed. Since the advent of nuclear arms,
however, everyone "gets into the act." Disarmers, amateurs, politicians, social
scientists and public relations "buffs" all want a voice in devising our
military strategy. Whether or not this is justified, it certainly complicates
the selection process.

Any change in strategy is a traumatic experience. This is particularly so in
peacetime when we must overcome the old adage that "the military start each war
with the strategies found successful in the last one." The "system" inherently
resists change, and the more interests there are involved, the harder it is to
reach agreement. The easiest solution is to rationalize the status quo, which is
what we have been doing in NATO for some years. The question today – when change
is sorely needed – is: Are there any alternatives a majority could rally around?

In this article we are concerned only with NATO strategy. The problem is to
determine if a new and acceptable strategy can be introduced, taking into
account real-world trends in: (1) new weapons, (2) manpower and weapon costs,
(3) economic and political pressures to reduce U.S. forces overseas, (4) détente
and related, current or planned, East-West negotiations, (5) the implications of
the Nixon Doctrine, and (6) the multilateral power balance evolving from the
Sino-U.S. rapprochement, the expansion of the European Community, and the growth
of Japanese power and self-sufficiency.

A change in NATO strategy is dictated by one or more of the above trends, and it
must come to pass in the 1970’s; it is already overdue. Unfortunately, the
strategic alternatives theoretically available far exceed those that could be
implemented in practice. The difference between what might be done and what can
be done results from the real world of politics, public attitudes, inertia and
other factors severely limiting the extent to which a new strategy can be "sold"
to NATO governments – particularly in the near term.

By first establishing the boundaries of probable acceptability, we can prevent
wasted effort and make the acceptance of proposals more likely. These boundaries
are not fixed except at a specific time. They widen as time passes because the
farther one goes into the future, the greater the options become; time is a key
factor in creating conditions favorable to change.

If no alternatives can be found that satisfy both the current pressures for cost

will know that prospects for the survival of an effective NATO alliance during
the next decade are poor to nil. We should then consider unilateral U.S.
alternatives.

what we could build and field by 1977. Our nuclear-weapons options have been

Any NATO (or U.S.) strategy that is to be effective in 1977 must be based on

There can be no others if our availability dates are valid. These are hard
boundaries and generally represent the outside limits, because the assumed

A summary analysis of weapons availability can be readily made. It should lead
to conclusions along the following lines, though these are only illustrative:

(1) Real-time, space-based observation and reporting of exposed items, including
men, can be envisioned under clear-weather daylight conditions.

(2) Tactical nuclear weapons with negligible fallout or contamination side
effects and with yields of from 50 tons to 55 kilotons can be envisioned.

(3) Precision guided missiles and rockets, now available, will be improved in
reliability, accuracy and diversity of types.

(4) Laser-kill weapon systems will not be sufficiently developed to have a major
impact on strategy or force requirements within this time frame.

These forecasts may be considered quite accurate due to the long lead time in
new weapon developments.

ECONOMIC AND COST BOUNDARIES

Next to technology and hardware, budget considerations establish the most rigid
boundaries. If we assume that no dramatic international events, such as a Sino-
Soviet war, will occur unexpectedly, the limits on defense spending by NATO
governments for the next few years can be fairly accurately forecast.

Except as required by inflation, NATO nations will probably budget no more
annually for defense throughout the 1970’s than they did in 1972. Thus, we can
reasonably assume that they will not increase the percentage of GNP spent on
defense beyond that spent in 1972.

All NATO nations suffer from inflation and feel steadily rising pressures for
increased domestic spending. Most are at or near peacetime limits of taxation.
The international atmosphere, exemplified by SALT, MBFR, CSCE and expanding
East-West trade, is one of detente. When we add to this the rationale that the
China threat limits the Soviet Union’s flexibility to deploy its full

The only indicators that may potentially argue for significant increases in NATO
defense budgets are a growth of Soviet strategic power and a concurrent waning

major U.S. force reduction would also argue for a European increase.

The factors arguing for spending, however, are far less tangible than those
arguing against it. For this reason, our planned economic boundaries can hardly
allow for larger defense allocations than those that would be realized if all
NATO nations were to budget annually the same percentage of their GNP for

calling for spending in excess of these totals will be politically unacceptable.
We can eventually refine our economic boundaries by breaking down NATO budgets
among manpower, operations and maintenance, and procurement. Minimum levels of
flexibility to shift funding among these accounts on a nation-by-nation basis
could be readily identified. Such boundaries could further restrict the scope
and nature of proposed strategy changes, but this degree of detail is not
considered necessary in a first effort. If we accept the overall boundaries
established by using a percentage of GNP, we can conclude:

(1) The forces and major weapon systems now deployed in NATO are not likely to
be increased significantly.

(2) Because the current rate of rise in manpower costs and replacement systems
(aircraft, ships, tanks) exceeds the average nation's GNP growth rate, present
levels of manpower and major weapon systems in NATO cannot be maintained
indefinitely within our assumed budget boundary.

(3) Any introduction of new weapons will require offsetting quantitative
reductions in force requirements or in existing systems, and the higher the unit
cost of new weapons, the greater the offsetting reductions. The same is true in
modernizing the existing force with more costly equipment.

These conclusions suggest that any new strategy whose cost is to be acceptable
must do the job credibly with fewer forces or weapon systems, or both.

Political Boundaries
This section deals with purely political limits to acceptable strategies, as
differentiated from those reflecting public attitudes, economics, collateral
effects or escalation. These latter boundaries, though in essence political, are
treated later. The principal U.S. political boundaries relate to:

(1) The extent to which the Nixon Doctrine can counteract U.S. assumption of
responsibility for overall, initial, partial, nuclear or other defenses for
other nations. The boundary here could well be that no increased American
responsibilities could be realistically proposed. The goal is to decrease U.S.
responsibilities while still honoring past commitments. Thus, no new strategy
will be politically acceptable if it calls for additional U.S. commitments.

(2) The priority currently given to détente and related SALT, MBFR and other
arms control efforts. The boundary here appears to be that no strategy requiring
a major trimming of these efforts will be politically acceptable.

(3) Political unacceptability of any substantial increases in U.S. peacetime
overseas bases or forces, regardless of economic acceptability. Another boundary
should probably be set on any plan calling for a significant U.S. presence in
areas where it does not now exist.

The above three boundaries will probably remain fixed at least until 1976, the
minimum period over which the present Republican administration will confront a
Democratic Congress. Until then, the known views of such men as Senators
Fulbright, Mansfield and Kennedy cannot be ignored.

The European political boundaries for the acceptability of new
NATO strategies are more diverse and less clear.
(1) France now has little say at the military level, so her
political views are pertinent only if the strategy demands her
collaboration. France’s attitude could affect ultimate political
acceptability, however, through North Atlantic Council deliber-
ations and her European Community ties. This boundary may best
be described as a need to avoid antagonizing the French
politically, and thus appears to be more a criterion for selecting
options than a boundary on options.
(2) There are clear boundaries for what is politically acceptable
to Germany today, but the situation in 1977 is hard to forecast.
The most likely development is that Germany will move farther
to the left, increase her East-West ties, adopt a more nationalistic
foreign policy, and voice stronger demands for defense funds. If
this trend materializes, any NATO strategy that is judged to be
more “provocative” or to impede Ostpolitik will be politically
unacceptable. The political boundary could be any new posture
deemed more “overly aggressive” than the present one.
(3) Boundaries to political acceptability in Great Britain ap-
pear to be less restrictive. Those in Scandinavia relate mainly to
the presence of nuclear weapons and the participation of Spain,
and these are treated later under public attitude boundaries. No
political constraints on the acceptability of otherwise sound plans
supported by a NATO majority seem to exist in the southern
flank countries. The boundaries there will be more logistical and
economic than political.

Security Goals

Security goals fall into two categories according to their
association with deterrence or with defense. Since the
requirements for effective deterrence are not necessarily the same
as those for effective defense, they must be dealt with separately.

There is a great deal of confusion over the contribution of
deterrence and its association with “war-fighting capability.” Let
us consider for a moment that deterrence is a function of how an
adversary assesses the military potential of your force and your
willingness to use that force. It is not difficult to apply this
definition to a conventional or a tactical nuclear force. For either
force, one links military potential with war-fighting capability. If
you assume that one is willing to commit his tactical nuclear force,
the close association of deterrence with war-fighting capability is
understandable. At present, the American President would have
great difficulty in releasing the NATO tactical nuclear force;
hence, it serves little use, i.e., it has little or no deterrent value.
The situation is less clear with regard to strategic forces. The
military potential of the U.S. or Soviet strategic force can be placed

zero, its deterrent effect can be great, or at least undetermined. Not only is
its military potential enormous, but it has an impersonal and unreal character —
vastly removed from the concrete features that define a conventional or tactical
nuclear force. For these reasons, one hardly associates war-fighting capability
with strategic forces.

Since arguments on deterrence versus war-fighting capability in NATO are almost
always confused by definitions of theater, strategic and tactical forces, it is
understandable why a close association was seldom made and, indeed, why the two
subjects were treated separately. They also need to be treated separately from
the viewpoint of boundary definition.

The primary NATO political objective is to deter aggression. Any NATO strategy
that is to be acceptable must contribute to, or independently satisfy, this
goal.

A strategy and force posture that can clearly defeat any attempt at aggression
is a deterrent. However, a strategy and force posture that cannot possibly
contain aggression, and yet promises inevitable retribution to an extent making
the overall cost of aggression unacceptable, is also a deterrent. We thus start
with two extremes: (1) a perfect defense and (2) unacceptable retaliation in
lieu of defense. Both will deter. In theory, if deterrence is the only goal,
either extreme should be acceptable.

A perfect defense is impossible. Though cost and other factors may be under our
control, we can never be certain of the scope and nature of the threat. However,
retaliatory capabilities that are unacceptable to the enemy are possible. The
problem is that use of such capabilities in other than extreme situations is not
credible. Any strategy and associated force posture will thus be a compromise.

Are there identifiable boundaries based on defense policy and conceptual (not
economic or technological) grounds that should limit the extent to which we (1)
seek a perfect defense or (2) rely on retaliation (punitive offensive capacity)
to deter?

If an effective forward defense cannot be provided and enemy military
superiority is inevitable under some conditions of aggression in certain areas,
there is no deterrent to such aggression except the promise of retaliation. To
date, the principal retaliatory deterrents have been the U.S. strategic forces
and, to a lesser degree, French and British strategic forces and SHAPE tactical
nuclear forces. As stated before, low credibility reduces the value of
retaliatory deterrents. Growth of Soviet retaliatory capability has steadily
diminished the credible-use scenarios for allied strategic forces. At present,
strategic retaliation (as opposed to possible defensive assistance) for anything
short of an overall attack on Western Europe is just not credible.

only in extremes and, thus, would no longer be a deterrent to local aggression
in Europe. The deterrence we provide must come from a combination of local
defense capabilities and the aggressor's concern that, should these prove
inadequate, we would resort to tactical nuclear firepower that could lead to
escalation levels beyond those he could accept.

A seldom-mentioned key consideration is that no aggressor wants to provoke
escalation beyond his pre-planned limits, for under this circumstance the
initiative is surrendered to the defender, who chooses the time, place and
weapons.

If strategic retaliation is not available to respond to local aggression in
Europe and any tactical weapons used are limited to military targets in combat
zones, there is no rational nuclear deterrent to local aggression so far as fear
of unacceptable losses is concerned; instead, we return to relative defense
effectiveness and war-fighting capabilities. Retaliatory deterrence is not a
factor in this situation; it can only be a factor when losses to the aggressor —
not directly related to battlefield successes — are unacceptable.

This rationale needs to be developed further, but it strongly suggests that
there is no purely deterrent role for theater tactical weapons, only a backup
war-fighting role. Their deterrent value would lie in either (1) the combined
conventional and tactical nuclear capacity to defeat aggression, as assessed by
the aggressor, or (2) the aggressor’s conviction that, rather than accept local
defeat, we would use our tactical nuclear weapons and the conflict would reach
levels unacceptably out of proportion to his local objectives.

One point that should be emphasized is that conventional and nuclear wars differ
with regard to the acceptability of escalation. In conventional wars, escalation
relates primarily to force buildups, deployment capabilities, and industrial and
manpower mobilization. It is a form of wartime arms race that is normal to
attrition-type conflict. In nuclear wars, the maximum means of destruction are
on hand from the onset. There is no race to build, deploy and commit these
means, only a decision to use them. Logic thus dictates that an aggressor start
at the maximum level of intensity he considers allowable by both sides for the
particular situation. To do less would be foolhardy, as it would invite the
defense to pre-empt escalation without exceeding the allowable limits.

To give a simple example, a potential aggressor who believes we will go to total
war rather than permit him to achieve a limited objective should start at the
total-war level. If he believes we would use tactical nuclear weapons, he should
start out by using them. The threat of escalation could be a deterrent only if
the aggressor had misjudged our willingness to escalate in the given situation.

Tactical nuclear capabilities thus deter only to the extent that they actually
improve war-fighting capabilities. The only way they might deter when
ineffective for defense would be if they were

targeted on countervalue targets whose loss could not be justified by the
aggressor's success in achieving his local objectives. This form of retaliation
is essentially the rationale for the French force de frappe and operates on the
assumption that the Soviets would not trade Moscow and Leningrad, for example,
to achieve the conquest of Paris, while the United States stood by uninvolved
and undamaged.

The boundary condition that develops from this rationale is that, if war-
fighting defense is inferior, nuclear attack can be deterred only if we manifest
the will and capability to commit theater nuclear forces to countervalue
targets. If we do this, we should be prepared to start with strategic forces for
maximum effect, so as not to surrender the strategic initiative; or else we
should be sufficiently decoupled from participation in the war to feel secure
that subsequent Soviet retaliation would be limited to theater forces and
targets.

Since both the above assumptions are unrealistic, as long as the United States
is directly involved in European defense from the onset, a tactical nuclear
countervalue strategy would seem politically unacceptable even as a deterrent.
This is a boundary that limits our tactical nuclear strategies to those that can
make an active defense truly effective and thus provide deterrence.

A strategy is designed to achieve some given objective. Suppose we rule out the
possibility of a local deterrent to local aggression in Europe because it would
unrealistically require an attack on Soviet countervalue targets without
triggering Soviet strategic retaliation. NATO strategy would then have to
provide for deterrence through obviously effective local defenses, whether
conventional or nuclear. Our weapons and forces would have to be designed for
war-fighting, with deterrence a by-product, not a separate or "sizing"
objective. This is a boundary.

Any concept of theater weapons or forces designed solely to deter aggression
against NATO Europe can make sense only if the European Community’s defense and
war-fighting posture is totally separate from that of the United States. This is
a third-power concept wherein the United States is sufficiently decoupled, both
politically and militarily, to be isolated from unwilling involvement regardless
of targets selected or weapons used by either side.

To summarize: (1) A tactical nuclear strategy that fails to provide an effective
defense is not a credible deterrent to either local aggression or subsequent
escalation.

(2) A strategy that surrenders a large chunk of the territory of any member
nation as a prerequisite to implementation will not be politically acceptable to
the nation concerned. A strategy that accepts destruction of most of the values
in a member nation as the price of its success is also unacceptable.

(3) A strategy that relies solely on European strategic or tactical retaliation
either to deter or defeat aggression will be unacceptable to the United States
as long as U.S. forces are directly involved in any conflict in Europe.

RATE- AND DEGREE-OF-CHANGE BOUNDARIES
The extent of change that can be introduced in large organizations is limited.
Changes in concepts, strategies and doctrines are more difficult to achieve than
changes in hardware.

Individuals and organizations tend to welcome changes in hardware which improve
capabilities, but they tend to resist major changes in the way they learned to
use their hardware (changes that affect such areas as doctrines, force
deployments, employments, tactics and strategies) for the following reasons:

Experience of Leadership. Most leaders exploit the methods and techniques that
earned them their leadership roles. New ways of doing things often destroy the
advantage they have held over subordinates, because their expertise acquired
through experience no longer counts.

Efficiency and Economy. In large organizations like national defense
establishments or alliances, efficiency and economy depend heavily on pre-
planning and on everyone adhering to established rules, regulations and
procedures, all of which have to be revised when major changes in methodology
occur. Changes that threaten the routine, day-to-day operations are resisted by
those who champion, and are responsible for, efficiency and economy.

Management by Committee. Neither Free World alliances nor national defense
establishments are ruled by dictators. Matters such as roles, missions, forces
and distribution of service and national responsibilities are decided mostly by
compromises and joint agreements arrived at within committees, councils and
similar bodies. All these bodies will resist changes that tend to upset past
agreements. Resistance will be particularly strong from those members who will
lose out in the process of change.

Absence of Battle Testing. The military resists changes that have not been
proved in war. Historically, most changes involving doctrine or tactics have
been introduced and tested in conflict. If they led to success, they were
adopted, taught as the new method and used at the start of the next war. Changes
that have not been war-tested are inherently resisted, particularly those that
cannot be realistically war-gamed in peacetime (e.g., tactical-nuclear-weapon-
related changes). At best, the changes are accepted on the condition that the
capability to fall back on tested methods be retained. This last requirement
results in dual capabilities and defeats the potential benefits to be gained
from introducing changes.

conditions are ripe today for acceptance of a new NATO strategy, any proposal
will be resisted for one or more of the above reasons. Any new strategy proposed
should thus permit as gradual a changeover as possible from current concepts.
Radical changes are not likely to be accepted, no matter how sound they are in
theory. The digestive capability of the "system" is a boundary condition in

be a goal in any strategy.

Collateral-Damage and Other Effects Boundaries
Collateral damage has always been a major inhibiting factor in the acceptance
and use of tactical nuclear weapons (TNW's) in the NATO area. The problem stems
partly from the heavy population of prospective battlefields in Europe and the
historic and social value of urban areas close to the most likely targets. The
solution lies in selecting weapons whose characteristics minimize undesirable
collateral damage.

There is a slight conflict of requirements in seeking minimum collateral damage
while at the same time ensuring the benefits of economy of force and the
advantages for defense inherent in a

TNW strategy. The TNW's characteristics that contribute most to eliminating
relative mass as a key factor are their potential for wide-area destruction or
kill. This capability allows a trade-off between accurate knowledge of target
location and the weapon size required for effective destruction with a single
sortie.

As capabilities for battlefield surveillance and for target location and
identification increase, the exploitation of the TNW's area effects along with
the associated collateral damage can be decreased. Thus, the collateral-damage
problem need not be solved by new weapon technology alone.

The potential for collateral damage with current stockpile characteristics
appears to far exceed the area effects needed for a successful TNW strategy. A
first step should be to correct these characteristics, but there are limits on
how far the area kill capabilities of TNW's can be reduced without defeating
their very purpose.

If all targets could always be pinpointed and hit directly with conventional
weapons, the only need for TNW would be to destroy those targets that could not
be destroyed with a direct hit by the largest nonnuclear weapons. Under these
conditions, the cost-effectiveness of conventional firepower would be so great
that an effective defense with minimum manpower could almost be realized without
TNW's.

The relationship between target identification, delivery accuracy and the need
for TNW's, with associated collateral-damage probabilities, is crucial in
development planning. The relationship suggests that, as battlefield
surveillance techniques and delivery accuracies improve, the absolute
requirement for TNW's should decrease, and so should the need for area-effect
(hence, essentially "dirty") characteristics in those TNW's that are still
required.

In an extreme and purely theoretical situation, defensive forces could be
reduced to near zero if aggression on land were to be prevented by interposing a
scorched-earth, total-destruction, near-impassable radiation belt or barrier.
Collateral damage would be total within and adjacent to the barrier area. In a
situation closer to the other extreme, where defensive firepower is selective
and is no more effective than that of the aggressor, relative force levels will
determine the outcome.

The major goal in developing a new NATO strategy is to provide a credible
defense and a deterrent to aggression at a force and weapon level the NATO
countries are willing to provide today. Since the acceptable level appears to be
well below that needed to meet the threat, our firepower effectiveness must
compensate for the lower force level. Until we can devise better target
identification and delivery systems, however, this effectiveness can be provided
only at the expense of some collateral damage.

MILITARY-ACCEPTABILITY BOUNDARY
Military acceptability of any strategy depends on several considerations,
principal among which, in probable order of priority, are:

(1) Impact on service roles, missions and budgets
(2) Extent of "changes" involved
(3) General opinion as to potential effectiveness
(4) Extent of testing in war or ability to be simulated realistically in
exercises

Few changes will satisfy all these considerations, and any major violation will
normally generate sufficient military resistance to prevent the change from
being introduced.

Service roles and missions have been argued since before World War II, and
present assignments are the result of years of debate, competition and
compromise. Since strategy can seldom be changed without some impact on one or
more of the services, each change will be opposed by those who consider it
detrimental to their future budgets, force levels or responsibilities.
Conversely, those who benefit from the change will support it. This unfortunate
situation suggests that changes in strategy must not drastically alter the
responsibilities of more than one of the three military services.

Within certain limits, military views on a new strategy can be overruled by
political considerations. The military as a whole never considered the NATO
strategy of flexible-conventional response to be adequate or effective at the
force levels provided in the 1960's. This opinion was made evident by extensive
allied resistance and some U.S. resistance to its adoption between 1961 and
1966, and by constant military demands for more forces to make the strategy
credible and effective once it had been imposed by U.S. political pressures.

However, while a low military opinion of strategies imposed by political
considerations may not guarantee their rejection, a

political merit.

Boundaries imposed by lack of wartime experience can be quite restrictive.
Changes based on new approaches applied successfully in wars fought by others

The net effect of resisting strategy and doctrinal change while accepting new
and improved weapon systems is that a new war will commence with a period of
confusion while everyone improvises to bring old tactics and doctrines into
phase with the new weapons. This circumstance in itself is a major contributor
to deterrence.

Boundaries to military acceptability are reasonably clear in the case of
strategies whose success depends on weapons untested in past wars, such as
TNW's. They occur at the point where it is proposed to introduce these weapons
and to rely on them, as contrasted with merely adding them to an existing and
proven capability.

ESCALATION AND CONTROL BOUNDARY CONSIDERATIONS
The possibility of escalation in any limited conflict has been a concern to NATO
governments ever since the advent of nuclear weapons. The escalation potential
of any NATO strategy can thus affect its acceptability.

The optimum strategy for avoiding escalation would be for each side to allow
maximum time and warning between each major increase in commitment of forces or
new weapons. The least acceptable strategy would be one that automatically
expands the scope and nature of the conflict with no advance warning and no
opportunity to consult at the political level.

Increased force and weapon commitments can best contribute to military success
when they exploit the advantage of surprise and occur as soon as possible after
local commanders recognize the need. This military fact conflicts with the
political desire to defer action and consult before authorizing increased
commitments. A compromise is clearly required between military requirements and
political considerations.

A general misconception exists as to what factors control escalation in limited-
war situations. A superficial view acquired by some during the Viet Nam war is
that, if defensive military action threatens not only to contain but to defeat a
communist aggressor, he will never accept that defeat and will inevitably
escalate, even to general war. An example of that attitude was the often-stated

This view is not logical, nor can it be tolerated as a boundary to defensive and
counteroffensive military operations. To do so would be to admit the
ineffectiveness of any deterrent to aggression, because it would leave the
aggressor in an assured "no-loss" situation. It would inevitably lead to losses
by the defender, since successful containment, even where possible, would
invariably be at the expense of some loss of territory or sovereignty. Also, it
usually results in a breakage of morale and loss of the "will" to win on the
part of the defenders. Such results have been well documented during the Viet
Nam experience; one hopes that some lessons were learned.

The truth is that neither side in a conflict will escalate above the level where
the consequences of escalation are likely to exceed the potential gains. This
level establishes a relatively automatic ceiling on the extremes either side
will go to before seeking victory or accepting defeat.

Escalation ceilings are normally established by the evaluation by both sides of
the aggressor's original objective. The assumption, for instance, that the
Soviet Union, having tried militarily for control of Berlin access routes, would
risk the destruction of Moscow rather than back down is ridiculous; yet such
beliefs are seriously argued in some circles. That assumption is no more
credible than one that would have the United States launch a strategic
retaliatory attack on the USSR in response to any minor border incursion in the
NATO area. Yet the same voices that discredit such a massive retaliation
strategy on our part support the notion that our enemies will initiate massive
escalation rather than accept defeat of their incursions.

A strategy that allows no possibility of either containing or defeating, and
hence punishing, the aggressor has little military and no deterrent value. This
strategy is not an acceptable boundary even though some advocate it.

A strategy that permits automatic escalation of any conflict and accepts failure
only after total defeat in global war is equally unacceptable. Aggression that
is less than total from the aggressor implies an objective short of total world
domination. The relative worth of the objective must be the factor that
establishes the ceiling on force commitments. The problem is to ensure that our
strategy and the procedures for implementing it will not permit this ceilinging
to be inadvertently or automatically violated. This is the point at which there
must be provisions for deliberate decision-making.

Conclusions
Theorists, planners and politicians with differing views on what should be done
about NATO are plentiful. Library shelves are filled with books on NATO strategy
and the U.S. role. Hardly a week goes by without an editorial advocating some
change in American force levels in Europe or in other transatlantic politico-
military relationships.

This is not to belittle the value of studies and analyses of NATO. Taken as a
whole, they help to shape public opinion, to define the options, both
theoretical and real, and to tabulate the advantages and disadvantages of each
option. They highlight the fallacies in the existing order of things. But rarely
can substantive change be traced to any single study or analysis.

Major change is brought about only by deliberate and concerted decisions on the
part of responsible national authorities. In accordance with a natural law of
bureaucracy, authorities resist the solutions offered by outsiders. They are the
ones most directly constrained by the boundary conditions discussed above.

If we consider the recent official pronouncements of the Nixon Administration
with regard to NATO, and the boundary conditions that have been outlined, we can
safely draw the following salient conclusions:

There will be no major change in NATO strategy in the near term. Our leaders
have made clear that they are opposed to a force reduction in Europe except in
the context of mutually agreed reductions. A principal argument for changing
strategy is to accommodate such a force reduction. Therefore, to propose such a
change officially at this time would be to anticipate, or admit to, the
desirability of a reduction, and this will not be done unless Congress
translates the Mansfield Resolution into force cutbacks.

The loss of U.S. strategic superiority in recent years has led some observers to
advocate formally decoupling U.S. strategic forces from the defense of NATO
Europe. To divorce the NATO sword from the shield, on the grounds that the use
of U.S. strategic force to succor Europe is no longer credible, might sound
logical in theory, but it is not logical in the real world. There can be no

realistic defense or deterrence in Europe without balanced sword and shield

capabilities, the United States has no alternative, in its own self-interest, to

Finally, the "system" can only absorb change slowly, even when change is sought

evolution and osmosis. The "system" will resist any revolution in NATO strategy
or force posture, particularly when proposed unilaterally. If one adds to this
the new element of mutual change in force levels inherent in present U.S.-Soviet
disarmament discussions, and the primacy of domestic problems in most NATO
member countries, one can surely forget about drastic alterations in the near
term. Nothing short of full-blown crisis or war will bring these about.

The purpose of this writer is not to be either cynical or negative with respect
to NATO strategy. It is only to point out from long years of experience with
politico-military planning that more progress can be made in the right direction
by advocating small evolutionary steps — so as over time to bring about
necessary changes in the existing system — than by arguing for drastic,
immediate changes. Today's strategy is far from the optimum, as are the force
levels, but these have deterred aggression for over twenty years. This is the
reason for NATO. If these force levels can be maintained and progressively
improved, it is hard to see how we can do much better. It will be interesting to
see whether the alliance review, now formally taking place under the auspices of
NATO Secretary General Joseph Luns, will challenge this conclusion.

THE REALITIES OF TACTICAL NUCLEAR WARFARE
by James H. Polk
It is an almost universally accepted fact that any use of tactical nuclear
weapons will lead inevitably up the destructive escalation ladder to large-scale
thermonuclear war. Alain Enthoven can take credit for this distortion of
reality, for it was in his carefully prepared speech of February 1963, delivered
at Seattle University, that the notion of the "nuclear fire break" was first
advanced. The talk was planned to explain the official military and intellectual
basis of the Kennedy defense policy for the decade of the 1960's. It was a well
reasoned and important speech and the "fire break" portion was limited to one
small paragraph, yet this notion persists some ten years later. Whatever one's
personal views of the matter, there is no question but that the inevitable
escalation theory, like the "Guns of August" syndrome, has had a
disproportionate effect on intellectual thought and national policy. It has
somehow become an article of faith that once general mobilization begins, or
once the nuclear threshold is crossed in any degree, the march of events must
move irreversibly toward Armageddon and total disaster.

The Soviet Union has adopted this same idea, endorsed it on numerous occasions,
and even made it a primary declaratory policy for the 1970's. This is not
surprising, since the obvious conventional force superiority the Soviet bloc
enjoys in Central Europe contrasts with NATO's distinct advantage in small-yield
battlefield warheads; such a declaratory policy has the clear aim of deterring
the Western allies' first use of low-yield nuclear artillery or demolition
munitions. Thus, in the one area of nuclear technology where we have a clear-cut
advantage -- the one, incidentally, that in no way threatens Soviet Russia -- we
are largely deterred from recourse to the lower end of the nuclear spectrum.
This mental block, which is so evident in current literature on the subject, is
also displayed in the high councils of NATO, to such an extent that rational
discussion of possible employment options is impeded.

Practically all of the academicians and most of the governmental hierarchy who
address the problem of tactical nuclear war think almost exclusively in terms of
deterrence and thrust aside the warfighting role as distasteful or unthinkable.
The usual American design for the future is to place reliance in a Europe-based,
strong but somehow nonthreatening countervalue force that deters tactical
nuclear war on NATO's part, just as our triad of strategic forces deters
thermonuclear war. On the other hand, the European view, not often openly

now and an independent nuclear-armed FRG would terrify them.

From another angle, our own military services, and particularly the U.S. Army,
have been attempting for years to provide guidance to their leaders on the
conduct of the limited nuclear battle, and to this end they have sponsored a
series of studies, war games and exercises. In August 1971 the Army published an
unclassified field manual entitled FM 100-30 (Test) Tactical Nuclear Operations
that provides tentative doctrine for senior military commanders. It is a good
general distillation of what the Army knows or thinks it knows about limited
nuclear warfare and it has a conservative and cautious approach, just as one
would expect. The manual is devoted almost totally to nuclear war-fighting,
including the roles of combat arms and combat support, but generally excluding
consideration of the roles of the other services. Any deterrent value, either
real or implied, of Army dual-capable or single-purpose nuclear delivery systems
or forces is not mentioned, but at least the requirement for and importance of
mutual restraint and observed limits on actions of the beligerents is stressed.
Two primary restraints are recognized: (1) the requirement for limiting the
employment of nuclear weapons to military targets while minimizing civilian
casualties, and (2) the need to restrict yields, numbers of weapons and types of
burst in any operation that one hopes to limited. Unfortunately, neither the
Army's nor the other services' weapons are optimized for these highly
commendable aims.

It appears that all these tentative proposals and doctrines for guidance of both
high governmental authorities and senior military leaders manage to miss the
mark in some respect. Certainly there is a "nuclear fire break," but with the
many yields and options available this benchmark is far more psychological than
actual. Deterrence and war-fighting are the dual and equally critical elements
required to maintain NATO as a viable entity in the decade ahead, and most of
the well-informed scientists and leaders agree that the NATO warhead stockpile
needs refinement and updating. Moreover, perhaps we should plan for an
independent West European stockpile and delivery force to be active, possibly in
the next decade. But as we look ahead, we should understand how deterrence and
war-fighting operate together, how deterrence is possible at any point in the
spectrum of nuclear combat, and in what areas both our policies and our
capabilities needed to support these critical concepts are to some extent
deficient.

A STRATEGIC CONCEPT FOR THE DEFENSE OF EUROPE

by Wynfred Joshua

T HE key problem of the 1970's for America's policy toward Western Europe
remains the same as that of the 1960's: how to ensure a credible deterrence and
defense posture for the Western alliance. But the strategic and political
context has changed and the answer in this decade is likely to differ
considerably from the one attempted some ten or twelve years ago.

The 1960's brought to Washington a group of policymakers determined to reduce
NATO's dependence on nuclear weapons and emphasize instead the conventional
dimension of the Western posture. As a result of the search for a more credible
defense strategy against a limited attack than the massive retaliation provided,
the strategic concept of the 1960's called for a largely conventional defense
and for postponing resort to the use of nuclear weapons, including tactical
nuclear weapons, as long as possible. A consequence of the high nuclear
threshold was the need to keep the nuclear weapons available to the alliance
under central American control. Carried to its logical conclusion, the flexible
response concept of the 1960's also required the United States to oppose the
development of national nuclear forces by its allies.

American efforts to put the nuclear genie back into the bottle reflected an
understandable concern over Soviet progress in nuclear weapons. The prospects of
a Soviet strategic nuclear capability meant that the United States would be
vulnerable to a nuclear strike in the event of an armed conflict with the Soviet
Union. In the eyes of West Europeans, however, the changing U.S. strategy seemed
to place increasing stress on containing a conflict in Europe, if deterrence
should fail. The Kennedy-Johnson approach eventually resulted in 1967 in NATO's
reluctant endorsement of the flexible response concept, but precipitated
France's withdrawal from the integrated NATO commands and left the alliance in a
protracted crisis that has weakened the sinews of transatlantic cohesion.

THE NEED FOR FLEXIBILITY AND UNCERTAINTY
Central to the West's strategic predilections is the desire for deterrence. The
specter of another devastating conflict sweeping across the continent continues
to haunt our European allies. They have no desire to fight a war, long or short.
Their concern is to make deterrence as strong as possible, and, if deterrence
should fail, to force the USSR to terminate its aggression on acceptable terms
in the shortest time and with as little damage to Western Europe as possible.

Deterrence brackets political and military dimensions. The goal is to convince
the opponent that the United States and its allies have the military capability,
the will to use this capability, and an effective strategy which, if deterrence
broke down, would make it impossible for him to calculate in advance how the
West would respond to his contemplated aggression. The objective would be to
convince the Soviet Union that any attack would entail unacceptable risk. This
means that the Western allies should have available a wide array of options
permitting them to respond at any level to a Soviet attack and thus induce
Moscow to halt its aggression and withdraw. It also means that the specific
option to be used by the Western allies should not be determined in advance, but
should remain uncertain. The desideratum is flexibility for the allies and
uncertainty for their Warsaw Pact opponents. A viable strategic concept should
be based on these fundamental principles of flexibility and uncertainty.

The Role of Tactical Nuclear and Conventional Forces

The threat posed by the U.S. strategic forces and the French and British nuclear
forces serves to deter Soviet aggression, and, if deterrence fails, to preclude
escalation to nuclear war on the theater level. Contributing to deterrence
requirements are the American nuclear systems located in Europe that can reach
the Soviet homeland. Currently, U.S. forward based systems (FBS), notably long-
range nuclear-armed aircraft, fulfill this role in the chain of deterrence. For
the European allies, these aircraft present visible evidence of the U.S.
commitment to defend Western Europe. They are the only U.S. nuclear systems in
the European theater that can hit Soviet targets. This does not necessarily mean
that, if deterrence failed, the European allies would wish to strike the Soviet
Union immediately; rather, it means that they value the political utility of
having a visible system with this capability. For these reasons the quick-
reaction alert (QRA) aircraft contribute to the cohesion of the alliance. The
widely reported Soviet efforts to include the U.S. forward based systems in SALT
I agreements were viewed with great apprehension in allied capitals as another
attempt to remove the U.S. nuclear umbrella from the continent.

These aircraft are, however, vulnerable to Soviet pre-emptive attack; their
ability to break through Warsaw Pact air defenses is limited; and the cost of
the planes and their maintenance is relatively high. If some of the current
aircraft were one day replaced by more suitable systems, such as missiles, the
allies would have to be carefully prepared for this step and convinced that it
would not presage a U.S. strategic decoupling from Europe. Any substitute
systems, moreover, should meet allied demands by being visible on the continent
and having a range sufficient to cover targets in the USSR, if necessary. Still,
the FBS and the U.S. and allied strategic forces alone would not suffice to
deter either a limited or a massive conventional aggression.

If the Soviets were to believe that, despite the presence of American forces,
the United States no longer had the will to defend Europe and, if necessary,
resort to the use of nuclear weapons, or that the will of America's allies had
been seriously weakened, they might be tempted to adopt an increasingly
belligerent policy. Such a policy could eventually lead, either deliberately or
through accident or miscalculation, to armed aggression against Western Europe.
Under such circumstances, the most likely Soviet thrust would be conventional
rather than nuclear, to avoid unnecessary collateral damage and to capture
Europe as intact as possible. To avoid providing the United States with targets
suitable for nuclear counterattack, the Soviet Union would probably attack in
relatively dispersed formations.

In order to halt an armed Soviet probe or a limited conventional thrust, it is
imperative that the United States and its allies maintain adequate conventional
forces. If the Soviet Union persisted in its aggression, the Western allies
would need their conventional troops to force enemy formations into areas more
favorable for counterattack and into nuclear targets. If the Soviets still
sought to press on, the Western allies would have to resort to tactical nuclear
weapons (TNW's) to liquidate these penetrations. An early recourse to TNW's
would be necessary in light of Soviet superiority in conventional forces, for a
significant increase in Western conventional power is clearly not possible. The
United States, already struggling to contain domestic pressures for reducing
overseas manpower commitments, cannot augment its forces abroad, and the
European allies are unlikely to increase their armed forces. Besides, the allies
want to avoid a protracted conventional war on their continent.

Consistent with, the requirement for uncertainty, the stage at which TNW's are
to be introduced should not be fixed in advance, but should be determined by the
developing military situation. Tactical nuclear firepower should be introduced
as a supplement to conventional firepower, to stop penetrations before
breakthroughs are achieved. The nuclear strikes should be selective and directed
against only military targets, but executed in such a way as to keep collateral
damage to a minimum.

In response, the Soviet Union could, of course, consider escalation, but in view
of the demonstrated resolve of the Western allies to use nuclear weapons, this
would be tantamount to risking a nuclear strike on the homeland. Unless the USSR
had determined to complete the capture of Europe regardless of consequences, the
calculated use of tactical nuclear weapons by the allies would induce it to halt
its aggression and withdraw.

To provide for a credible tactical nuclear defense, however, these components of
the force need to be improved. According to recent analyses, the current U.S.
tactical nuclear stockpile is ill-suited to meet European political demands for
limiting collateral damage, because the fission content of the weapons is high,
their yields are too big, and their accuracy is not satisfactory. The technology
for cleaner and more accurate tactical nuclear weapons is available, and they
could help to make the Western defense posture on the continent politically more
acceptable to the allies and militarily more viable.

To assess the political and military utility of "smart weapons" and their
implications for manpower and expenditures as well as for doctrine and strategy
requires a great deal of study. At this stage, however, four tentative
conclusions may be drawn. First, because terminal homing permits a weapon to be
placed in the general area above the target, whence it will find its own way,
launchers and the missile's initial guidance can be relatively simple and
inexpensive. Second, as has been mentioned, the surehit capability of smart
weapons permits savings in manpower and weapons, and hence also in expenditures.
Third, the new technology promises to offset the Soviet advantage of massive
quantities with quality. Fourth, precision-guided conventional weapons permit
small units, dispersion, mobility and flexibility. These characteristics suggest
a force structure and posture similar to the one that would be feasible for
advanced accurate tactical nuclear weapons. Moreover, smart conventional weapons
and small, accurate tactical nuclear weapons appear to be able to cover
complementary targets: the former would effectively cover many point targets;
the latter, hard fixed targets and many area targets. For these reasons the
dual-capability problem that has bedeviled the military planner since the
deployment of tactical nuclear weapons now seems to be on the road to a
solution.

The introduction of precision-guided conventional and nuclear weapons in Europe
would provide an effective capability against Soviet armor and tactical air
elements, which form a major part of the Soviet offensive threat. By emphasizing
anti-tank and anti-air systems, the West would maintain a truly defensive
posture. Precise and clean tactical nuclear weapons would help to deter the
Soviet Union from attacking with massed formations and serve to cover the
'larger targets. Conventional forces, equipped with weapons of improved
accuracy, would help to deter the USSR from attacking with more dispersed
formations, and would cover smaller targets and participate in nuclear defense.
Together these two components of the Western posture, if protected by the
umbrella of U.S. and European strategic forces, would seriously vitiate the
Soviet Union's tactics and its war-fighting capacity as an instrument of
military quagmire and political coercion.

So far, our focus has been on the problems of Western Europe, but the need to
retain an effective posture on Europe's northern and southern flanks should not
be forgotten. Geographic circumstances and the terrain inhibit an effective
local defense of the northern periphery. So do the political factors. Neither
Norway nor Denmark permits the stationing of foreign troops or nuclear weapons
on its territory, although the Danish army has Honest John surface-to-surface
missile systems in its weapons inventory. Denmark, however, is planning to
reduce its military forces and defense spending substantially. Probably more
than any other front, the northern flank derives its security from the strength
of the alliance as a whole.

On the southern flank the Sixth Fleet is a major U.S. instrument underwriting
the Western position. Withdrawal of the U.S. naval presence would seriously
