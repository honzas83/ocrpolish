> # Technology Study – Part II of a Briefing by Dr. Milton J. Minneman
> 
> This document is a transcript of a briefing given by Dr. Milton J. Minneman on February 18, 1975, at NATO Headquarters. The briefing focuses on 'Precision Guided Weapons, Target Acquisition Systems and Improved Conventional Systems'. It details United States anti-submarine precision weapons, including the Mk-46, Mk-37, and Mk-48 torpedoes, and their operational sequences. The presentation covers target acquisition systems used by the U.S. Army and Marine Corps, such as MTI radars, counter-mortar and counter-battery radars, and the Stand-off Target Acquisition System (SOTAS). It also discusses the same for the Air Force and Navy, including the A-7 TRAM and A-6E TRAM systems for night operations and the PAVE SPIE pod for daylight laser designation. Finally, the briefing touches upon the various types of cluster bombs in the inventory, such as the CBU-58/71 and CBU-24/29, comparing the different casing technologies used in their dispensers. The briefing was distributed by the Secretary, M.I.G. Wilson, and includes illustrations and a bibliography.

SLIDE 1
This presentation is a continuation of the one given at the January 21, 1975 MFC
Technology Study Group meeting.

SLIDE 2
This slide supplements the material on precision guided weapons by illustrating
several United States anti-submarine precision weapons. All use torpedos.
Typical acquisition and endurance ranges are shown – those depend on various
factors, such as depth. The ASROC is a Mk-46 torpedo which is ballistically
launched from surface ships. The Mk 37 and Mk 48 are delivered by submarines;
the Mk-46 is carried by aircraft and surface ships. Although characteristics of
the advance lightweight torpedo are not defined, this weapon is being considered
as a replacement for the Mk 46.

SLIDE 3
The next scene illustrates the operational sequence of the Mk-43 torpedo. While
the target shown in the viewgraph is a surface ship, the Mk-48 is designed for
use against highly maneuverable submarines or surface ships capable of speeds to
30 knots. The operational sequence is essentially the same against either. The
torpedo, which is impulse launched, runs a preset or wireguided trajectory at 55
knots or 40 knots prior to the enable point. When the torpedo enables, it runs
at either 40 knots or 23 knots in one of four acoustic search modes at search
depths from 55 to 2000 feet. Both the pre-enable trajectory and the search modes
can be changed by wire guidance commands. The torpedo acoustic panel, which is
designed to detect, classify, and localize targets, must also guide the torpedo
close enough to the target for detonation to occur. During attack, the torpedo
speed increases up to 55 knots if the target signal-to-noise ratio is
sufficient. If the torpedo loses the target after it has locked on, one of
several reattack programs is initiated by the torpedo's logic-based (except in
the passive homing case) on the last known target range.

The next two slides itemize target acquisition systems that are principally used
by the U.S. Army and Marine Corps. I shall cover the airborne systems of the

While some older systems are not indicated, the list is fairly comprehensive and
does include all systems in research and development.

I believe most of you have a general awareness of the items employed by forward
observers: i.e., night vision devices and laser range finders and designators.
These smaller devices are lightweight and portable by one man.

However, a few comments may be informative concerning some of the other systems.
You notice there are five MTI radars employed to detect moving people or
vehicles. Two are man-pack, the PPS-15 being an improved version of the PPS-3.
These devices typically have detection ranges of a few kilometers, as compared
to detection ranges on the order of a few 10's of kilometers for the larger,
more powerful vehicular mounted or portable radars.

SLIDE 6
Both a countermortar and counterbattery radar are being developed. The U.S. does
have operational counter-mortar and counterbattery radars - the MFC-4 and IPQ-10

The counterbattery or CB radar, which is to track the trajectory of shells
perhaps 15-25 km away to determine their point of origin, is more sophisticated
than the countermortar or CM radar which will be deployed in large numbers in
the forward area. By "more sophisticated" I mean the CB radar will have higher
power, greater resolution

SLID 7
This slide shows an artist's conception of the TPO-37. The phased array radar
vehicle is removed from the control van as depicted in the cutaway drawing. A
generator van is also required (for right).

The SOTAS (Stand-off Target Acquisition System) will be the mainstay of Army
target acquisition in the future through heli-borne detection capable of
monitoring large areas.

The SOTAS will also serve to direct or flush mini-RPVs to designated areas for
confirmation of SOTAS detections.

SLIDE 8
This slide depicts SOTAS operation. The bilateration technique is employed. Two
helicopters with ITI radars surveill large areas. The returns from each radar
are telemetered to the control van. Only moving targets are displayed. To
position the target on a grid the position of the helicopters must be known.
This is accomplished by 2 ground-based DME stations (one for each helo) that
track the helicopters and relay that data to the display van. As moving objects
are displayed, reconnaissance may be required to determine if it is enemy, and
if it is, fire may be directed at that position.

The various REMBASS sensors will also flush mini-RPVs. For those of you not
familiar with "mini-RPVs", we are typically talking about aircraft with wing
spans of 2-3 meters, speeds of about 100 kt, endurances of 1 1/2-2 1/2 hours.

SLIDE 9
This slide shows the Lockheed "little n" vehicle. Four phases or levels of
sophistication of surveillance gear will be tested. Those and their uses are
indicated in the picture and include unstabilized IV (blue spot) with and
without a camera and stabilized IV with zoom optics with and without laser
ranging. A final configuration will incorporate laser designation and will
enable attack with laser seeking weapons.

SLIDE 10

The next two charts show acquisition system used by Air Force and Navy aircraft.
The comments that appear on the charts are largely self-explanatory. You will
notice that much of the current development effort addresses acquisition and
delivery at night. You can trace the development of the technology of first to
second generation daylight laser designation with wing-mounted pods, then
efforts to make the system internal or integral with the aircraft, followed by
efforts to extend the system into a night capability.

SLIDE 11

The next viewgraph concerns the A-7 TRAM. The A-7 TRAM is fitted with a FLIR to
extend its weapons delivery capability into night operations. The picture you
see is what the pilot would see when looking through the HUD at a ship at night.
Heading, speed, altitude and aimpoint/impact point are displayed. The weapon is
automatically released when the aircraft is flown to a position where the impact
point coincides with the aimpoint.

SLIDE 12

The next slide shows a schematic of the A-6E TRAM systems and operation. This
effort is to fulfill the requirement for passively detecting, classifying and
identifying sea and marine support targets and conducting an accurate first pass
attack at night from stand-off distances with improved weapon delivery
performance. The A-6E TRAM system will include a passive infrared imaging sensor
for target classification and identification, a laser target designator range
boresighted with the IR sensor for delivery of laser guided weapons, and a laser
search set to locate targets by the search and acquisition radar. The imagery
will be presented to the Bombardier/Navigator for control of the system, and the
line-of-sight angle and range will be provided to the computer for the weapon
delivery solution. The A-6E aircraft modifications will include incorporation of
a chin turret housing the TRAM sensors, additional cockpit displays and
controls; and integration with the computer and aircraft weapon delivery system.

SLIDE 15
This viewgraph shows a sampling of cluster bombs representative of the
inventory. There are numerous combinations of bomblets and dispensers (from air
deployment to rear of pod, downward ejection from pod, clam shell deployment)
that are not shown on this list, although it does include all items currently in
production.

A few comments can be made for illustration. For instance, the CBU-58/71
replaces the CBU-24/29. The CBU-58/71 represents scored steel metal casing
technology which is an improvement of the steel ball in aluminum matrix
technology of older items, such as the CBU-24/29. (The scored metal approach
permits higher initial fragment velocities, although the irregular shape of the
fragment loses energy faster than the steel ball fragment. Also, the U.S. has a
broader industrial capability to produce scored metal items as compared to
matrix item. On balance, scored metal is considered the superior approach). The
CBU-24/58 bomblets all instantaneously fuse; the CBU-56/74 contain a random
distribution of fusing - some instantaneously and others randomly exploding up
to 1 1/2 hours after delivery.

SLIDE 16
This shows CBU-59 with its complement of bomblets spread in front of it. The
container, or canister, which is about 2 1/2 in. long, is dropped and at a
preset altitude the canister unidues along its long dimension (like a clam
opening) and the bomblets deploy. The bomblets typically deploy at less than
1000 ft above ground level. (This is the case regardless of mode of delivery).
The bomblets have dual fuze and dual warheads.

If the bomblet strikes a "hard" object it immediately fuzes in the shaped charge
mode; if it senses hitting a soft target; e.g., the ground, it buries itself,
ejects the explosive charge back-up into the air (5 m. or so) at which time a
delay fuse explodes the charge.

SLIDE 18

This chart shows the U.S. artillery delivered ICM and mine effort. The chart is
largely self-explanatory. As with the cluster weapons, the projectile delivers
and deploys bomblets.

You notice that three of the live development efforts address artillery
delivered mines. This concept is considered to have the potential for high
military utility. In the past, a mine field could only be deployed in front of
an enemy and could only impede (if at all) his progress in the advance. With
artillery delivered mines, the capability to deploy a mine field on top of an
enemy is at hand. This will blunt his movement in any direction, both exacting a
toll in tank losses to mines and increasing their vulnerability to other weapons
because their mobility is encumbered.

SLIDE 19

The last viewgraph shows the operation sequence of the XII-718 anti-tank mine
which I showed you last time. A time rube deploys the projectile base plate and
ignites a small charge in the nose of the projectile which forces the mines out
the rear of the projectile. (Some projectiles use a gas generator to accomplish
deployment). If the mine fails to properly arm the battery explodes the mine.
Then a target (or tampering) is sensed or the life time of the mine clapses the
mine self-destructs. Although the viewgraph indicates only a 4-hour life time,
an alternate fuzing can be used to provide 48-hour life.
