> # Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) Consultation Procedures and Facilities
> 
> This working paper, prepared by the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Staff Group, presents a revised report by Permanent Representatives on the procedures and facilities for political consultation on the use of nuclear weapons in time of war. The document emphasizes the need for rapid, secure, and unambiguous exchanges of information, such as release requests and national views. It addresses specific issues identified during Exercise WINTEX 73, noting that delays in transmission were partly due to the non-use of the NATO-wide communications system by the Major NATO Commander. To improve efficiency, the document recommends the use of Address Indicating Groups (AIGs) 6006 and 6007 to ensure messages reach both Foreign and Defence Ministries. It further suggests the introduction of a new AIG to cover both ministries within a single group to reduce misrouting. Regarding message precedence, the report concludes that existing arrangements are workable, but notes that messages related to nuclear consultation in wartime would typically justify the highest precedence, known as FLASH. The ultimate responsibility for ensuring timely delivery of critical traffic within a government rests with the individual nation concerned.

Handling and Identification of Nuclear Consultation Messages.
3. Since a significant portion of the above-mentioned exchanges of information

message traffic, it is clear that steps taken to facilitate rapid handling of

the effectiveness of the consultation process. Conversely, the process could be
seriously degraded if messages fail to reach responsible officials or reached
them too late, due to handling delays or due to failure on the part of those
involved in handling messages to recognise their relevance to the consultation
process.

7. Precedence. Noting the concern that has from time
to time been expressed that the precedence assigned to nuclear
consultation messages may be insufficient to ensure timely
delivery to political authorities, we have in an earlier report
investigated(2) the question of whether a special precedence
for such traffic might be warranted. We concluded that the
present arrangements regarding precedence are workable, and
that they should function effectively in time of war in support
of the political consultation process. In this connection,
it is worth emphasising that the precedence assigned to a
message by the originator not only gives instructions to
communications personnel with regard to the relative priority

8. There appears to be general acceptance within NATO of a view that in time of
war messages related to the nuclear consultation process would usually be of
such urgency and importance as to justify the assignment of the highest
precedence, FLASH(1). In view of this and in the light of the strong and
continuing efforts that are made in NATO to indoctrinate all message originators
to use the FLASH precedence very sparingly, we confirm our earlier conclusion
that the precedence system now in effect is workable and should ensure that
critical traffic would be handled rapidly and reliably in time of war.
Accordingly, we feel that there is no valid requirement at the present time for
a new precedence, higher than FLASH, to be reserved only for nuclear
consultation messages.

9. Special Operating Signal. The above conclusions notwithstanding, we note that
SACEUR, in recognition of the need to ensure rapid handling of nuclear release
traffic by communications personnel, has promulgated instructions to identify
such message traffic by a special operating signal. This distinguishing signal,
consisting of the letters ZYA, is to be placed in the heading of certain high-
priority traffic, including nuclear release messages, originated by SACEUR or by
his Major Subordinate Commanders, to indicate to communications personnel that
such messages are to be handled (transmitted, relayed, written up, delivered)
before all other messages of equal precedence(1). Thus, a nuclear release
message originated by SACEUR and carrying this special operating signal in its
heading would be handled within the communications system prior to all other
messages of the same precedence that do not carry the special operating signal,
even though these other messages had been introduced earlier into the system. At
the present time, only SACEUR and his Major Subordinate Commanders have been
authorised to use this special operating signal, and only communications

facilitate expeditious handling outside of ACE of high-priority messages

Content of Selective Release Messages.
14. Discussions within the NPG concerning the content of selective release
messages have tended to focus on two distinctly different aspects. On the one
hand, continuing attention has been paid to the kinds and substance of the
information to be covered. A significant step in this context was taken, for
example, in the Provisional Political Guidelines for the Initial Defensive
Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons by NATO which, inter alia, set out(1) in
considerable detail the kinds of information needed by political authorities, a
good part of which would be included in the nuclear release messages. The other
aspect of message content to which the NPG has addressed itself concerns form
and terminology. NPG interest in this regard was reflected, for example, in our
preliminary observations on VINTEX 73 which reported(2) that some difficulties
and delays were encountered by political authorities in fully understanding the
meaning of the nuclear release messages because the explanation of the precise
formats used and of certain abbreviations employed to identify weapons
categories is contained only in the Major NATO Commanders' Nuclear Weapons
Release Procedures documents which are given very limited distribution.

17. This report deals with a number of separate but related aspects of the
nuclear consultation process. We have discussed some other aspects in earlier
reports to the Ministers(1) and some additional elements of the process, such as

appears to us that the time has arrived for these various elements to be
consolidated into a single package. We note that this step would be consistent
with, and similar to, work now being carried out in NATO under the overall
coordination of COEC in the field of NATO crisis management procedures and
arrangements(2). Subject to Ministerial concurrence, we propose therefore to
undertake the development of a general document on nuclear consultation
procedures which will incorporate the diverse elements mentioned above. This
