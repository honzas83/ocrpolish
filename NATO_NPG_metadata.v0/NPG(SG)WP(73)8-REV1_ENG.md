> # NATO Operational Concepts and Doctrine Currently Governing Employment of Nuclear Weapons for Tactical Purposes
> 
> This document is a draft report by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Staff Group regarding the current operational concepts and doctrine for the tactical use of nuclear weapons within NATO. The report comments on a basic report prepared by the Supreme Headquarters Allied Command Europe (SHAPE) and related comments from the Military Committee. It outlines that the operational concepts and doctrine govern current planning for tactical nuclear employment within Allied Command Europe (ACE) and are designed to be flexible and responsive to political and military developments. The Military Committee endorses the view that these concepts provide NATO with the necessary flexibility to respond to enemy aggression and are consistent with the other Major NATO Commanders, including the Supreme Allied Commander Atlantic (SACLANT). The report further asserts that these concepts are compatible with the specific guidance provided by the Defence Planning Committee (DPC) and basic NATO strategy. It emphasizes the importance of updating these doctrines to account for changes in the threat environment, force structure, and weaponry. Additionally, the report discusses the objective of weakening the enemy's will to continue aggression and the influence of non-military factors on tactical nuclear employment. It also touches upon the characteristics of nuclear weapons, specifically the speed and distance capabilities of delivery methods such as PERSHING and POSEIDON, and the authority required for the release of nuclear weapons.

I. PURPOSE
This report comments on NATO operational concepts and doctrine currently
governing employment of nuclear weapons for tactical purposes.

NATO Military Authorities to prepare a report outlining operational concepts and

III. DISCUSSION
General Comment
3. The SHAPE Report provides information on the operational concepts and
doctrine which govern current planning for tactical employment of nuclear
weapons within Allied Command Europe (ACE), and which would apply to ACE
operations in time of war. The Report identifies the broad political guidance on
which the ACE operational concepts and doctrine are based; outlines the general
and specific military considerations that are relevant to the development of
such concepts and doctrine; and describes these operational concepts and
doctrine in considerable detail. In the Report, SHAPE emphasises(3) the dynamic
nature of the concepts and doctrine, pointing out that they have evolved over a
period of years and that they are kept under continuing review in order to be
responsive to political and military developments. They are intended to be
detailed enough to provide the necessary direction for effective planning and
operations by subordinate commanders without, however, restricting the
flexibility of future military operations through rigid contingency planning.

6. The Military Committee draws attention(1) to one aspect of the possible
effects of the use of nuclear weapons in the tactical rôle, namely the objective
of weakening the enemy's will to continue aggression, a point that is covered in
the SHAPE Report as well(2). We agree with the Military Committee's observation
that this aspect has significant political implications. Although the main focus
of the operational concepts and doctrine outlined in the SHAPE Report is
naturally on military applications, these concepts and doctrine also reflect an
appreciation of non-military factors that influence the tactical employment of
nuclear weapons(3). We feel, therefore, that the operational concepts and
doctrine currently governing NATO employment of nuclear weapons for tactical
purposes as set forth in the SHAPE Report offer a framework within which
Alliance political as well as military objectives can be effectively supported
in time of war, as regards both the defence and deterrence aspects.

Specific Comments

8. Nuclear Weapon Characteristics. With regard to general military
considerations that are relevant to the development of operational concepts and
doctrine, the SHAPE Report lists(1) a number of ways in which nuclear weapons
differ significantly from previous munitions. One of the differences mentioned
is the speed and distance capabilities of delivery methods. There are, of
course, some systems exclusively dedicated to the delivery of nuclear weapons,
such as PERSHING or POSEIDON, that have speed and distance capabilities far
greater than delivery methods used before nuclear weapons were available. On the
other hand, many of the improvements in delivery methods made since the
introduction of nuclear weapons are applicable in large measure to the delivery
of either nuclear or conventional munitions. Advances in aircraft, battlefield
missiles and tube artillery come to mind in this regard.

9. Authority to request nuclear weapon release. The SHAPE Report states(1) that
SACEUR's decision to request nuclear weapon release could be based, among other
things, on requests of governments. It is, of course, clear that governments
always have full freedom to suggest to SACEUR that he make a nuclear weapon
release request. It is equally clear that SACEUR would not only have the right
to take such governmental views into account in reaching his decision but would
be expected to do so. On the other hand, mention of this possibility in the
SHAPE Report should not be viewed as any infringement on the option of a
government to communicate a nuclear weapon release request direct to the
Council/DCP and member governments, as provided for in the General Guidelines
for Consultation Procedure on the Use of Nuclear Weapons(2). If a government
originates such a release request, it would, of course, ensure that the message
was sent simultaneously to the Major NATO Commanders in order that their views
and assessments could be made available to the Council/DCP and capitals as soon
as possible(3).
