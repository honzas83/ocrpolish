> # Military Committee Function in the Nuclear Consultation Process
> 
> This document is a draft report by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) reviewing the role of the Military Committee in the Alliance's nuclear consultation process during times of tension or hostilities. It emphasizes that while the ultimate decision to use nuclear weapons rests with the nuclear power, the process aims to permit the rapid expression of national views from member governments to inform that decision. The report outlines that nuclear consultation fits within a general consultation framework, which is intensified during crises. It highlights the importance of preserving the clear identity of individual government views to avoid distortion and ensure they reach the nuclear powers in a timely manner. The Military Committee's role is defined as advising the North Atlantic Council and the Defence Planning Committee on the use of military power, including nuclear weapons, without interposing itself between Major NATO Commanders and political authorities. The document references the General Guidelines for Consultation Procedure on the Use of Nuclear Weapons adopted in 1969 and discusses the importance of time-sensitivity in the release decision for nuclear weapons.

I. PURPOSE
This report reviews the function of the Military Committee in the various
aspects of the process of Alliance consultation in times of tension or
hostilities on the possible use of nuclear weapons.

II. BACKGROUND
2. At their meeting in Ankara in May, 1973, during the discussion of a report(1)
by the Permanent Representatives containing some preliminary observations on the
nuclear consultation aspects of WINTEX 73, the Ministers noted that there were
uncertainties concerning the ways in which the Military Committee in Permanent
Session would be involved in the process, particularly with respect to dealing
with specific requests or proposals for use of nuclear weapons. Consequently,
they invited(2) the Permanent Representatives to review the function of the
Military Committee in the nuclear consultation process.

III. DISCUSSION
Characteristics of the Nuclear Consultation Process
4. The basic guidance governing the procedures to be used within the Alliance in
consulting in time of tension or hostilities on questions of nuclear weapon use
is contained in the General Guidelines for Consultation Procedure on the Use of
Nuclear Weapons(2). These Consultation Guidelines were adopted by the Defence
Planning Committee in 1969 on the recommendation of the NPG and the Nuclear
Defence Affairs Committee in the light of intensive consideration of the subject
in the NPG during the first three years of its existence(3). They also reflect a
significant body of opinion and experience developed within the Alliance over a
period of years prior to the creation of the NPG. The Consultation Guidelines,
although not explicit with regard to the roles played by the various
participants, including the Military Committee, provide the benchmark against
which these roles are to be assessed.

5. The Consultation Guidelines state that nuclear consultation would fit into
the framework of a procedure of general consultation between member countries
that is already operating(1). General consultation, which functions on a
continuing basis in peacetime, is designed to be intensified in time of tension
or war, and might reach a point at which the possible use of nuclear weapons
becomes an issue. Depending on the circumstances, this possibility might be
raised in general terms in assessments of the evolving situation prepared by
member governments, the Council/DPC, or the Major NATO Commanders, even before
any specific requests or proposals for nuclear weapon use are made(2). In the
context of this report, the nuclear consultation process could be considered to
begin at this point and to culminate, according to developments, in action on a
specific request or proposal for use originated by a member government or a
Major NATO Commander.

6. It is at this final stage that the nuclear consultation process, as the
Consultation Guidelines point out(3), would be assuming those particular
characteristics that differentiate it from the general consultation procedure.
These are reflected in the treatment given to individual country views on
specific requests or proposals for use of nuclear weapons.

7. On the question of country views, the Consultation Guidelines point out that,
although the ultimate decision on the use of a nuclear weapon will always lie
with the nuclear power, it is essential that there should be the greatest
possible degree of Alliance consultation compatible with the time available and
the relevant circumstances, before any ultimate decision is taken(1).
Consequently, a general aim of the nuclear consultation process is "to permit
the rapid expression of views of member governments on any request or proposal
for the use of nuclear weapons"(2). These views will be communicated to the
nuclear power(s) concerned by the most expeditious means available(3) for its
consideration in reaching a decision whether or not to release the weapons for
the proposed use(4). In reaching its decision, the nuclear power would give
special weight to the views of the NATO countries most directly affected, i.e.,
the country on or from whose territory nuclear weapons would be employed, the
country or countries providing the nuclear warheads, and the country or
countries providing or manning the contemplated means of delivery(5). In this
overall process, each member government would wish to have as much information
as possible about the views of other members so as to enable it to take a
position that would most effectively serve its own particular national interests
as well as the interests of the Alliance as a whole(6).

8. It is clear from the foregoing that, on the precise question of whether to
support a specific request or proposal for use of nuclear weapons, the effective
operation of the nuclear consultation process depends critically on the
expression of clearly-identifiable national views that can be assessed
individually by other Alliance members and given the particular weight foreseen
by the Guidelines. Procedures within the Council/DCP that prevent the
transmission of individual government views to the nuclear power, or that might
distort them, would be inconsistent with the Consultation Guidelines.

9. Closely linked with the need to preserve the clear identity of individual
government views on a specific request or proposal for nuclear weapon use is the
requirement to do everything possible to ensure that these views reach the
nuclear power(s) concerned in time to have an influence on the release decision.
This decision is time-sensitive, since the political, psychological and military
impact of the proposed use can be critically dependent on the timing of that
use. Much of the considerable effort that the Alliance has devoted toward
ensuring that suitable facilities and procedures are available for the nuclear
consultation process is designed to permit the rapid expression of views of
member governments(1).

Military Committee Rôle
10. Nuclear consultation within the Alliance is carried out both through direct
channels between member governments and in the Council/DCP which has been
designated as the normal forum for country representatives to express national
positions(1). It is implicit, we think, that the Military Committee, as a
collective body, has no function with respect to any direct exchange of views
between member governments. However, in connection with the consultation
activity in the Council/DCP, the Military Committee(2) rôle in wartime includes,
besides other responsibilities, "advising the North Atlantic Council/Defence
Planning Committee on the use of military power, including, if time permits, the
use of nuclear weapons by NATO forces or forces acting in support of the
Alliance. This is not to be construed as interposing the Military Committee
between the Major NATO Commanders and the appropriate political authorities with
respect to the authorisation for release of nuclear weapons"(3). Such Military
Committee advice to the Council/DCP could, in the earlier stages of the nuclear
consultation process within NATO Headquarters, cover such matters as
contributing to the assessment of the developing situation through the

11. In the light of the overriding emphasis in the Consultation Guidelines on
rapid transmission of clearly-identified government views, we feel that some
clarification is necessary with respect to the Military Committee's statement
that "as the consultation process draws closer to the ultimate decision, it will
become more difficult to differentiate between national military advice and
national political advice, since in many countries military and political
leaders confer jointly in reaching a national position. However, shortage of
time may make it difficult for the Military Committee to provide NATO military
advice based on up-dated national views"(2). We think that any effort to
differentiate between "national military advice," "national political advice"
and "national position" in the Alliance-wide expression of country views is
likely to be more confusing and disruptive than helpful.

12. The "national position" most relevant to the ultimate decision is the
"government view," mentioned in the Consultation Guidelines, which forms the
national input to the nuclear consultation process. The specific content of the
"national position" or "government view" and the way in which it is developed
within a country is a national responsibility(1). What is important in terms of
the Alliance-wide nuclear consultation process, it seems to us, is the need to
avoid presenting conflicting versions of an individual country's view on the
precise question of whether to support a specific request or proposal for use of
nuclear weapons. This is particularly true at NATO Headquarters where a
situation could arise in which a Permanent Military Representative could be
presenting views in the Military Committee, based on "national military advice,"
which differed from the government view passed directly between member
governments and presented in the Council/DCP by his country's Permanent
Representative. We think this possibility carries a serious risk of frustrating
the basic objectives of the Alliance-wide nuclear consultation process. It
follows from this that continuing and close collaboration between a country's
Permanent Representative and its Permanent Military Representative in all phases
of nuclear consultation assumes great importance(2).

13. In this regard, we note that the individual Permanent Military
Representative might be expected to function in such circumstances in two
distinctly different ways: as a spokesman reflecting the instructions of his
Chief of Staff(1), or as an uninstructed expert drawing on his personal military
knowledge, experience and judgement. We suggest that expressions such as
"professional military advice," used frequently in the MC Report, should be
interpreted to cover either of these possibilities.

14. In its Report, the Military Committee mentions the possibility of a need to
reconcile the points of view of the Major NATO Commanders during the critical
period just prior to and after the start of preliminary consultation on the
release of nuclear weapons(2). To be consistent with the previously-cited
description of the Military Committee's specific task in connection with nuclear
consultation(3), we think that such activities must be achieved in a way that
does not interpose the Military Committee between the Major NATO Commanders and
the appropriate political authorities with respect to the authorisation for
release of nuclear weapons.

activities with those of the Council/DFC. In keeping with the stress in the

believe that such coordination should be worked out in a pragmatic way as
circumstances dictate during the course of consultation and that no specific
arrangements should be prescribed in advance.
