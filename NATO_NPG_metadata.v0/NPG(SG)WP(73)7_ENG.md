> # Consultation Procedures and Facilities
> 
> This draft report by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) examines selected aspects of consultation procedures and facilities for political consultation in time of war regarding the use of nuclear weapons. The report addresses the need for rapid, secure, and unambiguous exchanges of information, such as release requests and national views. It confirms that the current system of message precedence, specifically the use of the FLASH precedence, is sufficient for the urgency of nuclear consultation traffic. However, it suggests that the special operating signal used by the Supreme Allied Commander Europe (SACEUR) and his Major Subordinate Commanders be extended to other authorized NATO authorities to ensure top-priority handling. The document also discusses the challenges posed by the ATOMAL classification, noting that it can complicate and delay the processing of messages and potentially limit the scope of consultation for governments not cleared by the United States. The report proposes to continue examining the ATOMAL classification problem once the United States authorities have clarified the interpretation of the rules. Finally, it emphasizes the importance of clear identification of nuclear consultation traffic to avoid delays and misrouting within capitals and headquarters.

Identification and Handling of Nuclear Consultation Messages.
3. Since a significant portion of the above-mentioned
exchanges of information among participants in the nuclear
consultation process will be in the form of message traffic, it
is clear that steps taken to facilitate rapid handling of such
message traffic, and to ensure its ready identification, will
contribute to the effectiveness of the consultation process.
Conversely, the process could be seriously degraded if messages
fail to reach responsible officials, or reached them too late,
due to handling delays or due to a failure to recognise the
relevance of the messages to the consultation process.

4.   /Precedence
With respect to message handling, we indicated in an earlier report(1) that the
present arrangements regarding precedence are workable, and should function
effectively in time of war in support of the political consultation process.  In
this connection, attention is invited to the fact that the precedence assigned
to a message, for example, FLASH, not only gives instructions to communications
personnel with regard to the relative priority to be accorded to the message in
handling it in communications channels but also provides guidance to the
officials responsible for taking action on its contents with regard to the
originator's assessment of the urgency of the matter.  Thus, if an originator
assigns a FLASH precedence to an outgoing message, he is directing
communications personnel to process the message through the communications
channels before any other messages of lower precedence, such as OPERATIONAL
IMMEDIATE, PRIORITY or ROUTINE.  At the same time, he is also advising the
officials responsible for taking action on the content of the message that he
considers the subject matter warrants immediate attention.

Special Operating Signal.
6. /The above conclusion notwithstanding, we note that SACEUR, in recognition of
the need to ensure rapid handling of nuclear release traffic by communications
personnel, has promulgated instructions to identify such message traffic by a
specific operating signal. This distinguishing signal is to be placed in the
heading of certain high-priority traffic, including nuclear release messages,
originated by SACEUR or by his Major Subordinate Commanders, to indicate to
communications personnel that such messages are to be handled (transmitted,
relayed, written up) before all other messages of equal precedence(1). Thus, a
nuclear release message originated by SACEUR and carrying this special operating
signal in its heading would be handled within the communications systems prior

9. Clear Identification of Nuclear Consultation Traffic.
In addition to ensuring that nuclear consultation messages are handled without
delay within the NATO communications systems, it is essential that the relevance
of such messages to the nuclear consultation process be made as obvious as
possible in order to avoid inadvertent delays and misrouting within capitals and
headquarters after the messages have moved out of the communications channels
themselves. In our preliminary observations on WINTEX 73, we noted(1) that
certain messages pertaining to the release of nuclear weapons for selective
employment for tactical purposes originated by the Major NATO Commanders, such
as WHISKEY, SIERRA and ROMEO messages, contain within the message texts
themselves a distinctive label (WHITE DOT for exercises; BLUE DOT for actual
crises) that clearly identifies them as nuclear release messages. It should be
pointed out, however, that no provisions have been made for other traffic also
essential to the nuclear consultation process, such as reports of Council/DPC
consultation, pertinent
