> # Early Combined Use of NATO Conventional and Nuclear Capabilities
> 
> This document is a draft report by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) discussing the military and political advantages and disadvantages of an early combined use of NATO conventional and nuclear capabilities in response to major aggression. The report draws on comments from NATO Military Authorities, including SHAPE and the Military Committee, to evaluate the possibility of employing nuclear weapons alongside conventional forces before conventional forces suffer substantial losses. It traces the background of the discussion back to a 1971 paper by former Secretary General Brosio, which suggested examining the early combined use of nuclear weapons as soon as a major aggression is identified. The document notes that previous studies had generally assumed nuclear weapons would only be used after impending conventional defeat, and this report seeks to explore a different part of the conflict spectrum. It emphasizes that early combined use is consistent with the NATO strategy of flexible response and the guidance provided by the Defense and Political Committee (DPC). It further clarifies that any decision to resort to such use would depend on the magnitude of the aggression and an analysis of both political and military factors.

CONVENTIONAL AND NUCLEAR CAPABILITIES
Draft Report by the Permanent Representatives
I. PURPOSE
This report discusses advantages and disadvantages that might accrue from an
early combined use of NATO conventional and nuclear capabilities, drawing on
comments by the NATO Military Authorities that deal with the military aspects.

II. BACKGROUND
2. At their meeting in Mittenwald in May, 1971 the NPG Ministers discussed a
paper on unresolved problems related to tactical nuclear defence written by
former Secretary General Brosio(1). This paper suggested, among other matters,
an examination of the possibility of an early combined use of our conventional
and nuclear capabilities as soon as an enemy attack is identified as a major
aggression and before our conventional forces have suffered substantial losses,
and a constrained but continuing combined use if the aggressor persists in his
attack(2). In a subsequent report containing suggestions for investigating the
unresolved problems, the Permanent Representatives proposed(3) that the NATO
Military Authorities might be requested to indicate in general terms what
military advantages and disadvantages might accrue from an early combined use,
noting at the same time that the question of constrained but continuing follow-
on use could be considered at a later date when more progress had been made in
the Follow-on Use Work Programme. Agreeing with this approach,

therefore suggested looking at the possible tactical use of nuclear weapons by
NATO, together with continued use of its conventional strength, at a somewhat
earlier stage in the evolution of the fighting. In other words, PO 226 proposed
examination of a part of the spectrum of conflict that had not yet been given
full attention.

Emphasising that no attempt is made to answer the question of whether NATO
should resort to such use, the SHAPE Report observes that early tactical use is
envisioned within the approved strategy of flexible response and that the
question of adherence to NATO strategy is not at issue(1). In this context, we
note that the most recent DPC Ministerial guidance to the NATO Military
Authorities states that NATO must maintain a capability for the tactical use of
nuclear weapons, the primary purpose of which is to add to the deterrence of
conventional attacks of any magnitude and counter them if necessary, by
confronting the enemy with the prospect of consequent escalation of the
conflict, and, to deter, and if necessary respond to, the use of tactical
nuclear weapons by posing the threat of escalation to all-out nuclear war. This
capability should be of such a character and so deployed and organised as to
present the aggressor with the clear risk that an attack on NATO at any level
may escalate into the nuclear dimension, while preserving for NATO as much
flexibility as possible in the exercise of that option(2). This is consistent
with the statement in the Provisional Political Guidelines that a variety of
situations can be envisaged in which tactical use might have to be initiated by
NATO at an early stage, but that NATO should not be committed in advance to such
use(3). In view of the foregoing, we concur that early combined use is one of
the options open to NATO within the current strategy.

6. Although early combined use is within the current NATO strategy which
embraces both major and limited aggression(1), it is clear that a NATO decision
on such use would be dependent, inter alia, on the magnitude of the aggression,
as the SHAPE Report intimates(2). This decision would be based on an analysis of
political and military factors, such as those mentioned in the SHAPE Report and
in our comments that follow, but the relative weight given to each factor in
striking an overall balance is likely to vary considerably according to the
scale of aggression. Having said this, we do not mean to suggest that NATO would
have to make a formal declaration of whether the aggression was major or limited
as defined by MC 14/3 before reaching a decision on early combined use since
this would unnecessarily complicate the political consultation and decision-
making process.

Deterrent Rôle of Early Combined Use
10. While confirming the necessity of maintaining a convincing NATO capability
to use nuclear weapons as a deterrent, the SHAPE Report points out that, in
accordance with its terms of reference, the development of the analysis is
confined to assessment factors concerning potential early combined use itself
without examining the possible deterrent effect of the threat of an early
combined use(1). We observe that a viable and visible NATO capability to resort
to early combined use is in itself an inherent threat that a potential aggressor
must take into account and should serve, together with other deterrent elements,
to dissuade him from initiating an aggression.

11. In addition to the foregoing, we note the Military Committee's observation
that an advantage of the early use of nuclear weapons would lie in its deterrent
value(2). We agree that the actual tactical employment of nuclear weapons in the
early combined use context is likely to have a significant deterrent as well as
war-fighting aspect. With respect to the latter, such use would be designed to
inflict damage on the enemy forces and cause him to reconsider his aggression
and to cease his attack. But such use could also have a deterrent aspect of a
different kind because it could induce him not to increase the intensity of his
attack or to expand its scope, even if he did not immediately cease the
aggression. It can be argued that this deterrent effect would be greater if NATO
resorts to the tactical use of nuclear weapons early in the evolution of the
fighting before it has suffered substantial losses in its conventional strength.
This deterrent aspect of actual use is reflected in the Provisional Political
Guidelines(3).

Rationale for Combined Use
12. The SHAPE Report reiterates(1) the point that, to achieve their tactical
purpose, nuclear weapons must not be used as a substitute for conventional
weapons but rather as a complement to them. We agree with this position which is
in line with the Provisional Political Guidelines(2). Tactical use of nuclear
weapons together with conventional means of defence would not only be sound from
a military point of view(3) but would also have the political advantage of
retaining finer gradations and hence greater flexibility in the escalatory
process.

Escalation
13. The SHAPE Report defines escalation as a significant increase in the
intensity or magnitude of the combat action, or enlargement of the battle area,
noting also that escalation could result from the use of nuclear weapons(4).
Since first use of nuclear weapons in a conflict by one side or the other will
represent a significant increase in the intensity or magnitude of the combat
action, it automatically becomes escalation under the SHAPE definition. On the
other hand, later tactical use would not in itself be escalation under this
definition unless it involved a further significant increase in intensity or
magnitude of combat action, or enlargement of the battle area. Although the
formal definition of escalation in the SHAPE Report is couched in terms that
describe a phenomenon, we note at the same time that the Report, in examining
escalation and its implications in the early combined use context, also

Political Aspects of Other Critical Factors

16. In many, if not most, respects, the analysis of the advantages and
disadvantages of early combined use in the context of the various factors
mentioned in the SHAPE Report is as pertinent from a political point of view as
from the military standpoint and there is no need to repeat the examination in
our report. There are, however, some additional comments of a political nature
that we would wish to make on certain of the factors discussed in the SHAPE
Report.

17. Geographical Considerations. If the exercise of the early combined use
option enabled NATO to meet and stop enemy aggression before deep penetrations
of NATO territory have been made, this would contribute directly to the
achievement of a primary NATO political objective of effective forward defence.
Such use, particularly in thinly populated border areas, could also have the
political advantage of reducing the risk of collateral damage. On the other
hand, if early combined use were to occur in close proximity to the Soviet
Union, it might have the political drawback of increasing the possible risk of
an enemy nuclear response, as pointed out in the Provisional Political
Guidelines(1).

18. Force Deployments. If NATO is to have a capability to exercise the early
combined use option effectively, nuclear-capable forces might have to deploy in
forward areas prior to the outbreak of hostilities. While this could be
politically disadvantageous if it is interpreted by a potential aggressor as a
provocative action, it has the political advantage of forcefully demonstrating
NATO's determination to defend itself. Another possible implication of early
forward deployment is the probable requirement for timely political actions,
such as alert measure decisions.

19. Weapon Selection and Targeting. There could be circumstances in which it
would be advantageous from a political point of view to make a distinction among
the individual enemy countries insofar as targeting their forces, installations
and territory is concerned.

20. Command and Control. The SHAPE Report stresses the need for a responsive and
reliable NATO command and control system, particularly in the context of early
combined use, and points out that any appreciable disruption would be to NATO's
disadvantage. The Report also notes that some effects of nuclear weapon
detonations by NATO, such as electro magnetic pulse, might degrade the
Alliance's own communications systems, but that the commander's careful
selection and employment of weapons could, to a large degree, limit the amount
of degradation(1). We believe that, from a political viewpoint, the preservation
of an effective command and control system against disruption resulting from
nuclear weapons use is of crucial importance if control of the selective use of
nuclear weapons is to be exercised effectively by political authorities or NATO
military commanders. Conversely, serious disruption of NATO communications could
frustrate the political consultation process as well as interfere significantly
with the execution of NATO's military operations.

Broader Political Implications
22. The Military Committee has expressed the view that consideration of
associated political factors would be necessary to complete a study of all
advantages and disadvantages of early combined use(2). In the preceding
paragraphs, we have commented on the political implications of a number of the
factors mentioned in the SHAPE Report. There are, however, a number of other
considerations having broader political implications that must be taken into
account in reaching a decision in time of hostilities about early combined use.
These considerations include, for example, the utility of such use in
demonstrating NATO resolve; its impact on NATO internal cohesiveness; its
relationship to the domestic political situation within member countries; its
effect on the range of NATO's political choices; its psychological impact on the
populations and governments of neutral and enemy nations; and its effect on NATO
civilian and military morale. We are of the opinion that the full implications
of these considerations cannot be analysed in advance.

Concluding Remarks
23. From the analysis in the SHAPE Report and the related Military Committee
comments, we gain the impression that there are likely to be some military
advantages from early combined use that would not be realised under later use
circumstances. In each instance there could also be military disadvantages to be
taken into account. As noted earlier, a complete assessment of the military
advantages and disadvantages, and the resulting balance, can only be made in the
light of the particular situation that obtains at the time that early combined
use is under active consideration. The same reservation applies to the
assessment of the political implications. From the political point of view, the
evaluation of advantages and disadvantages is complicated further by the fact
that many of the factors to be considered cannot be defined and analysed in
advance to the degree of detail that was achieved in the examination of military
factors. Nevertheless, political authorities should make themselves aware of the
possible political and military implications of early combined use so as to be
ready to deal effectively with a real situation. This should also help NATO to
avoid losing by default the opportunity to implement the early combined use
option. The importance of effective political consultation machinery is also
self-evident.

24. It is clear that NATO's ability to exercise the early combined use option in
time of hostilities will depend on the readiness of the existing conventional
and nuclear forces, the availability of suitable concepts and doctrine for their
employment, and the capability of military commanders to execute plans rapidly
for their effective utilisation. In this context, our recent examination of
concepts and doctrine currently governing the employment of nuclear weapons for
tactical purposes(1) leads us to the conclusion that they are fully responsive
to the needs of the early combined use option.

IV. RECOMMENDATIONS
25. The Ministers may wish to:
(a) take note of the SHAPE Report and the related MC Comments(1) as well as the
comments in this report;

(b) agree that these documents make a valuable contribution toward providing a
basis on which political authorities might assess the advantages and
disadvantages of early combined use in an actual crisis;

(c) note that early combined use is one of the options open to NATO within the
current strategy;

(d) invite the NATO Military Authorities to continue to plan for the exercise of
this option if NATO should elect to do so in time of crisis, and to maintain the
necessary military capability to implement the option if directed; and

(e) note that the operational concepts and doctrine currently governing
employment of nuclear weapons for tactical purposes(2) are sufficiently broad
and flexible to accommodate the early combined use option.
