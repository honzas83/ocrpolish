> # Illustrative Studies and Examples of Release Requests Presentation at the Autumn 1971 Ministerial Meeting
> 
> This document is a draft report by the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Staff Group concerning the presentation of illustrative studies and examples of nuclear weapon release requests at the Autumn 1971 Ministerial meeting. It outlines a proposed general approach to ensure that political authorities are aware of the implications of military recommendations for the tactical employment of nuclear weapons. The proposed process involves the preparation of illustrative studies by Major NATO Commanders, selection of material by Permanent Representatives in collaboration with military authorities, and the delivery of an oral presentation supplemented by visual aids. This framework is intended to stimulate a continuing dialogue between NATO Military and Political Authorities on nuclear policy and to help Ministers understand how Provisional Political Guidelines are applied in practice. The report emphasizes that these discussions are for illustrative purposes and should not be interpreted as restricting the positions countries might take in actual crises. It further suggests that for the Autumn 1971 meeting, only one illustrative situation should be addressed due to time constraints, specifically avoiding material already discussed at the Mittenwald meeting. The document also references contributions from the Supreme Allied Commander Europe (SACEUR) and the Supreme Allied Commander Atlantic (SACLANT).

PURPOSE

At the conclusion of their discussion at Mittenwald under the agenda item on
illustrative studies and examples of release requests, the Nuclear Planning
Group Ministers invited the Permanent Representatives, in collaboration with the
NATO Military Authorities, to conduct ar analysis of the procedural and
substantive questions involved and to arrange for a suitable presentation to be
given at the next meeting(1). The purpose of this report is to explore possible
responses to this requirement and to propose that certain actions should
accordingly be initiated.

3. The basic requirement for illustrative studies stems from the Provisional
Political Guidelines for the Initial Defensive Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons
by NATO(1), which states that it is most important that political authorities be
aware of the implications of the courses of actions which may be recommended by
military authorities. To this end, illustrative studies and analyses bearing on
the tactical employment of nuclear weapons should be available(2). At their
meeting in October 1970, the Ministers stressed the importance they attached to
illustrative studies, in general and in the context of stimulating a continuing
dialogue between the NATO Military Authorities and Political Authorities on
nuclear policy, and agreed that, at future meetings, they should discuss the
problems arising from such studies and sample release requests with a view to
assessing their suitability in fulfilling the data requirements for decision-
making(3). They re-emphasized this view at Mittenwald(4).

GENERAL APPROACH

6. Against the foregoing background and bearing in mind that the discussion of
illustrative studies is a continuing project which might extend over several
future meetings, it is suggested that an effective approach might consist of the
following elements:

(a) Preparation by the Major NATO Commanders of illustrative studies with
appropriate examples of release requests for distribution in the NP/Study series
of.documents;

(b) Selection by the Permanent Representatives, in collaboration with the NATO
Military Authorities, of a suitable portion of the available study

7. Although the general approach described above is quite similar to the one
used for the Mittenwald meeting, it has been considerably strengthened by the
addition of the presentation. This arrangement should create a much more
effective framework for the Ministerial discussion, because the presentation
provides the means for highlighting the salient features of the developing
situation, the analytical and procedural processes by which a Major NATO
Commander reaches his decision to recommend the use of a nuclear weapon, the
ways in which political guidelines might be applied in practice, and the kinds
of information and advice that would be available to higher authority in dealing
with a request for use. The availability of visual aids at the meeting should
also facilitate the discussion.

SPECIFIC ARRANGEMENTS FOR THE AUTUMN 1971 MINISTERIAL MEETING
a. Selection of Material to be addressed. A number of practical considerations
have to be taken into account, such as

(a) Amount of agenda time that can be allocated to this subject;
(b) Scope and complexity of the material;
(c) Relationship of the material to other topics that will appear on the autumn
1971 or future agendas;

(d) Time and effort involved in preparing for the meeting, in particular with
respect to the presentation.

For the autumn 1971 meeting, it would appear reasonable to assume that about an
hour would be available for an agenda item on illustrative studies, of which
perhaps 20-25 minutes would be devoted to the presentation. In this event, it
would be unrealistic to attempt to address more than one illustrative situation.

9. Some illustrative study material has already been received from the Major
NATO Commanders and has been circulated in the NPG/Study series(1). Certain
portions of these studies were placed on the Mittenwald agenda but other parts
have not yet been addressed by the Ministers. Even though the form of
presentation at the next meeting may be somewhat changed from that used at
Mittenwald, it would appear both unnecessary and inadvisable to put the material
of the Mittenwald agenda again on the agenda of the autumn Ministerial session.
The material not yet discussed by the Ministers includes several examples of
selective release procedures in the SACLAN T package(2) and an illustrative
scenario with accompanying release requests in the SACEUR package(3). Moreover,
it has been reported that SACEUR has some additional illustrative scenarios and
related requests in the drafting stage. In its present form, the SACLAN T
material does not provide an adequate basis for preparation of a presentation
and considerable additional time and effort might be needed to expand it.

10. Preparation of the Presentation.
Although it is conceivable that a country, the International Staff or the
International Military Staff could be given the task of developing and giving a
presentation based on a study document produced by a Major NATO Commander, it
would obviously be preferred that the Commander concerned be invited to draft
the presentation and to make arrangements for it to be given at the appropriate
Ministerial and Permanent Representatives meetings. It would not be expected
that this presentation would include points covered in the report by the
Permanent Representatives, or possible areas for Ministerial discussion. These
matters could be addressed immediately after the presentation by the Minister(s)
designated as discussion leader(s) for that particular agenda item or, if none
is designated, by the Chairman.

11. Timing Considerations.
To be consistent with the general approach outlined above, for a Ministerial
meeting in late October or early November the selected illustrative study should
be available to Nuclear Planning Group members as a document in the NPG/Study
series no later than 1st September, 1971.
