> # Proposed Changes to Draft Report on Demonstrative Use Illustrative Study
> 
> This document, a note by the Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group), proposes several changes and additions to the Staff Group Working Paper on the demonstrative use illustrative study. The amendments focus on clarifying the distinction between demonstrative use and other illustrative studies regarding battlefield or maritime use, and refining the terminology used to describe risks. It specifically addresses the risk of 'no target' demonstrative use and the potential for the Warsaw Pact to respond with nuclear weapons in north Norway. The document also suggests adding a new paragraph regarding the timing of demonstrative use, emphasizing that if a use has primarily a political orientation, it should be timed early enough to allow for subsequent attacks with greater military effectiveness if the political aim is not achieved. Furthermore, it introduces a section on the communication of NATO's intentions, stressing the importance of conveying a message of explanation and warning to the enemy to ensure the initiated nuclear warfare is seen as a deliberate but controlled use rather than an all-out nuclear exchange. It highlights the potential time constraints between the release authorization and the weapon employment, and suggests that Ministers discuss the political consultation, timing, and channels for such communications.

6. Page 9, insert new paragraph 13:
"13. Another aspect of timing should be mentioned. If a proposed demonstrative
use has primarily a political orientation (to show NATO's determination to
defend itself with nuclear weapons if necessary and to force the enemy to
reconsider the risks involved in continuing his aggression), its military impact
may be relatively indecisive. An example of such use in the given hypothetical
scenario might be the use of a smaller number of weapons of lower yield against
the Warsaw Pact amphibious force which might merely damage the force without
rendering it incapable of carrying out its landing. If the desired political aim
of causing the enemy to cease his aggression is not achieved, there would be
sound military justification and probably persuasive political reasons for
wanting to attack the enemy force again with sufficient nuclear weapons to
prevent it from landing the embarked troops. This strongly suggests that a
demonstrative use with limited or indecisive military effect should be timed
early enough to permit NATO to reattack with greater military effectiveness if
required."
