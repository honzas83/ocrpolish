> # Illustrative Studies and Sample Release Requests: Possible Approaches to the Preparation of the Ministerial Discussion
> 
> This document, a note by the Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Staff Group, proposes various approaches for preparing the Ministerial agenda item regarding illustrative studies and sample release requests for nuclear weapons. The primary goals of these discussions are to demonstrate how initial use guidelines are applied by NATO Military Authorities in practice, familiarize political authorities with scenarios where nuclear release might be requested, and allow political authorities to assess the suitability of sample release requests for decision-making. The Chairman suggests that these discussions be treated as a continuing project over several meetings due to the variety of possible scenarios and changes in Ministerial composition. He proposes limiting the scope of each meeting to a selected portion of material to avoid superficial treatment, specifically suggesting that the May meeting focus on Part I of SACLANT's contribution and SACEUR's maritime study. Additionally, the note suggests that Permanent Representatives prepare a list of questions to facilitate advance preparation in capitals. It also discusses the potential for short presentations by SACEUR or SACLANT and the importance of political authorities being familiar with the Major NATO Commanders' release procedures, suggesting that a briefing or reference documents be provided. The document includes a draft report by the Permanent Representatives as an annex, which reiterates the goals and recommended focus for the initial Ministerial discussions.

POSSIBLE APPROACHES TO THE PREPARATION OF THE MINISTERIAL DISCUSSION
Note by the Chairman
At our meeting on Monday, I was invited to circulate a brief note summarizing
the various alternatives that were raised during our discussion concerning the
preparation of the Ministerial agenda item dealing with illustrative studies and
sample release requests.

2. At the outset, it may be useful to note that Ministerial discussion of the
illustrative studies and of the sample release requests is expected to serve at
least three purposes:

(a) It should show to the political authorities how their initial use guidelines
might be applied by the NATO Military Authorities in practice in a given
illustrative situation;

(b) It should familiarize the political authorities with illustrative situations
in which the NATO Military Authorities might request release of nuclear weapons,
and with the way in which this would be done.

(c) It should enable political authorities to assess the suitability of sample
release requests in fulfilling the data requirements for decision-making in the

These studies, unlike the follow-on studies, are not designed to produce
insights and conclusions that would lead directly to the development of
guidelines or other policy.

3. Because of the large variety of possible illustrative situations that could
be fruitfully discussed, the changes in composition of the NPG at Ministerial
level as nations and individual Ministers change, and the anticipated arrival of
later contributions from the Major NATO Commanders, Ministerial discussion of
illustrative studies should be viewed as a continuing project if the full
benefits with respect to familiarization are to be realized. There would appear
to be some advantage in stretching the discussions over several meetings.

I. SCOPE OF MINISTERIAL DISCUSSION

4. There is already a fairly large and varied store of material available for
Ministerial discussion and additional contributions are expected. An attempt to
address all of the available material, or even all of the material that has not
been previously discussed, could lead to superficial and incoherent treatment
which would frustrate the basic objectives of the exercise. It could be more
productive to deliberately limit discussion at any one meeting to a selected
portion. Ministers could then go into as much detail as they wanted and could
explore issues in depth.

5. One approach would be to arbitrarily confine discussion at any particular
meeting to a single scenario, but this would mean that the project would have to
extend several years into the future to cover material now available or
anticipated. It would seem wiser to tailor this particular agenda item for each
meeting in the light of other business before Ministers as well as the
complexity and scope of the available studies. In this way, several studies
might be addressed at a single meeting, either by allocating a larger share of
agenda time or by considering two or more studies of similar nature at one time.
In this regard, however, it should be recognized that one complex study could
reasonably take as much Ministerial discussion time and advance preparation
effort as several relatively uncomplicated studies might require.

6. With respect to the May meeting, it is estimated that an hour and a half (two
hours maximum) might be available for the illustrative studies agenda item. This
would appear to be insufficient for an in-depth treatment of all of the material
in the SACEUR and SACLANT contributions; some part should be held for discussion
at a later meeting. On the other hand, similarities of subject matter, e.g.
maritime use, in the two contributions suggest the possibility of drawing on
both for the May agenda item.

7. The Ministerial discussion could be structured to take place in two parts:

(a) An initial consideration of Part I of SACLANT's contribution whose
conceptual nature would permit a discussion in general terms of policy
questions;

(b) A subsequent discussion of SACEUR's maritime study which would take place
against the background of a concrete scenario and would cover the issues arising
during the evolution of various phases of a developing situation.

This might provide most of the advantages of the other approaches with a minimum
of their disadvantages.

II. PREPARATION OF QUESTIONS FOR POSSIBLE DISCUSSION

8. The preparation by the Permanent Representatives of a list of questions based
on the illustrative studies and sample release requests before the Ministers may
aid the Ministerial discussion, and facilitate advance preparation in capitals.
As in the case of the issues paper for the agenda item on follow-on use studies,
the circulation of such a list of questions would not preclude Ministerial
discussion of any other points or questions not on the list that they might in
addition wish to address.

III. PRESENTATION OF STUDY SCENARIOS

9. A short presentation by SACEUR or SACLANT of the scenario under consideration
could be helpful as an introduction to the discussion of a particularly complex
study, especially if it were the only study before the Ministers. Presentations
would be of doubtful value if several studies were on the agenda. In any event,
the decision to schedule a presentation should be recognized as a departure from
the normal policy of avoiding briefings at Ministerial meetings that merely
reiterate material that has already been made available in documentary form.

IV. BRIEFING ON RELEASE PROCEDURES
10. The Ministers and high government officials should be familiar with the
Major NATO Commanders' release procedures. A briefing by SACEUR or SACLANT would
therefore be desirable from a point of view of familiarization. However, this
might run counter to the character of Ministerial meetings and may be too time-
consuming. Another approach would be to list the paper containing the text of
SACEUR's recent briefing to the Defence Planning Committee as a reference
document under this agenda item and urge that participants familiarize
themselves with the procedure in advance of the meeting. In addition, request
formats and other appropriate information could be put on display in the
conference room in Mittenwald for easy reference during the discussion.

V. CONCLUSION
11. In the light of the earlier Staff Group discussions, as summarized and
analyzed in the foregoing paragraphs, I have developed a first draft of a
possible Permanent Representatives paper which is attached at Annex. The Staff
Group may wish to address the draft in connection with its general discussion of
the approach to be adopted for handling the illustrative studies and sample
release requests at the May meeting.

(Signed) H. B. SEIM

Draft Report by the Permanent Representatives

The provisional Political Guidelines for the
Initial Defensive Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons by NATO(1),
approved by the Defence Planning Committee in December 1969,
identified and discussed in general terms a variety of
practical aids to decision-making that had potential usefulness
from the point of view of political authorities(2). Among
these were the availability of illustrative studies and
analyses bearing on the tactical employment of nuclear
weapons and the development and discussion of examples of
nuclear release requests. These illustrative studies and
sample release requests would be helpful to political
authorities in at least three ways:

(a) They should show to the political authorities how
their initial use guidelines might be applied by the
NATO Military Authorities in practice in a given
illustrative situation.

3. The objective of this series of Ministerial discussions
of illustrative studies and sample release requests is a free-
ranging, exhaustive consideration in depth of the choices open
to decision-makers when the initial use of nuclear weapons is
proposed in a particular illustrative situation, together with
an exchange of views on the implications and consequences of
the various possible choices. On the other hand, it is not
the objective of the Ministerial discussion to reach judgments
or conclusions on the substantive issues raised in a given
illustrative case, nor should countries be forced to declare
themselves in any way about their possible positions relative
to the merits of any particular release request forwarded as
an example in the context of an illustrative situation.
Finally, Ministerial discussions should not be interpreted as
restricting or prejudging decisions that might be taken in
actual crises.
