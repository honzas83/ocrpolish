> # Draft Report on WINTEX 73: Consultation Procedures and Facilities
> 
> This document is a draft report by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) providing preliminary observations on the nuclear consultation aspects of the WINTEX 73 exercise. The report outlines the purpose and background of the NPG's efforts to improve political consultation procedures for the use of nuclear weapons in wartime. WINTEX 73 was a large-scale NATO-wide Command Post Exercise conducted from February 7 to March 16, 1973, divided into two phases: FIRMSAND and BROKEN PLEDGE. The report focuses on the FIRMSAND phase, which simulated a pre-hostilities crisis and a conventional warfare phase culminating in a request for the selective release of nuclear weapons. It notes that France and Iceland did not participate in the exercise. The document evaluates the effectiveness of the Supreme Allied Commander Europe (SACEUR)'s procedures for providing timely information to political authorities, noting that 27 early notification messages were used. It also addresses the concerns of Ministers regarding the dissemination of assessments and evaluations pertinent to nuclear consultation and the Council Operations and Exercise Committee's role in strengthening these capabilities.

5. With respect to the nuclear consultation aspects of WINTEX 73, the exercise
arrangements stipulated that, during the period of active participation by the
Defence Planning Committee (which began at 1100 Brussels time on 12th March),
SACEUR could make a single request for release of nuclear weapons. However, it
was agreed that, if both British and US weapons were involved, this submission
could consist of two separate messages designed to arrive in capitals and at
NATO Headquarters at essentially the same time in order to allow them to be
considered as a single package. The exercise rules also provided that active
play of the Defence Planning Committee would not exceed 36 hours and would end
with the communication of the decisions of the nuclear powers concerned. These
exercise artificialities should be borne in mind when considering the comments
on WINTEX 73 that follow.

Dissemination of Assessments and Evaluations pertinent to Nuclear Consultation
9. The Ministers have noted(1) that nuclear consultation would take place
against the background of the flow of information envisaged in the process of
general consultation, and have expressed some concern about the adequacy of
procedures to ensure that assessments and evaluations pertinent to nuclear
consultation will be available. The Permanent Representatives, in commenting on
this problem, pointed out(2) that the Council Operations and Exercise Committee
had been charged by the Council with the general responsibility for
strengthening capabilities in this regard. The Ministers agreed with the
Permanent Representatives' suggestion that the Nuclear Planning Group migh' wish
to monitor, in an informal way, progress being made by the Council Operations
and Exercise Committee, paying particular attention to this aspect of the WINTEX
73 play (3).

Clear Identification of Interrelated Requests
14. The two release requests originated by SACEUR in WINTEX 73 concerned the
tactical use of nuclear weapons to support two adjacent corps. During
consultation on these requests in the DPC, it was pointed out by some members
that the two requests were complementary and that their military effects would
be mutually supporting. This suggests that, in cases where two or more release
requests are directly connected, specific mention of this linkage in each of the
messages concerned would ensure prompt recognition by political authorities and
possibly forestall actions or decisions that would inadvertently reduce the
military effectiveness of the proposed use.

Content of Selective Release Messages
15. It has been reported that some difficulties and delays were encountered by
political authorities in understanding the content of the WHISKEY and ROMEO
messages received in WINTEX 73 because the explanation of the precise formats
used and of certain abbreviations employed to identify weapons categories is
contained only in the Major NATO Commanders' Nuclear Weapon Release Procedures
documents which are given very limited distribution. Since it is in the mutual
interest of both the Major NATO Commanders and political authorities to minimise
possibilities for confusion or delay.insofar as communications on nuclear
release questions are concerned, further examination of this matter appears to
be warranted.

that the lack of a commonly agreed and easily recognised distinguishing mark to
identify other traffic relating to nuclear consultation, such as the early

Consultation on Communicating NATO's Intentions to the Enemy, to other non-NATO
Countries and to the Public

18. Although the WINTEX 73 experience with respect to this question was rather
limited, there were several references in reports of exercise DPC meetings and
in messages originated by participating capitals to actions that were being
taken. These ranged from making political demarches in the United Nations and
directly to third world countries to warn that a rapidly deteriorating situation
might force NATO to resort to tactical use of nuclear weapons to the general
agreement that the nuclear powers, if they decided to release weapons requested
by SACEUR, should communicate NATO's intention to the enemy and make a public
announcement. Such actions are compatible with the Provisional Political
Guidelines for the Initial Defensive Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons by NATO(2).
It is clear that there would need to be close and continuing coordination among
NATO members on such measures in order to maintain Alliance cohesion and mutual
support, similar to the coordination required in connection with many other
Alliance policies and activities in times of crisis and hostilities. However,
the fundamental significance of any NATO decision to initiate the tactical use

Actions Following Nuclear Power Decision to Release Weapons
19. In the earlier report describing the nuclear consultation process, the
Permanent Representatives noted that it can be expected that the Major NATO
Commander would inform capitals and Council/DPC when weapons have been used and
what results were achieved. The Permanent Representatives also pointed out that
a Major NATO Commander would probably inform capitals and the Council/DPC if for
any reason he was unable to use a weapon that had been released to him, although
there are no directives that require him to do so(1). In WINTEX 73 SACEUR
reported the expenditure of the weapons released to him; these messages were not
circulated to delegations in view of the prior agreement to end active play in
NATO Headquarters when the decisions by the nuclear powers had been taken. The
importance of such reports to political authorities is easily recognised.
Knowledge of whether and when released weapons have been employed and of the
results achieved would probably have considerable impact not only on political
consultation on later requests for weapon release but also on many other
Alliance actions.

20. During WINTEX 73, some other aspects of actions following a nuclear power
decision to release weapons came to light. For example, it might be important
for political authorities not only to be informed by the nuclear power that a
release had been granted but also to receive confirmation from the Major NATO
Commander concerned that he had received the release authorisation. Furthermore,
in view of the close linkage between timing of use and timing of other related
actions, such as communications of NATO's intentions, it would seem to be
necessary for the Major NATO Commander to keep political authorities informed of
any significant changes in the time of intended use.
