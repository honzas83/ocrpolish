> # Anti-Ballistic Missiles
> 
> This document is a note from the Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group), R.G. Shreffler, dated March 15, 1969, which forwards a paper by H. L. Agnew titled 'What's Wrong With Sentinel'. The attached paper argues that the United States' strategic deterrence is based on the ability to survive and respond to a first strike, and that the current Sentinel anti-ballistic missile (ABM) system, designed to protect population centers from a limited Chinese attack, is technically impractical. Agnew proposes that the Sentinel system should be modified to protect strategic missile forces, command centers, and the command structure, which is a more technically feasible 'hard point' terminal defense. He outlines several technical advantages of this approach, including limited area of space to defend, atmospheric separation of decoys, and the lower yield warheads required. The document also includes a New York Times article from February 16, 1969, reporting on potential Congressional opposition to the Sentinel deployment based on Agnew's position paper, and the Army's rebuttal to these arguments. The overall purpose is to bring these perspectives to the attention of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) of NATO.

WHAT'S WRONG WITH SENTINEL

H. L. Agnew
January 2, 1969

For over 20 years the U.S. has successfully protected its people, their
property, and the country itself against any aggressor by maintaining a policy
of strong military deterrence. This deterrence has been based primarily on the
known power and capabilities of our nuclear strategic offensive forces. These
include aircraft and missiles of all the armed services.

Shortly after World War II the U.S. was in a position, having a nuclear
monopoly, of annihilating any country completely without fear of any similar
counter attack. It was undoubtably unique in the history of mankind that a
nation had this capability and did not take advantage of it. Historians will
note many outstanding events in the 20th century. Hopefully the aforementioned
fact will not go unheralded as an indication that there was a time when nations
lived together peacefully.

However, today, world events tend to make one feel that if the Communist Bloc
nations now possessed the advantage we held in 1945 they might exercise it, or
at least threaten to do so. Consequently, until better understanding and respect
is achieved between Bloc nations and the world, the U.S. is forced to maintain
our strategic deterrent capability. More important is the necessity to
demonstrate credibly our capability to survive and respond overwhelmingly to a
first strike against the U.S.

If one accepts the premise, and I believe one must, that maintaining the
credibility of our strategic offensive nuclear forces is the number one priority
for our nations' survival then recent decisions which have been made with regard
to deployment of the Sentinel system must be reexamined.

In brief, the announced purpose for the present Sentinel deployment is to
minimize some degree of the fatalities which the U.S. population will incur if
attacked by the Chinese using a very small missile force or by the inadvertent
launch at the U.S. of a nuclear tipped missile by any nation. The possibility of
protecting our strategic forces with some form of active defense was recognized
but has not been seriously addressed. It is this very point which causes me
concern. Bethe and Garwin have very clearly, in their articles in the Scientific
American of March 1968, pointed out the technical problems of providing an area
defense for cities or population centers. Unfortunately in their effort to
highlight the utility of an active defense for large population areas they chose
to ignore the technical feasibility of providing active defense for our
strategic forces and the impact this would have on strengthening the credibility
of our deterrent.

The purpose of maintaining a strong deterrent is to make certain that a nuclear
exchange never takes place and that no nation is tempted to launch its forces
because it feels it can do so without exposing itself to equal treatment through
retaliation. It should be realized by all parties that if an all out nuclear
exchange were ever to take place everyone would lose and I believe that the
Communists or any nuclear power must realize this as well as we do. The Sentinel
system could add significantly to the credibility of the U.S. deterrent by being
deployed to provide additional survivability for our strategic missile forces,
the command centers which control their use, and the command structure. An ABM
system in this role would rely primarily on terminal defense using the Sprint
system but would also require a finite number of Spartan's to break up high
density raids which might tax the firing rate of Sprint which would operate on a
one-on-one basis.

Technically hard point terminal defense is a much easier problem than that
associated with area defense.

First, only those enemy warheads coming within a very limited area in space need
be considered for attack.

Second, low altitude intercepts allows one to take full advantage of the
separation of real RV's from chaff and decoys by the atmosphere.

Third, since encounters are in the atmosphere on a one-on-one basis the
defensive warheads can be of relatively low yield compared to Spartan. The radar
blackout problems are minimized due to this and to the fact that ion
recombination times are relatively short in the atmosphere.

Fourth, and perhaps even more important, the total time over which one must
defend ones forces is finite, since command and control centers need only be
defended for the time required to exercise the forces under their control and
the missile forces need only be defended until they are launched; (in contrast
cities need to be defended for the lifetime of their inhabitants which presents
an extremely difficult task).

Fifth, more time is available for engagement, and with lower yields engagements
can be made at lower altitudes with little additional self inflicted damage to
ground installations.

Sixth, defensive warhead development is technically easier because the smaller
yields so required do not present the basic problems which the Spartan test
program has under the limited Test Ban Treaty.

And seventh, defense installations are primarily located in areas of existing
military bases thus minimizing problems presently being posed by citizens
worried over safety matters or angered over dislocation problems.

Finally, we could accept destruction rates of 30 or even 60 percent on the
defended missiles, which would be unthinkable for defended cities.

Deploying Sentinel to defend strategic forces and their command and control
centers would be a project of finite size and would not extend on and on such as
a defense of cities might be forced to do through pressures arising from many
quarters.

An active defense to supplement the passive defense (hardening) of our strategic
forces is vital and should lend stability to the world situation by maintaining
our nuclear second strike capability. While the Sentinel Chinese-only
orientation is not self evident, a defense of the deterrent would for a long
time be needed only against the Soviet Union. Hopefully, the new administration
will realize this and direct that the Sentinel deployment be appropriately
modified.

THE WASHINGTON POST
Friday, Feb. 28, 1969
U.S. Aide Opposes ABMs Near Cities
By William Chapman
Washington Post Staff Writer
A nuclear weapons specialist has advised Congressmen that the controversial
Sentinel anti-ballistic missile system should be deployed to protect missile
sites instead of populated areas.

A paper submitted by Harold Agnew of the Los Alamos, N.M., laboratories argues
that the Sentinel should be used to shield missile sites and control centers
against the possibility of enemy missile attacks.

The paper, called "What's Wrong With Sentinel," was circulated on Feb. 6 among
members of the Joint Committee on Atomic Energy and made available to several
reporters.

Its distribution was the latest indication that Sentinel proponents on the
Committee now believe they must seek deployment away from cities if the program
is to survive in the Senate this year. Strong local opposition to Sentinel sites
in at least five states has created doubts that a majority of the Senate will
vote for procurement appropriations this year.

The most likely alternative, the Sentinel's Congressional supporters have said,
is to place the system near remote U.S. offensive missile sites and avoid the
wrath of citizens. Agnew's paper provides a technological rationale for that
kind of deployment.

Agnew observes that missile installations generally are located on established
military bases and would create no problems for "worried citizens."

However, Agnew's main argument is that deployment for missile site defense is
technologically easier than for "area defense" of cities.

For one reason, he wrote, the area of space to be defended is smaller.
Another reason is that incoming missiles could be intercepted in the atmosphere
because the hazards of nuclear fallout would be fewer than in populated areas.
This means, Agnew wrote, that fewer enemy penetration aids and decoys would be
effective because they would be "separated out" by the atmosphere.

By defending relatively small missile sites instead of wide areas, Agnew wrote,
the Sentinel could use low-yield warheads, thus minimizing the hazard of
blocking out radar defenses.

Agnew, head of the weapons division at Los Alamos, has been a consultant for the
Joint Committee. The Congressional sources who made his paper available
yesterday emphasized that his views are respected by the Committee members, most
of whom favor ABM deployment.
