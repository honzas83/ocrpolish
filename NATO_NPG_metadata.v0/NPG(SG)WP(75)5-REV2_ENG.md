> # Report on Phase II of the Work Programme on Follow-on Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons
> 
> This document is a draft report by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) regarding the second phase of a study on the follow-on tactical use of nuclear weapons by NATO. The report identifies policy issues for Ministerial discussion and suggests implementation for Phase III, which involves formulating political guidelines for follow-on tactical use. The work programme was divided into three phases: Phase I focused on individual regional studies, Phase II provided a comparative analysis and synthesis of those findings, and Phase III aims to formulate political guidelines for endorsement by the Nuclear Defence Affairs Committee and approval by the Defence Planning Committee. The report concludes that follow-on tactical nuclear use is not a viable alternative to a strong conventional defence posture but can serve as a signal to compel an enemy to cease attacks and withdraw. It emphasizes that the possession and ability to execute nuclear options are key to ensuring Alliance deterrence. The document also discusses the relationship between conventional forces and nuclear weapons, noting that conventional forces must be able to exploit and support nuclear operations. It mentions that the the theatre nuclear threat affects all military operations and that a higher conventional capability increases the nuclear threshold. Finally, it addresses the imbalance of reserve formations between NATO and the Warsaw Pact, noting that reinforcements may become a decisive factor in a controlled nuclear exchange.

I. PURPOSE
1. This report identifies, for Ministerial discussion, a number of policy issues
related to the 'Report on Phase II of the Work Programme on Follow-on Tactical

III.

II. BACKGROUND
2. In November 1969, the Nuclear Planning Group approved the 'Provisional
Political Guidelines for the Initial Defensive Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons
by NATO'(2) as policy guidance for national authorities and as a directive to
NATO Military Authorities At the same meeting, Ministers recognised the need for
a study of follow-on tactical use of nuclear weapons, to examine the phases of
tactical use subsequent to initial use by NATO and to explore NATO responses to
an initial tactical use of nuclear weapons by the Warsaw Pact. Rather than
attempt this task in a single comprehensive study, they agreed to proposals put
forward by the Permanent Representatives which defined(3) the objectives,
general assumptions and broad structure of an overall work programme to be
carried out in a series of interrelated steps.

Political Objectives and Implications of Follow-on Use

II. Political Objective and Scale of Use. If NATO's initial use, whatever its
magnitude and duration, failed to induce an aggressor to make the political
decision to cease his attack and withdraw, and if other available political
steps were unsuccessful in achieving this objective, the Alliance would have to
consider further nuclear use in order to achieve its objectives. The Phase II
Study Team report concludes in general(1) that in the follow-on phase NATO might
need to provide more escalatory evidence of its resolution. On the other hand,
the report does not define any upper limits of follow-on use(2).

12. Given the political objective of follow-on use (induce the enemy to make the
political decision to cease the attack and withdraw) and also given the failure
of achieving this objective through initial use, follow-on use on a smaller
scale than initial use(3) may be less credible, and therefore less successful in
convincing an enemy to terminate his attack. Therefore, the selection of
particularly sensitive targets would be essential to ensure that the political
objective is achieved.

examined interrelationships between available theatre options and the

Follow-on Use and NATO Strategy.

18. During former NIG Ministerial discussions on implications of NATO strategy,
the fundamental importance of theatre nuclear together with conventional forces,
in the era of strategic equivalence has been recognised. The Phase II Study Team
Report has likewise concluded(1) that the tactical nuclear use option in
particular is a cardinal component in NATO's whole strategy of deterrence and
flexibility in response. Furthermore, it is evident that the basic NATO military
strategy, MC 14/3, provides the Alliance with sufficient conceptual flexibility
for a wide range of options for the use of nuclear weapons.

19. While it is clear that implementation of the strategy can only be determined
in light of the circumstances existing in the time of crisis, it is essential
that the response be such that NATO's overall objective (the preservation or
restoration of the integrity and security of the NATO area) is achieved.
Flexibility thus refers to the degree of selectivity in the forms of NATO
reactions as well as to the related process through which such reactions are
implemented. It not only requires the actual ability to cope with various types
of military aggression, but also assumes that NATO has the resolve to apply the
appropriate degree of military power. Such a strategy, in emphasising forward
defence, therefore encompasses a denial military concept as well as the aim to
terminate the hostilities at the lowest level possible consistent with achieving
NATO's objectives.

strategy of flexible response and the concept of political crisis management(4).
These observations underline the necessity of considering possible further
developments, i.e., after the nuclear threshold has been crossed, when
consulting on the nature of initial use.

Summary

21. :c agree with the fundamental conclusions of the Phase II Study Team
Report(1). While follow-on use of nuclear weapons (as well as initial use) does
not provide a viable alternative to an adequate alliance conventional defence
posture, the tactical use option is an essential link between the Alliance's
conventional capabilities and its strategic nuclear forces and might provide the
means of bringing the conflict to an end. In possessing this option, NATO could
not be threatened with a form of attack to which it could not reply. It could
resort to the tactical use option for direct defence or for deliberate
escalation(2). Therefore, the option for the tactical use of nuclear weapons, as
one leg of the triade, is a cardinal component in NATO's whole strategy of
deterrence and flexibility of response. In this regard, the greater the
capability of conventional forces to defend against an attack, the higher the
threshold for the tactical use of nuclear weapons. However, if NATO must
escalate to achieve its objectives, we conclude that NATO can demonstrate its
resolve to continue with nuclear defence measures through combinations of
various factors such as different targets, magnitude of use, location of targets
including use in an extended geographical area, and time or duration of the
follow-on use. Since political guidance for follow-on use (Phase III) would,
among other matters and in connection with the implementation of agreed NATO
strategy, have to concentrate on ways to provide more escalatory evidence, we
consider the detailed treatment of this critical element in the Phase II Study
Team Report as most useful.

IV. FOLLOW-ON USE WORK PROGRAMME - INITIATION OF PHASE III

22. With 'IG Ministers having considered the Phase II Study Team Report and this
related NFG Permanent 'Representatives' Report, the question of the scope,
purpose and timing of Phase III of the Follow-on Use Work Programme requires
Ministers' attention and direction. The Phase III Task, as envisaged by the NFG
Ministers in 1971(1) was the "formulation of Policy Guidance." The Ministers did
not, at that time, attempt to provide specific terms of reference to guide the
Phase III effort in advance of the identification of policy questions(2) flowing
from the comparative analysis and synthesis of findings during Phase II. Now,
however, on the basis of the work done in Phases I and II, the process of
formulation of policy guidance could be initiated.

inception the NFG has devoted much attention to problems related to the

were the preparation of political guidelines for initial defensive tactical use
of nuclear weapons(3), consultation procedures(4), guidelines for the use of
ADMS(5) and the role of theatre strike forces(6). These efforts,
