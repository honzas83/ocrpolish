> # United States Military Procurement Authorisation Act
> 
> This document consists of a cover note from the Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) and an annex containing a letter from the United States Permanent Representative to the North Atlantic Council and excerpts from the 1975 United States Military Procurement Authorisation Act. The act's Section 302(d) mandates a study on the overall concept for the use of tactical nuclear weapons in Europe, their relation to deterrence and conventional defense, and the steps to develop a coordinated nuclear posture for the North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) Alliance. Additionally, Section 302(c) requires the Secretary of Defense to assess the costs and loss of non-nuclear combat effectiveness resulting from a lack of standardisation in weapons systems, ammunition, and fuel among NATO members. The document also includes a correspondence from Donald Rumsfeld to Paolo Pansa Cedroni, the Acting Secretary General of NATO, and a notice for the next meeting of the Staff Group on September 23, 1974, to discuss these legislative changes and other reports. The United States recognizes the importance of these studies to NATO and commits to keeping Allies informed of the results provided to Congress.

UNITED STATES MILITARY PROCUREMENT AUTHORISATION ACT
Note by the Chairman
The United States Permanent Representative to the North Atlantic Council has
recently circulated a letter concerning certain provisions of direct interest to
NATO contained in the 1975 United States Military Procurement Authorisation Act
which became law on August 5, 1974(1).

2. One of these provisions (contained in Section 302(d) of the law) calls for a
study on the overall concept for use of tactical nuclear weapons in Europe. It
falls, therefore, within the range of matters to which the NP is particularly
committed. Members of the NP Staff Group may wish to discuss this issue at their
meeting on Monday, 23rd September, 1974.

3. For ease of reference, the above mentioned letter and the attachment to it
are reproduced at Annex.

(Signed) R.E. SHEARER.
NATO, 1110 Brussels.

Excerpts from Military Procurement Authorisation Act of 1975

Sec. 302.

(a) The United States military forces in Europe can reduce head-
quarters and noncombat military personnel relative to the number of
combat personnel located in Europe. Therefore, except in the event
of imminent hostilities in Europe, the noncombat components of the
total United States military strength in Europe authorised as of
June 30, 1974, shall be reduced by 18,000. Such reduction shall be
completed not later than June 30, 1976, and not less than 6,000 of
such reduction shall be completed on or before June 30, 1975;
however, the Secretary of Defense is authorised to increase the combat
component strength of United States forces in Europe by the amount
of any such reduction made in noncombat personnel. The Secretary of
Defense shall report semi-annually to the Congress on all actions
taken to improve the combat proportion of United States forces in
Europe. The first report shall be submitted not later than March 31,
1975.

(b) For purposes of this section, the combat component of the
Army includes only the infantry, cavalry, artillery, armored, combat
engineers, special forces, attack assault helicopter units, air
defense, and missile combat units of battalion or smaller size;
the combat component of the Navy includes only the combat ships
(aircraft carrier, cruiser, destroyer, submarine, escort and
amphibious a sault ships) and co-bat aircraft wings (fighter, attack,
reconnaissance, and patrol); the combat component of the Air Force
includes only the tactical fighter reconnaissance, tactical airlift,
fighter interceptor and bomber units of wing or smaller size.

(c) The Secretary of Defense shall undertake a specific
assessment of the costs and possible loss of nonnuclear combat
effectiveness of the military forces of the North Atlantic Treaty
Organisation countries caused by the failure of the North Atlantic
Treaty Organisation members, including the United States, to
standardise weapons systems, ammunition, fuel, and other military
impedimenta for land, air, and naval forces. The Secretary of
Defense shall also develop a list of standardisation actions that
could improve the overall North Atlantic Treaty Organisation non-
nuclear defense capability or save resources for the Alliance as
a whole. He shall also evaluate the relative priority and effect
of each such action. The Secretary shall submit the results of
these assessments and evaluations to the Congress and subsequently
shall also cause them to be brought before the appropriate North
Atlantic Treaty Organisation bodies in order that the suggested
actions and recommendations can become an integral part of the
overall North Atlantic Treaty Organisation review of force goals
and development of force plans. The Secretary of Defense shall

His Excellency
P. Pansa Cedroni
Acting Secretary General
North Atlantic Treaty Organization
Brussels, Belgium
Dear Paolo:
As you are probably already aware, the 1975 United States Military Procurement
Authorisation Act became law on August 5. Section 302 of this law contains
certain provisions which are of direct interest to NATO. These provisions are
quoted in the annex to this letter.

With respect to Section 302(a), you will note that the law does not require or
presage a reduction in the overall authorised strength of U.S. armed forces in
Europe. It does require a reduction by June 30, 1976 of 18,000 spaces in the
authorised strength of the non-combat components of these forces, but it
empowers the Secretary of Defense to increase the number of authorised spaces
for combat components by an amount equal to the reduction in non-combat spaces.
Thus, the law will not require unilateral U.S. force reductions; it simply
permits a restructuring action of the type that has been underway in U.S. forces
in Europe for some time. The United States will, as it has in the past, keep you
and our Allies fully informed of significant restructuring actions affecting our
forces committed to NATO.

Under the terms of Section 302(c), the Secretary of Defense must take several
actions to respond to the Congress on matters of standardisation. First, he must
assess the costs and loss of non-nuclear combat effectiveness of the NATO
military forces which result directly from the failure of NATO nations,
including the United States, to standardise weapons systems, ammunition, fuel,
and other military material. Concurrently, he will develop a list of recommended
standardisation actions for improvement of the existing situation and evaluate
the priority and effect of each such action. The Secretary will then submit the
results of these assessments and evaluations to the Congress and subsequently to
NATO for inclusion in the overall review of force goals and development of force
plans. The Secretary must report semi-annually to the Congress on progress
achieved in these areas, with the first report due not later than January 31,
1975. The reporting cycle, in general, will follow by a few months the semi-
annual Conference of National Armaments Directors (CNAD), the primary NATO body
charged with responsibility for review and coordination in research, development
and production.
