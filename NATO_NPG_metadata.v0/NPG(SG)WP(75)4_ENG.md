> [!abstract] 
> # Observations on WINTEX 75
> 
> This report, drafted by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Staff Group, outlines preliminary observations on nuclear aspects of Exercise WINTEX 75. It emphasizes that the initial use of nuclear weapons must be clearly detectable by the enemy and controlled by NATO to satisfy political objectives, such as inducing the enemy to cease attacks and withdraw. The document notes that initial use at sea or in air-defence may not be suitable for conveying clear political messages because the timing and location are often controlled by the enemy. It further discusses the importance of a pause between initial and follow-on use to allow the enemy time to make a decision, noting that follow-on use would likely be more intense and extended. The report also confirms that existing consultation procedures for the use of nuclear weapons are responsive to crisis management needs, including the role of the Military Committee. It highlights a first-time occurrence in a NATO exercise where a country requested nuclear retaliation in response to massive chemical attacks. Additionally, the document addresses the communication of NATO's intentions, including public declarations and warnings to the enemy, suggesting that warning (WHISKEY) messages should contain more detailed information. The report concludes with recommendations for Ministers to take note of the report and discuss its implications for further study or action.
> 
> ## Categories/Topics
> 
> #Doctrine-and-Strategy/Flexible-Response — The document discusses the 'nuclear threshold', 'initial use' versus 'follow-on use', and the need for a 'pause' between them to allow the enemy to withdraw, which are core components of the Flexible Response strategy (MC 14/3) aimed at deliberate escalation rather than immediate total war.
> #Doctrine-and-Strategy/Nuclear-Strategy-and-Strike-Planning — The text focuses on the technical and political requirements for target selection for 'initial use', the timing and intensity of strikes, and the specific types of release messages (SIERRA and ROMEO) used in execution.
> #Internal-Alliance-Dynamics/Command-and-Control-Authority — The document explicitly addresses the mechanisms for authorizing nuclear use, including 'pre-conditioned release' (SIERRA messages), the role of the Military Committee, and the consultation procedures for 'country requests' for nuclear retaliation.

II. DISCUSSION
Remarks on Initial Use
2. The Provisional Political Guidelines(1) state that the fundamental objective
of initial use, as of any military act, will always be political, and, as any
selective tactical use, initial use is aimed at inducing the enemy to cease his
attack and withdraw. It is also noted that initia? use should implicitly contain
a message of warning to the enemy(2). It appears, therefore, that such use must
have certain peculiar characteristics to satisfy these two requirements. First
of all, the initial employment of nuclear weapons should be clearly detectable
by the enemy and be in direct connection with the ongoing military
operations(3). Secondly, the use should be such that the choice of the time (and

Remarks on Follow-on Use
6. Initial use, while crossing the nuclear threshold, has the political purpose
of warning the enemy that he miscalculated NATO's reaction and willingness to
defend itself by even escalating to the use of nuclear weapons, and consequently
force him to cease the attack and to withdraw(1). Follow-on use, being the
consequence of initial use having failed, must clearly carry the greater warning
that NATO is prepared to escalate further. It is recognised in fact(2) that
follow-on use would very likely be more intense and/or more extended than
initial use and that it should achieve effective military results. If the
difference of political purposes between initial and follow-on use is
recognised, these forms of use should be separated by time sufficient for the
enemy to make a decision and show clearly his further intentions. It follows
that a pause between initial and follow-on use would be very useful for
political purposes. The length of such pause would obviously depend upon
political and military factors that can be evaluated only in an actual
situation.

7. The foregoing remarks and experience gained from WINTEX 7 support the view
expressed above(3) that the time of initial use must be known with sufficient
accuracy to permit sound consultation on follow-on use to take place. If for any
reason the execution time of already approved initial use were uncertain, any
immediately following consultation on further requests for use (i.e., for
follow-on use) would be unwise.

Implementation of Consultation Procedures
8. In general, WINTEX 75 confirmed that those portions of existing procedures
for consultation on possible use of nuclear weapons which were implemented
during the exercise are responsive to crisis management necessities in case of
hostilities. This applies also to the role played by the Military Committee(1).

9. Country request. For the first time in a NATO exercise, a country suffering
from what was perceived as a selective but major escalatory enemy step (massive
chemical attacks) suggested, on its own and in a general sense, nuclear use in
retaliation. This action was in line with the Provisional Political
Guidelines(2) and with an earlier Permanent Representatives' Report(3)
mentioning the possibility of country requests. It is also in line with the
spirit of the Consultation Guidelines(4) which refer to the special weight which
should be given to countries most directly affected. Since such requests may not
be laid out in the same format used by the Major NATO Commanders, but have to be
implemented by them, immediate military advice would be needed for political
consultation. Experience from WINTEX 75 demonstrates that existing procedures
for consultation on possible use of nuclear weapons are responsive to this
objective.

10. Communicating NATO's Intentions. During WINTEX 75, a number of CNI aspects
were discussed during the DPC meetings: declaration to the public (NATC, enemy
and third countries), warning to the enemy, explanation to the enemy on the use
and value of a demonstrative use as one of the options available to NATO(1).
While all these issues have so far been raised in various nuclear policy
documents, the DPC refrained from any specific action in this context pending
the approval of policy guidance by ministers(2). Nevertheless, there was a
common understanding on the importance of such actions and on the necessity of
starting related consultation prior to receipt of requests (ROMEO). This might
be particularly facilitated, if the warning (WHISKEY) messages contain as much
as possible of the information needed for this purpose.
