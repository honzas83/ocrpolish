> # Articles Related to Nuclear Defence
> 
> This document is a notice from the North Atlantic Council's Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) dated October 31, 1978, which provides four annexes containing articles on nuclear affairs. Annex A, written by G. Philip Hughes, discusses the challenges of maintaining a coherent NATO theater-nuclear doctrine for deterrence in Europe, specifically addressing the 'Gordian Knot' of compensating for conventional force inadequacies while deterring Soviet nuclear attacks. The text explores the evolution of the Cold War nuclear doctrine from 'massive retaliation' to 'flexible response' and the current efforts by the United States Army to develop a new employment doctrine for tactical-nuclear weapons. Other annexes include articles by Hugh O. Muir and William W. Wade regarding President Carter's decisions on the production and deployment of neutron weapons. The notice is signed by R.E. Shearer and originated from Brussels, Belgium.

CUTTING THE GORDIAN KNOT: A THEATER-NUCLEAR FORCE FOR DETERRENCE IN EUROPE*
by G. Philip Hughes
For more than twenty years, the United States has deployed a varied array of
theater-nuclear weapons and delivery systems in Europe. Systems for short-range
nuclear delivery, such as nuclear-capable artillery and short-range surface-to-
surface missiles (currently Lance and Honest John), are intended to defeat enemy
forces on the battlefield. Systems for longer-range nuclear delivery, such as
land- and carrier-based nuclear-capable tactical aircraft, Pershing short-range
ballistic missiles, and Poseidon submarine-launched ballistic missiles (SLBMs),
are intended to destroy military objectives deep in Warsaw Pact territory. In
addition, nuclear-armed Nike-Hercules air-defense weapons are intended to thwart
massed Soviet air raids, while atomic demolition munitions (ADMs) are to be
emplaced as mines to create obstacles to enemy troop movements.

Despite these varied capabilities, there have been persistent criticisms that
the United States and its allies have had no coherent doctrine for the use of
these weapons. It has been objected that this incoherent, incrementally
accumulated force structure was not well suited to support any particular
doctrine.

Recently, there has been renewed interest in developing a coherent NATO theater-
nuclear doctrine, and a number of new weapons-development programs are under way
to support the emerging doctrine. Before these efforts progress too far, the
premises on which they are based should be critically examined to determine if
this is the way U.S. theater-nuclear forces should be developed for the future.
If not, then an alternative course should be chosen.

The Gordian Knot: Two Missions for Theater-Nuclear Forces
One of the most fundamental problems of West European security in the postwar
period has been to find a way of successfully

without sustaining commensurately large Western forces in peacetime. This
problem forms the basis for the U.S. policy of maintaining theater-nuclear
weapons in Europe. These weapons were first deployed overseas in the mid-1950s,
at a time when the Soviet Union lacked both an effective strategic-nuclear force
for use against the United States and comparable tactical-nuclear weapons for
use in Europe. The original purpose of these weapons was to compensate for
inadequate Western conventional forces by serving as a substitute for them. The
weapons were intended to supplement U.S. strategic-nuclear forces in delivering
the nuclear retaliatory blow, which was the threatened response to any Soviet
aggression under the "massive retaliation" doctrine. The unprecedented
devastation resulting from such an attack would presumably deter the Soviets
from aggression. Moreover, the United States could credibly threaten such a
response with impunity, since the Soviets lacked effective nuclear retaliatory
forces. Under this doctrine, the U.S. nuclear strikes would determine the
outcome of the conflict. Since the role of conventional forces would be
relatively unimportant, and certainly not decisive, large conventional forces
would not have to be maintained.

Soviet acquisition of strategic-nuclear capabilities in the late fifties and
early sixties made the massive retaliation doctrine incredible. The United
States could no longer threaten to respond immediately to any Soviet aggression
with strategic and tactical-nuclear strikes without risking large-scale
destruction to U.S. territory. Since nuclear weapons could no longer substitute
almost completely for conventional forces, efforts were made to strengthen NATO
conventional capabilities in order to deal with Soviet non-nuclear aggression
under the doctrine of "flexible response." However, since NATO was either
unwilling or unable to neutralize Soviet conventional-force advantages through
these efforts, theater-nuclear weapons retained the role of compensating for
conventional-force deficiencies. Instead of providing a "cheap" substitute for
conventional forces, theater-nuclear weapons were now intended to provide an
ultimate back-up for conventional defense. A Soviet conventional attack would be
met initially with a NATO conventional defense. Should the Soviet attack prove
to be overwhelming and threaten the defeat of NATO, however, theater-nuclear
weapons would be used to provide massive firepower in support of conventional
defenses. These weapons would destroy attacking Soviet forces and important
rear-area targets, and it was expected that this

would halt and defeat the Soviet attack. Since the use of theater-nuclear forces
could ultimately be relied upon to destroy an overwhelming Soviet conventional
attack, NATO had little reason to provide extensive conventional forces adequate
to deal with this threat independently.

Soviet deployment of nuclear-armed medium- and intermediate-range ballistic
missiles (M/IRBMs) as well as shorter-range tactical-nuclear missiles in the
late fifties and early sixties led to the addition of a second mission to U.S.
theater-nuclear forces. These forces became increasingly important for deterring
a Soviet nuclear attack against NATO Europe. It was felt that the threat to
retaliate initially with theater-nuclear forces would be more credible than with
strategic forces. Their limited capabilities would not threaten the total
destruction of the Soviet Union, and their use would not require launching
strikes from U.S. territory, thus exposing the United States to nuclear
retaliation.

These two missions of theater-nuclear forces — compensating to NATO conventional
force inadequacies and deterring a Soviet nuclear attack — raise three problems
for the United States and its allies. First, why are NATO theater-nuclear forces
assumed to deter a Soviet nuclear attack, but Soviet nuclear forces do not deter
NATO theater-nuclear use in support of faltering conventional defenses? Second,
since theater-nuclear weapons would probably be used only late in the war, after
Soviet troops had penetrated Western Europe, many of these weapons would have to
be used on NATO territory. How, then, could NATO credibly threaten the use of
such destructive weapons, knowing that to carry out the threat would be to
destroy much of the territory being defended? If NATO refrained from using
short-range nuclear weapons on its territory, and sought instead to halt an
overwhelming Soviet conventional attack with nuclear strikes on Warsaw Pact
rear-area targets, a third problem would be faced. How would the destruction of
these distant, rear-area targets relieve the military pressure on NATO troops
faced with the imminent collapse of their defenses? These three problems —
engendered by the dual mission of NATO’s theater-nuclear forces — have
constituted a Gordian Knot that NATO has been attempting to unravel for many
years.

for threatened NATO defenses without endangering the safety of NATO troops; (2)
minimal levels of unintended damage to civilian facilities, so that NATO will
not be inhibited from using tactical-nuclear weapons for fear of destroying its
own territory, and (3) minimal likelihood of Soviet nuclear retaliation, also so
that NATO will not be deterred from using these weapons.

In recent years, the U.S. Army has attempted to develop a new employment
doctrine for tactical-nuclear weapons 'nuclear artillery shells and short-range
missiles embodying the above characteristics. This doctrine emphasizes the
delivery of pre-planned 'packages' of nuclear weapons in previously identified
areas of probable enemy advance or operations. The numbers and types of weapons
and the aim-points in each package would be designed to maximize the lethal
nuclear effects over the area without causing unintended damage to nearby
civilian concentrations. The delivery of these packages would be specifically
ordered or approved by the president. Several of these packages, each including
perhaps 150 nuclear weapons,' would be delivered suddenly in a short period of
time, either in small areas or across the front of operations, to destroy large
numbers of attacking Soviet forces very rapidly. It is hoped that the stunning
effect of such a strike would halt the Soviet advance and induce the Soviets to
cease hostilities or enter into negotiations.

Recently, the U.S. Air Force has begun to study new employment concepts, which
would be consonant with the new Army planning, for air-delivered theater-nuclear
weapons. The Air Force concepts would emphasize the delivery of nuclear weapons
against identified targets in the Warsaw Pact's tactical rear area, between the
forward edge of the battle area and the troop-assembly areas. These plans, then,
would maximize the prompt military effects of air-delivered nuclear strikes,
which would presumably be carried out in such a way as to minimize collateral
damage to nearby civilian facilities.

The current inventory of theater-nuclear weapons, most of which were developed
between the mid-fifties and the mid-sixties, would be unsuitable for the new
Army and Air Force plans. They are said to have high or inappropriate yields,

Army and Air Force theater-nuclear employment concepts, appear to resolve
successfully the three logical problems engendered by the dual missions of
NATO's theater-nuclear forces. First, by facilitating the delivery of nuclear
strikes directly against attacking forces, the new weapons and employment
concepts would relieve the immediate pressure on NATO defenses. Second, by pre-

the new plans and weapons would negate any appearance that NATO might be self-
deferred from using theater-nuclear weapons for fear of destroying its own
territory or troops. This would improve the credibility of the U.S. threat to
use nuclear weapons first if confronted with an overwhelming Soviet conventional
attack. Third, by planning for a sudden and stunning delivery of the nuclear
strike, with clear spatial and temporal limitations and with refined nuclear
effects, these plans seek to minimize the risk of Soviet nuclear retaliation.
This expectation may be based upon a presumed absence of comparable Soviet
weapons or employment plans, or upon a presumed Soviet desire to emulate U.S.
restraint. In either event, minimization of the risk of Soviet retaliation both
reduces the deterrent effect of Soviet nuclear forces and enhances the
credibility of the U.S. threat to use nuclear weapons first in support of
faltering conventional defenses.

Cutting the Knot: A Single Mission for Theater-Nuclear Forces

The new theater-nuclear employment concepts and enhanced-radiation developments
appear to focus largely on the question of how NATO can avoid being deterred, or
self-deterred, from using nuclear weapons in support of deficient conventional
forces. The implicit assumption that seems to underlie the current policy
approach is this: if the obstacles in the way of using these weapons to
compensate for conventional force inadequacies can be removed, the second
theater-nuclear mission (detering a Soviet nuclear attack) will take care of
itself. Thus, the new doctrinal and weapons developments for U.S. theater-
nuclear forces seem to concentrate more heavily on improving U.S. capabilities
to use nuclear weapons first, in support of faltering conventional defenses,
than on deterring a Soviet nuclear attack against NATO.

There are reasons, however, to doubt that the current emphasis is well placed.
First, it is not clear that the new, more discriminate U.S. theater-nuclear
weapons will help deter a Soviet attack. Since they are supposedly more readily
usable in combat, it is doubtful that U.S. reliance on the threat of nuclear
escalation to back up deficient conventional forces will decrease: if anything,
this reliance on nuclear weapons will probably increase. Consequently, it will
be even more important for the Soviets to pre-emptively

an inherent difference between them.

The deterrent mission of theater-nuclear forces came to be assigned as a result
of circumstances beyond the control of the United States or NATO. The Soviet
deployment of M/IRBMs and, later, of tactical-nuclear missile systems — whether
in response to NATO capabilities or for independent motives — probably could not
have been prevented by any NATO action. If the United States and NATO could not
have prevented these deployments, they had no choice but to deter their use
against Western Europe. NATO, for the reasons mentioned earlier, could not rely
exclusively on U.S. strategic-nuclear forces to deter a Soviet nuclear attack on
Europe. Thus, NATO was forced to rely primarily on U.S. theater-nuclear weapons,
supplemented by European national nuclear forces, for this deterrence. The
point, then, is clear. The deterrent mission of theater-nuclear forces is
indispensable and paramount.

maintained by the Warsaw Pact. However, means other than theater-nuclear weapons
are available to offset Soviet conventional force advantages. These could

numerical advantages. Since it is theoretically possible for NATO to offset
Soviet conventional force advantages with improvements in its own conventional
forces, the following conclusion emerges. If only one of the two current
missions of NATO’s theater-nuclear forces can logically be fulfilled, and if the
deterrence mission is indispensable while the mission of supporting conventional
forces is not, then the deterrence mission should receive priority. Other means
should be sought to reduce the deficiencies of NATO’s conventional forces. The
ultimate reliance of these forces on the use of theater-nuclear weapons to save
them from an overwhelming Soviet conventional attack must be substantially
reduced. Stronger and more costly conventional forces may be the price NATO must
pay for a credible and effective theater-nuclear deterrent

Even if increased emphasis is placed on the NATO theater-nuclear mission of
deterring a Soviet nuclear strike against Western Europe, current and planned
forces are not adequate for this role. To understand why this is so, it is
necessary to explore some of the features of the current force structure.

Limitations of Current Forces as a Deterrent
It has long been recognized that for a nuclear deterrent force to be effective,
it must be able to survive an enemy attempt to destroy it and then be able to
carry out the threatened response. In addition, some theorists contend that the
deterrent force should be capable of delivering a variety of retaliatory
responses proportional to the provocation. Should the force be capable of only
one response, there is the danger that the adversary might launch some
provocation in which the consequences of carrying out the response would be
worse than doing nothing. In this situation, the evident probability that the
threatened response would not be carried out weakens the credibility of the
deterrent. These, then, are the general requirements for a nuclear deterrent
force. In the NATO context,

however, additional, political requirements intrude. The first is that the
allies be able to participate in the allied nuclear deterrent by having a role
in the delivery of nuclear strikes. This ensures that their status in the
alliance will not be so obviously inferior to that of the United States, and
that they will have some voice in the momentous decisions about the use of
nuclear weapons in their defense. A second political requirement is that the
weapons stored in Europe be safe and secure in peacetime. It is here that our
discussion of the shortcomings of the current force as a deterrent must begin.

In deploying theater-nuclear weapons to Europe, the United States has long faced
two important constraints: not only must the weapons be safe and secure from
theft, sabotage or unauthorized use in peacetime, they must also be ready for
use and as invulnerable as possible to attack in time of war. This last
constraint implies the dispersal of weapons over a large number of locations,
the periodic movement of weapons from place to place, and concealment of the
weapons — all of which would make it more difficult for an enemy to locate and
destroy them. The first, however, implies the concentration of weapons at fixed
locations, under heavy guard and with numerous, observable physical-security
precautions, since it is obviously far easier and cheaper to maintain the
physical security of weapons under such conditions. Because the problem of
maintaining the peacetime security of the weapons is of greater immediate
concern to NATO governments than is the problem of wartime readiness — peace
being the expected day-to-day condition — NATO’s nuclear-weapons deployment
policies reflect this concern. The 7,000 or so theater-nuclear weapons in Europe
are stored in a rather small number of storage facilities under continuous guard
and are protected by fences, sensors, floodlights and other devices which
contribute to their peacetime security. These security precautions are even
being increased in response to the growing incidence of international terrorism
in recent years. All of these measures, however, make the nuclear-storage sites
obvious, readily identifiable targets for a Soviet attack.

Given the dominating NATO concern with the peacetime security of these weapons,
NATO’s approach to the problem of maintaining the wartime readiness of its
weapons has been twofold. First, some U.S. and allied nuclear-armed tactical
aircraft and Pershing short-range ballistic missiles are maintained on peacetime
"quick-reaction alert" (QRA), capable of being launched against enemy targets on
short notice. QRA supposedly enhances the sur-

A second NATO approach to wartime readiness is to disperse the weapons from the

However, concern over the security of these weapons in transit during peacetime

reluctant to disperse its theater-nuclear weapons until after the actual
outbreak of war, for fear that the Soviets would interpret this extraordinary
step as an indication of NATO preparations for nuclear use. This might trigger
the pre-emptive Soviet nuclear strike which Soviet military doctrine emphasizes
as the proper response. If allowed to remain in their peacetime deployment,
however, nearly all of NATO’s theater-nuclear forces in Europe could be put out
of action by a well-designed surprise nuclear attack. Such attacks are also
emphasized by Soviet military doctrine. These forces could also probably be
seriously degraded by purely conventional attacks. The presence of NATO’s QRA
forces, poised to conduct nuclear strikes against the Warsaw Pact, probably
increases the urgency for the Soviets to destroy these forces before they can be
used. Such a surprise attack on these alert forces could probably destroy them
on the ground, since warning would be very short, while the consultations and
decision to launch QRA forces would be time-consuming.

usually counted as part of the U.S. strategic-nuclear forces. In the event of a
Soviet surprise nuclear attack on NATO theater-nuclear forces in Europe, the
primary surviving retaliatory asset available to NATO would be the Poseidon
warheads. The United States would seem to have no choice but to accept the loss
of its theater-nuclear forces or to use Poseidon — an element of the United
States’ strategic retaliatory forces — and thus risk a Soviet strategic
response.

There are, of course, those who claim that the magnitude of a Soviet strike
against the theater-nuclear forces in Europe would require U.S. execution of the
“single integrated operational plan” (SIOP) for strategic forces. However, it is
difficult to see what could be thus gained in a situation where Europe has been
prostrated by a nuclear attack and where any NATO conventional defenses would
probably be in chaos. It is easier to see the costs to the United States, in the
form of Soviet strategic retaliation, which could arise from such a course of
action. Given the disproportion between the potential costs and gains of
invoking U.S. strategic forces in response to a Soviet attack on the theater-
nuclear forces, it is difficult to believe that the United States would actually
carry out such an escalatory threat. If this threat is not fully credible,
moreover, it is doubtful that it would deter the Soviets from destroying the
NATO nuclear forces which constitute an impediment to the success of any Soviet
conventional offensive.

Thus, as currently configured, NATO’s theater-nuclear forces seem to serve
primarily as a “lightning rod,” inviting and attracting a Soviet attack and
hastening, rather than forestalling, the

The land-based tactical-nuclear component of the proposed force is intended to
deter Soviet use of tactical-nuclear weapons against defending NATO troops, thus
providing NATO with the capability of responding to such attacks in kind. This
component would also provide NATO with a responsive capability to attack time-

theater-nuclear forces prove unavoidable. This tactical-nuclear component could
be provided by some combination of three systems: nuclear-capable artillery
(8-inch and/or 155-mm), Lance missiles or a mobile, truck-mounted of the Nike-

systems would be chosen for the tactical-nuclear deterrent role would depend
upon the nature of the deterrent threat at the tactical level (e.g., destruction
of enemy troops, destruction of enemy tactical-nuclear weapons), the number of
delivered weapons required to carry out the threat, and the required level of
system survivability in the field.

Unresolved Questions

Two fundamental and related questions have been left unresolved: What must be
threatened to deter a Soviet nuclear attack on Western Europe, and How large
must the sea-based cruise missile and land-based tactical-nuclear force
components be? As noted earlier, the size of the theater-nuclear forces, and the
number and types of ships chosen to carry the cruise missile force component,
depend upon the nature of the theater-nuclear deterrent threat. Should this
threat require the delivery of, say, only 100 or 200 long-range nuclear weapons,
then the required cruise missile forces could be achieved by deploying two or
three cruise missiles on existing NATO attack submarines. This would probably
not represent an unacceptably large diversion of conventional weapons spaces for
theater-nuclear missions.

However, replacement of NATO's currently numerous long-range nuclear-strike
forces with a deployed force of only 200 or so cruise missiles might be
politically unacceptable to NATO. Despite the expected dramatic improvement in
the survivability of this small force, NATO might fear that the gross reduction
of theater-nuclear forces would be seen as weakening, not strengthening, the
theater-nuclear deterrent. Consequently, a larger cruise missile force may be
required. Two means would be available to increase the size of the sea-based
cruise missile force: (1) increase the number of cruise missiles deployed on
each attack submarine to, say, eight, or (2) expand the sea-based cruise missile
platforms to include surface ships. Each of these options has its drawbacks.

An increase in the number of cruise missiles on each NATO attack submarine to
consume a third or more of the weapons spaces begins to significantly detract
from the primary conventional capability of these ships. To offset this,
additional attack submarines may have to be built. If there are already backlogs
of construction orders for new submarines, this might take a while, and in any
event, it would greatly increase the cost of the proposed theater-nuclear force
improvements.

The disadvantage of adding to the sea-based cruise missile force by deploying
these weapons on surface ships is that those ships are more vulnerable to Soviet
detection and attack than are submarines. For this reason, the submarine-basing
potential for cruise missiles probably should be fully exploited before
deployment on surface ships is considered.

Proposed answers to this question differ widely, depending on the perspective of
the commentator. Some strategists believe that NATO must be able to destroy
attacking enemy ground forces with nuclear weapons and deny the enemy his
military objectives under all conditions. Others believe that NATO should
threaten the destruction of rear-area targets in Warsaw Pact territory which
would both disrupt the conduct of military operations against NATO and convey a
political message to Soviet leaders influencing them to cease hostilities. Still
others argue that Moscow would probably not be significantly influenced by the
fate either of its troops in Europe or of Warsaw Pact territory, and that the
Soviets can be deterred from nuclear warfare only by the threat of NATO theater-

obscuring whatever distinctions are possible between theater-nuclear and
strategic-nuclear warfare, and thus reduces the chances of containing a war
below the strategic-nuclear level. Finally, the most eclectic theorists maintain
that, since we cannot know in advance what will deter a Soviet nuclear strike on
NATO, the allies should be capable of conducting nuclear strikes on all of the
above types of targets.17

CARTER ORDERS PRODUCTION OF NEUTRON WEAPON. DEFERS DEPLOYMENT
by Hugh O. Mulr
USICA Security Affairs Correspondent
WASHINGTON -- PRESIDENT CARTER HAS ORDERED PRODUCTION OF COMPONENTS OF THE SO-
CALLED ENHANCED RADIATION DEVICE, A DEFENSIVE WEAPON DESIGNED TO DISABLE AND
KILL BY INTENSIVE NEUTRON BOMBARDMENT.

THE STEP IS THE SECOND IN MR. CARTER'S PROGRAM TO MAKE THE WEAPON AVAILABLE,
PRINCIPALLY, FOR THE DEFENSE OF WESTERN EUROPE. THE FIRST, TO MODERNIZE THE
DELIVERY SYSTEMS FOR THE NEUTRON DEVICE, WAS TAKEN LAST APRIL. THE THIRD STEP,
ACTUAL DEPLOYMENT, REMAINS DEFERRED BY THE PRESIDENT.

THE U.S. DEPARTMENT OF ENERGY -- WHICH INCLUDES THE FORMER ATOMIC ENERGY
COMMISSION -- ANNOUNCED OCTOBER 18 THAT IT HAD RECEIVED DIRECTION FROM MR.
CARTER TO BEGIN TO OBLIGATE FISCAL YEAR 1979 FUNDS FOR THE PRODUCTION OF NEUTRON
WEAPON COMPONENTS.

SPECIFICALLY, THE ORDER INVOLVES PRODUCTION OF THE NEUTRON WARHEAD FOR THE LANCE
TACTICAL MISSILE AND OF THE NEUTRON SHELL FOR THE EIGHT-INCH ARTILLERY HOWITZER.
THE MISSILE REPORTEDLY HAS A RANGE OF 146 KILOMETERS; THE EIGHT-INCH SHELL, 16
KILOMETERS.

"THE OBLIGATION OF FUNDS," THE DEPARTMENT STATEMENT SAID, "WILL IMPLEMENT
PRESIDENT CARTER'S DECISION OF APRIL 7, 1978, TO PROCEED WITH MODERNIZATION OF
THESE TWO THEATER NUCLEAR SYSTEMS."

SECRETARY OF ENERGY JAMES SCHLESINGER SAID IN THE DEPARTMENT STATEMENT THAT "IN
IMPLEMENTING THE PRESIDENT'S DECISION, WEAPONS PRODUCTION WILL BE DESIGNED TO
MAINTAIN THE OPTION OF INSTALLING ENHANCED RADIATION ELEMENTS AT A LATER DATE;
THIS WILL PROVIDE NECESSARY FLEXIBILITY."

THIS LATEST ACTION, THE SECRETARY SAID, "WILL HELP CARRY OUT THE PRESIDENT'S
PLEDGE TO MOVE AHEAD WITH OUR ALLIES TO MODERNIZE AND STRENGTHEN OUR MILITARY
CAPABILITIES, BOTH CONVENTIONAL AND NUCLEAR. THESE STEPS REFLECT HIS
DETERMINATION TO DO WHATEVER IS NECESSARY TO ASSURE OUR COLLECTIVE SECURITY AND
THE FORWARD DEFENSE OF EUROPE."

AT THE WHITE HOUSE, THE PRESIDENT'S SPEAKSMAN POINTED OUT THAT THE ORDER TO
PROCEED WITH PRODUCTION OF THE ELEMENTS OF THE NEUTRON WARHEAD DOES NOT MEAN
PRODUCTION OF THE TOTAL DEVICE.

"YOU WILL HAVE PRODUCTION OF THE ELEMENTS WHICH WILL BE NEEDED TO ASSEMBLE OR
PRODUCE A NEUTRON WARHEAD," PRESS SECRETARY JODY POWELL TOLD REPORTERS. HE ADDED
THAT "THE ELEMENTS WHICH ARE NECESSARY TO PRODUCE OR ASSEMBLE AN ENHANCED
RADIATION WARHEAD WILL BE KEPT HERE IN THE UNITED STATES," PENDING A
PRESIDENTIAL DECISION TO DEPLOY THE WEAPON.

MR. POWELL SAID HE WAS "NOT AT LIBERTY" TO SAY EXACTLY HOW MUCH TIME ASSEMBLY OF
THE COMPONENTS WOULD TAKE, ONCE A DECISION TO DEPLOY WAS MADE. BUT HE SAID "IT
WILL TAKE MUCH LESS TIME ONCE THE ELEMENTS THEMSELVES ARE IN EXISTENCE, THAN IF
WE HAD NOT DONE WHAT THE PRESIDENT SAID HE WAS GOING TO DO ON APRIL 7."

DEPLOYMENT, THE PRESS SECRETARY SAID, "IS AN OPTION THAT IS FACILITATED BY THE
PRODUCTION OF THE COMPONENTS. WHETHER THAT EVENTUALLY WILL BE AN OPTION TAKEN BY
THE PRESIDENT IS SOMETHING HE AND OUR ALLIES WILL HAVE TO DECIDE."

THE PRESIDENT'S APRIL 7 STATEMENT, ANNOUNCING HIS DECISION TO DEFER AT THAT TIME
PRODUCTION OF WEAPONS WITH ENHANCED RADIATION EFFECTS, SAID FUTURE DECISIONS
WOULD DEPEND IN PART ON SOVIET UNION "RESTRAINT."

"THE ULTIMATE DECISION REGARDING THE INCORPORATION OF ENHANCED RADIATION
FEATURES INTO OUR MODERNIZED BATTLEFIELD WEAPONS WILL BE MADE LATER," THE CARTER
STATEMENT SAID AT THE TIME, "AND WILL BE INFLUENCED BY THE DEGREE TO WHICH THE
SOVIET UNION SHOWS RESTRAINT IN ITS CONVENTIONAL AND NUCLEAR ARMS PROGRAMS AND
FORCE DEPLOYMENTS AFFECTING THE SECURITY OF THE UNITED STATES AND WESTERN
EUROPE."

PRESS SPOKESMAN POWELL SAID OCTOBER 13 THAT HE WAS NOT AWARE OF A DIRECT SOVIET
RESPONSE TO THE APRIL 7 STATEMENT, ADDING THAT IN ANY CASE THERE HAD NOT SEEN A
RESPONSE THAT WOULD CHANGE THE PRESIDENT'S APRIL 7 FORMULATION.

ASKED ABOUT THE POSSIBLE IMPACT OF THE LATEST ANNOUNCEMENT ON THE STRATEGIC ARMS
LIMITATION TALKS WITH THE SOVIETS, MR. POWELL SAID THE ENHANCED RADIATION
WARHEAD "IS NOT RELATED AT ALL" TO THE SALT DISCUSSIONS. HE ADDED THAT HE DID
NOT THINK THAT "EITHER THE UNITED STATES OR THE SOVIET UNION HAD MADE ANY
COMMITMENTS THAT EITHER NATION WOULD NOT PROCESSED WITH DEFENSE-RELATED
ACTIVITIES THAT ARE NOT RELATED TO SALT JUST BECAUSE THE SALT DISCUSSIONS ARE
GOING AHEAD."

ASKED IF THERE WAS ANY RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN THE NEUTRON WARHEAD AND THE MUTUAL
AND BALANCED FORCE REDUCTION (MBFR) NEGOTIATIONS INVOLVING NATO AND THE NARSAV
PACK NATIONS, MR. POWELL SAID "I ASSUME (THERE IS A RELATIONSHIP) ONLY IN THE
SENSE THAT WE ARE TALKING ABOUT DISPOSITIONS IN EUROPE AND SO FORTH. IT IS NOT A
MATTER RELATED TO THE NEGOTIATIONS."

THE NEUTRON DECISION
by William V. Wade
WOA News Analyst
PRESIDENT CARTER HAS ORDERED THE PRODUCTION OF ELEMENTS THAT WOULD GO INTO A
NEUTRON WARHEAD, AS WELL AS MODERNIZATION OF BATTLEFIELD WEAPONS THAT CAN BE
USED TO CARRY IT.

THE SO-CALLED NEUTRON BOMB HAS BEEN CONTROVERSIAL WELL BEYOND THE ACTUAL FACTS
THAT DESCRIBE IT. MILITARY EXPERTS CALL IT AN "ENHANCED RADIATION WEAPON" -- A
CLINICAL PHRASE INDICATING THAT IT SENDS OUT RADIOACTIVE PARTICLES THAT KILL,
WHILE ITS BLAST EFFECTS ARE RELATIVELY SMALL.

IT IS NOT A BOMB TO BE DROPPED ON CITIES FROM A PLANE -- BUT RATHER TO BE USED
AS AN ARTILLERY SHELL OR AS A WARHEAD ON SHORT-RANGE MISSILES AGAINST ATTACKING
TANKS.

IT HAS NOT YET BEEN PRODUCED OR DEPLOYED -- AND MAY NEVER BE DEPLOYED.
LAST APRIL PRESIDENT CARTER DECIDED TO DEFER PRODUCTION OF THE NEUTRON WEAPON
BUT ALSO TO MODERNIZE THE SYSTEMS THAT CAN DELIVER IT TO A TARGET. HIS LATEST
DECISION IS TO GO AHEAD WITH MODERNIZATION OF THESE SYSTEMS AND TO PRODUCE PARTS
THAT WOULD GO INTO A NEUTRON WARHEAD -- IF AND WHEN SUCH A WEAPON IS DEEMED
NECESSARY.

THE KEY CONSIDERATION IN WHETHER IT MAY BE DEEMED NECESSARY IS WHAT MOSCOW DOES
NOW. MR. CARTER SAID LAST APRIL HIS ULTIMATE DECISION WILL BE INFLUENCED BY THE
DEGREE TO WHICH THE SOVIET UNION SHOWS RESTRAINT IN THE DEPLOYMENT OF ITS
FORCES. SINCE APRIL THE PRESIDENT HAS NOT BEEN PLAYING HAMLET -- VACILLATING
ABOUT WHETHER THE NEUTRON WEAPON IS TO BE, OR NOT TO BE. RATHER, OFFICIALS IN
WASHINGTON SAY, HIS LATEST ACTION KEEPS HIS OPTIONS OPEN IN ORDER TO BEST DEFEND
THE SECURITY OF THE UNITED STATES AND WESTERN EUROPE.

FOR THE NEUTRON WEAPON IS DESIGNED TO MEET A GREAT FORCE OF TANKS -- AND THAT'S
WHAT THE SOVIET UNION AND ITS WARSAW PACT ALLIES HAVE BEEN STEADILY ASSEMBLING
IN CENTRAL EUROPE IN RECENT YEARS. THE LATEST FIGURES FROM THE LONDON-BASED
INSTITUTE FOR STRATEGIC STUDIES PUT THE WARSAW PACT ARMORED FORCES IN NORTHERN
AND CENTRAL EUROPE AT MORE THAN 22,000 TANKS, AS AGAINST 7,000 FOR NATO -- A
RATIO OF THREE TO ONE.

WHAT'S MORE, MOSCOW HAS BEEN DEPLOYING ITS BIG NEW SS-20 MISSILES, EACH CAPABLE
OF CARRYING THREE NUCLEAR WARHEADS AND HITTING WEST EUROPEAN CITIES. IN THIS
AREA, U.S. OFFICIALS SAY, MOSCOW HAS NOT SHOWN THE RESTRAINT PRESIDENT CARTER
ASKED FOR LAST APRIL.

NEITHER THE NEUTRON WARHEAD NOR THE SS-20 IS A STRATEGIC WEAPON -- THAT IS,
CAPABLE OF TRAVELING FROM ONE CONTINENT TO ANOTHER. THEREFORE, THEY DO NOT FIT
INTO THE SOVIET-AMERICAN STRATEGIC ARMS LIMITATION TALKS NOW NEARING A SALT TWO
AGREEMENT. BUT THE MARSAW PACT TANK BUILDUP DOES FIGURE IN EAST-WEST VIENNA
TALKS ON FORCE REDUCTIONS. THOSE TALKS HAVE BEEN STALEMATED FOR FIVE YEARS AND
ARE ONLY NOW BEGINNING TO SHOW SIGNS OF MOVEMENT.

OFFICIALS IN WASHINGTON ADMIT THE NEUTRON WARHEAD, IF BUILT, WOULD BE A DREADFUL
WEAPON. BUT SO ARE NUCLEAR WEAPONS ALREADY IN EXISTENCE, INCLUDING THE SOVIET
SS-20 AND THE PROSPECT OF A WAR BEGUN WITH CONVENTIONAL WEAPONS, LAUNCHED WITH
SUPERIOR TANK FORCES, IS ALSO DREADFUL. OBVIOUSLY, THE WAY TO WEET SUCH TERRIBLE
RISKS IS MUTUAL RESTRAINT AND GREATER PROGRESS AT THE NEGOTIATING TABLE SO THAT
THERE IS SAFE AND SAME CONTROL OF MODERN ARMS, NUCLEAR AND CONVENTIONAL ALIKE.

CARTER ORDERS PRODUCTION OF NEUTRON WEAPON COMPONENTS
WHILE CONTINUING TO DEFER FINAL PRODUCTION
AND DEPLOYMENT DECISIONS

by Hugh O. Muir
USICA Security Affairs Correspondent

WASHINGTON -- PRESIDENT CARTER HAS ORDERED PRODUCTION OF SOME OF THE COMPONENTS
OF THE SO-CALLED ENHANCED RADIATION DEVICE, A NUCLEAR WEAPON DESIGNED TO DISABLE
FORCES BY INTENSIVE NEUTRON BOMBARDMENT.

SPECIFICALLY, THE ORDER INVOLVES PRODUCTION OF SOME OF THE COMPONENTS FOR THE
NEUTRON WARHEAD FOR THE LANCE TACTICAL MISSILE AND OF THE NEUTRON SHELL FOR THE
EIGHT-INCH ARTILLERY HOWITZER. BOTH ARE NOW DEPLOYED IN EUROPE WITH CONVENTIONAL
NUCLEAR ARMAMENT. THE MISSILE REPORTEDLY HAS A RANGE OF 146 KILOMETERS; THE
EIGHT-INCH SHELL, 16 KILOMETERS.

THE ORDER FOLLOWS MR. CARTER'S ANNOUNCEMENT OF LAST APRIL IN WHICH HE ORDERED
MODERNIZATION OF THE DELIVERY SYSTEMS FOR THE NEUTRON DEVICE. U.S. OFFICIALS
POINTED OUT, HOWEVER, THAT THE DECISION ANNOUNCED OCTOBER 18 DOES NOT MEAN
PRODUCTION AND DEPLOYMENT OF THE COMPLETE WEAPON.

"IT IS MY UNDERSTANDING," SAID DEPUTY STATE DEPARTMENT SPOKESMAN TOM RESTON,
"THAT THE DECISION INVOLVES ONLY THE PRODUCTION OF SOME OF THE COMPONENTS -- NOT
ALL OF THE COMPONENTS -- WHICH WOULD BE NECESSARY FOR CONVERSION INTO ENHANCED
RADIATION WEAPONS."

IN ANY CASE, MR. RESTON SAID, "OBVIOUSLY, THE PRESIDENT HAS NOT -- I REPEAT, NOT
-- DECIDED TO DEPLOY THE ENHANCED RADIATION WEAPONS IN EUROPE."

THE INITIAL ANNOUNCEMENT OF THE PRESIDENT'S DECISION CAME FROM THE DEPARTMENT OF
ENERGY, WHICH SAID IN A STATEMENT THAT IT HAD RECEIVED DIRECTION FROM MR. CARTER
TO BEGIN TO OBLIGATE FISCAL 1979 FUNDS FOR THE PRODUCTION OF NEUTRON WEAPON
COMPONENTS.

THE STATE SPOKESMAN POINTED OUT THAT THE MODERNIZATION OF THESE TWO WEAPONS
SYSTEMS ORDERED IN APRIL BY MR. CARTER WAS ONLY TO IMPROVE THEIR CURRENT NUCLEAR
WEAPONS CAPABILITY. "THAT IS SEPARATE FROM THE DECISION TO GO AHEAD AND PRODUCE
SOME OF THE COMPONENTS WHICH WOULD BE NECESSARY TO CONVERT THESE TWO SYSTEMS
FROM A TRADITIONAL NUCLEAR SYSTEM INTO AN ENHANCED RADIATION SYSTEM."

THE OBLIGATION OF FUNDS, THE ENERGY DEPARTMENT STATEMENT SAID OCTOBER 18, "WILL
IMPLEMENT PRESIDENT CARTER'S DECISION OF APRIL 7, 1978, TO PROCEED WITH
MODERNIZATION OF THESE TWO THEATER NUCLEAR SYSTEMS."
