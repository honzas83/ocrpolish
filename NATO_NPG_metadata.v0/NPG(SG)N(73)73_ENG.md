> # Early Combined Use
> 
> This document, a note by the Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group), provides a detailed content analysis of a SHAPE report concerning the Early Combined Use (ECU) of nuclear weapons. The analysis examines the methodology, definitions, and specific applications of ECU, defining it as the tactical use of nuclear weapons against enemy military formations as part of the strategy of flexible response. It outlines the potential advantages of ECU, such as gaining time for conventional reinforcements, minimizing friendly losses, and demonstrating NATO's resolve. The document also addresses potential disadvantages, including the risk of escalation and the impact on civilian populations in populated border areas. It further discusses the potential disadvantages of not employing ECU, such as the increased vulnerability of forward deployed forces. The analysis concludes that the realization of ECU would depend on political resolve, adequate decision-making machinery, and political agreement on authorization. The document emphasizes that ECU tactical and political considerations are fundamental to the defence of the Alliance.
> 
> ## Categories/Topics
> 
> #Doctrine-and-Strategy/Flexible-Response — The document explicitly identifies 'EARLY COMBINED USE' (ECU) as 'part of the strategy of flexible response' (Page 3, Paragraph 4a) and discusses the tailored, graded use of nuclear weapons to complement conventional efforts.
> #Doctrine-and-Strategy/Extended-Deterrence-and-Tactical-Nuclear-Sharing — The text focuses on the tactical use of nuclear weapons in Europe, including the deployment of nuclear capabilities to 'border regions' and 'areas where conventional forces are particularly thin' (Page 6, Paragraph 7c) to strengthen initial defense.
> #Internal-Alliance-Dynamics/Command-and-Control-Authority — The document emphasizes that the effectiveness of ECU is 'critically dependent on a responsive and reliable NATO command and control system' (Page 5, Paragraph 6c) and notes that military realization depends on 'adequate decision-making machinery' and 'political agreement on authorisation' (Page 9, Paragraph 11a).

EARLY COMBINED USE

CONTENT ANALYSIS OF THE SHAPE REPORT(1)

1. This analysis examines the use of the term "EARLY COMBINED USE" (ECU) (and
equivalent terms) in the SHAPE Report on this subject. A similar analysis
dealing with the use of the term "escalation" in the SHAPE Report has also been
circulated(2).

2. For purposes of analysis, the mention of the term EARLY COMBINED USE (early
tactical use, early nuclear use, etc.) is treated under the following headings:

(a) methodology
(b) definitions
(c) specific applications
(d) general assessment considerations
(e) specific assessment considerations: potential advantages of ECU
(f) specific assessment considerations: potential disadvantages of non-ECU
(g) specific assessment considerations: potential disadvantages of ECU
(h) other judgements
- overall value
- assumptions for military realisation
- timing consideration
- possible impacts and needs.

3. Methodology
(a) ECU analysis is not combined with consideration of the possible influence of
political factors(7)

(b) ECU analysis does not raise the question of adherence to NATO strategy(7)
(c) ECU analysis is distinctive from analysis of the "threat of early combined
use"(5)

(d) ECU analysis does not examine the deterrent aspects of nuclear weapons to
prevent aggression(5)

(e) ECU analysis is undertaken without introducing the limitations of a single
scenario(6)

(f) ECU analysis is forwarded without any real life experience(8)
(g) ECU military advantages and disadvantages are not always clearly offered but
only in relation to other feasible military courses of action.(14)

4. Definitions
(a) What? ECU is
- tactical use of nuclear weapons against enemy military formations (9a)
- part of the strategy of flexible response(7)

6. General Assessment Considerations
Advantages and disadvantages of ECU
(a) have to be weighed through consideration of the essential elements as how,
when, and where(47)

(b) are critically influenced by such factors as:
- geographical considerations
- force deployments
- weapons selection and targeting
- command and control.
- military mission
- collateral damage
- escalation(14)
(c) are more critically dependent on a responsive and reliable NATO command and
control system than in other cases of possible nuclear employment(31)
