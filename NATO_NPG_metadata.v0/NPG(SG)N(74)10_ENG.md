> # US Representative's Talking Points on Developments in the US Strategic Nuclear Posture
> 
> This document consists of a { "//": "Note by the Chairman" } note by the Chairman of the NPG Staff Group, H.B. Seim, and attached talking points used by the U. "//": "Representative" } representative at the meeting on 21 January 1974. The talking points address recent statements made by Secretary of Defense Schlesinger on 10 January 1974 before the Overseas Writers Association. The primary goals of the United States administration are to achieve greater flexibility in the employment of strategic forces and to maintain a strategic position of essential equivalence with the Soviet Union. The United States emphasizes that the shift in targeting emphasis toward military installations and the use of Multiple Independently Targetable Re-entry Vehicles (MIRV) deployment permits wider target coverage and greater discrimination. The administration asserts that these developments are not a sign of a 'first-strike doctrine' but are intended to enhance deterrence and strengthen extended deterrence for other countries. The sizing of strategic forces is noted to be influenced by the Strategic Arms Limitation Talks (SALT), and the U.S. must maintain essential equivalence if negotiations fail. The representative emphasizes that development programs are designed to ensure an invulnerable second-strike force, general symmetry in nuclear options, and a perceived equality between the United States and the Soviet Union.

U.S. REPRESENTATIVE'S TALKING POINTS
The following are talking points used by the U.S. Representative at the NPG
Staff Group Meeting of 21 January, 1974 as a basis for his response to requests
from his colleagues for further information concerning Secretary of Defense
Schlesinger's 10 January remarks before the Overseas Writers Association:

(a) Secretary Schlesinger's comments reflect policy of U.S. administration on
strategic forces and its desire to explain policy more fully to public.

(b) The two major themes are not new: (1) the necessity to achieve greater
flexibility in possible employment of strategic forces, and (2) the
determination of U.S. to retain a strategic position of essential equivalence
with the Soviet Union, if possible through negotiations, but if necessary by
unilateral measures.

(c) Regarding flexibility, the Secretary's remarks elaborate on points made for
several years in Presidential Foreign Policy Reports, and on his own comments at
recent NATO Ministerials.

(d) Shift in targeting is one of emphasis. U.S. has always targeted some
military installations as well as urban industrial centers. MIRV deployment
permits wider target coverage. Increases in accuracy permit greater
discrimination.

(e) Aim of administration is to enhance deterrence. The shift in emphasis does
not presage a "first-strike doctrine."

(f) Flexibility is not viewed as decoupling the U.S. strategic force from the
defense of other countries, but rather is an effort to strengthen extended
deterrence.

(g) The sizing of our strategic forces will be influenced by what happens at
SALT and is separable from our targeting doctrine. The purpose of our SALT
negotiations is the limiting of strategic forces, but if the talks fail to reach
key agreements, the U.S. must be in a position to have essential equivalence in
strategic capabilities.

(h) The Secretary discussed several development programs which are not designed
to match the Soviet on every specific front, but rather to insure (1) that the
U.S. can maintain an invulnerable second-strike force, (2) that the U.S. and the
USSR have a general symmetry in nuclear options, and (3) that there is perceived
equality between the two countries.

He stressed that the overall power represented by our strategic forces must be a
match for the overall power of the Soviet strategic forces.
