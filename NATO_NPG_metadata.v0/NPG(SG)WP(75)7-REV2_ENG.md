> # Nuclear Consultation Procedures Compilation
> 
> This document, a second revision of a working paper from the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group), Nuclear Consultation Procedures Compilation, consolidates existing guidance on procedures for political consultation within the Alliance in time of hostilities on the question of using nuclear weapons. It outlines the purpose, scope, and historical background, including the reference to the 1962 Athens Guidelines and the 1969 General Guidelines for Consultation Procedures on the Use of Nuclear Weapons. The document specifies that nuclear consultation is part of a broader crisis management process and is designed to provide the views of Alliance members to nuclear powers for consideration before a decision to release weapons is requested. The compilation includes a detailed table of contents covering introduction, nuclear consultation procedures, and descriptions of various messages such as the Country Request Message, Early Notification Message, and various 'Blue Dot' messages (Whiskey, Romeo, and Sierra). It also includes annexes listing NATO policy documents and definitions. The document was signed by R.E. Shearer of NATO in Brussels, Belgium, and was intended for approval by the Staff Group and Permanent Representatives via the silence procedure before being included in the Crisis Management Manual.

PART I
INTRODUCTION

PURPOSE
1. This document consolidates existing guidance on procedures for political
consultation within the Alliance in time of hostilities on the question of using
nuclear weapons.

SCOPE
2. The guidance herein is concerned with the procedural aspects of political
consultation on the question of using nuclear weapons which is part of a broader
consultation or crisis management process. It is based on and consistent with
approved policy guidance in the General Guidelines for Consultation Procedure on
the Use of Nuclear Weapons(1) which should be read in conjunction with this
document. It is also compatible with and complementary to the general
consultation procedures set forth in C-M(72)46. Major NATO Commanders Nuclear
Weapons Release Procedures (NWRP)(2) also apply for that part which concerns the
submission of requests to political authorities. This document will be updated
as required

BACKGROUND
3. General consultation among Alliance members is an important objective and a
prominent feature of NATO. Although each of the Alliance members retains its
full independence in foreign affairs and defence matters, the consultation
process is an essential element of collective planning and co-ordinated action.
This consultation activity takes place in the Council/DPC as the normal forum.
Carried out continuously in peacetime and designed to be intensified in times of
tension and war, it covers a wide-range of political and defence subjects
including questions of possible NATO use of nuclear weapons.

4. One of the first major steps in the specific field of nuclear consultation
was the adoption in 1962 of the Athens Guidelines which outlined in general
terms the possibilities for consultation in the Council under three different
assumptions

recommendations of the Special Committee of Defence Ministers to the DPC on
Consultation in Times of Tension and Crisis(2) in 1966, and the General
Guidelines for Consultation Procedures on the Use of Nuclear Weapons in 1969(3)
are the basic policy documents designed to strengthen the consultation process
in general and the nuclear consultation process in particular.

5. A basic objective of the nuclear consultation process is the timely provision
of the views of Alliance members concerning a prospective use of nuclear weapons
in defence of NATO to the nuclear power(s) for their consideration in reaching a
decision whether or not to release the weapons for the proposed use. Nuclear
consultation should take place within the context of the ongoing consultation
and crisis management process. While it is expected that the nuclear
consultation process would begin as early as practicable, procedures contained
in this guidance would mainly apply to situations in which consideration of the
use of nuclear weapons is based on a request or proposal for nuclear weapon use,
or a warning that such a request or proposal might soon be forthcoming. Such
actions can be initiated by any NATO member government, whether a nuclear power
or not, or by a Major NATO Commander.

6. Part II describes the procedures to be followed in these two situations. In
line with the General Guidelines(3), such procedures have been kept as simple
and as flexible as possible, and capable of modification at any time in the
light of prevailing circumstances. Nevertheless, a number of practical
procedural arrangements have, by their very nature, a more permanent character.

Annex A. Some definitions are contained in Annex B.

PART II
NUCLEAR CONSULTATION PROCEDURES
GENERAL
1. Consultation on the use of nuclear weapons is one particular aspect of a
broader process of consultation that takes place continuously among member
governments of the Alliance in peacetime and which would increase in intensity
should the international situation worsen due to tension, crisis and
hostilities.

2. Nuclear consultation in a specific sense begins as soon as the possible use
of nuclear weapons becomes an issue. Depending on the circumstances, this matter
can be raised in general terms in assessments of the evolving situation prepared
by member governments, the Council/DFC, or the Major NATO Commanders before any
specific requests or proposals for nuclear weapon use are made. In more specific
terms, it can be raised by a member government or by a Major NATO Commander
through a request or proposal for nuclear weapons use or a warning that such a
request or proposal might soon be forthcoming.

3. The nuclear consultation process takes place on the basis of information
concerning the politico-military situation as available to the Council/DFC and
to capitals. It would be conducted between capitals and in the Council/DFC. It
culminates with a decision by the nuclear power(s) concerned on the proposed
use. Time and circumstances permitting, such a decision, in conformity with the
Consultation Guidelines(1), would take into account the views expressed during
the process.

4. The following chapters deal with:
(a) the means by which information concerning the possible use of nuclear
weapons is made available to Council/DFC and capitals;

(b) the development of the consultation process;
(c) the ways by which the views of countries on MNC and country* requests are
made available to the nuclear power(s) concerned;

(d) how and when the final decision is communicated, if desired, to the enemy,
to third countries and to the public (CNI).

INFORMATION SOURCES
5. The effectiveness of the nuclear consultation process is critically dependent
on the amount of available information and on the ability of the participants in
that process to exchange information and views in a rapid, secure and reliable
way. Nuclear consultation would take place against the background of the flow of
information which is envisaged in the process of general consultation during
peacetime and which is expected to increase in time of tension, crisis and
hostilities. Furthermore, part of the context for nuclear consultation would be
the basic information available to capitals and the Council/DFC in peacetime,
such as the summary of political guidance, concepts and doctrine of NATO
military forces, and weapons characteristics and effects. The Situation Centre
of NATO Headquarters has the continuing task of supporting the Council/DFC in
its consultation rôle and acting as a clearing house for the exchange of
information and intelligence that forms the essential common data base for
consultation(1). In fulfilling the latter task, the NATO Situation Centre is
assisted by the Current Intelligence Group (CIG), which sifths and collates the
political, politico-military, economic and technological information that is
made available to NATO Headquarters from many sources, such as national
capitals, the NATO Military Authorities and public information media, and which
produces regular summaries of this information for circulation both within the
Headquarters and throughout the Alliance.

6. In time of war, political authorities will need to be kept fully informed of
developments that may foreshadow the use of nuclear weapons. This requirement
will extend from the early stages of a conflict, when only very general
indications are likely to be available, through later stages when specific
release requests may be made.

7. In the early stages of a conflict, the main sources of information are
"Intelligence Summaries" on enemy activities, "Situation Summaries" concerning
the operation of our own forces and "Assessments". Assessments can be
evaluations of the military situation (prepared either by MNCs or by the
Military Committee) or politico-military appraisals developed by individual
governments or by the Council/DFC. Such assessments might contain general
indications of the possible need for use of nuclear weapons by NATO or
indications of impending Warsaw Pact use, as reflected in the evolving
situation.

ACTION INITIATED BY A MEMBER GOVERNMENT
10. In line with the General Guidelines for Consultation Procedures on the Use

use of nuclear weapons or may make a definite request. To allow modification in
the light of actual circumstances, procedures related to such initiatives have
been kept as simple and flexible as possible.

Assessments
11. Politico-military assessments prepared by member governments in time of
hostilities could properly contain national views on a possible need for the use
of nuclear weapons in defence of the Alliance. These assessments need not
conform to any rigid format, but they should contain sufficient information to
assist member governments and the Major NATO Commanders concerned in preparing
their views. The address on a national assessment of this kind will be AIG 6014,
which includes all political and military authorities concerned with the
consultation process. The precedence should be FLASH, while the classification
would be determined by the originator. The consultation process should start as
soon as a country assessment dealing with the possible need for the use of
nuclear weapons is received at NATO Headquarters and in capitals. To assist
nuclear consultation, the MNC(s) concerned should provide, as soon as possible,
their views on the national assessment to the NAC/DFC and capitals.

24. Though it is not formally part of the nuclear consultation process, the

ACTIONS ON COMMUNICATING NATO'S INTENTION TO USE NUCLEAR WEAPONS (CNI)(2)
25. CNI actions within the nuclear consultation process may take the form of
messages of explicit warning or messages of information.

(a) A Message of Information is oriented towards explaining the selective and
limited character of an actual or immediately forthcoming NATO use of nuclear
weapons and warning against the danger of further escalation. Such a message may
be sent before implementation of a decision to use nuclear weapons, or in
parallel with, or immediately after such implementation.

(b) A Message of Explicit Warning is oriented towards inducing an opponent to
take the action desired by the Alliance without NATO’s initial or further use of
nuclear weapons. This message may be sent after consultation on a specific use
of nuclear weapons has started, but before a decision has been taken, or after a
decision has been taken but before the use itself has occurred.

26. The basic distinction between consultation and decision-making on message
texts and on finally sending a message should be observed. While a major
objective of NATO’s message to an enemy would be the demonstration of cohesion
and solidarity, circumstances and timing considerations might not always allow
for a full consultation on the content of a message.

COUNCIL/DFC COMMUNICATION TO THE NUCLEAR POWER(S)

4. This message is used by MNCs to warn NATO political authorities and NATO

BLUE DOT SIERRA MESSAGE

6. This message is used by MNCs to request preconditioned authority for
selective release of nuclear weapons for use against the enemy when, and if, a
depicted set of conditions, or a described situation occurs. The SIERRA message
may be originated by an MNC on his own initiative or as a follow-up action to a
similar request or warning received from a subordinate commander. It is intended
to avoid severe loss of forces that might occur during the period required to
obtain authorization to employ nuclear weapons using the ROMEO message procedure
and to enable the timely and effective use of such weapons against fleeting
targets, when attacks on friendly forces develop very quickly. In some of these
cases, the release authorization must be received by the executing commander
before specific tactical contact with the attacking enemy unit(s) has been made.
Time permitting, the SIERRA message will normally be preceded by a BLUE DOT
WHISKEY message concerning the same request for preconditioned authority for
selective release and sent to the same addressees.

The consultation based on a SIERRA message will culminate with a decision of the
nuclear power concerning that request.

Definitions: The following terms are frequently used in the document. In
accordance with agreed NATO understanding, they are defined as follows:

1. Assessment. Unformatted message or document containing the evaluation of the
military and/or political situation by the originator, based on intelligence and
other available information. Might contain views on possible use of nuclear
weapons.

2. ATOTAL Classification. The classification covering Atomic Information made
available by the Government of the United States of America to the North
Atlantic Treaty Organisation and its member states, pursuant to the Agreement
between the Parties to the North Atlantic Treaty for co-operation regarding
Atomic Information dated 18th June, 1964. Guidance for identification and
handling of ATOMAL information is contained in CM(68)41(Revised), 24th November,
1970.

3. CNI Action. Any communication concerning the possible or actual use of
nuclear weapons by NATO that may be made by the Alliance or on its behalf to an
opponent or enemy, the public or third countries, at such time when it is deemed
necessary and by any means which may be deemed suitable.

4. Early Notification Message. An unformatted message, originated by SACEUR and
directed to proper political and military authorities, dealing with the possible
impending use of nuclear weapons.

5. Executing Commander. The Commander designated by the Releasing Commander as
responsible for ordering the delivery of the weapon(s) to the target(s)
authorised in the release-message.

6. Follow-on Use. The selective use of nuclear weapons after initial selective
use by NATO.

7. Initial Use. The selective first use of nuclear weapons by NATO.
