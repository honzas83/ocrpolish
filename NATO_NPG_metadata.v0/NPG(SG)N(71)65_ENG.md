> # Studies of Follow-On Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons: Procedural Arrangements for Subsequent Stages of the Work Programme
> 
> This document, a note by the Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group), details the procedural arrangements for the subsequent stages of a work programme concerning the follow-on tactical use of nuclear weapons. The programme was initiated following a May 1969 decision by the Ministers of the Nuclear Planning Group to explore the implications and interactions of follow-on tactical use and NATO responses to Warsaw Pact initial use. Due to the complexity of the task, the work was divided into a series of smaller, interrelated steps rather than a single comprehensive study. The process is divided into three major phases: Phase I involves the preparation of basic data inputs through regional and transition studies; Phase II focuses on comparative analysis and synthesis to determine generally applicable conclusions; and Phase III involves the formulation of policy guidance. Phase I is currently underway, involving eight regional studies and a transition study conducted by national civilian and military personnel. The document emphasizes that Phase II will require considerable operational research expertise to analyze the military and non-military factors affecting follow-on use. The goal is to ultimately provide a basis for policy proposals for adoption by the Nuclear Defence Affairs Committee and the Defence Planning Committee.

I. BACKGROUND

"to develop terms of reference for exploring in more detail the implications and
interactions of follow-on tactical use of nuclear weapons in defence of NATO, as
well as NATO responses to Warsaw Pact initial use"(1).

this Ministerial decision, it became apparent that a single comprehensive study
to explore all aspects of follow-on use would be very cumbersome and complex,
would overtax the resources available to countries and the NATO Military
Authorities to conduct such a study and would require an unacceptably long

4.   In approving the general work programme, the Ministers invited the Nuclear

(a) make arrangements for the fulfilment of the initial work;
(b) monitor on a continuing basis the work programme and the specific studies

5.   Subsequently, in February 1970, the Permanent Representatives approved
specific arrangements(2) for the fulfilment of the initial work in the
programme. Eight regional studies analysing battlefield situations in limited
areas were commissioned to be conducted by ad hoc study teams. At the same time,
a study of the transition from conventional to nuclear phases was initiated.
Interest was also expressed in some other ancillary problems, such as political
control of battlefield use of nuclear weapons in an escalating situation, and
modes of analysis of military effectiveness of nuclear weapons and of collateral
damage which may be relevant to the question of defining constraints on the
utilisation of such weapons. The Staff Group was to explore these issues to
provide a basis for possible later decisions on whether or not reports could be
usefully forwarded to the Ministers(3).

II. GENERAL OUTLINE OF THE MAJOR PHASES OF THE FOLLOW-ON
USE WORK PROGRAMME

9. The overall process within the Nuclear Planning Group
leading to the formulation of policy proposals on follow-on
use for adoption by the Nuclear Defence Affairs Committee and
Defence Planning Committee can be divided into three major
phases:
(a) Phase I: Preparation of basic data inputs;
(b) Phase II: Comparative analysis and synthesis;
(c) Phase III: Formulation of policy guidance.

10. As will be noted from the detailed descriptions
in subsequent paragraphs, each of these phases covers a
part of the process that is distinctively different from the
other parts. Moreover, the process itself flows logically
from one phase to the next in the order shown above.
Nevertheless, in carrying out the overall programme, the
phases may overlap in time; that is, work may be initiated
in a particular phase even though activity in a preceding
phase may still be continuing.

11. This is the initial part of the work programme that
is currently under way. At present, it consists mainly of

comment on it in writing, if they desire. Each study is also reviewed by the
Major NATO Commander(s) concerned.

studies are not due until the second part of 1972. Phase I is therefore likely
to extend through several more Ministerial meetings, at least through the autumn

B. Phase II: Comparative analysis and synthesis
15. The results of the regional studies conducted under Phase I are to a large
measure dependent on the specific scenarios, assumptions and planning factors
selected by the study teams. Great caution will have to be applied in the
process of drawing generally applicable conclusions. The first step in Phase II
would therefore be a careful comparative analysis of all of the completed
regional and other studies, together with any comments submitted by countries
and the Major NATO Commanders. This comparative analysis would explore the
military and non-military factors affecting follow-on use as set out in the
studies and attempt to determine whether they are generally applicable or are
relevant only to a specific study situation. The process would seem to demand
considerable operational research expertise.

16. During this analytical effort, the necessity for further investigation of
specific aspects of follow-on use may become apparent. This could lead to the
initiation of additional Phase I work, either by building on completed regional
studies or by initiating separate ancillary studies.

17. The end product of this process of comparative analysis would be a synthesis
of insights and findings and the formulation of policy questions of a general
nature and the definition of possible policy choices.

18. Both analysis and synthesis could be undertaken either by the Nuclear
Planning Group collectively or by a single country or a small number of
countries, perhaps with the collaboration of the NATO Military Authorities. Both
possibilities have been suggested and there is precedent for both.

19. If the work is not done by the Nuclear Planning Group collectively,
provision should be made for multilateral consideration of the results with a
possibility of national inputs at Nuclear Planning Group Staff Group and
Permanent Representatives levels.

20. A final report will then be submitted to the Ministers for discussion of the
general policy questions and the possible policy choices.

21. The complexity of the work involved in Phase II would seem to indicate that
it would take some time to complete. At the moment, however, it is neither
feasible nor desirable to attempt to decide its precise duration. Similarly, it
would appear to be premature to determine now the date for starting Phase II or
to reach decisions on who will carry it out. These questions should be addressed
after further progress has been made in implementing Phase I.

C. Phase III: Formulation of policy guidance
22. On the basis of the work done in Phases I and II, the process of formulation
of policy guidance (guidelines) can be initiated in Phase III. The
considerations set out in paragraphs 17-20 above with respect to timing and
participation apply to Phase III as well. In any case, sufficient opportunity
will have to be provided for the timely introduction of national views of all
Nuclear Planning Group countries during the formulation process.

III. MILITARY ADVICE
23. Ample opportunity for the introduction of military advice should be provided
throughout the work programme. More specifically, this can be done

(a) during the conduct of the studies through participation by staff officers
from Major Subordinate and Major NATO Commands;

(b) during the discussion stages at Staff Group and Permanent Representatives
levels through representatives of the Major NATO Commands and the International
Military Staff;

(c) through written comments on studies and policy documents;
(d) through the submission of studies, as appropriate;
(e) at Ministerial meetings through the Chairman of the Military Committee and
the Major NATO Commanders present.
