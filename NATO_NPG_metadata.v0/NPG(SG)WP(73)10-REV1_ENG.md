> # Early Combined Use of NATO Conventional and Nuclear Capabilities
> 
> This draft report by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) examines the potential advantages and disadvantages of the early combined use of NATO conventional and nuclear capabilities. The discussion originates from a 1971 proposal by former Secretary General Brosio, who suggested analyzing the possibility of initiating combined use as soon as a major aggression is identified, rather than waiting for impending conventional defeat. The report draws on analysis from the Supreme Headquarters Allied Powers Europe (SHAPE) and comments from the Military Committee, noting that early tactical use is permissible within the current strategy of flexibility in response. It emphasizes that a full assessment of the balance of advantages and disadvantages can only be made based on the the specific situation prevailing at the time of a potential conflict. The document also notes that improving the awareness of political and military planners regarding these variables contributes to the goal of deterrence and a viable defense of NATO. The report incorporates material from previous drafts and corrigenda to be considered by the NPG Staff Group at its meeting on September 4, 1973.

I. PURPOSE
This report discusses advantages and disadvantages that might accrue from an
early combined use of NATO conventional and nuclear capabilities, drawing on
comments by the NATO Military Authorities that deal with the military aspects.

II. BACKGROUND
2. At their meeting in Mittenwald in May, 1971 the NPG Ministers discussed a
paper on unresolved problems related to tactical nuclear defence written by
former Secretary General Brosio. This paper suggested, among other matters, an
examination of the possibility of an early combined use of our conventional and
nuclear capabilities as soon as an enemy attack is identified as a major
aggression and before our conventional forces have suffered substantial losses,
and a constrained but continuing combined use if the aggressor persists in his
attack(1). In a subsequent report containing suggestions for investigating the
unresolved problems, the Permanent Representatives proposed(2) that the NATO
Military Authorities might be requested to indicate in general terms what
military advantages and disadvantages might accrue from an early combined use,
noting at the same time that the question of constrained but continuing follow-
on use could be considered at a later date when more progress had been made in
the Follow-on Use Work Programme. Agreeing with this approach, the Ministers
invited(3) the NATO Military

General Comment
5. The SHAPE Report provides an analysis of the possible military advantages and
disadvantages that might accrue to NATO as a result of the early combined
tactical use of conventional and nuclear weapons in Allied Command Europe (ACE).

Emphasising that no attempt is made to answer the question of whether NATO
should resort to such use, the SHAPE Report observes that early tactical use is
permissible within the approved strategy of flexibility in response and that the
question of adherence to NATO strategy is not at issue(1). Bearing in mind the
statement in the Provisional Political Guidelines that a variety of situations
can be envisaged in which tactical use might have to be initiated at an early
stage(2), the Ministers may wish to discuss the SHAPE observation and confirm
that early combined use is one of the options open to NATO within the current
strategy.

6. The SHAPE Report indicates that its analysis is based on existing political
guidance and reflects current ACE concepts and doctrine governing the tactical
use of nuclear weapons(3). The analysis deliberately excludes consideration of
political factors that would not be known before an actual situation arises but
that could nevertheless have a direct impact on striking a balance of military
advantages and disadvantages(4). The Report concludes, in essence, that a full
and accurate assessment of this balance could only be made in the light of the
particular situation that prevails at the time that early combined use might be
under consideration(5). It points out, however, that an improved awareness on
the part of political and military planners and decision-makers of the variable
elements discussed in the Report (as well as others that might be identified in
the future) would make an important contribution to the goal of deterrence and
toward a viable defence of NATO in the event of any future military
conflicts(6).

7. On the basis of certain general assumptions and without reference to any

which early combined use might be advantageous or disadvantageous from a

damage and escalation(1). In this context, the Report notes that analysis of

early combined use would not offer a clear advantage or disadvantage to NATO,
but only relatively more advantage or disadvantage than some other feasible
military course of action(2). We believe, however, that the SHAPE Report
usefully develops in considerable detail the ways in which these separate
factors might operate to the military advantage to NATO as well as the ways in
which they might work to NATO's disadvantage. Subject to clarifying comments
later in this report, we concur in general terms with the thrust of the SHAPE
analysis. Without detracting from the value of the SHAPE Report, we note in
passing that a number of the considerations mentioned in its analysis in

Implications of the Definition of Early Combined Use
8. The request to the NATO Military Authorities for the study of the military
advantages and disadvantages of early combined use was based on two qualifying
criteria mentioned in Mr. Brosio's paper: (a) as soon as any enemy attack is
identified as a major aggression, and (b) before our conventional forces have
suffered substantial losses. The second of these criteria would be in effect at
the outbreak of hostilities and would continue to be met up until the point in
the evolution of fighting where substantial losses in NATO conventional strength
had been incurred. The first criterion would not be satisfied until the moment
in the evolution of the crisis when a determination is made that the aim and
scope of the attack are such as to imperil, directly, either one or more NATO
countries, to the extent that survival as free and independent nations is
immediately at stake, or the integrity of military forces, to the extent that
capabilities essential to the effective accomplishment of NATO strategic
objectives are imminently subject to unacceptable deterioration(1). In
evaluating this approach, we note that the objective of the study of early
combined use was to look at a part of the spectrum of conflict that had not yet
been given full attention. Accordingly, we interpret the mention of identifying
an attack as a major aggression to be a way of defining the part of the spectrum
to be examined, and not as a suggestion to introduce a further condition in the
Provisional Political Guidelines(2) to require that major aggression must always
occur before NATO could resort to the use of nuclear weapons. If this further
condition were introduced, undesirable limitations would be imposed on the range
of flexible response options and an unnecessary linkage would be created between
recognition of major aggression and decisions to use nuclear weapons.

9. The definition of early use in the SHAPE Report expands this criterion by
including limited aggression as well as major aggression(1). NATO has defined
limited aggression as any armed attack against NATO forces or territory, or
actions at sea or in the air, under conditions of self-imposed military
restraint, in which it appears that an armed attack imperils neither the
survival of nations nor the integrity of military forces as described above.
Since limited aggression is considered to include overt incursions and hostile
local actions, it covers all forms of military attacks except covert actions,
infiltrations and major aggression(2). Although it is quite possible that the
analysis of the military advantages and disadvantages in the SHAPE Report might
not be significantly different if it were based solely on the criterion of major
aggression, it is clear to us that an assessment of the net advantage to NATO of
early combined use, based on political as well as military factors, could reach
a markedly different conclusion if it were to be made in a limited aggression
rather than major aggression context. This is not to say that the factors
themselves would necessarily be different, but the relative weight accorded to
each in striking an overall balance is likely to vary considerably according to
the scale of the aggression.

10. The question might be asked whether another aspect of the definition of
early use as given in the SHAPE Report, namely that such use could encompass
initial use, follow-on use, or both(3), affects the validity of the SHAPE
analysis. White it might be argued that early combined use should cover only the
first use by NATO, we note that the period of time covered by the NPG
description of early use could extend from the moment when an aggression is
determined to be major to the moment when NATO conventional forces suffer
substantial losses.

This period of time could be of considerable length in some circumstances during
which it is not inconceivable that tactical use of nuclear weapons by NATO might
continue. Furthermore, it will be recalled that the original suggestion for
investigating the possibility of early combined use assumed that it would be
followed by further combined use if the aggressor persists in his attack(1). An
assessment of the advantages and disadvantages of early combined use in this
context is likely to be considerably different from an assessment that assumes
early combined use would be a single initial action and that NATO would have no
further recourse to tactical use no matter what the enemy reaction might be.
Since the SHAPE analysis is based on a definition of early use that does not
rule out the possibility of continuing tactical use by NATO, we consider it to
be compatible with, and responsive to, the general thrust of the problem being
investigated by the NPG.

Deterrent Role of Early Combined Use

11. While confirming the necessity of maintaining a convincing NATO capability
to use nuclear weapons as a deterrent, the SHAPE Report points out that, in
accordance with its terms of reference, the development of the analysis is
confined to assessment factors concerning the actual use itself without any
consideration of the possible deterrent effect of the threat of an early
combined use(2). We observe that, if NATO maintains a viable and visible
capability to resort to early combined use and does not rule out such use as a
matter of policy, this capability itself represents an inherent threat that a
potential aggressor must take into account and should serve, together with other
deterrent elements, to dissuade him from initiating an aggression(3).

12. In addition to the foregoing, we note the Military Committee’s observation
that an advantage of the early use of nuclear weapons would lie in the deterrent
value of a use of such weapons(1). We agree that the actual tactical employment
of nuclear weapons in the early combined use context is likely to have a
significant deterrent as well as war-fighting aspect. With respect to the
latter, such use would be designed to inflict damage on the enemy forces and
cause him to reconsider his aggression and to cease his attack. But such use
could also have a deterrent aspect because it could induce him not to increase
the intensity of his attack or to expand its scope, even if he did not
immediately cease the aggression. It can be argued that this deterrent effect
would be greater if NATO resorts to the tactical use of nuclear weapons early in
the evolution of the fighting before it has suffered substantial losses in its
conventional strength. This deterrent aspect of actual use is reflected in the
Provisional Political Guidelines(2).

Rationale for Combined Use

13. The SHAPE Report reiterates(3) the point that, to achieve their tactical
purpose, nuclear weapons must not be used as a substitute for conventional
weapons but rather as a complement to them. We agree with this position which is
in line with the Provisional Political Guidelines(4). Tactical use of nuclear
weapons to the exclusion of conventional means of defence would not only be
unsound from a military point of view in most cases(5) but would also have
serious political implications. For example, an abrupt shift to an exclusive
nuclear defence carries a high risk of giving the enemy an incorrect perception
of NATO intentions, by leading him to attribute to NATO a much greater
escalatory intent than was

Disruption of Command and Control

16. The SHAPE Report stresses the need for a responsive and reliable NATO
command and control system, particularly in the context of early combined use,
and points out that any appreciable disruption would be to NATO's disadvantage.
The Report also notes that the use of nuclear weapons by NATO might degrade the
Alliance's own communications systems, but that the commander's careful
selection and employment of weapons could, to a large degree, limit the amount
of degradation(4). We believe that, from a political viewpoint, the preservation
of an effective command and control system against disruption resulting from
nuclear weapons use, whether by the enemy or by NATO, is of crucial importance
if control of the selective use of nuclear weapons is to be exercised
effectively by political authorities or NATO military commanders. Conversely,
serious disruption of NATO communications could frustrate the political
consultation process as well as interfere significantly with the execution of
NATO's military operations.

19. The SHAPE Report mentions that a significant military objective in combat is
the destruction of the enemy's command and control system(1). Although this
destruction might be of military advantage to NATO, it is not so clear that it
would necessarily be of benefit from the political point of view, particularly
in the early combined use context. For example, the Provisional Political
Guidelines state that the fundamental objective of the tactical use of nuclear
weapons and the threat of further escalation is to induce the enemy to make the
political decision to cease his attack and withdraw(2). Implicit in this
statement is the assumption that the enemy still has military forces in the
field at his disposal to continue the attack. The question might therefore be
raised whether it is to NATO's political advantage to disrupt the enemy's
command and control system to the point that a political decision to cease the
attack could not be transmitted to the forces in the field.

Broader Political Implications
20. As we have noted earlier, many of the observations in the SHAPE Report are
as relevant from a political point of view as from a military standpoint.
Nevertheless, there are a number of considerations not covered in the SHAPE
Report that have broader political implications that must be taken into account
in reaching a decision in time of hostilities about early combined use. These
factors include, for example, the utility of such use in demonstrating NATO
resolve; its impact on NATO internal cohesiveness; its effect on NATO's
political flexibility; its relationship to the domestic political situation
within member countries; its psychological impact on the populations and
governments of neutral and enemy nations; and its effect on NATO civilian and
military morale. Although

Summary

21. From the analysis in the SHAPE Report and the related Military Committee
comments, we gain the impression that there are likely to be military advantages
from early combined use that would not be realised under later use
circumstances, although there would also be military disadvantages to be taken
into account in both cases. As noted earlier, a complete assessment of the
military advantages and disadvantages, and the resulting balance, can only be
made in the light of the particular situation that obtains at the time that
early combined use is under active consideration. The same reservation applies
to the assessment of the political implications. From the political point of
view, the evaluation of advantages and disadvantages is complicated further by
the fact that the various factors to be considered cannot be defined and
analysed in advance to the degree of detail that was achieved in the examination
of military factors. Nevertheless, political authorities should make themselves
aware of the possible political and military implications of early combined use
so as to be ready to deal effectively with a real situation. This should also
help NATO to avoid losing by default the opportunity to implement the early
combined use option. The importance of effective political consultation
machinery is also self-evident.

IV. RECOMMENDATIONS

23. The Ministers may wish to:
(a) take note of the SHAPE Report and the related MC Comments(2) as well as the
comments in this report;

(b) agree that these documents make a valuable contribution toward providing a
basis on which political authorities might assess the question of early combined
use in an actual crisis;

(c) confirm that early combined use is one of the options open to NATO within
the current strategy;

(d) invite the NATO Military Authorities to continue to plan for the exercise of
this option if NATO should elect to do so in time of crisis, and to maintain the
necessary military capability to implement the option if directed; and

(e) note that the operational concepts and doctrine currently governing
employment of nuclear weapons for tactical purposes(3) are sufficiently broad
and flexible to accommodate the early combined use option.
