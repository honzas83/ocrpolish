> # Implementation of Special ADM Guidelines
> 
> This document is a revised draft report by the Nuclear Planning Group Permanent Representatives on the status of the implementation of Special Political Guidelines for the possible use of Atomic Demolition Munitions (ADMs). The report summarizes and comments on the current status of implementation based on information received from the Military Committee and the Supreme Allied Commander Europe (SACEUR). It addresses four broad headings: planning for the employment of ADMs, national constraints, advance preparation, and release procedures. The report notes that while planning aspects have been implemented where Major NATO Commanders have sole responsibility, work continues where tasks are shared with national authorities. It highlights that target folders have been prepared for possible ADM use in the Northern, Central, and Southern regions of Allied Command Europe (ACE). The document also discusses national constraints, noting that Italy and Greece have no particular constraints beyond the guidelines, while Turkey's position remains based on its 1967 stance. The report mentions that the United States has several questions regarding ADM programs of cooperation, allocation, and stockpiling under active consideration. It further emphasizes that overall military plans should not be predicated on an assured release of ADMs.

III. DISCUSSION
5. The Special ADM Guidelines state that the employment of ADMs is one of the
options available to NATO for defensive tactical use of nuclear weapons. The
Guidelines direct that the NATO Military Authorities, in conjunction with the
national authorities concerned, should take appropriate steps to ensure that
this option can be exercised(1). In this context, the MC Report summarises the
status of implementation of the Special ADM Guidelines under four broad
headings: planning for the employment of ADMs, national constraints, advance
preparation, and release procedures. Within this framework, certain specific
questions, such as training of non-US ADM teams and transportation of ADMS in
non-US helicopters, are also treated. The most recent MC Report, like earlier
similar reports, is based on information provided by the Supreme Allied
Commander, Europe (SACEUR) and Ministries of Defence of countries concerned.

Planning for the Employment of ADMS
6. The Special ADM Guidelines stipulate that the NATO Military Authorities
should develop plans for the use of ADMS, either singly or on a package basis,
as appropriate, to complement conventional defensive obstacle systems where
terrain features are suitable and where timely use of ADMS may be critical to
the defence. The Guidelines caution, however, that overall military plans should
not be predicated on an assured release of ADMS(2).

7. The MC Report reinforces, in substance, the opinion we reached in an earlier
document(1) that the planning aspects of the Special ADM Guidelines have been
implemented in those fields for which the Major NATO Commanders have sole
responsibility but that work is still continuing in those fields where Major
NATO Commanders share the implementing task with national authorities. The
Military Committee reported(2) in 1971, for example, that the existing plans of
the NATO Military Commands had been amended as necessary to conform to the
Guidelines. Their most recent Report indicates(3) in general terms that planning
by SACEUR and his subordinate commanders for possible employment of ADMS
continues to be actively pursued. For example, target folders have been prepared
for possible locations where ADMS could be used in support of conventional
defensive systems in the three regions (Northern, Central and Southern) of
Allied Command Europe (ACE). This planning has been carried out in collaboration
with national military authorities, except in those cases where it is precluded
as a matter of national policy.

Advance Preparations
13. The Special ADM Guidelines state(1) that the NATO Military Authorities
should, subject to agreement by the host country, make arrangements to improve
ADM effectiveness, to improve reaction times, and to minimise where possible the
risk of fallout from ADMS. Among other things, these arrangements could include
advance preparations such as prechambering(2) in peacetime or improving
readiness for rapid site preparation(2) in times of tension and initial
hostilities. The MC Report mentions(3) a number of actions that have been taken
by the NATO Military Authorities in this regard with the countries concerned.

14. German authorities have indicated(4) that national policy considerations
preclude prechambering on German territory. Consequently, advance arrangements
have been made by NATO commanders and national authorities for expediting the
preparation of ADM sites in periods of tension or initial hostilities, in the
event that such work is authorised by the German authorities(5). In this
connection, the Military Committee has expressed the view(6) that there is a
need for further research, by nations, into methods of constructing burial
shafts in the minimum possible time. We concur.

wherever possible.

arrangements to improve ADM effectiveness, to improve reaction times,

storage sites to the vicinity of potential employment locations and for
their rapid emplacement. The Guidelines also mention(2) that increasing
the availability of faster means of transportation, such as helicopters,

present time, only US helicopters may be used to carry ADMS prior to a
release decision, although current US regulations permit the use of non-
US aircraft, including helicopters, to be employed for transportation

Conclusion
25. The foregoing survey of the status of implementation of the Special ADM
Guidelines and other related measures leads to a general conclusion that good
progress has been made in those fields, such as planning for the possible
employment of ADMS and the development of abbreviated release procedures, where
the Major NATO Commander (SACEUR) has the primary responsibility. In other
fields where responsibility for implementation is shared with countries, such as
advance preparation of potential ADM sites and arrangements for rapid movement
and emplacement of ADMS, the task has proceeded more slowly and has, in some
cases, been complicated by or forestalled by uncertainties regarding national
positions. Where such uncertainties remain, the countries concerned should be
urged to resolve them if at all possible. Where this cannot be done, the NATO
Military Authorities should be encouraged to develop contingency plans and other

IV. RECOMMENDATIONS
26. The Ministers may wish to :
(a) take note of this report and the MC Report(1);
(b) discuss the issues brought out in these reports;
(c) urge countries concerned to resolve remaining uncertainties that affect the
implementation of the Special ADM Guidelines;

(d) invite the NATO Military Authorities to continue contingency planning and

uncertainties; and

(e) indicate, in the light of their discussion, whether there are any particular
