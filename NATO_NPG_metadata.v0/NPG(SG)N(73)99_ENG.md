> # Aspects of Deterrence
> 
> This document consists of a cover note from H.B. Seim and a letter from David M. Clinard to Harvey B. Seim, attaching a working paper titled 'Aspects of Deterrence'. The working paper argues that a credible deterrent posture, based on both conventional and nuclear forces, is fundamental to NATO's defense efforts. It defines deterrence as a combination of 'capability' (the ability to inflict unacceptable damage on an aggressor) and 'will' (the intent to use that capability). The text emphasizes that the United States strategic nuclear guarantee is fundamental to NATO's security. It discusses the the transition from automatic massive nuclear retaliation to a strategy of flexibility in response. The paper highlights the role of tactical nuclear weapons in signaling the risk of escalation to the strategic level. It also stresses the importance of maintaining flexibility and uncertainty to prevent an enemy from predicting NATO's response. Finally, it mentions that the the lack of nuclear weapons deployment in Denmark and Norway weakens the deterrent posture, and that the ability of command and control systems to support the strategy effectively is critical.

ASPECTS OF DETERRENCE

1. The maintenance of a credible deterrent posture based on adequate
conventional and nuclear forces underlies and is fundamental to all NATO's
defence efforts. It is axiomatic that the work done in the follow-on use
programme is based upon the doctrine of deterrence against aggression at any
level. In Phase II, therefore, we must weigh up the contribution that our
thinking about follow-on use makes to deterrence; indeed it could be said that
this is our main task. The follow-on studies contain much that is relevant to
deterrence, but deterrence is a very broad subject, which this paper therefore,
while written against the background of the studies, attempts to treat as a
whole.

2. We recognise at the outset that there is a certain artificality about
examining the follow-on tactical use of nuclear weapons as if it were a discrete
aspect of NATO strategy. We need to bear in mind that the deterrent posture as a
whole must carry conviction in all circumstances and there may be a danger in
considering aspects of it in isolation. For example, the fact that NATO is known
to be prepared for follow-on use of nuclear weapons should enhance the deterrent
effect of any initial use. In an actual crisis the solidarity, cohesion and
firmness of purpose demonstrated by NATO in the early stages should have a
deterrent effect on the enemy and influence the judgement he makes of the likely
NATO responses to any further steps he may take. Again, evidence that NATO is
determined to pursue the goal of conventional force improvements will have a
bearing on the credibility of our nuclear deterrent.

3. We do not wish to attempt a complete definition of deterrence. But we believe
that its two most important elements are capability and will; "capability" means
having sufficient forces to be able, in any conceivable circumstances, to impose
a degree of damage on a potential aggressor which he will regard as unacceptable
in advance, and "will" means having the evident intent to use this capability in
a timely fashion if necessary. In the case of an alliance strategy such as
NATO's "will" clearly includes the preparedness of all member countries to
accept the risks of escalation.

4. What constitutes an unacceptable level of damage can be judged only in
relation to the enemy's objectives; however, we believe that the strike forces
assigned to NATO together with US national strategic forces could inflict, even
after absorbing a first-strike, a level of damage on the Soviet Union that in
any rational consideration would be disproportionate to any conceivable
objectives and would indeed make any such objectives unattainable. Provided,
therefore, that the Soviet Union clearly perceives that any attack on NATO
territory carries with it the risk that it could ultimately lead to strategic
nuclear strikes on Soviet territory, deterrence should not fail. The US
strategic nuclear guarantee is, therefore, fundamental to deterrence and to
NATO's security.

5. But our strategy has to be credible in our own eyes, in the sense that we
must have not only the capability but also the determination to use it if need
be. NATO's former strategy of automatic massive nuclear retaliation was
discarded because with the achievement by the Russians of an assured second
strike capability it had come to lack credibility. The present strategy of
flexibility in response envisages meeting any aggressor at an appropriate level
and also indicating our intention to escalate the conflict, if need be to the
highest level.

6. Tactical use of nuclear weapons has a vital rôle here, in emphasizing the
danger of escalation to the strategic use of nuclear weapons. This is a key
aspect of initial use by NATO, which would undoubtedly involve communication to
the enemy on the political channel concerning the risks of continued aggression.
If we are ever faced with the need for follow-on use we will undoubtedly be in a
grave situation for in a sense deterrence will have failed not once but twice.
First when aggression occurs and second when it continues despite the
qualitative change in the conflict marked by NATO's first use of nuclear
weapons. Thus it is possible that the Soviets could attack NATO recognising that
this might lead to initial use by NATO but believing that there would be no
nuclear follow-up and that if they nevertheless continued to press their attack
(or indeed remained in possession of such NATO territory as they had gained)
there would be a collapse of further NATO defence. We must, therefore, continue
to make it clear that NATO has both the capability and the intention in such
situations to continue to use nuclear weapons both for the military effect
thereby achieved and to demonstrate that the enemy is risking escalation to the
strategic level. But this is where we must register what is at first sight a
paradox. Only by convincing the enemy that we are ready and capable to use
nuclear weapons can we minimise the risk that we shall need to do so.

7. It may be appropriate here to touch on the factor of uncertainty which is
cardinal to any consideration of deterrence. We cannot hope that a potential
enemy will be certain that we will inflict a given level of damage but he must
be persuaded that there is an unacceptable risk of such damage. Similarly, it is
vitally important that we should not have rigid intentions, for example, as
regards the timing and scale of initial use. We must have a complete range of
options and we must maintain flexibility, otherwise the enemy could frame his
plans in such a way as to discount our first use of nuclear weapons. Thus, while
NATO should make general contingency arrangements to enable, for example, any
desired deployment of nuclear weapons to be smoothly carried out in a crisis
situation, NATO should not draw up specific and detailed plans for the initial
and follow-on use of nuclear weapons.

8. It can be seen that the requirements of flexibility and uncertainty point to
the retention by NATO of a wide range of weapon systems and nuclear warheads,
widely deployed. They also point to the need to be able to demonstrate our
ability to use nuclear weapons quickly in any part of NATO territory. In
particular the deployment of nuclear weapons must be such as to accord with
NATO's commitment to forward defence. In this context the fact that it is known
that nuclear weapons are not presently deployed in Denmark and Norway weakens
NATO's deterrent posture to some extent. Conversely, active planning in
peacetime, including physical measures whenever and wherever politically
acceptable, for the use of ADMs in war, would contribute to deterrence.

9. The credibility of NATO's deterrent also depends on the ability of command
and control, including political control, to support the strategy effectively.
There is a danger that the NATO system of political control might impose delays
such that the release of nuclear weapons would come too late for the particular
tactical situation to which a request was originally related. We must face up to
this issue. There are things that can be and are being done to improve
communications. Work in the NPG and experience in exercises should facilitate
the process of alliance consultation in a crisis (indeed, the very fact that we
practice and perfect our consultation procedures in exercises contributes to
deterrence). Nonetheless we must recognise that extended battlefield use of
nuclear weapons, as portrayed in some of the follow-on studies, may not be
compatible with detailed political control over each individual release.

If it were judged from a political and military point of view that there would
be advantage in undertaking follow-on use on the scale envisaged in the land/air
studies, particularly those in the Central.Region, then a system of release with
a substantial element of pre-conditioned authority whereby military commanders
would be authorised to use nuclear weapons in defense subject to constraints in
overall numbers, yields, etc., might be necessary to make such options
practicable. This need not imply any prejudgement that such options would be
politically acceptable in the event, but deterrence is clearly enhanced by our
having the physical capability and the consultation procedures to implement
them.

10. There is another important aspect of deterrence which should be mentioned.
Without adequate resources (and we realise that the word "adequate" begs a
question – see below) of tactical nuclear weapons we could conceivably be open
to "nuclear blackmail" tactics by the Warsaw Pact who could threaten NATO with
the use of their tactical nuclear weapons, judging that it would not be a
credible response on our part to threaten immediate strategic escalation.

11. We have asked ourselves whether the follow-on use studies have thrown any
new light on the minimum numbers of nuclear weapons systems that are necessary
for deterrent purposes. We have concluded that this is not a question
susceptible of any precise answer. However, the studies have demonstrated that
during a follow-on use phase, there could be a wide range of options open to
NATO. It could be argued that the more of these options that NATO could
implement the greater the deterrent effect.

12. At the outset of this paper we made it clear that deterrence is a very broad
subject, and in discussing certain factors which underlay the work done in the
Phase I Follow-on Use Programme, we have not attempted to undertake a
comprehensive treatment of the question. The NPG Ministers have invited
Permanent Representatives to consider the value of a conceptual analysis of the
nuclear aspects of deterrence, and this paper may be of some value in that
consideration. Our primary purpose, however, has been to identify some of the
important factors which the NPG will need to take into account when considering
the problems of follow-on use.
