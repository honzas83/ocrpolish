> # Consultation Procedures and Facilities
> 
> This document, a draft report by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG), details the examination of procedures and facilities for political consultation regarding the use of nuclear weapons in time of war. It focuses on strengthening the consultation process to ensure rapid, secure, and unambiguous exchanges of information. The report addresses two primary aspects of message content: the substance of information required by political authorities and the form and terminology used in selective release messages. To reduce misunderstandings, it proposes the use of plain language in release requests, such as using descriptive phrases like 'weapons required' instead of complex codes. Additionally, the report discusses the handling and identification of nuclear consultation messages to prevent delays or misrouting. Proposed measures include the use of the NATO-wide Communications System, Address Indicating Groups (AIGs), and the insertion of the phrase 'Nuclear Consultation Message' at the beginning of the text. The document also confirms that the current precedence system for critical traffic is workable and reliable for wartime use.

I. PURPOSE
This report continues the examination of selected aspects of procedures and
facilities to be used by the Alliance for political consultation in time of war
on questions of using nuclear weapons, summarises steps that have been taken to
strengthen this consultation process, and makes proposals for further work in
this context.

selective release messages. Similarly, after their consideration at Ankara of
some preliminary observations on WINTEX 73, the Ministers invited(2) the
Permanent Representatives to look into the matter of identification and handling

5. With respect to format and terminology, there is no question that political

reducing, as far as possible, the chance of a misunderstanding of, or delay in

Handling and Identification of Nuclear Consultation Messages
6. Since a significant portion of the exchanges of information among
participants in the nuclear consultation process will be in the form of message
traffic, it is clear that steps taken to facilitate rapid handling of such
mes...age traffic, and to ensure its ready identification, will contribute to
the effectiveness of the consultation process. Conversely, the process could be
seriously degraded if messages fail to reach responsible officials or reached
them too late, due to handling delays or due to failure on the part of those
involved in handling messages to recognise their relevance to the consultation
process.

Further Action
9. In addition to the several separate but related aspects of the nuclear
consultation process that we have discussed in this report, we recall that we
have covered a number of other aspects in earlier reports to the Ministers(1).
It appears to us that these various elements should now be consolidated into a
single, cohesive package that would contain information and guidance on the
details of the procedural aspects of the nuclear consultation process. We note
that this step would be consistent with, and similar to, work now being carried
out in NATO under the overall coordination of the Council Operations and
Exercise Committee in the field of NATO crisis management procedures and
arrangements(2). Subject to Ministerial concurrence, we propose therefore to
undertake the development of a compilation of nuclear consultation procedures
that will incorporate the diverse elements mentioned above. The package could,
when completed, form a contribution to the crisis management work being carried
out under the aegis of the Council Operations and Exercise Committee. The
detailed procedures contained in the compilation would, of course, support, and
be compatible with, approved policy guidance on nuclear consultation
procedures(3).

HANDLING AND IDENTIFICATION OF NUCLEAR CONSULTATION MESSAGES
Introduction
1. The effectiveness of the nuclear consultation process is critically dependent
on ability of participants in that process to exchange information and views in
a rapid, secure and reliable way. Since a major part of this information
exchange will be in the form of message traffic, measures that facilitate rapid
handling of such messages and that ensure their ready identification to
responsible officials will contribute to the effectiveness of the consultation
process. We discuss some of these measures in the following paragraphs.

Use of the NATO-wide Communications System

containing preliminary observations on WINTEX 73 said(1) that exercise
experience gave the general impression that the NATO-wide Communications System,
to the extent that it was used, did in fact lead to an improvement in the speed
and reliability of transmitting traffic related to nuclear consultation. We
reported, however, that there were some apparent problems and delays in the
handling or transmission of release requests addressed to political authorities.
After considering our WINTEX 73 report at Ankara, the Ministers noted(2) that

Precedence

10. Noting the concern that has from time to time been expressed that the
precedence assigned to nuclear consultation messages may be insufficient to
ensure timely delivery to political authorities, we have in an earlier report
investigated(1) the question of whether a special precedence for such traffic
might be warranted. We concluded that the present arrangements regarding
precedence are workable, and that they should function effectively in time of
war in support of the political consultation process. In this connection, it is
worth emphasising that the precedence assigned to a message by the originator
not only gives instructions to communications personnel with regard to the
relative priority to be accorded to the message in handling it in communications
channels but also provides guidance to the officials responsible for taking
action on its contents with regard to the originator's assessment of the urgency
of the matter. Thus, if an originator assigns the highest precedence, FLASH, to
an outgoing message, he is directing communications personnel to process the
message through communications channels before any other messages of lower
precedence, i.e., IMMEDIATE, PRIORITY or ROUTINE. At the same time, he is also
advising the officials responsible for taking action on the content of the
message that he considers the subject matter warrants highest priority
attention.

11. There appears to be general acceptance within NATO of a view that in time of
war messages related to the nuclear consultation process would usually be of
such urgency and importance as to justify the assignment of the highest

with other measures, ensure that critical traffic would be handled rapidly and
reliably in time of war. Accordingly, we feel that there is no valid requirement
at the present time for a new precedence, higher than FLASH, to be reserved only
for nuclear consultation messages.
