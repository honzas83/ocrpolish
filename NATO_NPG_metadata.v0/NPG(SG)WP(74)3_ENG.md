> # Military Committee Function in the Nuclear Consultation Process
> 
> This working paper, prepared by the Permanent Representatives, reviews the function of the Military Committee in the process of Alliance consultation regarding the possible use of nuclear weapons. It emphasizes that while the ultimate decision to use nuclear weapons rests with the nuclear power, the North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) aims for the greatest possible degree of consultation among member governments. The document highlights that the nuclear consultation process differs from general consultation by requiring the exchange of clearly identifiable national views rather than a collective position. It argues that the Military Committee should not obscure individual country views or force a commonly agreed position, as this would be inconsistent with the the General Guidelines for Consultation Procedure on the Use of Nuclear Weapons. The process is described as time-sensitive, requiring rapid expression of views to influence the release decision of the nuclear power. The Military Committee's role is essentially the same as in general consultation, provided it does not conceal or distort national positions. The document includes an annex providing clarifying comments on the distinction between national military advice and government views.

I. PURPOSE

This report reviews the function of the Military Committee in the process of
Alliance consultation in times of tension or hostilities on the possible use of

III. DISCUSSION

3. In making a review of the function of the Military Committee in the nuclear
consultation process, it is essential to have a clear appreciation of the
process itself. The relevant basic guidance is contained in the General
Guidelines for Consultation Procedure on the Use of Nuclear Weapons(1). These
Consultation Guidelines were adopted by the Defence Planning Committee in 1969
on the recommendation of the NPG and the Nuclear Defence Affairs Committee in
the light of intensive consideration of the subject in the NPG during the first
three years of its existence(2). They also reflect a significant body of opinion
and experience developed within the Alliance over a period of years prior to the
creation of the NPG. The Consultation Guidelines are the benchmark governing the
roles played by the various bodies that participate in the nuclear consultation
process, including the Military Committee.

4. The Consultation Guidelines state that nuclear consultation would fit into
the framework of a procedure of general consultation between member countries
which is already operating(3). Accordingly, the bulk of the activity associated
with the nuclear consultation process follows very closely the pattern of
general consultation. Nevertheless, the Guidelines point out that nuclear

the proposed use(5). In reaching its decision, the nuclear power would give

6. It is clear from the foregoing that certain aspects of the nuclear
consultation process differ in a very fundamental way from the general
consultation approach used most frequently in conducting Alliance affairs,
namely, to seek to reach a collective position that all members support but
that, in many cases, may not fully reflect the individual views of each member.
In direct contrast, the effective operation of the nuclear consultation process
depends critically on the exchange of clearly-identifiable national views on
specific requests or proposals for use of nuclear weapons. Attempts to reach a
commonly-agreed position in the Council/DFC that prevent, delay or distort the
transmission of individual government views to the nuclear power(s) would be
inconsistent with the Consultation Guidelines. In a similar vein, we suggest
that it would be incompatible with the basic philosophy of the Consultation
Guidelines, and therefore impermissible, for the Military Committee to carry out
its deliberations concerning a request or proposal for nuclear weapons

7. Closely linked with the need to preserve the clear identity of individual

requirement to do everything possible to ensure that these views reach the
nuclear power(s) in time to have an influence on the release decision. This
decision is time-sensitive, since the political, psychological and military
impact of the proposed use can be critically dependent on the timing of that
use. Much of the considerable effort that the Alliance has devoted toward
ensuring that suitable facilities and procedures are available for the nuclear
consultation process is designed to permit the rapid expression of views of
member governments(1). Consequently, the participation of the Military Committee
in the nuclear consultation process must be carried out in a way that
contributes to this basic objective.

COMMENT ON THE MILITARY COMMITTEE REPORT
1. Some clarifying comment might be useful with respect to the Military
Committee's statement "As the consultation process draws closer to the ultimate
decision, it will become more difficult to differentiate between national
military advice and national political advice, since in many countries military
and political leaders confer jointly in reaching a national position."(1) The
"national position" most relevant to the ultimate decision is the "government
view", mentioned in the Consultation Guidelines(2), which forms the national
input to the nuclear consultation process. Under the procedure envisaged in the
Consultation Guidelines(3), member governments would express and compare their
views in the Council/DPC as the normal forum, although it is fully recognised
that other channels might, and probably would, be used in the process(4).
