> # Technology Study
> 
> This document, issued by the North Atlantic Council, Atlantic Council, establishes the terms of reference for the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Technology Study Group. The primary purpose of the { "//": "Corrected OCR error: 'NPC' and 'NIG' are likely 'NPG'" } Nuclear Planning Group Technology Study Group is to examine the political and military implications of new weapons systems technology for the tactical use of nuclear weapons between 1975 and 1985. The study's scope includes assessing how improved technology might contribute to deterrence and defense capabilities within the NATO strategy framework (MC 14/5). The group is divided into a Military Implications Team and a Political Implications Team, supported by a Technology Advisory Panel. The study will evaluate technologies such as improvements in delivery accuracy, precision guidance, and enhanced radiation effect weapons, as well as alternative delivery methods like earth penetrators. General assumptions include the continued threat from the Warsaw Pact and the continued improvement of NATO's own capabilities. The Military Implications Team will analyze effectiveness compared to existing technologies, focusing on on operational, doctrinal, and force structure terms. The Political Implications Team will assess deterrence, the nuclear threshold, risks of escalation, and population safety hazards. The document concludes by suggesting that the Military Implications Team develop a specific set of questions to evaluate each technology's military benefits and costs.

TECHNOLOGY STUDY
PREAMBULE
1. (to be provided at a later date)
PURPOSE
2. To examine the political and military implications of new weapons systems
technology for the tactical use of nuclear weapons. This will involve an
evaluation of how new improved technology considered to be available in the near
future might contribute to, or improve, deterrence and defense capabilities
within the broad framework of NATO strategy (MC 14/5).

SCOPE
3. The task assigned the Technology Group, by NPG document NPG/D(74)3,
essentially establishes the scope of the study:

"The study effort will be carried out by an ad hoc group (hereafter referred to
as the Technology Study Group) with a broad mandate to undertake whatever work
it feels would be necessary and useful to respond to the Ministerial interest in
the subject. In general terms, the task of the Technology Study Group includes
contributing to NPG knowledge of technological developments that may have an
impact on the whole complex of questions surrounding the tactical use of nuclear
weapons, identifying and assessing the political and military implications of
these technological developments, and making recommendations to the NPG, as
appropriate, for follow-up action. The specific factors to be considered in
assessing the political and military impact of technological developments are
left for the

4. It is intended that the primary effort be devoted to nuclear technology, but
non-nuclear technologs that are directly related should be considered as
appropriate.

5. In the context of this general guidance, the Study Group is invited to
consider appropriate aspects of the following:

(a) The relationship of the new/improved capability(s) to the tactical use of
nuclear weapons, over the general time frame of the study, 1975-1985.

(b) How new/improved capability(s) might contribute to, or improve, deterrence
and defense capabilities.

(c) The hazards to civilian populations and property.
(d) Possible implications for the nuclear threshold and ... escalation.
(e) The impact of the new/improved capability(s) on the requirement for the use
of nuclear versus conventional weapons.

(f) The cost of savings in general terms that result from the increased
effectiveness of the new/improved capability(s) (considering such factors as
manpower, structure, support, complexity of use and vulnerability).

(g) The impact on NATO concepts, guidelines, tactics, and procedures for the
employment of nuclear weapons.

(h) The possible consequences of the development of similar capabilities by the
Warsaw Pact.

GENERAL ASSUMPTIONS

6. Most assumptions will be developed as found necessary during the conduct of
the study. Basic assumptions common to all groups might include the following:

(a) The Warsaw Fact will continue to pose a significant threat and its
organization, deployments and capabilities will continue to improve.

(b) Similarly, NATO organization, deployments and capabilities will continue to
improve as currently planned.

(c) Throat will be addressed under two alternatives:
(i) Comparable capability with new technology
(ii) Lacking comparable new technology
(d) Weapons and systems considered will be those believed available in the time
frame of the study (1975-1985).

(e) NATO strategy will continue to be based upon flexible response as set forth
in IC 14/3 and will continue to rely on the NATO Tried.

(f) Current guidelines and consultative procedures will remain in effect.

METHODOLOGY

7. It is envisioned that in the conduct of the study, two principal efforts will
be undertaken: to assess the military impact of new technologies; and to assess
the political implications.

(a) Military Implications Team, considers the information provided by the
Technology Advisory Panel and determines the military implications, advantages
and disadvantages in operational, doctrinal and force structure terms, of
incorporating the new weapons technology in forces available in the near term.

(b) Political Implications Team, considering material provided by the Technology
Advisory Panel, and appropriate results of the military assessment,

8. As to the technologies that should be considered, paragraphs 2 and 4 of
NPG/D(74)3 state that the Technology Study Group has a broad mandate to
undertake whatever work it feels would be necessary and useful to respond to
Ministerial interest on the subject. More specifically, paragraph 3 indicates
that the Study Group should consider those technologies that may have an impact
on the whole complex of questions surrounding the tactical use of nuclear
weapons. Paragraph 4 goes on to explain that "technological developments" should
be broadly construed to cover devices and techniques that might be within the
state-of-the-art. It further states that "developments in nuclear weapon
technology are included in this context".

9. Some technologies which are offered for the general consideration of the
Military and Political Implications Teams follow.

(a) Improvements in accuracy of delivery
(i) Target acquisition and designation means
(ii) Precision guidance

(b) Improvements in nuclear firepower effects
(i) Suppressed radiation
(ii) Enhanced radiation effect weapons
(iii) Reduced fallout and collateral damage per weapon resulting from lower
yield/more accurate weaponry

(c) Alternative delivery methods
(i) Earth penetrators (vs airburst, surface-burst or pre-emplacemnt).

MILITARY IMPLICATIONS
11. The new weapon technologies should be evaluated, in part, by analyzing their
effectiveness, compared to existing technologies, in the same postulated
military situations. Some of the factors to be considered in identifying, and
assessing the military implications might include:

(e) Nuclear employment concepts and procedures, considering such factors as
weapons effects, collateral damage, flexibility, command and control, readiness,
and reaction times.

(b) NATO defense capabilities, and force structure requirements considering the
need to maintain a strong conventional defense against the Warsaw Pact,
supported by nuclear capabilities.

(c) Impact on theater nuclear stockpile.
(d) Doctrine, tactics, and procedures.
(e) Implications of the introduction of similar new technology by the Warsaw
Pact, to include consideration of Warsaw Pact overall capabilities and doctrine.

12. It is suggested that the Military Implications Team develop a series of
questions that should be addressed when considering each of the technologies.
The questions would be designed to bring out factors such as: the military
benefits that would accrue; the costs and/or savings in terms of manpower,
support, etc.; and how the new technologies would enhance the use of tactical
nuclear weapons and the overall defense capabilities of NATO.

POLITICAL IMPLICATIONS
13. Considering the military implications of the new technologies, the political
implications might be assessed from the standpoint of:

(a) Deterrence, nuclear threshold, risks of escalation, considering
(1) Conventional conflict
(ii) Initial use of nuclear weapons
(iii) Follow-on use of nuclear weapons
(b) Command and control, including political control and decision time.
(c) Population and safety hazards.
(d) Implications for the Warsaw Pact, to include Warsaw Pact reactions.
(e) Cost acceptability.
(f) Public attitudes.
(g) Alliance cohesiveness.
(h) Warsaw Pact cohesiveness.
(i) Economic advantages or constraints.
(j) Unique factors considered likely in wartime.
(k) Rationalization/standardization.
