> [!abstract] 
> # General Political Guidelines for the Employment of Nuclear Weapons in Defence of NATO
> 
> This document, a cover note and an annex extracted from a larger study, details the General Political Guidelines for the Employment of Nuclear Weapons in Defence of NATO. It emphasizes that NATO's fundamental objective is to deter aggression by the Warsaw Pact through a credible deterrence based on the warfighting capabilities to respond to any type of aggression. The document discusses the NATO Triad, consisting of conventional, theater nuclear, and strategic nuclear forces, and argues that the interrelatedness of these components is essential for effective deterrence. It distinguishes between selective nuclear employment, intended to induce a political decision to cease aggression by demonstrating miscalculation, and general nuclear war, where employment is governed by military objectives. The annex further analyzes the specific utility and escalatory value of various nuclear options, including Atomic Demolition Munitions (ADM), land-based nuclear air defense weapons, and maritime nuclear capabilities. It notes that while some options like ADM and friendly soil emplacement have lower escalatory value, maritime options may be more militarily attractive due to lack of collateral damage to populations. The document concludes that the use of nuclear weapons in direct support of conventional forces provides a broad range of tactical options.

PART II
ROLE OF TNF WITHIN NATO STRATEGY
7. (NS): As stated in the political guidelines, NATO's fundamental objective is
to deter aggression by the Warsaw Pact. To achieve credible deterrence, NATO
must have the warfighting capabilities to respond to any type of aggression,
appropriate to the level at which it has been initiated. Further, the enemy must
be convinced of the certainty of NATO's response, but uncertain as to its
nature. To achieve the necessary degree of uncertainty requires the full
spectrum of force capabilities, both conventional and nuclear, with the
requisite mix and quantity of TNF systems. TNF capabilities, ranging from
defensive, through battlefield and maritime, to deep strike systems, provides
the basis to demonstrate to the enemy the calculability of risks involved in any
type of aggression against NATO. If the Pact believes that NATO will escalate
the conflict in its own defense, if necessary to general nuclear war, and its
force capabilities and dispositions reflect this determination, then a decision
to initiate aggression is made increasingly more difficult. Conversely, if
NATO's force capabilities do not provide the necessary basis to execute flexible
response, the enemy's risk calculations are less complex and the decision to use
military force is simplified. TNF, in particular, the battlefield systems, by
their very existence complicate the enemy's planning for conventional
aggression. Thus, nuclear capable forces serve to strengthen the Alliance's
conventional defense and, in the proper mix, contribute to NATO's overall
deterrence.

8. (NS) The NATO Triad with its component parts of conventional, theater
nuclear, and strategic nuclear forces was developed to enable the Alliance to
execute the strategy of flexible response. The deterrent effect of the Triad can
only be accomplished when all parts are interrelated and visible as an entity.
Any impression that the three components can be viewed independently may
undermine deterrence and lead the Warsaw Pact to the belief that the risks of
aggression against NATO are both calculable and acceptable. For TNF the
requirement for close interrelationship of the three elements of the Triad is of
particular importance because of the coupling function of these capabilities to
the other two components, the conventional as well as the strategic nuclear
forces. This best can be assured conceptually by providing sufficient overlap
within the Triad on the basis of well balanced force capabilities.

9. (NS) It must be recognized that the defensive character of the Alliance, the
conventional force ratio and the geostategic situation in Europe, will not allow
defeat of the enemy in the classic sense. In addition, intensified use of
nuclear weapons, in an era of near nuclear balance, with the express objective
of overcoming the Pact's conventional superiority may not be to NATO's

10. (NS) For all these reasons, selective nuclear use is intended to confront
the enemy with the threat of further escalation, thereby inducing him to
recalibrate his risks and to make the political decision to stop his aggression
and withdraw. Current political guidance does not envisage selective use of
nuclear weapons primarily as a means of attriting aggressor forces. Consistent
with that objective, there is a clear distinction between selective nuclear
employment and general nuclear war. In the latter case, where an aggressor has
clearly committed himself to all-out victory and thus to all the risks entailed,
nuclear employment is governed primarily by military objectives. In this
situation, in which nuclear weapons are used extensively by both sides, such
weapons become simply another means to impose NATO's will on the enemy through
the attrition of his physical capacity to continue the war.

11. (NS) In contrast, selective employment assumes a situation in which it is
likely that the aggressor has mis-calculated NATO's resolve, and thus his own
risks. In this case, the objective of nuclear use is primarily political -- to
demonstrate his miscalculation, and in the process, to convince him that the
risks of continuing his action outweigh any possible benefits. Far from seeking
to impose a military defeat on the aggressor by destroying his capabilities,
selective employment is intended to induce a political decision to cease
aggression even though his capacity to continue remains. Moreover, selective
employment does not seek to reduce the enemy's capabilities to the point where
he might feel compelled to escalate in order to survive.

12. (NS) Should NATO decide to initiate the use of nuclear weapons, the scope
and scale of such use should be based on the actual and projected political and
military situation. Moreover, NATO's selective employment of nuclear weapons
must be militarily effective and have sufficient shock and decisiveness to
induce a reassessment by the aggressor, while still being clearly recognizable
as a deliberately constrained, selective application of force. A militarily
effective use is one which the enemy cannot afford to ignore, and which,
therefore, has a high assurance of inducing the political recalibration of risks

16. (CTS) Use of Atomic Demolition Munitions. Use of ADM could impede enemy
mobility, and thus help to stabilize a fluid conventional battle. ADM could be
effective where geography itself canals attacking forces, and where political
restrictions and warning time permit emplacement. Such conditions normally would
restrict use of ADM to friendly soil. This, coupled with the fact that ADM are
unlikely to impose damage on enemy forces, tends to reduce their escalatory
value. Therefore, use of ADM as a separate option may lack the necessary shock
and decisiveness to achieve the desired political effect. These considerations
tend to limit the utility of this option by itself, but use of ADM in
conjunction with other nuclear employment may provide operational advantages.

17. (CTS) Land-Based Nuclear Air Defense Weapons. Like ADM, nuclear air defense
weapons have limited escalatory impact. Their military effectiveness depends
heavily on the actual character of the air threat; even so, use of such systems
may not have a sufficiently decisive effect on the enemy. Employment of air
defense weapons in selective use is further complicated by the fact that it
depends upon enemy air activities, making control of the extent and timing
extremely difficult. This leads to the conclusion that nuclear air defense
systems may have utility mainly during follow-on use as an independent means of
combatt[ing] a serious air threat.

18. (CTS) Maritime Use of Nuclear Weapons. The growing Soviet maritime threat
highlights the military importance of maritime nuclear capabilities. The
military attractiveness of maritime nuclear options is further enhanced by the
fact that such options normally would not involve collateral damage to territory
and populations. Strikes against surface ships, anti-submarine and anti-air
warfare could be used singly or in combination during selective use. However,
such options may have less impact on enemy risk calculations than options which
more directly threaten Warsaw Pact territory. At the same time, the dispersed
targets and fleeting contacts, particularly submarines, may complicate control
of time, place and nature of use. The utility of maritime employment in
selective use will thus tend to be dependent upon the prevailing overall
situation. Decisiveness may be enhanced by coupling maritime employment with
other nuclear options, provided that such combined employment can be
coordinated.

19. (CTS) Battlefield Use of Nuclear Weapons. Employment of nuclear weapons in
direct support or engaged conventional forces offers a broad range of options
which will immediately -- but may only temporarily affect the overall tactical
situation. Because of NATO's defensive strategy, battlefield use would probably
take place mainly on friendly soil. Escalatory impact may be reduced
accordingly, and enemy risk assessments may be further influenced by a
calculation that response in kind will produce military advantages without risk
to his territory. Because of their mobility and changing nature, battlefield
targets present the least stable of

20. (CTS) Nuclear Interdiction. Nuclear interdiction comprises a broad spectrum
of selective employment options, ranging from the disruption of second echelon
and reinforcing elements prior to their commitment on the battlefield, to the
disruption of enemy lines of communication deep in Warsaw Pact territory. Unlike
battlefield use, interdiction may not immediately affect the situation at the
forward edge of the battle area, but can seriously disrupt the momentum of
Warsaw Pact operations. Because such employment would take place at least in
part on Warsaw Pact territory, interdiction has a high probability of
influencing the enemy's risk calculation. At the same time, concentration of
effort against fixed targets permits preplanning and close control of execution,
contributing to the delivery of a perceivable escalatory signal.

21. (CTS) Counter-Air Use of Nuclear Weapons. This type of employment
encompasses strikes against airfields on Warsaw Pact territory, and "KIEV-type"
ships, and offers the same advantages of preplanning and control provided by
interdiction options. Counter-air use can be tailored by selection of targets
and weapons, by including or excluding airfields collocated with nuclear storage
sites, and by varying constraints. This provides the same capability as
interdiction to deliver perceivable escalatory signals, while directly
influencing the military situation. Hence, counter-air options are attractive
for all phases of selective use, separately or in conjunction with other
employment options.

22. (CTS) Counter-Nuclear Use of Nuclear Weapons. Employment of nuclear weapons
against Warsaw Pact nuclear delivery and weapons storage sites offers the same
operational advantages as interdiction and counter-air use. However, because
such employment would immediately affect the Warsaw Pact's capacity to
retaliate, it would be highly escalatory, and could possibly lead to general
nuclear war. Therefore, such options may be considered only if the aggressor has
already employed nuclear weapons on NATO territory or against Alliance nuclear
capabilities.

23. (CTS) Use of Nuclear Weapons Against War Support and Counter-Value Targets.
This option encompasses strikes in enemy territory on installations which
provide the enemy's capacity for aggression. Targets could include factories,
power plants, refineries, and port facilities. Because such resources are
essential for the Warsaw Pact's war support capabilities, and because the

24. (NS) General Nuclear Response may become necessary should deliberate
escalation fail to convince the enemy to stop his aggression, or as a result of
enemy actions in response to NATO’s escalation. Such employment also would be
required were the Warsaw Pact to initiate general nuclear war. Unlike selective
employment, the objective of General Nuclear Response is the destruction of the
Warsaw Pact’s capability and will to wage war. Maintenance of NATO’s capacity to
execute General Nuclear Response throughout the entire escalation process is
fundamental to deter the enemy from viewing general nuclear war as an
advantageous course of action. Finally, a vital factor in both selective
employment and General Nuclear Response is the close relationship between NATO’s
TNF and the strategic nuclear forces, assured by joint planning and by the
integration of SACEUR’s Scheduled Programs with the US Single Integrated
Operational Plan (SIOP).

25. (NS) An additional vital factor to be taken into account in determining the
role of TNF is its impact on Warsaw Pact doctrine and operational planning for
conventional and nuclear aggression against NATO. The size, mix, and deployment
of TNF significantly influences and complicates enemy planning for optimum force
structures and doctrine. Moreover, the uncertainty provided by NATO’s strategy,
which does not permit him to calculate time, scope, and scale of nuclear weapons
use by the Alliance, may force him to operate in a nuclear survivable posture
even in a conventional attack. This greater dispersal will make it much more
difficult for the aggressor to concentrate the forces required for a massive
breakthrough making any quick victory highly questionable. Further, sufficient
TNF systems with ranges that can threaten all echelons of enemy forces will
result in greater separation and dispersal of these forces as well, thus
disrupting and delaying timely reinforcements. These forced adjustments to
Warsaw Pact tactical formations and doctrine caused by TNF provides significant
advantages to NATO in countering the threat.

DELIVERY SYSTEM CHARACTERISTICS

27. (CTS) Range. The role of TNF requires the capability to strike targets at
distances ranging from the forward edge of the battle area to locations deep in
enemy territory. At the same time, because the design range of a system may have
important impact on the escalatory signal it delivers, this capability cannot be
adequately provided by a single long-range system, even though it might have the
technical capabilities to strike targets at lesser ranges. Use of such a system
against short-range targets might seriously blur the intended escalatory signal,
increasing the risk of enemy misperception and thus unintentional escalation.
The TNF role thus requires not merely a spectrum of range capabilities, but also
a mix of systems each clearly identifiable with a specific capability. Further,
examination of potential employment options suggests that many of the most
attractive options require the capacity to strike targets in the middle to
longer ranges. Systems providing that capability are therefore essential; such
systems are also of particular importance in establishing the critical link
between theater and strategic nuclear forces.

28. (CTS) Penetration Capability. Because it is vital to selective employment
that an escalatory signal be delivered as intended, systems used for such
employment must have a high assurance of reaching their designated targets. This
assurance can be increased both through the improvement of individual system
penetrability and by the deployment over a wide geographical area of systems
with differing penetration characteristics, preventing the Warsaw Pact from
concentrating its defensive efforts. Such systems need not all have the same
penetration capabilities. However, the smaller the number of weapons to be
employed in a given option, the greater the requirement for individual system
penetrability, because the greater the impact of system attrition on the
intended signal. Finally, since smaller options are more likely to be executed
in the initial stages of selective use before hostile defenses have been greatly
degraded, high penetrability is especially important for the systems likely to
be used in those early stages.

29. (CTS) Accuracy. Like assurance of penetration, predictability of effect is a
key requirement of selective nuclear employment, essential both to the
achievement of the desired political impact and to the demonstration of
deliberate self-restraint. Such predictability depends heavily on the accuracy
with which a system can deliver a weapon on a specific target. At the same time,
because different options seek to achieve different degrees of escalatory
impact, the requirement for system accuracy will vary according to the
employment objective and the nature of the target. Generally speaking, the
greater the self-restraint to be demonstrated, the greater the need for system
accuracy. Systems likely to be used for employment of options of relatively low
escalatory character may therefore require greater accuracy than those

30. (CTS) Responsiveness. The responsiveness of a delivery system comprises
several related characteristics, including the ease with which it can be
targeted once assigned a mission, the rapidity with which it can be launched
once released, and the precision with which it can meet a designated time on
target. Generally, the earlier in selective employment a system is to be used,
the more important will be the requirement to meet a precise time on target
which demonstrates clearly the limited nature of the strike, and thus
contributes to escalation control. During subsequent stages of selective use,
that requirement may become less important than the rapidity with which a system
can respond to unanticipated mission changes, including last-minute strike
approval and cancellation. Finally, for those systems whose principal function
is to support the Scheduled Programmes, the key requirement is the ability to
launch rapidly and in the desired sequence once released. Systems intended for
such employment must thus be capable of remaining in a high state of readiness
for extended periods.

31. (CTS) Versatility. Because of the sensitivity of selective employment to the
political and military conditions prevailing at the time of option selection, a
premium is placed on systems capable of executing a variety of options without
major redeployment or reconfiguration. The availability of such systems
inherently increases overall responsiveness and enhances penetration assurance.
The redundancy they contribute to the overall posture may also be an important
factor in deterring or surviving Warsaw Pact pre-emption. Conversely, because an
aggressor cannot confidently forecast the likely targets of highly versatile
systems, the latter must be employed with care to avoid transmitting a false
escalation signal. This requirement suggests such systems may be most useful
during the early stages of selective employment, when responsiveness is
critical, close control of execution is facilitated, and options are more
narrowly constrained.

32. (CTS) Survivability. Delivery system survivability comprises invulnerability
both to nuclear pre-emption and to conventional loss or capture. It can be
achieved by deployment in sufficient numbers, concealment, hardening, mobility,
and dispersal, or a combination of these attributes. However, improvement of
survivability by any of these means must normally be purchased at some cost to
other desirable system characteristics. Accordingly, efforts to improve the
overall survivability of the posture must reflect the fact that not all systems
need to be equally survivable. Rather, the appropriate degree of survivability

SURVEILLANCE

37. (NS) A reliable and responsive surveillance capability is required for
acquiring time-sensitive targets, particularly enemy mobile forces on or near
the battlefield as well as maritime targets. In addition, surveillance must
provide for rapid post-strike assessment, also at extended range, to determine
if a selective use option was executed as intended in order to evaluate enemy
response.

FORCE DEPLOYMENT AND WEAPON STORAGE

38. (NS) Force deployment and weapon storage are closely related to system
characteristics as key influences on the development and execution of employment
options. They directly affect both responsiveness and survivability; in
addition, they carry special deterrent implications because of their impact on
perceived Alliance cohesion and risk-sharing. Generally, modernization
considerations related to deployment and weapon storage tend to revolve around
one or the other of two issues: concentration versus dispersal, to include the
related issues of regional balance and nuclear storage; and forward versus
rearward deployment, to include the special issue of land-based versus sea-based
systems.

39. (CTS) Concentration versus Dispersal. A number of considerations argue for
greater concentration of theater nuclear assets, by system and location. Apart
from obvious economic attractions, concentration would simplify command and
control, enhancing responsiveness; reduce the maintenance and logistical burdens
entailed by a wide diversity of geographically dispersed systems; and facilitate
the maintenance of peacetime security both of delivery systems and associated
weapons storage sites. Conversely, concentration suffers from equally
significant disadvantages. Most obvious of these is the increase in
vulnerability -- especially to pre-emption -- which results from the
concentration of nuclear assets in a few known locations. This disadvantage
would be compounded by any degradation in penetrability resulting from the
simplification of the aggressor's defensive task. Together with the loss in
versatility resulting from the diminution in targets which can be ranged from a
given location, these liabilities could significantly degrade the spectrum of
options available for execution, especially in later stages of selective use. An
additional, fundamental requirement is to maintain the broad national
participation which is essential to the perception of Alliance cohesion and risk
sharing. Because of this requirement, systems and related weapons storage which
might otherwise be concentrated without serious operational penalty must
nevertheless be dispersed to all regions of NATO.

40. (CTS) Forward versus Rearward Deployment. Many of the same considerations
influencing the relative concentration of nuclear capabilities apply with equal
force to decisions concerning their forward versus rearward stationing. System
survivability and security considerations are again central; in this case,
however, the controlling concern is less vulnerability to loss or capture as a
result of enemy conventional ground and air operations, and the importance of
maintaining close control over nuclear assets, both argue for rearward
deployment in peacetime and in the early stages of hostilities. The advantages
of such stationing must however be balanced against the loss of versatility to
strike targets at greater ranges, and the reduced responsiveness to potential
battlefield use requirements. Rearward stationing is most desirable for systems
essential to execution of Scheduled Programs, for which survivability and rapid
response to release are essential. Current efforts to increase reliance for
execution of the Scheduled Programs on sea-based and especially submarine-
launched systems reflect this consideration. However, as in the issue of
concentration, this consideration must be balanced against the essential
requirement for broad Alliance participation.

COMMAND, CONTROL AND COMMUNICATIONS (C3)

41. (CTS) No matter how carefully improved, NATO's theater nuclear posture will
be no more effective in deterrence or warfighting than the command and control
systems which support it. Shortcomings in this area influence both conventional
and nuclear effectiveness and are particularly damaging to the latter, for which
continuous high-level control is especially crucial. Preplanning of selective
employment options in peacetime may facilitate decision making in crisis or
conflict; but the probability that actual political and military conditions will
require modifications of those options assures that coordination, consultation
and decision making will be no less necessary than heretofore, and insists on
command, control, and communications systems as flexible and reliable as the
forces they support. In conflict, the survivability and responsiveness of
command and control networks will influence both the extent of NATO's ability to
control escalation, and the capacity of nuclear forces to react to a changing
tactical situation. In peacetime, therefore, they will directly influence the
deterrent credibility of NATO's strategy.

42. (CTS) Like command and control mechanisms, responsive procedures for
requesting and releasing nuclear weapons are essential to the effective
execution of deliberate and controlled escalation. Improved capabilities for the
exchange of information would enhance the nuclear decision process and thus
increase the chances that selective nuclear employment will achieve its primary
political objective. Improved procedures should allow for early coordination and
preplanning of further escalatory steps, should they become
