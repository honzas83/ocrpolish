> # Observations on WINTEX 75
> 
> This document provides preliminary observations on the nuclear consultation aspects of the NATO exercise WINTEX 75. The report, prepared by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG), was intended to provide a basis for an initial exchange of views among Ministers during the Spring 1975 Ministerial meeting. The exercise involved NATO capitals, NATO Headquarters in Brussels, and Major NATO Commanders, with a scenario covering the pre-hostilities crisis period, the outbreak of hostilities, and a conventional warfare phase. The report notes that WINTEX 75 had fewer artificialities regarding the nuclear phase play compared to previous exercises, although the political consultation process was limited to a 38-hour period. The document discusses the requirements for the initial use of nuclear weapons, emphasizing that such use should be clearly detectable by the enemy and controlled by NATO to achieve political objectives. It concludes that initial use at sea and in air-defence may not always fulfill these requirements because the timing and location of engagement are often determined by the enemy. The report also mentions the use of SIERRA and ROME0 messages for pre-conditioned release of weapons in anti-submarine warfare and air-defence scenarios.

Exercise Outline and Participation
3. WINTEX 75 was a large-scale NATO-wide Command-Post Exercise involving most
NATO capitals, NATO Headquarters in Brussels and all of the Major NATO
Commanders, as well as many of their subordinate commands. Actual involvement of
NATO Headquarters in Brussels and of those political authorities in capitals who
participated in the exercise was confined to that part of the Active Phase of
the exercise, which ran from 10 a.m. (Brussels time) on Sunday, 9th March, to
midnight on Monday, 10th March. The scenario in the Active Phase covered the
final stage of pre-hostilities crisis period, the outbreak of hostilities and a
conventional warfare phase culminating in requests by SACLANP and SACEUR for the
release of nuclear weapons for tactical use. The political consultation process
related to these requests was confined to the limited period (38 hours), in
which the Defence Planning Committee, advised by the Military Committee,
participated in the Active Phase. The preliminary observations contained later
in this report are therefore related only to this limited period.

Exercise Limitations
4. In comparison with previous exercises of this type, there were in WINTEX 75
less restricting artificialities as far as the nuclear phase play was concerned.
In spite of the limitation of DPC active participation to 38 hours, and to the
consideration of request for initial use only, additional requests were allowed
to reach the DPC in this limited period both from SACLANP and SACEUR, thus
creating a more realistic situation in the context of the nuclear consultation
process. Nevertheless, as required by the exercise arrangements, the play was
stopped when the consultation (the requested initial use from SACLANP was
completed and only preliminary discussion was held on three other requests from
SACEUR which closely followed the request from SACLANP. In view of the political
interrelationship between these various requests and the impact their complete
analysis might have had on other related DPC actions (e.g., CNI), the above
mentioned limitation affected, even though not invalidating, some of the remarks
in the following paragraphs. Another artificiality concerned the amount of
background information available to political authorities. As noted in the
report on WINTEX 73(1), the amount of information was considerably reduced from

affected the momentum of the consultation process. In this connection, it is

III. DISCUSSION
Remarks on Initial Use

5. The Provisional Political Guidelines(1) state that the fundamental objective
of initial use, as of any military act, will always be political, and as any
tactical use, initial use is aimed at inducing the enemy to cease his attack and
withdraw. It is also noted that initial use would implicitly contain a message
of warning to the enemy(2). It would appear, therefore, that such use should
have certain characteristics to satisfy these two requirements. First, in the
spirit and the letter of the P.F.G., the initial employment of nuclear weapons
should be clearly detectable by the enemy and be in direct connection with the
ongoing military operations(3). Secondly, the use should be such that the choice
of the time (and possibly location)can be controlled by NATO. For example, any
use which after authorisation, can take place only when, where, and if the
selected target(s) is available, would leave a certain amount of uncertainty
about the time, the location and even the implementation itself. Under many
circumstances, this situation

6. The foregoing observations lead to the conclusion that the choice of the
target(s) for initial use deserves the most careful political analysis.
Experience gained from WINTEX 75, during which possible initial tactical uses of
nuclear weapons at sea, in air-defence, and in the land battle were discussed,
confirms such conclusion. It appeared from the discussions between those member
of the DPC participating in the exercise, that initial use at sea and in air-
defence might offer no suitable means to convey to the enemy the well-timed and
clear message that should be both implicitly and explicitly carried by the use
itself. This is due to the fact that in both situations, the choice of the time,
the target and the location for the engagement for NATO use of nuclear weapons
is entirely in the hands of the enemy. For example, in anti-submarine warfare
(ASW), it is not possible to foresee where and where a submarine will attack,
nor whether it will attack at all. The same remark is valid for air-defence
(AD). Furthermore, such an uncertainty would impact negatively the decision that
NATO may wish to take on whether and when to send to the enemy a warning

Remarks on Follow-on Use

9. Initial use, while crossing the nuclear threshold, has the political purpose
of warning the enemy that he miscalculated NATO's reaction and willingness to
defend itself by even escalating to the use of nuclear weapons, and consequently
force him to cease the attack and to withdraw(1). Follow-on use, which is the
consequence of the failure of initial use, must clearly carry the greater
warning that NATO is prepared to escalate further. It is recognised(2) that
follow-on use would very likely be more intense and/or more extensive than
initial use and that it should achieve effective military results. If the
difference of political purposes between initial and follow-on use is
recognised, it would be desirable that these forms of use be separated by time
sufficient for the enemy to make a decision and show clearly his further
intentions. It follows that a pause between initial and follow-on use would be
very useful for political purposes. However, the length of such a pause would
depend upon political and military factors that can be evaluated only in an
actual situation.

10. The foregoing remarks and experience gained from WINTEX 75 support the view

accuracy to permit sound consultation on follow-on use to take place. If, for
any reason, the execution time of previously approved initial use were

of the previous political decision on initial use, with a consequent extension
and increased complexity of the consultation process. While we recognise that
current MNCs' release procedures provide some means for holding the nuclear
release authority already granted by the Nuclear Power (for example, BLUE DOT
NINE message), we express serious concern with regard to the delays that the
implementation of such a procedure might possibly impose on subsequent
consultation.
