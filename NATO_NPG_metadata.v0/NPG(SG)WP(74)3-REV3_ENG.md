> # Military Committee Function in the Nuclear Consultation Process
> 
> This working paper, a third revision of a draft report by the Permanent Representatives, examines the function of the Military Committee in the process of Alliance consultation concerning the possible use of nuclear weapons during periods of tension or hostilities. The document outlines that the nuclear consultation process is designed to permit the rapid expression of views from member governments to be communicated to the nuclear power concerned before a release decision is made. It emphasizes that the ultimate decision on the use of nuclear weapons remains with the nuclear power, but stresses the importance of Atlantic consultation compatible with the time available. The report highlights the need for clearly identifiable government views to be transmitted without distortion to ensure the nuclear power can assess them individually. It further discusses the existing guidance for the Military Committee, noting that its role is to advise the North Atlantic Council and the Defence Planning Committee on the use of military power, including nuclear weapons, without interposing itself between Major NATO Commanders and political authorities. The document references the General Guidelines for Consultation Procedure on the Use of Nuclear Weapons adopted in 1969 and notes that the process is intended to be intensified during times of war or tension.

I. PURPOSE
This report reviews the function of the Military Committee in the various
aspects of the process of Alliance consultation in times of tension or
hostilities on the possible use of nuclear weapons.

II. BACKGROUND
2. At their meeting in Ankara in May, 1973, during the discussion of a report(1)
by the Permanent Representatives containing some preliminary observations on the
nuclear consultation aspects of WINTEX 73, the Ministers noted that there were
uncertainties concerning the ways in which the Military Committee in Permanent
Session would be involved in the process, particularly with respect to dealing
with specific requests or proposals for use of nuclear weapons. Consequently,
they invited(2) the Permanent Representatives to review the function of the
Military Committee in the nuclear consultation process.

III. DISCUSSION
Characteristics of the Nuclear Consultation Process
4. The basic guidance governing the procedures to be used within the Alliance in
consulting in time of tension or hostilities on questions of nuclear weapon use
is contained in the General Guidelines for Consultation Procedure on the Use of
Nuclear Weapons(2). These Consultation Guidelines were adopted by the Defence
Planning Committee in 1969 on the recommendation of the NPG and the Nuclear
Defence Affairs Committee in the light of intensive consideration of the subject
in the NPG during the first three years of its existence(3). They also reflect a
significant body of opinion and experience developed within the Alliance over a
period of years prior to the creation of the NPG. The Consultation Guidelines,
although not explicit with regard to the rôles played by the various
participants, including the Military Committee, provide the benchmark against
which these rôles are to be assessed.

5. Nuclear consultation within the Alliance is carried out both through direct
channels between member governments and in the Council/DFC which has been
designated as the normal forum for country representatives to express national
positions(1). The Consultation Guidelines also state that nuclear consultation
would fit into the framework of a procedure of general consultation between
member countries that is already operating(2). General consultation, which
functions on a continuing basis in peacetime, is designed to be intensified in
time of tension or war, and might reach a point at which the possible use of
nuclear weapons becomes an issue. Depending on the circumstances, this
possibility might be raised in general terms in assessments of the evolving
situation prepared by member governments, the Council/DFC, or the Major NATO
Commanders(3), or in early notification messages originated by the Major NATO
Commanders(4). For the purposes of this report, the nuclear consultation process
would begin at this point and would culminate, according to developments, in
action on a specific request or proposal for use originated by a member
government or a Major NATO Commander.

Consultation Guidelines point out(5), would be assuming those particular

7. On the question of country views, the Consultation Guidelines point out that,
although the ultimate decision on the use of a nuclear weapon will always lie
with the nuclear power, it is essential that there should be the greatest
possible degree of Atlantic consultation compatible with the time available and
the relevant circumstances, before any ultimate decision is taken(1).
Consequently, a general aim of the nuclear consultation process is "to permit
the rapid expression of views of member governments on any request or proposal
for the use of nuclear weapons"(2). These views will be communicated to the
nuclear power(s) concerned by the most expeditious means available(3) for its
consideration in reaching a decision whether or not to release the weapons for
the proposed use(4). In reaching its decision, the nuclear power would give
special weight to the views of the NATO countries most directly affected, i.e.,
the country on or from whose territory nuclear weapons would be employed, the
country or countries providing the nuclear warheads, and the country or
countries providing or manning the contemplated means of delivery(5). In this
overall process, each member government would wish to have as much information
as possible about the views of other members so

8. It is clear from the foregoing that the effective
operation of the nuclear consultation process depends critically on
the expression of clearly-identifiable government views on the
precise question of whether to support a specific request or
proposal for use of nuclear weapons that can be assessed
individually by other Alliance members and given the particular
weight foreseen by the Guidelines. Procedures within the
Council/DPC that prevent the transmission of individual government
views to the nuclear power, or that might distort them, would be
inconsistent with the Consultation Guidelines.
9. Closely linked with the need to preserve the clear

proposal for nuclear weapon use is the requirement to do everything
possible to ensure that these views reach the nuclear power(s)

decision is time-sensitive, since the political, psychological and
military impact of the proposed use can be critically dependent on
the timing of that use. Much of the considerable effort that the

Military Committee Role
10. The existing guidance for the role of the Military
Committee relating to consultation on the use of nuclear weapons is
brief and very general in nature. In connection with the
consultation activity in the Council/DPC, the Military Committee
role in wartime includes, besides other responsibilities, "advising
the North Atlantic Council/Defence Planning Committee on the use
of military power, including, if time permits, the use of nuclear
weapons by NATO forces or forces acting in support of the Alliance.
This is not to be construed as interposing the Military Committee
between the Major NATO Commanders and the appropriate political
authorities with respect to the authorisation for release of
nuclear weapons"(2). We believe that this guidance provides a
useful starting point, but requires further explanation to gain a
more useful understanding of the role of the Military Committee in
nuclear consultation(3).
11. The Consultation Guidelines do not address directly
the role of the Military Committee. The most important point we
would make is that the Military Committee role must be consistent

12. Within the framework of these principles and procedures, we believe the
Military Committee would have a valuable rôle in the nuclear consultation
process. The function of the Military Committee should be viewed within the
context of the provision of military advice to political authorities, the
principal sources of which in NATO are the Military Committee and the Major NATO
Commanders. The Military Committee is the primary source of military advice for
the Council/DPC within NATO Headquarters. Such military advice to the
Council/DPC could, in the early stages of the nuclear consultation process,
cover such matters as contributing to the assessment of the developing situation
through the evaluation of available intelligence, operational summaries and
other sources; placing the points of view of the Major NATO Commanders in a
NATO-wide perspective, and contributing to the military rationale relating to
developing situations which may call for the use of nuclear weapons by NATO. In
this connection, we would expect that the Military Committee might use, comment
on and add to the general assessments of the situation and other information
developed by the Major NATO Commanders.

13. The primary responsibility for the provision of military advice in
connection with consultation specifically relating to possible use of nuclear
weapons lies with the Major NATO Commanders. This is inherent in approved
nuclear release request procedures by which the Major NATO Commanders' early
warning messages and nuclear release requests are transmitted by Major NATO
Commanders directly to the Council/DPC and capitals for appropriate action.
These procedures are necessary because of the time-urgency that would normally
be associated with situations in which the use of nuclear weapons might be
required and because the Major NATO Commanders are closest to the operational
situation.

14. In connection with consultation relating specifically to possible uses of
nuclear weapons, the rôle of the Military Committee must be governed by the
overriding emphasis in the Consultation Guidelines on rapid transmission of
clearly identified government views. What is most relevant to the ultimate
decision on the possible use of nuclear weapons is the government view,
mentioned in the Consultation Guidelines, which forms the national input to the
nuclear consultation process. The specific content of the government view, which
we would assume would reflect political and military elements, and the way in
which it is developed within a country is a national responsibility. It would,
of course, be important in terms of the Alliance-wide nuclear consultation
process to avoid presenting conflicting

versions of an individual country's view on the precise question of whether to
support a specific request or proposal for use of nuclear weapons. This is
particularly true at NATO Headquarters where the presentation of conflicting
views in the Military Committee and the Council/DPC would carry serious risks of
frustrating the basic objectives of the process. Therefore, a Permanent Military
Representative, if presenting views in the Military Committee, necessarily must
not differ from the government view passed directly between member governments
and presented in the Council/DPC by his country's Permanent Representative.

15. In light of the emphasis on government views, it follows that the Military
Committee, in contributing military advice in the nuclear consultation process,
should not have as an objective the development of a collective view by the
Military Committee on specific requests or proposals for the use of nuclear
weapons. We recognise that, in general consultation, the Military Committee
might render military advice as a collective body. However, in relation to
consultation on specific requests or proposals to use nuclear weapons, we would
expect that the emphasis in the participation of the Military Committee in this
process would focus on contributions by Permanent Military Representatives in
their national capacity. Accordingly, we would generally expect their
contributions to be based on instructions from capitals reflecting the same
government views that are presented

in the Council/DPC. We recognise, however, that, in the likely fast-moving

inconsistent with presentation of government views in the Council/DPC and

16. We have noted the emphasis on the rapid expression of views in the nuclear
consultation process since timing can be critical in a nuclear release decision.
The Military Committee should carry out its rôle in a way that would advance the
timely fulfillment of the nuclear consultation process. This should relate not
only to communications of the Military Committee outside of NATO Headquarters,
for example, with Major NATO Commanders, but also in connection with internal
communications within Headquarters, such as with the Council/DPC.

17. Related to the critical factor of time is the emphasis in the nuclear
consultation process on procedures that are as simple and flexible as possible,
and that are capable of modification at any time in light of prevailing
circumstances. The Military Committee should be guided by this objective in
carrying out
