> # Study on Communicating NATO's Intention to Use Nuclear Weapons
> 
> This study, prepared by the NPG Staff Group, examines the implications of communicating NATO's intention to use nuclear weapons to an enemy, other countries, and the public. It identifies the principal factors that political and military authorities within NATO would have to consider when deciding to communicate such intentions to prevent uncontrolled escalation and to induce the enemy to cease their attack. The document outlines a policy framework based on the NATO strategy of flexible response, emphasizing the need for controlled escalation and the 'negotiation while fighting' theme. It discusses the current policy on communicating NATO's intention (CNI), and the requirements for ensuring the enemy is convinced of NATO's readiness to proceed with nuclear escalation while minimizing the risks of misinterpretation. The study also covers the analysis of substantive implications, implementing a CNI action, and procedural matters including the selection of channels for communication and the selection of a communicating agent. The document references several previous reports and guidelines, including the Provisional Political Guidelines for the Initial Defensive Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons by NATO.

I. PURPOSE AND SCOPE
This Study explores the implications of communicating NATO's intentions to an
enemy, to other countries and to the public. It identifies and examines the
principal factors that political and military authorities in NATO would have to
take into account in dealing with the question of communicating its intention to
employ nuclear weapons in its defence. The Study also discusses procedural
aspects of handling the question in NATO. The Study focuses on the communication
that NATO might choose to make in support of a decision to use nuclear weapons
selectively.

II. BACKGROUND
2. Portions of two reports prepared by the NPG Permanent Representatives for
consideration by the NPG Ministers at their meeting in Ankara in May, 1973
commented on the question of communicating NATO's intentions. These were a
report(1) containing some preliminary observations on the nuclear aspects of
WINTEX 75, and a report(2) commenting on an illustrative study on demonstrative
use(3) prepared by SHAPE. In the light of their consideration of these reports,
the NPG Ministers invited(4) the Permanent Representatives to initiate a study
on the implications of communicating NATO's intentions to an enemy, to other
countries and to the public, and on related procedural matters.

III. POLICY FRAMEWORK
5. Strategic Concept. The fundamental objective of the tactical use of nuclear
weapons and the threat of further escalation is, according to the Provisional
Political Guidelines, "to induce the enemy to make the political decision to
cease his attack and withdraw"(3). The agreed NATO strategy of flexible response
set out in MC 14/3, paragraph 17, seeks to deter aggression by convincing a
potential attacker that the risks involved are out of all proportion to the
objective sought, and that NATO, in defending itself if aggression does occur,
is prepared to escalate the conflict, if necessary, to a point that would
jeopardise even-the-national existence of the aggressor. In the event it is
attacked, NATO would defend itself through determined but controlled use of
whatever mix of conventional tactical nuclear or strategic nuclear forces is
needed to bring about the desired-change of mind on the part of the 'aggressor.

38. In order to achieve the objective of the initial use of nuclear weapons set

requirements must be met:

(a) The enemy must be convinced of NATO's readiness to proceed with nuclear
escalation.

(b) The risks of the enemy misinterpreting the deliberately limited nature of

39. In order that NATO's intention in initiating tactical use of nuclear weapons
can be made clear to the enemy, it will generally be necessary to convey some
message of explanation and warning to him, additional to the message that would
be implicit in the use itself. The form, content and timing of the message would
have to be decided in the light of circumstances. It should reach the enemy in
sufficient time before the initial use of nuclear weapons for him to appreciate
NATO's purpose, and should threaten further escalation if he fails to comply
with the warning. NATO must, however, be aware of the loss of surprise which
would result, and of the danger that the enemy might exploit the explanation and
warning received by reacting in a manner jeopardising NATO's defence posture. If
such an event should occur, NATO should be prepared to escalate further.

40. All political and diplomatic channels available to the NATO governments
should be exploited to communicate the statement to the enemy and to explain
NATO's actions to the rest of the world, and to keep in contact with the enemy
governments in order to learn their reaction without delay and to influence that
reaction to the utmost. In this connection, particular importance must be
attached to the establishment of contacts between the nuclear powers, and the
existing "hot lines" between the capitals are obviously relevant. It must,
however, be kept in mind that, under certain circumstances, public communication
of NATO actions or intentions could have an adverse impact upon the effect of
the warning.

41. All estimates of the situation must take into account the psychological
effects of NATO's use of nuclear weapons and the reactions of the aggressor, and
the consequent need for suitable measures addressed to the general public and to
the forces.

11. CNI Decision to be Taken. There are two general kinds of CNI questions that
might confront NATO if, in time of hostilities, a decision is reached to resort
to the selective tactical use of nuclear weapons. First, NATO would have to
consider whether any steps should be taken to explain its intention with respect
to the specific use. If the answer to this query is yes, NATO would then have to
examine a complex of substantive and procedural questions dealing with the
details of carrying out the CNI action.

12. Relationship between CNI Decisions and the Use Decision. As noted earlier,
current policy guidance does not obligate NATO to take a CNI action on every
occasion of selective tactical use of nuclear weapons. Therefore, a decision to
employ nuclear weapons is not in itself a decision to take a CNI action. On the
other hand, a decision to employ nuclear weapons is a pre-requisite for reaching
a decision to take a CNI action;

IV. ANALYSIS OF SUBSTANTIVE IMPLICATIONS OF CURRENT POLICY

Introduction
13. This part of the Study identifies and discusses the principal substantive
factors that political and military authorities in NATO would have to take into
account in dealing with the two general kinds of CNI questions, namely, whether
to take a CNI action and, if so, how to carry it out. Although these factors are
all interrelated and inseparable in reality, for analytical purposes it is
necessary to treat them separately. CNI actions directed to the enemy are
considered in the first section; those directed to other audiences, insofar as
they are related to CNI actions toward the enemy are treated in the second. An
artificial distinction is drawn between the discussion of these substantive
factors and the examination of some procedural aspects which are at Annex to
this Study.

Deciding Whether to Take a CNI Action Towards the Enemy
14. Impact of the War Situation. The way in which a possible conflict evolves
might affect CNI decisions, particularly if NATO is forced to resort to first
use of nuclear weapons. NATO could be placed in the position of having to employ
nuclear weapons first, an act that the Provisional Political Guidelines have
described as a basic qualitative change in warfare and a distinct and highly
significant step in escalation(1). If this should occur, it would be
particularly important to assess the possible contribution that CNI actions
could make toward explaining both the nature of, and reasons for, NATO's initial
use. Similarly, CNI actions could play an important part in

15. If NATO is forced to resort to the selective use of nuclear weapons a
decision will have to be made whether a parallel CNI action should also be
taken. The Provisional Political Guidelines state that it will generally be
necessary to convey some message of explanation and warning to the enemy in
addition to the message which would be implicit in the use itself(1). However,
it is recognised that a CNI action has potential risks and disadvantages, both
political and military. These are discussed below.

16. Political Considerations. NATO's resolve to resist aggression, and its
policy of flexibility in response are continually made evident to the other
side. Policy statements, the structure of forces and the pattern of deployments,
and exercise behaviour, all signal NATO's intentions to the Warsaw Pact. In time
of tension these "signals" become even more important, and a CNI message would
rest in part whether it was consistent with past "signals" and whether the
Warsaw Pact had in the past been able to interpret correctly NATO's actions and
statements.

17. The Provisional Political Guidelines mention two requirements essential to
the achievement of the objective of initial use (and, by implication, of follow-
on tactical use as well) that have great political significance. These
requirements are that the enemy must be convinced of NATO's readiness to proceed
with nuclear escalation, and that the risks of the enemy misinter-[unreadable]

18. With respect to NATO's readiness to proceed with nuclear escalation, a CNI

NATO was willing to take in its own defence. This explanation could reinforce
the evidence of NATO's resolve to defend itself that is inherent in the use per

intra-war deterrence by continuing to confront the enemy with a credible but

regard, it should be recognised that the "negotiation while fighting" approach
embodied in current NATO strategy raises the possibility that the enemy reaction
to a CNI message could, in effect, represent a significant move in the direction
of making the political decisions that NATO seeks. In such a case,

22. Military Considerations. The Provisional Political Guidelines warn that a
CNI action could result in loss of surprise and could raise the danger that the
enemy might exploit the explanation and warning received by reacting in a manner
jeopardising NATO's defence posture(1). The extent to which the loss of surprise
might be militarily significant would seem to depend mainly on the amount of
time between the enemy's receipt of the CNI message and the detonation of the
weapon. Obviously, if no CNI action is taken or if it is not taken until the use
occurs, there can be no loss of surprise attributable to a CNI action per se. On
the other hand, if no CNI action is taken or if it is delayed until after the
detonation, its value in minimising the risks of the enemy misinterpreting the
deliberately limited nature of NATO's use would seem to be seriously degraded.
As noted earlier, one possible enemy misinterpretation could be the mistaken
view that NATO's limited use was merely a step in an uncontrolled and
uncontrollable escalation process. If this led to a wide-scale nuclear response
by the enemy, the consequences from a NATO military point of view could be much
more severe than the military consequences of loss of surprise.

23. A similar situation may apply with respect to the danger that the enemy
might exploit the explanation and warning received by reacting in a manner
jeopardising NATO's defence posture. While

intention to use nuclear weapons in its defence, if required. As mentioned
earlier, it can be expected that NATO would take steps to confirm and reinforce
this basic message if tension builds up and aggression occurs. In time of

reasonably accurate assessments of NATO's military situation in the light of the
evolution of the conflict, including estimates of the pressures that might be
pushing NATO toward nuclear weapon use. Finally, the possibility that specific
aspects of NATO's military operations may provide some indications to the enemy

CNI Actions to Own Public and Other Countries

25. As part of the process of reaching a decision whether to take a CNI action,
NATO political authorities would wish to consider the possible need to
communicate NATO's intention to other countries and to the public, particularly
the populations of NATO countries(1). For political reasons, it might be quite
important for other countries to receive specific advice from NATO concerning
the nature of, and reasons for, its selective tactical use, both in terms of
minimising the possibility that these countries would otherwise have an
exaggerated and distorted impression of the risk the NATO action posed to them,
and in terms of the rôle these countries might play in influencing the enemy to
make the political decisions NATO seeks. This would seem to be particularly true
in the case of countries who do not have their own means for learning the
deliberately limited nature of the NATO use. Similar considerations might apply
with regard to the public. With regard to the populations of NATO countries in
particular, a CNI action might contribute to domestic cohesion and public morale

V. IMPLEMENTING A CNI ACTION
General

27. It is evident from the foregoing discussion that a decision on whether or
not to implement a CNI action in connection with a particular selective tactical
use of nuclear weapons would be based on the overall assessment of a mix of
political and military considerations that are likely to be conflicting and
difficult to weigh. Although the Provisional Political Guidelines clearly
indicate that a CNI action is likely to be to NATO's advantage, the actual
decisions can only be reached in the context of the situation that exists at the
time that a use decision is being made.

importance to the process of "negotiating while fighting" and to communicating
NATO's intentions. NATO should therefore continue to ensure reliable channels of
communication with the Warsaw Pact and seek to improve them where possible.

becomes obvious to him. A demonstration that the risk levels taken by the
Alliance are carefully calculated and that the enemy's options are intelligently
perceived by NATO is likewise advisable. Furthermore, the analyses of former

32. General Features of a CNI Message to Other Countries and to the Public.

speaking, a CNI message to other countries and to the public should emphasise
NATO's resolve as well as its overall objectives, motivations and commitment to
controlled use of force in its own defence. The message should also reflect the
enemy responsibility in creating the conflict situation that led to the NATO

countries and the particular assurance to its own people that all measures are
being taken to avoid unwanted collateral damage would be indispensable
ingredients of this kind of message.

VI. SUMMARY
37. Since the adoption of the 'strategy of flexibility in response in 1967, NATO
has repeatedly stated in public and in official forums that, if attacked, it
would defend itself at whatever scale might be required, including the use of
nuclear weapons if necessary. NATO has also widely publicised the objective of
its defensive effort, namely, to induce an aggressor to make the political
decision to cease his aggression and withdraw by convincing him through NATO's
determined but deliberately controlled use of force that the risks of continued
aggression far outweigh any gains that might have been foreseen. The mix of
conventional, tactical nuclear and strategic forces maintained in peacetime by
NATO gives clear

sustain a readiness to use nuclear weapons in its defence. Therefore, it is
clear that NATO has already given strong indications to a potential aggressor of
its intention to use nuclear weapons if the need should arise.

38. If aggression occurs and NATO concludes at some point that it is necessary
to resort to the use of nuclear weapons, NATO would also have to decide whether
to communicate to the enemy, to other countries and to the public its intention

so in order to reinforce the evidence of NATO's resolve and at the same time to
reduce the risk of enemy over-reaction as a result of failure to appreciate the
controlled, limited character of NATO's nuclear effort. On the other hand, there

Procedural Aspects of Implementing a CNI Action
7. The aspects of particular interest in this regard concern the channels to be
used for communicating, the agents who would do the communicating, and the
coordination of multiple communications. It can be readily seen that these
aspects are inter-related and that choices with respect to any one are affected
in one way or another by the others. For example, selection of a particular
channel for communicating could, in effect, decide the choice of agent as well.
It should also be apparent that there is probably a great number of possible
combinations that could work effectively under varying circumstances. Since it
is not possible to forecast in any detail the circumstances that will exist when
occasions for communicating arise, the comments that follow should be seen as
points to be considered when choices have to be made, rather than as a
prescription of the procedures that must be used.

8. Selection of Channels for Communicating with the Enemy. The Provisional
Political Guidelines state that all political and diplomatic channels available
to the NATO governments should be exploited to communicate the statement of
NATO's intention to the enemy and to explain NATO's actions to the rest of the
world(1). This is, first of all, an implicit recognition of the fact that NATO,
as an institution, has no direct links with the Warsaw Pact countries
individually or collectively and must therefore rely on the facilities available
to individual members. Moreover, the statement in the Provisional Political
Guidelines reflects the need to be ready to use any of the channels in view of
the probability that a number of them would be used in the CNI process as well
as the likelihood that some of the normal channels may be disrupted. However,
the statement should not be construed to mean that all channels would
necessarily have to be used in every case. In addition, it should not be
interpreted to exclude the use of channels that have not been mentioned, such as
the news media.

10. Selection of Channels to Own Public and Third Countries.
A large number and variety of channels are also available to NATO for
communicating to other countries. Since diplomatic relations usually continue to
be maintained with other countries in time of hostilities, the normal bilateral
