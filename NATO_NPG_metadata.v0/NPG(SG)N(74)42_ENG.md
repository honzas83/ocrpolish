> # Future Work Programme
> 
> This document, a note by the Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Staff Group, outlines a proposed future work programme to be presented to the Secretary General for circulation prior to the June 1974 meeting in Bergen. The programme includes a detailed summary of possible future work, such as the Follow-on Use Work Programme and a study on the political and military implications of technological developments concerning the tactical use of nuclear weapons. It also addresses consultation procedures and facilities, including ATOMAL classification, WINTEX 73, and the compilation of nuclear consultation procedures. Additionally, the document discusses illustrative studies on air defence and the implications of communicating NATO's intentions to enemies, other countries, and the public. Other topics include the implementation of Special Atomic Demolition Munitions (ADM) guidelines and the review of the Military Committee's function in the nuclear consultation process. Finally, it mentions a study on Warsaw Pact Politico-Military Strategy and Military Doctrine led by the Netherlands. The Chairman emphasizes the lead time required for subjects to reach Ministerial consideration and the importance of initiating work well in advance.

FUTURE WORK PROGRAMME
Note by the Chairman
Continuing the procedure adopted for the last NPG Ministerial meeting, the
Secretary General intends to circulate prior the meeting in Bergen in June, 1974
a PO dealing with possible future activities of the NPG. To facilitate the
discussion of the subject by the Staff Group and to assist me in the preparation
of the PO, I have attached a list of topics that are related to work currently
underway, that might flow from Ministerial actions at their next meeting, or
that could be derived from the general remit to propose additional work in
various fields that has been given the Permanent Representatives. by the
Ministers.

2. In reviewing the future work programme, it is important to keep in mind that
a lead time of a year or more is usually needed before a subject has reached the
stage where it is ready for Ministerial consideration. It follows from this that
work has to be initiated well in advance of the time that it is expected to be
on a Ministerial agenda. Furthermore, it is difficult in many cases to predict
precisely the particular meeting for which a report or study will be available.

(Signed) H.3. SEIM

Summary of Possible Future NPG Work
1. Follow-on Use Work Programme. In its status report(1) circulated before the
last NPG Ministerial meeting, the Phase II Study Team stated that it appeared
possible that a preliminary draft of its report could be distributed to other
NPG nations some time in 1974. This still seems to be a reasonable prospect,
according to the informal report given to the Staff Group by the US member
following the most recent meeting of the Phase II Team in Bonn in early April.
Although it is too soon to make any firm predictions, a strong possibility
exists that Study Team Representatives and subsequent preparation of the final
Study Team report can be completed in time to permit it to be placed on a
Ministerial agenda in 1975. It is expected that the Permanent Representatives
will wish to submit an accompanying report which, among other things, might make
recommendations for initiating Phase III of the Work Programme (formulation, as
appropriate, of proposed political guidelines for follow-on tactical use of
nuclear weapons for NDAC endorsement and DPC approval). Concerning the general
responsibility vested by the Ministers in the Permanent Representatives to
propose further work, as appropriate, within the context of the Programme, there
would appear to be no specific suggestions to be made for Ministerial
consideration at the next meeting. There would also appear to be no need for a
separate Permanent Representatives status report for the next Ministerial
meeting, since this can be covered in the Secretary General's PO on future NPG
work, to which a Phase II Team status report can be attached if desired.

2. Implications of Technological Developments. At the last NPG meeting in the
Hague, the Ministers invited the Permanent Representatives to arrange for the
preparation of a study of the political and military implications of
technological developments concerning the tactical use of nuclear weapons(2).
The Permanent Representatives have commissioned an ad hoc group, composed of the
twelve NPG countries under the leadership of the United States, to carry out the
study effort(3). Since the Technology Study Group is just beginning to organize
itself and has not yet started any substantive work, no predictions can be made
as to when and in what form the results of its efforts would be available for
Ministerial consideration. As a related matter, it is recalled that the United
States Secretary of Defense will brief his colleagues at Bergen on US research
and development with respect to nuclear weapons for tactical use.

3. Consultation Procedures and Facilities. Three specific topics in this area
are subject to further investigation in the NPG. Each of these topics has been
raised at one or more Ministerial meetings in the past.

a. ATOMAL classification. The NPG has previously indicated a need to explore the
implications flowing from the requirement to use the ATOMAL classification for
certain nuclear consultation traffic(1), but has deferred further examination of
the matter until revised classification guidelines have been promulgated(2). The
process of developing revised guidelines is still in progress and no firm
forecasts can be made concerning when it may be completed.

b. WINTEX 73. After discussing a report by the Permanent Representatives
containing some preliminary observations on WINTEX 73, the Ministers at their
Ankara meeting last year asked(3) the NPG Permanent Representatives to review
the final WINTEX 73 reports and to comment, if appropriate, on nuclear
consultation aspects. Although these final reports are not yet available, it is
anticipated that the Permanent Representatives review can be completed later in
1974. The question of whether any aspects of the final reports should be brought
before the Ministers can be addressed at that time.

c. Compilation of Nuclear Consultation Procedures. At their last meeting at the
Hague, the Ministers invited the Permanent Representatives to develop a
compilation of nuclear consultation procedures(4). Work has already been started
by the International Staff to prepare a basis for Staff Group development of a
draft compilation for Permanent Representatives consideration. Completion of the
compilation might be foreseen for late 1974 or early 1975. In this connection,
it should be noted that the compilation would not necessarily be placed on a
Ministerial agenda.

5. Implications of Communicating NATO's Intentions. At their meeting in Ankara
in May, 1973, the NG Ministers invited the Permanent Representatives to initiate
a study on the implications of communicating NATO's intentions to an enemy, to
other countries and to the public, and on the related procedural matters(1). In
December, 1973, it was concluded that the work should be done directly under the
auspices of the Permanent Representatives. It is expected that the Staff Group
in the near future will begin developing a draft for the Permanent
Representatives consideration with a view toward having a report available for
the Ministerial meeting in the Autumn of 1974.

6. Implementation of Special ADM Guidelines. In fulfillment of their continuing
task(2) of monitoring the progress being made in implementing the Special
Political Guidelines for the Possible Use of Atomic Demolition
Munitions(ADMs)(3), the NPG Permanent Representatives prepared status reports in
1971, 1972 and 1973. The most recent of these reports indicated that further
progress was still required and the Ministers, at their meeting at the Hague,
urged countries concerned and the NATO Military Authorities to take that action
necessary for further implementation of the Special ADM Guidelines(4). It is
therefore suggested that it would be appropriate for the Permanent
Representatives to review the status of implementation some time in 1975.

7. Function of the Military Committee in the Nuclear Consultation Process. At
the Ankara meeting in May, 1973, the Ministers invited the Permanent
Representatives to review the function of the Military Committees in the nuclear
consultation process(5). The current draft of the NPG Permanent Representatives'
response recommends that the NPC Ministers send a report on this subject to the
DPC via the NDAC, but makes no proposals for further NPG work. Unless the DPC,
NDAC or Military Committee raise questions concerning the report, or exercise
experience so indicates, it is not expected that there will be a need for
additional NPG consideration of this subject.

8. Warsaw Pact Politico-Military Strategy and Military Doctrine. At the request
of the Ministers at Ankara(1), arrangements were made for the preparation of a
study on this subject by an ad hoc group under the leadership of the
Netherlands. The Study(2) with an accompanying Permanent Representatives report
will be considered by the Ministers at their next meeting. The current draft of
the Permanent Representatives report recommends that the NPG Ministers forward
the Study, with some related comments, to the DPC via the NDAC with the
suggestion that the DPC invite national authorities and appropriate NATO
activities to contribute on continuing basis toward improving NATO understanding
of the Warsaw Pact politico-military strategy and military doctrine for the
tactical use of nuclear weapons. While no specific NPG effort in the context is
anticipated, the NPG would wish to be alert to the continuing requirement and be
ready to make appropriate contributions if the opportunity arises.

9. General Political Aspects of Early Combined Use. When the Ministers discussed
at the Hague a study on Early Combined Use, they agreed that a number of general
political aspects of early combined use should be considered in advance of the
outbreak of hostilities and invited the Permanent Representatives to consider
whether a study on this subject should be initiated(3). It is suggested that the
Permanent Representatives, after having examined this question, may wish to
conclude that, because of the difficulty of forecasting the conditions that
would exist in a given future situation, a study of the general political
aspects at this time is not likely to be useful. However, NATO should begin
assessing the general political aspects of possible early combined use in a
developing crisis, well in advance of the outbreak of hostilities.
