> [!abstract] 
> # Consultation Procedures and Facilities
> 
> This document, a note by the Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group), outlines suggested revisions to a draft report on consultation procedures and facilities. The primary focus is to ensure that nuclear consultation traffic is clearly identified to avoid delays and misrouting within capitals and headquarters. It proposes that messages related to nuclear consultation, which do not already have distinctive labels like BLUE DOT, should include the words 'Nuclear Consultation Message' at the beginning of the text. The document also discusses the precedence system, confirming that the FLASH precedence is sufficient for the urgency of nuclear consultation messages without the need for a new, higher precedence. It addresses the special operating signal used by the Supreme Allied Commander Europe (SACEUR) to prioritize traffic within Allied Command Europe (ACE) networks, suggesting that expanding this signal's use to all NATO networks and a wider list of originators would be more effective. Additionally, it describes the use of Address Indicating Groups (AIGs) 6006 and 6007 to facilitate the delivery of messages to Ministries of Foreign Affairs and Ministries of Defence, recommending that originators include both AIGs to prevent routing delays.

SUGGESTED CHANGE TO NPG(Staff Group)WP(73)7(3rd Revision)

Pages 5-18, delete paragraphs 6-17 and substitute the following:

Clear Identification of Nuclear Consultation Traffic

6. In order to avoid inadvertent delays and misrouting of nuclear consultation
traffic within capitals and headquarters after messages have moved out of the
communications channels themselves, it is important that their content clearly
show their relevance to the nuclear consultation process. In the report
containing preliminary observations on WINTEX 73, it was noted(1) that certain
messages pertaining to the release of necler weapons for selective employment
for tactical purposes originated by the Major NATO Commanders, such as WHISKEY,
SIERRA and ROMEO messages, contain within the message texts themselves a
distinctive label (WHITE DOT for exercises; BLUE DOT for actual crises) that
clearly identifies them as nuclear release messages. It was pointed out,
however, that other messages also essential to the nuclear consultation process,
such as reports of Council/DFC consultation, pertinent communications from
member governments and certain other traffic from the Major NATO Commanders
(e.g., Early Notification messages), do not carry within their texts any easily
recognisable distinguishing mark that would clearly show their relevance to the
nuclear consultation process. Since clear identification of all traffic directly
related to consultation among political authorities in time of war on questions
of nuclear weapon release would be helpful, possible origins of such traffic to
be transmitted on NATO circuits should be advised to insert the words "Nuclear
Consultation Message" at the beginning of the text of each message. This would
not apply to selective release request messages that already carry a distinctive
label, such as BLUE DOT.

Handling of Nuclear Consultation Messages

7. In one sense the foregoing measure to identify the substance of nuclear
consultation messages within their text also contributes to their rapid handling
on the part of responsible officials and their advisers. There are a number of
equally important steps that could facilitate the handling of nuclear
consultation messages within the communications channels themselves, such as
having and using an effective precedence system, introducing special operating
signals to ensure rapid handling of selected traffic, making effective use of
address indicating groups, eliminating or reducing delays associated with
classification requirements, and transmitting the traffic by the fastest
available means.

8. Precedence. In view of the concern that has from time to time been expressed
that the precedence assigned to nuclear consultation messages may be
insufficient to ensure timely delivery to political authorities, the question of
whether a special precedence for such traffic might be warranted was
investigated in an earlier report(1). It was concluded that the present
arrangements regarding precedence are workable, and that they should function
effectively in time of war in support of the political consultation process. In
this connection, it is worth emphasising that the precedence assigned to a
message by the originator not only gives instructions to communications
personnel with regard to the relative priority to be accorded to the message in
handling it in communications channels but also provides guidance to the
officials responsible for taking action on its contents with regard to the
originator's assessment of the urgency of the matter. Thus, if an originator
assigns the highest precedence, FLASH, to an outgoing message, he is directing
communications personnel to process the message through communications channels
before any other messages of lower precedence,

9. There appears to be general acceptance within NATO of a view that in time of
war messages related to the nuclear consultation process would usually be of
such urgency and importance as to justify the assignment of the highest
precedence, FLASH(1). In view of this and in the light of the strong and
continuing efforts that are made in NATO to indoctrinate all message originators
to use the FLASH precedence very sparingly, the validity of the earlier
conclusion can be confirmed, namely, that the precedence system now in effect is
workable and should, in conjunction with other measures, ensure that critical
traffic would be handled rapidly and reliably in time or war. Accordingly, there
is no requirement at the present time for a new precedence, higher than FLASH,
to be reserved only for nuclear consultation messages.

10. Special Operating Signal. During Ministerial discussions of the need to
expedite the handling of nuclear consultation traffic, the Supreme Allied
Commander (SACEUR) informed the Ministers that he had promulgated instructions
to identify such traffic by a special operating signal to ensure rapid handling
by communications personnel. This distinguishing signal is to be placed in the
heading of certain high-priority traffic, such as alert messages and nuclear
release messages, originated by SACEUR or by his Major Subordinate Commanders,
to indicate to communications personnel that such messages are to be handled
(transmitted, relayed, written up, delivered) before all other

Further Work
16. This report and earlier ones(1) have discussed several separate but related
aspects of the nuclear consultation process, all of which should now be
consolidated into a single, cohesive package that would contain information and
guidance on the details of the agreed procedures that would govern such
consultation. This consolidation effort would be consistent with, and similar
to, work now being carried out in NATO under the overall coordination of the
Council Operations and Exercise Committee in the field of NATO crisis management
procedures and arrangements(2). The compilation of nuclear consultation
procedures could, when completed, form a contribution to the crises management
work now in progress under the aegis of the Council Operations and Exercise
Committee. These detailed procedures would, of course, support, and be
compatible with, approved policy guidance on nuclear consultation procedures(3).

IV. RECOMMENDATIONS
17. It is recommended that the Permanent Representatives:
(a) approve this report;
(b) invite the Major NATO Commanders to use plain language wherever possible in
drafting selective release messages addressed to political authorities;

(c) agree that others who may originate nuclear consultation messages should use
plain language wherever possible;

(d) agree that the words "Nuclear Consultation Message" should be inserted at
the beginning of the text of each nuclear consultation message to be transmitted
on NATO circuits (except selective release messages that already carry a
distinctive label, such as BLUE DOT) and invite the appropriate NATO authorities
to advise possible originators to do so;
