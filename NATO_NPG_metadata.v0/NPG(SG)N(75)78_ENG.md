> # Nuclear Consultation Procedures Compilation
> 
> This document is a note from the acting Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Staff Group, dated 30 September 1975, regarding the Compilation of Nuclear Consultation Procedures. The acting Chairman requests that Staff Group members provide comments on suggested editorial changes and amendments to the draft document, specifically those forwarded by the Canadian Delegation and SHAPE, by 3 October 1975. If no comments are received, the changes will be accepted via the silence procedure and incorporated into the 'white' working paper for Permanent Representatives. The document includes Annex A, which details extensive linguistic and technical editorial changes suggested by Canada, and Annex B, which contains editorial changes suggested by S.T.I.E. Additionally, Annex C provides a SHAPE amendment to the format of warning (WHIS-Y) messages, which was set to come into force on 1 October 1975. The correspondence focuses on refining the technical language, verb tenses, and procedural clarity of the nuclear consultation process within NATO.
> 
> ## Categories/Topics
> 
> #Doctrine-and-Strategy/Extended-Deterrence-and-Tactical-Nuclear-Sharing — The document focuses on the 'Compilation of Nuclear Consultation Procedures', specifically discussing the mechanisms for consultation among Alliance members regarding the use of nuclear weapons, including references to 'selective and controlled tactical use' and 'decision by the nuclear power(s)'.
> #Internal-Alliance-Dynamics/Command-and-Control-Authority — The document details the revised format for warning (WHIS-Y) messages, 'Early Notification Messages', and 'ROMEO' messages, and the 'assessment' part of the message describing circumstances necessitating the warning and the 'type of weapons which might be requested', which directly relates to the release authority and the chain of command for nuclear weapons.

A.N.EX 1 to NPG(Staff Group)WP(75)7(Cov.3)
L.N.T.: Canadian Editorial Changes to NPG(Staff Group)WP(75)7(Cov.3)
1. As it stands, paragraph 2, sentence 3 on page 2, implies that the nuclear
consultation procedures "will be followed if time and circumstance permit". This
confusion can be avoided if the "and" following "C-II(72)46" is replaced by
"which". The syntax of paragraph 3, sentence 1, can be improved if "of" is
substituted for "in" before "NATO". Similar improvements can be obtained, in
paragraph 4, by moving "in 1962" from after "Athens guidelines" to after
"adoption" (sentence 1), by substituting "that permitted" for "to permit" and by
inserting "the" before "selective and controlled tactical use" in sentence 3.
Likewise, in sentence 4, "in 1966," might be moved to follow "Tension and
Crisis".

2. We find the revised version of paragraph 5, sentence 1, most welcome,
although we would recommend one small additional change, that of inserting "the"
before "views of Alliance members". It seems desirable to maintain as consistent
a verb tense throughout the paper as is possible. The conditional nature of the
guidelines makes the conditional tense the preferred choice, for which reason we
suggest changing "will" to "would" in both instances in sentence 2 of paragraph
5.

3. In paragraph 6, sentence 2, "could be modified" appears to us a simpler way
of saying: what is meant by "capable of modification".

There may be some confusion as a result of the placing of the phrase "among
member governments of the Alliance" in Part II, Chapter I, paragraph 1. We
suggest that the sentence may read better if the phrase follows "which" (not
"that") takes place continuously. Further clarification would result from
inserting "which" before "would increase" in the same sentence.

4. In paragraph 3, sentence 2, the verb "unfold" does not seem to us to describe
adequately the operation of the consultation process. We suggest "would be
conducted" or "would operate" as alternative wordings. In addition, in sentence
3, we think it might be worthwhile to replace "the" with "a" before "decision by
the nuclear power(s)". The use of "the" may imply in some contexts (or the
French version) that a specific decision is intended.

5. The phrase "ability of participants in that process", in sentence 2 of
paragraph 5, lacks precision and could be improved by inserting "this" before
participants, since only a limited number will be involved in the consultation
process. To maintain uniformity of tense, "takes place" in sentence 2 should
read "would take place". The latter part of this sentence may be unclear as to
whether it is the flow of information or the process of general consultation
that is expected to increase. This could be remedied by inserting "which is"
before both "envisioned in the process" and "expected to increase". Since, in
technical terms, a data base cannot be composed of itself plus something else,
and since intelligence presumably forms part of the data, we suggest the phrase
"data and intelligence" in sentence 4 be amended to "information and
intelligence". (This follows the wording in paragraph 27 of
DPC/ID(75)7(Revised).) In sentence 5, a comma might be inserted after "many
sources", and "which" inserted before "produces regular summaries", in order to
avoid any possible confusion.

6. Paragraph 6, sentence 2 seems to require commas after "conflict" and
"available". Paragraph 6 could begin with "In the" rather than "At", and "is"
should be substituted for "are" before "provided". In paragraph 9, insert "the"
before "handling of information".

7. Chapter III, paragraph 0, sentence 2 might benefit from replacing
"implementation of" with "implementing". Clarity in paragraph 1 may be improved
by beginning with "Early Notification Messages are part of...". A comma should
properly go after "SACUR's procedures". In paragraph 2, substitute "would" for
"will" in all three instances, and insert "the" before "delegations" in
paragraph 5. In paragraph 4, sentence 2, there should be commas after "nuclear
Policy Team" and "International Military Staff". Substitute "would" for the
first "will"; the second one may be deleted.

8. We wonder whether "an understanding" rather than "a picture" in paragraph 5,

"could" instead of "can" and "would" instead of "will", and a comma might be

"will".
