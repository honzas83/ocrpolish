> # Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) Notes of a Meeting Held on Friday, 4th October, 1968
> 
> The Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) held a meeting on October 4, 1968, to discuss several key agenda items. The group discussed the forum for future 'Consultation' and the possibility of holding an NPG Ambassadors Meeting to allow Canada and other members to express their views on US and UK papers. The meeting focused on the detailed examination of a US paper on the demonstrative use of nuclear weapons, debating whether such use could be interpreted as a sign of weakness and the necessity of linking demonstrations to real military targets. The UK representative presented a paper on guidelines for the initial use of tactical nuclear weapons at sea, which prompted discussions on the pre-delegation of authority and the identification of hostile vessels. The document also includes a first draft of an Agreed Minute from a Meeting of Ministers in Bonn on October 10-11, 1968, which reviews reports on tactical nuclear use in the Southern Region of ACE and invites Permanent Representatives to continue examining issues such as the Nike-Hercules system and the ADM time factors. The meeting's conclusions emphasize the need for further elaboration of tentative guidelines and a study by NATO Military Authorities on the capability to conduct high-confidence nuclear demonstrations.
> 
> ## Categories/Topics
> 
> #Doctrine-and-Strategy/Flexible-Response — The document discusses the 'crossing of the nuclear threshold' and the development of 'tentative guidelines' for the initial use of tactical nuclear weapons, including 'demonstrative use' and 'selective use against battlefield targets,' which are core components of a graded, escalating response strategy (Flexible Response).
> #Internal-Alliance-Dynamics/Command-and-Control-Authority — The text explicitly discusses the 'problem of pre-delegation' of tactical nuclear weapons, the 'problems of Command and Control' for Military Commanders, and the 'dual-key' nature of release authority implied by the debate over whether political guidelines should be converted into military plans.
> #Doctrine-and-Strategy/Nuclear-Strategy-and-Strike-Planning — The document contains detailed discussions on target selection (battlefield vs. non-military targets), the effect of 'under-see nuclear explosions,' the 'identification of a hostile vessel' for naval strikes, and the request for NATO Military Authorities to study the capability to conduct a 'high confidence nuclear demonstration'.

NUCLEAR PLANNING GROUP (STAFF GROUP)
Notes of a meeting held on Friday, 4th October, 1968

Summary of matters discussed
- Consultation
The practicability of holding an NPG Ambassadors Meeting to discuss the US and
UK papers

- Press Release
- US Paper (Demonstrative use of Nuclear Weapons)
- UK Paper (Use at Sea)

CONSULTATION
2. The Chairman said that in his oral status report to Bonn the Secretary
General would draw the Ministers' attention to the recent paper NPG/D(68)9 and
ask whether the Ministers were prepared to take a decision on the forum in which
'Consultation' should be dealt with in the future. The United Kingdom Minister
is likely to support an immediate decision in favor of a transfer to the NDAC.
Several representatives stated that their Ministers are unlikely to be prepared
to reply.

PRACTICABILITY OF HOLDING AN NPG AMBASSADORS MEETING TO DISCUSS US AND UK PAPERS
3. Canada strongly favoured a meeting because this was the only opportunity for
Canada to express its views. The meeting should be at ambassadorial level to
give the Canadian comments more official status. Belgium said that ex-members
should have such an opportunity to express their views, but saw considerable
practical difficulties in holding a meeting. Denmark agreed with Belgium's view.

4. After detailed discussion, and after seeking advice from Ambassadors, it was
agreed that if the Ambassadors could not meet it would be most valuable to
continue the Staff Group meeting in order to discuss the two papers and in
particular to obtain the views of Canada.

11. He wished to ask two questions :
(a) Should the "Relevant considerations" on page 17 of the US Paper be
considered as draft guidelines? What in fact is a guideline?

(b) After the meeting in Bonn will the US envisage NATO Military Authorities
being asked to carry out the plans set out on page 18 of the US Paper?

12. The US Representative considered that Section B (Plans) was closer to
guidelines than Section A (Relevant Considerations).

13. The UK Representative suggested that a political directive should be
prepared during the next few months, perhaps with a view to a report at the
Berlin Meeting. Among the observations he made on the US Paper were the
following points :

- perhaps there is no such thing as purely demonstrative use.
- the explosion must have somesort of effect. A purely demonstrative use might
be interpreted as a sign of weakness.

- the paper points the way to a number of diffi- culties without suggesting the
best form of use.

14. The US Representative agreed that they had come to much the same conclusion
that there should be a link with a real military target, and that the message
should be convincing. He questioned whether it was a good thing to make a
demonstration far from the battlefield, and wondered what interpretation the
enemy might make.

15. The UK Representative, referring to the Canadian proposal for a study of
Soviet reactions, said that this was very relevant.

16. The Netherlands Representative, referring to the Canadian Representative's
observation, said that he also was not clear as to the proper definition of
guidelines. Should they be called guidelines ?

17. He said that his Ambassador would have expressed his appreciation of the US
Paper and made the following points :

- there was too much emphasis on the difficulties.
- the most important single factor was the crossing of the nuclear threshold.
- the US concentrates too much on battlefield targets.
- a clearer distinction should be made between battlefield and demonstrative
use.

- use over friendly territory (example NIKE-HERCULES) would reduce risk of
escalation.

- the warning to the enemy should be clear.

18. The US Representative said that work on demonstrative use was far from
finished. He suggested that the attention of Ministers should be drawn to :

- Demonstrative use against military targets
- Demonstrative use against non-military targets

19. The Italian Representative said that the US Paper was most useful. He asked
what was the follow-up to demonstrative use. He said that NATO would do after
that. Regarding guidelines he considered that the US Paper had produced good
basic ideas which might lead finally to the political guidelines which were our
objective.

20. The Greek Representative said that the US Paper was a most useful basis for
guidelines. He noted the weaknesses of the Alliance mentioned in it, and
suggested that Military Commanders should examine the problems of Command and
Control.

21. The US Representative agreed that such problems must be examined, but
reminded the Group of the very high cost factors.

UK PAPER – GUIDELINES FOR THE INITIAL USE BY NATO OF TACTICAL NUCLEAR WEAPONS AT
SEA.

22. The UK Representative briefly reviewed the paper and said that the views of
SACIANT had been taken into account when writing it.

23. The SACIANT Representative said that he had received no comments as yet from
his Commander.

24. The Netherlands Representative said that his Naval Authorities were most
interested in this paper. He made the following points :

- with regard to paragraph 15, he was doubtful whether much warning time would
be received.

Shadowing units might attack without warning.
- although contingency planning at sea cannot be as detailed as on land, he felt
that at least some planning in detail should be done.

the early identification between Soviet missile and conventional submarines
seemed a difficult task, and he presumed that missile submarines could be used
in a conventional role.

he thought that it might be a long-time before the enemy realised that a missile
submarine was lost, because of wireless silence rules, and because a nuclear
under-see explosion was difficult to detect.

25. The UK Representative, replying, said that he thought that shadowing would
be intensified in time of tension. With regard to paragraph 21, this was a
verbatim report on SACIANT's views concerning the possible Soviet maritime
strategy. He did not think that naval targets could be pre-planned.

With regard to under-see nuclear explosions he believed that their detection was
possible.

26. The Canadian Representative said that he considered the UK Paper to be
excellent and made the following points :

- with regard to paragraph 44 (Tactical Guidelines) he emphasised the difficulty
at of identifying a hostile vessel, and indeed of knowing who had attacked whom.
These were many uncertainties.

- the question of pre-delegation, considered necessary in the view of SACIANT
(paragraph 36), is presumed to be politically unacceptable (in paragraph 38),
and then (in paragraph 39) is put forward again in a disguised form. He
personally felt that pre-delegation would not be given.

- the tactical guidelines in paragraph 44 were good.

27. The UK Representative, replying, said that the sporadic and fleeting nature
of naval warfare might make it necessary to consider pre-delegation of a small
number of tactical nuclear weapons in a specific area, for a specific reason.
There could be no pre-delegation in peacetime but later, Ministers having
studied the problems, the problem might be easier to solve.

28. The US Representative thought that the UK were making a distinction between
initial release and identification of targets, but under severe constraints such
as in a specific situation, concerning specific weapons, in a specific time
frame etc.

29. He considered the UK Paper to be very good, and made the following points :

- some of the considerations applied also to the land battle. The enemy response
will depend on the importance of the target attacked.

- the problem of the floating target also occurs on land.

- initial use by NATO at sea might not be in the interest of NATO.

- would paragraph 39 be acceptable ? (a form of pre-delegation).

- the criteria in paragraph 41(a) was a political assessment.

- had any war-gaming been done on this matter ?

(Signed) M.H.G. WILSON

NUCLEAR PLANNING GROUP
MEETING OF MINISTERS
BONN, 10TH AND 11TH OCTOBER 1968
AGREED MINUTE
I. TACTICAL USE OF NUCLEAR WEAPONS
Ministers:
1. reviewed a report by the Southern Region Working Group on the possible
tactical use of nuclear weapons in the Southern Region of ACE and agreed that it
was an important contribution to the development of guidelines for the NATO
Military Authoritices;

2. discussed working papers submitted by the United States, Italy, Germany, and
the United Kingdom respectively relative to tentative guidelines for the NATO
Military Authorities covering the possible initial use by NATO of tactical
nuclear weapons under the following circumstances :

(a) use for demonstrative purposes;
(b) use of defensive systems;
(c) selective use against battlefield targets in a limited conflict;
(d) use at sea.
3. invited the Permanent Representatives to continue their examination of the
issues relative to

(a) the ADM time Factors;
(b) the Nike-Hercules system;
(c) the Canadian fighter-bomber paper;
(d) PO/68/478;
with a view to helping the four discussion leaders on guidelines amplify their
papers.
