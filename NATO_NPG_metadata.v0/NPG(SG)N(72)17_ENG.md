> # The Political Implications of Strategic Parity
> 
> This document consists of a notice from the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) and an annex containing extracts from an Adelphi Paper titled 'The Political Implications of Strategic Parity' by Walter Slocombe. The notice, signed by H.D. Seim, serves to distribute these extracts to Staff Group members for their interest. The text explores the transition from a state of unequal assured-destruction forces to strategic parity, arguing that the technical capabilities of equal-sized forces do not differ significantly from unequal ones. It discusses how mutuality of assured destruction, rather than parity itself, eliminates the possibility of a decisive first strike. The author examines the various doctrinal approaches to nuclear conflict, including 'demonstrative' strikes and limited nuclear wars, and argues that the technical changes brought by parity have little impact on the utility of these options. Furthermore, the author analyzes the 'counter-force' strategy, noting that parity virtually eliminates the plausibility of a massive attack on Soviet strategic nuclear forces as a decisive instrument of policy. The document emphasizes that the outcome of a crisis would depend more on subjective factors such as risk-bearing ability and the value systems of the opponent, rather than strictly technical force sizes.

III. EFFECTS OF PARITY ON NUCLEAR OPTIONS
That even the building of a Soviet assured-destruction second-strike capability
and the concomitant end of American ability to strike first and reduce the
maximum Soviet retaliation to 'acceptable' proportions was a significant
political event is not beyond debate. Doubts about massive retaliation as an
all-purpose strategy for using military power to achieve political ends came
long before the Soviet Union had even a minimum second-strike force. However,
whatever may have been the effects of the end of the American nuclear monopoly,
and the later progressive disappearance of a true US first-strike capability, in
restricting the ability of the United States to exploit its nuclear power for
political purposes and in freeing the USSR from a significant constraint on its
international actions, what has happened in recent years – and what has been
labelled the emergence of 'parity!' – is not the creation by the Soviet Union of
an invulnerable assured-destruction capability. That came, whenever the precise
moment, long before the number of Soviet missiles got close to American levels.
The pattern of the last few years has been transition from a strategic balance
in which a Soviet assured-destruction force faced a much larger American force
to one in which the sizes of the two forces are roughly the same, and it is this
change and not the earlier one which is considered here.

The technical capabilities of equal-sized assured-destruction forces do not
differ greatly from those of unequal ones. Even if it is much smaller than the
force it faces, an assured-destruction force, by definition, denies the stronger
side the possibility of a true first strike, in which the initial blow destroys
so much of the enemy's force that retaliation is either physically impossible or
so limited as to be an acceptable price for victory. Thus it is mutuality of
assured destruction and not parity which eliminates the possibility of the most
clearly decisive kind of attack. At the other end of the spectrum of possible
nuclear conflicts, the ability of either side to make small attacks on the
other, to attack third countries(8) or conduct 'tactical' i.e. geographically
limited, nuclear wars is, as a technical matter, unaffected by the size of the
forces on the other side.

Parity and 'Bargaining' by Nuclear Attack
Most of the doctrines for the conduct of American-Soviet nuclear exchanges which
have been advanced by strategic analysts as more realistic than all-out 'first
strike followed by a maximum retaliation' exchange are little affected – even
within their own terms of argument – by the change to parity. Broadly speaking,
this is because they take as a promise that no objectively

decisive attack (or defence) is possible – a condition which prevails under
mutuality of assured destruction as well as under parity – and discuss how
nations might still seek to manipulate the resulting technically indeterminate
situation. It is sometimes argued for example that in a time of great crisis
where important interests, but not national survival, were at stake, very
limited 'demonstrative' nuclear strikes might be made to show resolve or warn of
willingness to risk possible deliberate or even uncontrolled escalation.

Proposals – which also have something of the character of predictions – have
also been advanced which imagine a nuclear war between the superpowers as
involving attacks over a period of time on a scale far beyond mere
'demonstrations' but still less than all-out, restricted by some combination of
limits on type of targets attacked, geographical extent, number and rate of
attacks, or type of weapons used(9).

In both cases, the nuclear attacks which would occur are viewed not as efforts
to produce a militarily decisive result directly by destroying the opponent's
physical ability to continue fighting, but as means of 'negotiating' with him.
The objective of the 'negotiation' could be either 'success' – by threats of
still greater future losses or by destroying his population's willingness to
continue the struggle – or limitation of 'unavoidable' hostilities – by
conveying limits to be followed if he will reciprocate.

The transition from unequal assured-destruction forces to parity has effectively
no impact on the utility or plausibility of these doctrinal approaches. Indeed,
as noted earlier, the fundamental premise underlying those strategic concepts is
that differences in weapons capability are not decisive, that a strictly
technical analysis is far too limited, and that the outcome of a crisis in which
nuclear weapons are threatened or used would depend on the interplay in the
particular context of much more subjective factors of threat, inducement,
willingness to take risks, and bargaining. The determinants of ability to bear
risk – and of how others will evaluate a nation's risk-bearing ability – will be
many, including a stake in the underlying conflict, willingness to sustain
immensely painful but not mortal losses, and maintenance of control over forces
and population.

The differentials between countries in terms of these factors will not
necessarily or even probably match force sizes. Indeed, a nation could use
strategic devices of risk-threatening even in the face of a military
disadvantage, saying in effect, 'I know that if it comes to really big attacks,
you will come out better than I, but you will still suffer greater losses than
success is worth to you. Moreover, I can better close my eyes to the fact that I
may not come out at all than you can ignore the fact that you will come out
grievously hurt and you must simply accommodate yourself to my blindness to
risks.'

One may certainly debate the validity of any or all such strategic doctrines,
particularly with respect to whether any nation would regard to risk reaction of
the other side as sufficiently predictable to go beyond threats actually to
initiate the use of nuclear weapons deliberately, 'Out, by their very premises,
the technical changes brought by parity affect them little.

Parity and 'Counter-force' Strategies

The only place where the technical changes in capabilities brought about by
parity require a fundamental re-examination of doctrine is in connection with
the virtual elimination of whatever plausibility there may have been to the idea
that even after the United States lost first-strike superiority, it retained in
the option of a massive attack on Soviet strategic nuclear forces a potentially
decisive ultimate instrument of policy. Elimination of this once theoretically
possible nuclear option will be politically and strategically significant,
however, only if the possibility of such massive US counter-force attacks was in
practice a serious factor in American-Soviet relations in the period of unequal
assured-destruction forces.

The basic premise of the 'counter-force' concept is that merely physical
capability to retaliate by indiscriminate, massive attacks on cities may deter
only similarly massive first strikes which destroy cities and kill millions of
civilians. More discriminating first strikes, in which civilian casualties are
kept low – especially attacks directed at military targets well separated from
population and industrial centres – may, it is argued, still appear feasible to
a potential attacker, even though they would leave the victim a strategic force
physically capable of an 'assured-destruction' retaliation. Realising that his
losses so far were much less than those he would suffer if the initiator were to
use his 'reserve' assured-destruction force in a third strike, the victim, it is
argued, would not actually order the retaliation on the attacker's cities of
which he would be technically capable.

Massive counter-force strikes are said by the theory to offer a means by which
the stronger side could use nuclear weapons to dominate the outcome of conflicts
in which its fundamental interests short of immediate national survival were at
stake. Though an all-out nuclear strike would not be rational in such a case,
the stronger side could, by a less all-encompassing nuclear strike at his
enemy's forces, threaten or produce a fundamental change in the strategic
military balance, thus forcing a resolution of the pre-existing conflict on
favourable terms. This concept

The analysis of the previous paragraphs has taken the attacker's perspective.
The balance of risks is likely to look sharply different to the weaker side, if
he believes that the other is physically capable of a first strike which would
do him great damage (though still less than total destruction of his society)
and leave him no effective reply except forcing mutual suicide. A national
leadership on that side of such a strategic balance would be likely to focus on
the risks of its own position and be unsatisfied with assurances that the
stronger side also faces potentially paralysing risks.

A situation could, therefore, exist in which the stronger side would regard less
than all-out strikes as so risky as to be unthinkable, while the weaker(13)
would be obsessed with the danger of such attacks. Such an asymmetry of
perceptions could make each side more cautious, the stronger because he does not
want to be forced to expose the hollowness of his option, the weaker because he
fears the option may not prove hollow after all. Such a situation would not,
however, necessarily be stable or advantageous even to the stronger side, either
strategically or politically. Strategically a force physically capable of an
effective less than all-out counter-force strike is likely to seem, in the eyes
of the putative victim, dangerously like a force capable of a true first strike.
Either way, the weaker side is likely to feel a pressure in a crisis situation
to act first itself to forestall pre-emption.

For the longer run, the weaker side can expand his force and replace vulnerable
elements to reduce his strategic disadvantage. With each step of this programme
he will gain flexibility in his choice of responses. Meanwhile, he can act to
reduce the political

inhibitions imposed by his vulnerability to attack. He can engage in displays of
daring and belligerence, to increase anxiety about the possible 'irrational
overreaction' or to demonstrate that he is not cowed by the more physical
possibility of a massive counter-force attack. He can manipulate his declared
doctrine by talk of launch-on-warning and emphasis on his determination that any
nuclear attack will receive 'full' retaliation. Conversely, and perhaps more
potently, he can let it be known that he refuses to be caught in an all-or-
nothing dilemma, but will instead make a painful, though less-than-maximum
response to any attack.

Whatever may have been the effect in the early and mid-1960s on the Soviet and
the American governments of the American potential for a massive counter-force
strike, the changes in Soviet forces since that time make such strikes clearly
not a viable U.S. option today, even in the eyes of the USSR, much less those of
the United States. In destroying the technical feasibility of the option,
however, not equality of numbers but the reduced vulnerability of the Soviet
force has been the more important factor.

Even unequal but mutually invulnerable forces on both sides would make less than
all-out strikes so clearly unreliable technically and strategically as to be of
negligible effect politically. The plausibility of such an attack being made is
greatest when the victim would literally be faced with an all-or-nothing choice
– either do nothing or use his entire surviving force for an attack on cities.
If he has forces enough left to allow him a response in which he reserves an
assured-destruction force of his own, he may expect to be able to reply
adequately and still hope to deter a third strike(14). Recognition that such a
'minor' retaliatory capability exists is likely significantly to deter both
threatened and actual use of the stronger side's technically feasible option.
The direct losses the attacker would suffer himself – though much less than
'assured destruction' – would likely be themselves enough to deter in most
cases. More broadly, to strike in the first place in the hope of gaining
political advantage, an attacker faced with this situation would have to believe
that 'intra-war deterrence' would work for him but not against him, i.e., that
after the first exchange, in which each side suffered heavy but not fatal
losses, it would be his opponent and not him who would yield on the underlying
issues and refrain from further nuclear attack for fear of still worse losses.

In addition, less than all-out strikes against countries with even a small, but
invulnerable assured-destruction capability involve some risk of total
destruction for the initiator, if only because of the possibility of an
automatic or panic massive retaliation. For this reason, they would hardly be
employed except where success would have a very high pay-off indeed,

preferably a general dominance of international affairs, at least an unequivocal
forcing of a favourable resolution of a major pre-existing crisis(15).

Few sets of targets offer the possibility of such returns. Large-scale attempts
to disrupt the economy or society of the victim run too great a risk of
provoking an assured-destruction retaliation. Highly selective attacks on
isolated cities, factories, dams, or military installations would produce pay-
offs too small to justify running even a very small risk that the victim would
for reasons of revenge or confusion launch the maximum retaliatory attack of
which he was capable. It would appear, in fact, that it would require the
prospect of an immediate and decisive shift in military and political power, by
destroying most of the opponent's strategic force, to tempt an initiator into
the immense risks entailed by a deliberate nuclear attack on the homeland of a
power possessing even a minimal assured-destruction capability. Even a smaller
Soviet force, if it were mutually understood to be invulnerable, would eliminate
the American and Soviet governments, by denying the larger US forces any target
worth the risks of attack(16).

Emphasis on invulnerability as the dominant factor is not denial that relative
equality of numbers plays a role as well, at least in the current state of
technology. Invulnerability, particularly of land-based missiles on which the
Soviet Union has relied so heavily, is never absolute. Theoretically, forces
could be so greatly unequal that the attacker (if he were willing to run the
risk of a 'launch-on-warning' response) could afford to expend several of his
own missiles for each one destroyed on the other side, in the belief that the
final balance would still be more favourable than the initial. So long as
expected exchange ratios are not more favourable to the attacker than one-to-
one, numerical equality -- and, indeed, any numerical ratio not greatly
different from expected exchange ratios -- tends to insure against such
attacks(17).

VI. STRATEGIC PARTY AND ALLIANCES: DETERRENCE IN EUROPE

While the possibility of an all-out first strike by the Soviet Union on the
United States has received the most attention in the technical discussion of
strategic nuclear issues, efforts to put discussion of nuclear policy into more
concrete and 'political' perspectives have focused overwhelmingly on the role of
nuclear forces in alliances. The problem affects all US alliances and nuclear
guarantees (as well as such Soviet guarantees as may exist, e.g., Cuba) but
nowhere does it arise so directly and dramatically as in connection with
deterrence in Western Europe. It is to NATO that the United States has made its
strongest and most precise promises to use nuclear weapons in defence of third
countries. These promises are believed by most American and Western European
observers to be important both to the maintenance of US-West European cohesion
against potential Soviet ambitions in Europe and to containment of pressures for
development of independent European nuclear forces. Soviet attainment of
strategic parity could be thought likely to pose new challenges to the
credibility of the American-based NATO deterrent and to affect the general
politics of European security both through its impact on technical strategic
options and through the indirect, less tangible effects of changes in the
strategic balance on the cohesiveness of the alliance and the willingness of the
West European allies to continue to rely on American protection rather than seek
to equip themselves for independent defence or opt out of the problem by
accommodation with the Soviet Union.

Technical Factors: The Demise of Massive Counter-force

It was in an effort to increase the credibility of its declarations of readiness
to use nuclear weapons in the defence of Western Europe by employing a more
sophisticated doctrine than 'massive retaliation' that the United States made
its most extensive public exposition of the possible role of massive counter-
force attacks in American nuclear strategy. So the end of the technical
feasibility of such attacks could be expected to be significant, if anywhere, in
connection with deterrence in Europe.

Increasing NATO conventional forces in order to raise the threshold at which
NATO would be forced to resort to nuclear weapons, was only one arm of the
American 'flexible response' concept for the defence of Europe developed in the
early 1960s. The other, and for present purposes the more interesting, was the
development, articulation and implementation of a new doctrine for the
employment of nuclear weapons for the defence of Europe, centred around the idea
of a counter-force-only nuclear attack. Secretary McNamara stated the 'Ann
Arbor' doctrine(20):

'To the extent feasible, basic military strategy in a possible general nuclear
war would be approached in much the same way that more conventional military
operations have been regarded in the past. That is to say, principal military
objectives; in the event of a nuclear war stemming from a major attack on the
Alliance, should be the destruction of the enemy's military force, not of his
civilian population.

'The very strength and nature of the Alliance forces make it possible for us to
retain, even in the face of a massive surprise attack, sufficient reserve
striking power to destroy an enemy society if driven to it. In other words, we
are giving a possible opponent the strongest imaginable incentive to refrain
from striking our own cities. (Emphasis added.)'

By its reference to a nuclear response to a 'major' but not necessarily nuclear
attack on the Alliance, the doctrine embraced the possibility of a first use of
nuclear weapons by the United States in response to a massive Soviet
conventional attack in Europe. The concept underlining the doctrine was
apparently that a selective counter-force attack – against which the Soviets
would be deterred from retaliating against cities in either the United States or
Western Europe by fear of a further, counter-city attack – would wipe out most
of the Soviet missile and bomber forces aimed at the United States and Western
Europe and, by this major change in the strategic balance, nullify any advantage
the Soviets might gain from their attack in Western Europe.

The doctrine was an attempt to develop an intellectual concept of nuclear
deterrence in Europe which did not suffer the obvious credibility problems of
the earlier 'massive retaliation' theory or the technical and political
difficulties of planning to defend Europe with 'tactical' nuclear weapons.
Moreover, the doctrine appeared to offer a way to exploit the fact that at the
time it was promulgated, the United States enjoyed a substantial lead in numbers
of nuclear weapons, while the Soviet inter-continental strike force was not only
much smaller, but highly vulnerable to a first strike.

Even in early statements of the doctrine, McNamara acknowledged that the Soviets
had in their power to make it impractical by increasing the size and
survivability of their forces. As has been noted above, survivability, not
numbers, dealt a fatal blow to the technical premises underlying the doctrine.
As the Soviet force became increasingly survivable, so that an American knockout
blow was no longer technically possible,

confidence in the Ann Arbor doctrine waned, long before the Soviet Union
approached numerical equality.

In any case, the theory was of dubious validity even when first announced. In
addition to suffering from all the general difficulties about the "credibility
of massive counter-force strikes as political instruments which have been
outlined earlier(21), the concept was particularly difficult to manage in the
context of an alliance. Although the doctrine was intended by its authors as a
way to strengthen the American nuclear element in the defence of Europe, by
offering a plan which was militarily sensible and politically credible, some of
the European allies saw it quit differently. In their eyes the chief feature of
the doctrine was not that the Soviet Union was to be struck, but that ways
apparently had been found to insure that the United States would not risk
nuclear devastation in the process of aiding Europe(22). Whoever was right in
the doctrinal controversy, the very fact that there was one made the doctrine,
instead of a source of cohesion in the alliance itself, grist for division.

In any case, use of massive counter-force strikes in support of NATO presented
special technical problems if the objective was a militarily decisive blow, not
merely a demonstration of resolve or a deliberate "bargaining" escalation of
risks. Even in the early 1960s the Soviets had several hundred intermediate-
range and medium-range ballistic missiles (IR/MRBS) as well as a large number of
medium bombers. Irresumably the vast American numerical superiority allowed an
attack not just on Soviet inter-continental strike forces but on forces capable
of striking Europe as well. But because the numbers involved were large and some
at least of the units potentially mobile, substantial forces capable of striking
European targets would have been thought likely to survive, even if the inter-
continental force was reduced to a minimum. The existence of such surviving
medium-range forces would have opened to the Soviet Union a broad range of
options to attack military and "civilian targets in Europe without compromising
its surviving anti-US force. Making such strikes (or more probably threatening
to do so verbally or by token strikes) might not have "compensated" for the
losses to the Soviet inter-continental force, but might well have deprived the
United States of the dominance of the European situation which making the
counter-force attack was supposed to gain and have provided a cover for
continued Soviet conventional advance.

The Ann Arbor doctrine might, however, have been imagined to work in a less
decisive way, the idea being not that the "American attack on Soviet nuclear
forces would be militarily decisive," but that it would convey, in the most
vivid possible terms, the threat of still greater losses later, unless the
underlying conflict was settled on terms satisfactory to the United States. This
is in

many ways a more realistic concept of the use of US nuclear forces for the
defence of Europe than the 'decisive blow' theory, but unlike that theory, it is
not crucially affected by the Soviet Union having either an assured destruction
force or parity. The crucial element in this concept of deterrence in Europe is
not the selection of Soviet nuclear weapons as the target. In fact some other
non-population target, e.g. conventional military centres, could equally well be
chosen. Rather the point would be to show unmistakably that the United States
was ready to use nuclear weapons rather than see the USSR succeed in its assault
on Western Europe. The threat would be not so much that the United States would
prevail if a general nuclear war followed, but that it was willing to risk a war
in which both sides would suffer losses vastly greater than anything the Soviet
Union could gain by persisting in its course in Europe. Even under conditions of
strategic parity, the United States retains the choice of making a nuclear
strike – of whatever size – whose object is not a decisive counter-force blow,
but conveying to the USSR a threat that general war may result unless there is a
prompt settlement, even if neither side would in any rational sense 'benefit' or
'prevail' in the end.

Clearly this sort of contest of proclamations of 'resolve' by large-scale
nuclear attacks would be a vastly, perhaps even insanely, dangerous business. It
may offer neither a realistic description of how nations would in fact behave,
nor a prescriptive formula in any way attractive to the West. There is, for
example, no necessary reason to believe that in such a contest the Soviet Union
would be first to give in. But for present purposes, the important point is that
the state of the strategic balance would not be centrally relevant to the
process: neither side wants, could 'win', or would benefit from (or perhaps even
survive) general nuclear war. Rather the determinants of success would be
American ability to establish its stake in the result and its willingness to
increase the danger of uncontrolled escalation.

These factors depend much more on political than technical requirements and
should be but little affected by parity. Ultimately the credibility of the
American strategic force as a deterrent in Europe rests not on technical ability
to make a particular kind of response or the validity of any particular
strategic doctrine, but on the disastrous effects on the national self-interests
of the United States of a successful Soviet attack on Western Europe. Only
massive changes in the economics and political relations between the United
States and Europe, which undetermined this self-interest, could free a Soviet
government from fear that the United States would regard an attack on Europe as
literally the equivalent of an attack on the United States and use its strategic
nuclear power accordingly.

Moreover, the American interest in Western Europe's security and the grounds for
Soviet fears that a major threat to those interests would force the United
States into nuclear war are reinforced by the military dispositions of the NATO
alliance. The presence of a large American military establishment in Europe
means that a conventional defeat of NATO would be massive military defeat for
the United States as well. The large stocks of tactical nuclear weapons in
Europe are also an important link in strategic deterrence whatever their
potential direct contribution to stopping an imminently successful Soviet
conventional attack. Whatever the Soviet government might think about the
credibility of a deliberate American decision to launch a massive nuclear attack
on the USSR if a defeat of NATO seemed imminent, they would have to reckon with
the high probability that tactical nuclear weapons would be used, possibly
setting off a round of escalation whose consequences would be wholly
unpredictable.

Political Effects of Parity on NATO
The conclusion that parity in missile numbers with the Soviet Union should not,
as a technical or strategic matter, significantly affect American and NATO
capabilities or deterrence is only a part of the analysis. Broader political
inferences also may be drawn from parity, which could have unsettling effects on
the cohesion of the alliance, particularly because parity has come in an overall
political context in which other forces act to create difficulties for the
Alliance.

On the American side, it is at least logically possible that parity, with its
dramatic exposure of the problems of carrying out the commitment of US nuclear
forces to the defences of Europe, could, coupled with internal pressures to
reduce American forces in Europe, lead to a desire to dissociate the United
States from the security of Europe. But American doubts about the efficacy of a
general nuclear war in 'defence' of Europe are so longstanding that any chance
of parity requiring – or being thought to require – change in US NATO policy or
the basic US political commitment to the defence of Europe would be virtually
nil.

More serious are the possible effects of realisation of the existence of
strategic parity on the attitudes and policies of the European NATO members.
There is no reason to believe that the Ann Arbor doctrine was ever accepted
politically by the European members of NATO with sufficient conviction or
enthusiasm for them to be greatly troubled by its demise from elimination of its
technical prerequisites. However, while it may never have had great faith in the
particular concept embodied in the Ann /rb doctrine, Europe, rather than the
United States, has paradoxically been the citadel of faith in the credibility of
the American nuclear deterrent as a largely independent and self-sufficient
protection for Europe.

To some degree, this faith may have rested on the idea that US superiority in
numbers of nuclear weapons somehow gave the United States a way of responding to
a massive Soviet onslaught into Europe in a manner which would both insure
against unacceptable Soviet retaliation and save the position in Europe.
Strategic parity will destroy the basis of this myth once and for all, but the
myth has been fading for a long time, and parity is likely to be seen in Europe
not as a sudden or revolutionary development producing crucial changes in
military capabilities or political interests, but as the culmination of a long
process.

Parity may drive home, more dramatically than previous developments, that there
is no sort of deliberate large-scale nuclear attack which the United States can
make in 'defence' of Western Europe without virtually assuring the total
destruction of both itself and of Europe. This realisation may serve as a
pressure for greater realism in discussion of NATO policy, but parity
nonetheless underlines the dilemma currently facing the alliance. Pressures are
growing, in Europe as well as in the United States, against indefinite
continuation of the level of conventional military effort NATO now sustains.
Simultaneously, strategic parity makes it more obvious than ever that there is
no real nuclear alternative. While American and European interest in preventing
Soviet expansion in Europe is as strong as ever, the prospect of any attempt at
such expansion seems increasingly remote.

Within the confines of this dilemma, NATO, retaining its current basic structure
and doctrines, can make certain adjustments in concepts and plans. The pressure
to assign a high priority to conventional forces for Europe within whatever
level of defence effort is maintained theoretically be reinforced by parity. In
discussion of NATO's policy for use of 'tactical' nuclear weapons there may be
more attention to their role as symbols of resolve and escalators of risk than
to their direct military role. These, however, are modest measures, and they
will be adequate to keep NATO defence doctrine and capabilities in line with
perceptions of the overall political and strategic situation only if the members
of NATO, and particularly the Western Europeans, conclude that the changes
brought about by parity, as compared with mutual assured destruction, are quite
limited.

The argument of this Paper is that such a conclusion would be analytically
realistic and, indeed, that it is fairly likely in fact to be drawn. If,
however, the NATO states believe that strategic parity, in conjunction with
other developments affecting the United States' international role, has made
much more 'fundamental changes in the balance of power in the world, If the
European states believe that strategic parity renders inadequate a previously
adequate American nuclear deterrent as the answer to Europe's security problems,
they will have essentially three choices to meet the new situation: fundamental
reconstruction

of the US-European defence system with less reliance on American nuclear
deterrence; construction of a credible independent 'European' nuclear deterrent;
or accommodation with the Soviet Union.

The first course - adjustment of NATO forces and doctrines to the 'new'
situation - makes the most sense on the assumption that the basic NATO political
premises - a strong commonality of American and European interests in the face
of a substantial Soviet threat - remain valid and that only the technical
requirements for defence and deterrence are believed to have changed, however,
basically. It has, in one variation or another, been the customary American
formula for satisfying concern about the overall credibility of NATO's
deterrent. A NATO which saw parity as a major change in the world military
balance could in principle compensate for the changed military situation by
massive increases in conventional forces. Arguably, if the political impact of
parity were great enough to arouse fears of a substantial increase in Soviet
aggressiveness, the necessary political conditions for such a conventional
build-up by NATO would also be created. However, in present circumstances, the
European countries, not to mention the United States, seem quite unwilling to
undertake the economic and social costs of major increases in conventional
forces. So while this may be the most logical response to belief that parity is
highly important, it is also the least likely.

The second possible European response to parity-induced doubts about continued
reliance on the American nuclear deterrent would be the creation of an
independent European nuclear force on a scale going well beyond the present
british and French forces, probably under some form of multi-national 'European'
control. The obstacles to such a 'super-power'-scale regional European deterrent
are massive, the economic and technological ones being, though great enough,
generally speaking less formidable than the political and institutional. Much
else besides views of the prudence of continued reliance on the American nuclear
deterrent would have to change - particularly in concepts of the mutuality of
interest of the United States and Western Europe and of the urgency and scale of
the threat from the Soviet Union - for such programmes, with their great intra-
European political implications, to get a serious hearing. If the conditions for
such a development were favourable, parity by provoking a belated examination of
the nature of the American deterrent for the defence of Europe could serve as a
catalyst. But there is little evidence those conditions prevail, and without
them the catalyst has nothing on which to work.

The third course open to European nations in the face of realisation that the
United States has no nuclear magic to employ in their defence could be to seek
to reduce the threat by concessions to the USSR. Such a course of action would
require a judgment that a settlement could be reached on terms which would be
more satisfactory than continuing to tolerate the uncertainties of alliance with
the United States. But if Soviet objectives in Europe are modest enough that
they could be satisfied by concessions the European nations could agree to, that
same modesty of Soviet objectives would be likely to make the European states
feel that the threat of overt military attack was slight enough to make
tolerable considerable doubts about whether Western defences would be adequate
against a more vigorous challenge.

The effects of parity on European pressures for accommodation are likely to be
far more pronounced if other forces operate simultaneously to divide the United
States and its allies. If Europeans believed there were very great differences
between American and European interests, they might seek a bilateral European-
Soviet settlement even on unfavourable terms, fearing only worse be available in
the future. They might also wish, if they saw the United States as concerned
chiefly - and unskillfully - with extra-European problems, to separate
themselves from the risk of involvement in a conflict between the United States
and the Soviet Union over non-European issues. However, the apparently
widespread expectations of future American caution in involving itself
militarily in foreign conflicts should reduce the possibilities of such fears of
unwilling European involvement in an American quarrel. In any case, the
existence of strategic parity would have little effect on the development of
such a divergence or on European or American reactions were it to develop; the
problem is not the credibility of American protection of Europe but possible
unwilling involvement of Europe in conflicts precipitated by divergent American
interests.

The overall political as well as the technical-military effect of parity on NATO
is, thus, likely to be limited. Coming as it does in conjunction with Vietnam,
internal American tensions, and pressures in both the United States and Western
Europe for changes in traditional defence and foreign policies, it may
contribute to some re-examination and modification of past patterns. However, in
the constellation of factors changing the relationship of the United States to
Western Europe and its defence, strategic parity plays a modest role. This is
particularly true of the search -- perceived by some in the United States and
Europe as dangerous -- for bases of agreement with the Soviet Union on steps to
reduce sources of tensions.

Though strategic parity may symbolise the changed world context in which that

belief that bases exist for mutually beneficial agrements which lie behind such
negotiating initiatives as SALT, the West German "Ostpolitik," and the proposals

the American–Soviet relationship – can be expected to be essentially congruent

where parity: acts in parallel with other, stronger forces.
