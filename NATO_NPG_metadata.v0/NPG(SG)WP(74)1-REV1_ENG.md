> # Conceptual Analysis of Nuclear Aspects of Deterrence
> 
> This document is a revised draft report by the Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) regarding the purpose and background of a conceptual analysis of the nuclear aspects of deterrence. It traces back to a May 1973 meeting in Ankara where NATO Ministers discussed the implications of non-use of nuclear weapons and the credibility of the deterrent. The report argues that deterrence is a complex subject involving both concrete factors like force structure and abstract factors such as risk assessment and aggressor perception. While acknowledging the importance of the subject, the report notes that a Working Paper on 'Aspects of Deterrence' by the Phase II Study Team has already provided useful contributions to the understanding of the general workings of deterrence. Furthermore, the current and prospective projects of the Nuclear Planning Group, including the Follow-on Use Work Programme and studies on Warsaw Pact strategy, conclude that a separate, dedicated conceptual analysis of the nuclear aspects of deterrence would provide little additional gain at this time.

CONCEPTUAL ANALYSIS OF NUCLEAR ASPECTS OF DETERRENCE
I. PURPOSE
This report discusses the question of whether the potential value of a
conceptual analysis of the nuclear aspects of deterrence justifies the
preparation of a report on this subject for Ministerial consideration.

II. BACKGROUND
2. At their meeting in Ankara on 15th and 16th May, 1973, during consideration
of an illustrative study dealing with demonstrative use, the NPG Ministers
discussed, inter alia, the implications of non-use of nuclear weapons. Noting
that the credibility of the deterrent could be seriously impaired if NATO failed
to use nuclear weapons in its defence when such use would be militarily
unavoidable, the Ministers invited(1) the Permanent Representatives to consider
the value of a conceptual analysis of the nuclear aspects of deterrence for
Ministerial consideration at a future meeting.

III. DISCUSSION
3. Deterrence is a complex subject, an understanding of which must take into
account not only such concrete factors as NATO force structure, deployment,
readiness and declaratory policy but also equally significant, highly abstract
and often intangible factors such as a potential aggressor's perception of the
NATO deterrent posture and his risk assessment. Because of/fundamental
relationship of deterrence to Alliance strategy, it is nevertheless important to
have as clear an understanding as possible of the general workings of
deterrence, with particular emphasis on the nuclear aspects insofar as the NPG
is concerned. This is a difficult and challenging task to which the Alliance as
a whole and the NPG in particular have devoted, and must continue to devote,
considerable attention.

4. At the same time, it must also be recognised that deterrence is not only an
important subject in its own right, but it impinges upon, and is reflected in,
many other aspects of NPG work. This relationship is demonstrated by the annexed
Working Paper on "Aspects of Deterrence" that was prepared by the ad hoc Study
Team currently developing a report on Phase II of the Follow-on Use Work
Programme. Written by the Phase II Study Team to provide a general background to
other papers prepared in the course of its work, the Working Paper was made
available by the

Study Team in the belief that it might be of some value in resolving the
question of whether to undertake a conceptual analysis of deterrence. In doing
so, the Study Team pointed out that its Working Paper does not attempt to
provide a comprehensive treatment of deterrence, since its primary purpose was

5. Nevertheless, the Working Paper makes a very useful contribution toward
understanding the general workings of deterrence as well as toward the analysis
of follow-on use questions. It provides a good example of the way in which
consideration of deterrence aspects in the course of other NPG work can
strengthen our understanding of the concept itself. Most of the projects now
underway(1) or under consideration(2) in the NPG offer the potential to add in
one way or another to our knowledge of deterrence.

IV. CONCLUSION
6. In view of the continuing attention that is being paid to deepening and
refining our understanding of the concept of deterrence, the contribution to
that understanding that has been made by the Phase II Study Team Working Paper,
and the likelihood that a good part of the current and prospective efforts of
the NPG in other fields will also contribute toward that end, it is concluded
that little more would be gained by the initiation of a separate conceptual
analysis of the nuclear aspects of deterrence at this time.
