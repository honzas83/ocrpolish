> # Evolution of NPG Membership
> 
> This document, a note by the Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) Staff Group, outlines the the historical development of the NPG membership from its inception to 1972. It describes the initial proposal by a Special Committee of Defence Ministers in 1966 to create a permanent structure to advise the Council on nuclear defence affairs, leading to the establishment of the Nuclear Defence Affairs Committee (NDAC) and the subordinate Nuclear Planning Group (NPG). The text details the evolution of membership patterns, including the initial rotational system for non-permanent members and the subsequent adjustments to the transition from a six-to-seven member limit to a flexible alternating pattern of seven and eight members. It highlights the 'Gentlemen's Agreement' of 1966 and the 'Hague Agreed Minute' of 1968, describe the pragmatic development of the practice where current and former members participate fully at the Ambassadorial and staff levels. The document concludes by emphasizing that while limited membership is often viewed as essential for effectiveness, other factors such as personal involvement of Ministers and the sharing of essential information also contribute to the operational efficiency of the NPG.

BACKGROUND PAPER ON NPG MEMBERSHIP

I. BASIC CONCEPT
1. As a result of its examination during 1966 of nuclear planning arrangements,
the ad hoc Special Committee of Defence Ministers concluded that there should be
a permanent structure in NATO to advise the Council on nuclear defence affairs.
The Special Committee therefore proposed that the Council create an open-ended
Nuclear Defence Affairs Committee (NDAC) to be composed of the Defence Ministers
of those NATO countries who desired to participate. To do the detailed work, the
Special Committee proposed the creation of a second body of limited membership,
to be called the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG). The NPG would be subordinate to
the NDAC and would be drawn from the members of the NDAC. The main reason for
having an NPG of limited membership was seen in the fact that such smallness
would permit active personal participation by each Minister and encourage free
discussion. This reflected the experience of the ten-country Special Committee
itself, whose detailed work had been carried out by small working groups
composed of five countries each.

II. ORIGINAL ARRANGEMENTS
2. During their deliberations in 1966, Working Group III of the Special
Committee (working group on nuclear planning, composed of the Defence Ministers
of Germany, Italy, Turkey, the United Kingdom and the United States, with
Secretary McInnam as chairman) seriously considered proposing that the permanent
NPG be composed of five members. However, in its final recommendations to the
DPC, the Special Committee suggested that the "optimum" membership of the NPG
was six and that the composition should change every 12 months (DPC/D(66)39, 7th
December, 1966, paragraph 4(a).

3. Difficulties arose when this proposal was considered by the DFC Ministers.
Twelve countries (all NATO members except France, Luxembourg and Iceland)
indicated that they would join the NDAC. Ten of the twelve NDAC countries (all
except Norway and Portugal) said they wanted to participate actively in the NPG.
Since it was generally accepted that four places in a six-country NPG would be
filled on a continuing

basis by Germany, Italy, the United Kingdom and the United States, only two
places would remain to be shared by the six other NPG candidates. Each of the
rotating members could therefore expect to be active for one year and inactive
for two. (This unhappy prospect was further dimmed when it was proposed and
accepted that the composition of the NPG be changed every 18 months instead of
every year, which would result in a three year period of inactivity for each
rotating member.) The DPC Ministers therefore decided that the "maximum" number
of NPG members should be seven (DPC/R(66)11, paragraph 45) which would allow
three places for the six rotating candidates to serve on a cycle of 18 months on
and 15 months off. The specific composition of the NPG for the first two terms
(that is, for the period from 1 January 1967 to 31 December 1966) was agreed
later at a private meeting of the NDAC Permanent Representatives on 19th
December, 1966 – the so-called "Gentlemen's Agreement" (uncirculated summary
record dated 27th December, 1966).

4. Some additional arrangements were also included in the original charter to
provide for involvement of NDAC countries who were not serving actively on the
NPG. The document states that "in special cases representatives of NDAC
countries, whether or not members of the NPG, should be invited to participate
in specific studies which might be undertaken at a staff level by the NPG on
matters of particular interest to them" (DPC/D(66)39, paragraph 4(e).
Furthermore, all NDAC members were to receive the basic documentation of the
NPG, and the NDAC would discuss, review and approve the reports of the NPG for
transmittal to the DFC (DPC/D(66)39, paragraph 4(d)).

5. As the end of the first 18 months cycle approached, some of the countries
about to leave the NPG indicated that the rather limited arrangements described
above were not entirely satisfactory. Their position was generally supported by
the other rotators. Consequently, the NPG Ministers at their meeting in the
Hague in April 1968 agreed that "any country which in respect of a subject under
study had submitted a substantive paper or was directly involved on account of
its geographical position or its force contribution could, even after the
cessation of its period of membership of the Nuclear Planning Group, continue to
be associated with the work of the Group on that subject at Permanent
Representative level and at staff level" (Hague Agreed Minute, NPG/D(68)6, 2nd
April, 1968, paragraph VI(2)). The NDAC later endorsed this arrangement (NDAC
Decision Sheet, NDAC/D(68)2, 16th May, 1968, paragraph III(1)).

6. In implementation of this arrangement, a list of former NPG members showing
the specific subjects in which they could continue to be involved was circulated
(PO/68/511, 26th September, 1968). It soon became apparent, however, that an
approach which required countries to enter or leave Ambassadorial meetings as
the various items on the agenda were taken up was really not workable. (The
impracticality of this approach was even more obvious when it was noted that the
observers from the military staffs - IMS, SHAPE and SACLAN - were permitted to
remain throughout the meetings.) This led to the pragmatic development of the
practice now followed, namely, that current and former NPG members participate
fully in all NPG activities except Ministerial sessions. No distinction is made
between active and inactive members at the Ambassadorial or staff levels or in
other activities such as ad hoc study teams. This inconsistency with the charter
and the Hague Agreed Minute is tacitly acknowledged and accepted by all of the
NDAC countries. Any attempt to apply the league decision rigidly would probably
require significant changes in the present NPG practices.

III. PRESENT ARRANGEMENTS

7. When the Norwegian Government in 1969 expressed a desire to become active in
the NPG at the end of the second 18-month cycle, the original rotational pattern
was no longer workable. A number of schemes to accommodate the eleven countries
who wished to participate in the NPG were discussed. A number of private
meetings between the Secretary General and some or all of the NPG Permanent
Representatives were held in the fall of 1969 before a consensus was reached on
a new and expanded NPG membership pattern (summary records of these private
sessions are on file in the Nuclear Planning Directorate). This consensus was
reflected in the action of the NDAC Ministers in December 1969 who "agreed that
the number of members of the Nuclear Flaming Group will be alternately eight and
seven" (NDAC Decision Sheet, NDAC/D(69)4, 9th December, 1969, paragraph 4(a)). A
membership pattern was established which calls for alternating 18-month terms
for two groups of countries, one with eight members, the second with seven. Four
countries (Germany, Italy, the United Kingdom and the United States) belong to
both groups and hence participate in the NPG on a continuing basis. The
agreement also provides that, if Portugal elects to participate actively in the
NPG, it will be added to the second group, increasing its size to eight members.
Since the agreement allows for all current members of the NDAC to be
accommodated, the NDAC Ministers agreed that it would be permanent unless there
are changes in the NDAC membership.

8. One of the factors that facilitated the reaching of a consensus was the
confirmation by all of the existing practice that permitted all countries
involved in the NPG to participate fully at the Ambassadorial and staff levels.
This is reflected in the action of the NDAC Ministers who also agreed in
December 1969 that "existing arrangements will be continued for countries not
actively serving on the Nuclear Planning Group to be associated with the work of
the Group at Permanent Representative level and at staff level in a way which
would be consistent with the character of the Nuclear Planning Group as a NATO
organ with limited membership as established by Ministers in December 1966"
(NDAC/D(69)4, paragraph 4(f)).

IV. COMMENTARY

9. As can be seen from the foregoing, the question of NPG membership has been
both complex and difficult to handle. A view has persisted since the days of the
Special Committee that the NPG must have a limited membership if it is to be
effective. It should be noted, however, that there are other factors which
should not be overlooked, since they also contribute to effectiveness. These
factors, several of which are mentioned in the NPG charter (DPC/D(66)59,
paragraph 2), include, inter alia:

(a) personal involvement of Ministers;

(b) sharing of essential information on nuclear matters;

(c) limited delegation size;

(d) careful advance preparation for Ministerial meetings;

(e) freedom to focus MFG work on matters of interest to Ministers;

(f) absence of circulated verbatim or summary records of meetings, to facilitate
uninhibited discussion;

(g) heavy involvement of capital staffs in NPG work;

(h) extensive translation arrangements for meetings that permit every Minister
to listen and speak in the language in which he is most fluent;

(1) personal presence of top NATO military leaders at Ministerial meetings.
