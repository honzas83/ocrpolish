> # Practical Aids to Decision-Making: Illustrative Studies
> 
> This document, a note by the Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group), discusses the implementation of illustrative studies as practical aids to decision-making for the tactical use of nuclear weapons. It references the Provisional Political Guidelines for the Initial Defensive Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons by NATO (PPG), emphasizing the need for political authorities to understand the military implications of recommended courses of action. The note details recent discussions from the Monterey meeting, including views from the United States, United Kingdom, and Belgium regarding contingency planning, communicating NATO's intentions, and the necessity of updated illustrative studies. It proposes near-term steps, such as requesting the Supreme Allied Commander Europe (SACEUR) and the Supreme Allied Commander Atlantic (SACLANT) to provide updated versions of previous studies and new studies on modern technology. Long-term projects are identified, including selective employment planning, country requests, and procedures for Communicating NATO's Intentions (CNI). The document also includes an extract from the PPG concerning military planning and the importance of contingency plans for the employment of atomic demolition munitions (ADMs).

PRACTICAL AIDS TO DECISION-MAKING: ILLUSTRATIVE STUDIES
Note by the Chairman

1. At their meeting on 16th and 17th June, 1975 in Monterey, the Ministers of
the Nuclear Planning Group agreed(1) with the future work programme as outlined
in PO/75/69(2), thereby endorsing the Secretary General's comment(3) that the
concept of illustrative studies continues to be a useful procedure in promoting
a dialogue between political authorities and the NATO Military Authorities.

2. In order to facilitate a broader evaluation of this subject, it might be
useful to reconsider the context in which the idea of illustrative studies
originated and recall NPG achievements in this area.

contain a variety of subjects including such issues as contingency planning
studies on the selective use of nuclear weapons(7), illustrative studies and
analyses bearing on the tactical employment of nuclear weapons(8), general
target analysis(9), examples of nuclear release requests(10), and so-called

II. RECENT NPG DISCUSSIONS
5. At Monterey and during the preparation of this latest NPG Ministerial
meeting, several views touching on practical aids to decision-making were
expressed:

(a) At the NPG Permanent Representatives' meeting on 3rd June, 1975 the
Chairman, Military Committee stated that it would be helpful if the NATO
Military Authorities (NMAs) were requested to prepare an illustrative study
showing how initial and follow-on use meshed together. At the same time, he
emphasised that the NMAs should not be tasked with follow-on use illustrative
studies until completion of the Technology Study and Phase III of the Follow-on
Use Work Programme.

(b) At Monterey, the U.K. Secretary of State for Defence suggested the
illustrative study approach for such questions as country requests for the use
of nuclear weapons and for initiating signals to an enemy in conflict
situations. Also, in this case, it was recognised that such studies should only
be developed subsequent to approval of related procedures.

III. FOLLOW-UP ACTION

6. As a result of the Monterey meeting, as reflected by the Agreed Minute(1),
some immediate and some long-term actions deserve further consideration by NPG
countries:

7. Near-Term Steps. With regard to the type of studies which have been developed
for illustrative purposes (i.e., on IMC procedures in connection with initial
use requests), the NPG could ask the Chairman, Military Committee to make the
following arrangements with the Major NATO Commanders:

(a) for SACEUR and SACLANT to be ready to present updated versions of formerly
presented illustrative studies(2); this has been recognised as a useful effort,
since Defence Ministers change and the studies mentioned might be as valuable to
new as they were to former Ministers;

(b) for SACEUR and SACLANT to prepare additional studies on subjects not yet
covered, e.g., employment of ADs using modern technology.

3. Long-Term Projects. While the subjects mentioned in paragraph 7 all deal with
the traditional type of illustrative study, i.e., they are designed to
illustrate the procedural aspects in connection with the IMCs initial use
requests, other useful study areas which could facilitate NPG Ministerial
discussions are in the politico-military field which does not lend itself to the
purely procedural examination and analysis approach. Some examples of these are:

(a) selective employment planning;

(b) country requests;

(c) procedures for CNI (WINTEX 77, country requests, war-gaining);

(d) initial and follow-on use;

ANNEX A to NPG(Staff Group)N(75)59
Extract from the Provisional Political Guidelines for the Initial Defensive
Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons by NATO

D. PRACTICAL AIDS TO DECISION-MAKING
I. Military Planning

99. In order to facilitate the process of making timely political decisions and
to enable such employment to be carried out without delay, if possible, the
question arises whether it will be practicable to prepare contingency plans for
the initial and follow-on tactical use of nuclear weapons.

100. The United States, SACEUR and SACLANT have programmes that are applicable
in the event of general war. The selective use of nuclear weapons in limited
conflicts involves many possible developments of complex situations embodying
elements of a political, military, economic and psychological nature. In view of
the special operational characteristics of ADMS, it is necessary to prepare
detailed contingency plans for their employment. The feasibility of contingency
plans for other uses, without hampering the flexibility of political and
military decisions in an emergency, is still to be studied.

101. It is most important that political authorities be aware of the
implications of the courses of actions which may be recommended by military
authorities. To this end, illustrative studies and analyses bearing on the
tactical employment of nuclear weapons should be available.

102. A number of decision aids can profitably be maintained and developed in
peacetime by NATO Military Authorities, of which examples are as follows:

(a) The establishment of target categories for the selective use of nuclear
weapons, and target analysis of fixed targets, including targets in the extended
geographic area.

(b) The allocation of weapon systems in advance ii. close co-ordination with
strike plans.

(c) The development and discussion of examples of nuclear requests likely to be
forthcoming in different situations. These should cover the data requirements
set out in paragraph 106 below. By this means the political authorities could be
acquainted with the kind of 'decisions' which might have to be taken in time of
tension and war; this might help to shorten any period of consultation once a
request has been received.

103. In order that the political authorities can be made aware of the military
implications of any delay in the granting of release authority, the NATO
Military Authorities should also consider whether there are likely to be
situations in which it would not be possible, if the required military effect
was to be achieved, to await a decision from the political authorities, or a
release on a weapon by weapon basis. If so, NATO Military Authorities should set
out these situations and say what action they would propose should be taken to
overcome the problem.

104. There may be cases where a "request for consideration" of a release of
nuclear weapons for tactical use, preceding a possible formal "release request",
may reduce time needed for consultation and decision-making. However, this
requires further study.
