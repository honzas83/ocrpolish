> [!abstract] 
> # Nuclear Consultation Procedures Compilation
> 
> This document, a revised compilation of nuclear consultation procedures, provides guidance on the procedural aspects of political consultation within the North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) in time of hostilities regarding the use of nuclear weapons. It is designed to be consistent with approved policy guidance and complementary to general consultation procedures set forth in C-M(72)46. The document outlines the process for consultation between member governments, the North Atlantic Council (NAC) and the Defence Policy Committee (DPC), and Major NATO Commanders. It emphasizes that while member governments retain independence in foreign affairs and defence, the consultation process is essential for collective planning and coordinated action. The guidance covers the a-priori flow of information, the initiation of consultation by either a Major NATO Commander or a member government, and the the communication of NATO's intention to use nuclear weapons (CNI). It also describes various message types, such as Early Notification, Blue Dot Whiskey, Blue Dot Romeo, Blue Dot Sierra, and Blue Dot Echo messages. The process culminates in a decision by the nuclear powers concerned on the proposed use of weapons, taking into account views expressed during the process. The NATO Situation Centre and the Current Intelligence Group (CIG) support this process by acting as a clearing house for data and intelligence.

PART I
INTRODUCTION

PURPOSE
1. This document provides guidance on procedures for political consultation
within the Alliance in time of hostilities on the question of using nuclear
weapons.

SCOPE
2. The guidance herein is concerned with the procedural aspects of political
consultation on the question of using nuclear weapons which is part of a broader
consultation or crisis management process. It is based on and consistent with
approved policy guidance on nuclear consultation procedures contained in other
documents(1) which are not superseded and which should be kept in mind when
reading this document. It is also compatible with and complementary to the
general consultation procedures set forth in C-M(72)46, and will be followed if
time and circumstances permit. Major NATO Commanders Nuclear Weapons Release
Procedures (NWRP)(2) also apply for that part which concerns the submission of
requests to political authorities. This guidance will be up-dated as required.

BACKGROUND

3.   General consultation among Alliance members is an important objective and a
prominent feature in NATO. Because each of the Alliance members retains its full
independence in foreign affairs and defence matters, the consultation process is
an essential element of collective planning and co-ordinated action. This
consultation activity takes place in the Council/DPC as the normal forum.
Carried out continuously in peacetime and designed to be intensified in times of
tension and war, it covers a wide range of political and defence subjects
including questions of possible NATO use of nuclear weapons.

4.   One of the first major steps in the specific field of nuclear consultation
was the adoption of the Athens Guidelines in 1962, which outlined in general
terms the possibilities for consultation in the Council under three different
assumptions concerning the scale of Warsaw Pact attack(1). These Guidelines
reflected the necessity for consultation on the use of nuclear weapons in
relation to the development of a conflict as envisaged by the strategy of
massive retaliation effective at that time. As nuclear weapon assets in NATO
Europe expanded, and as NATO strategy shifted toward flexibility in response,
there was a growing recognition of a requirement for procedures and facilities
to permit consultation on selective and controlled tactical use of nuclear
weapons to be carried out in an effective and timely fashion. The conclusions
and recommendations of the Special

PART II

NUCLEAR CONSULTATION PROCEDURES

CHAPTER I

GENERAL

1. Consultation on the use of nuclear weapons is one particular aspect of a
broader process of consultation among member governments of the Alliance that
takes place continuously in peacetime and would increase in intensity should the
international situation worsen to tension, crisis and hostilities.

2. Nuclear consultation in a specific sense begins as soon as the possible use
of nuclear weapons becomes an issue. Depending on the circumstances, this matter
can be raised in "general terms in assessments of the evolving situation
prepared by member governments, the Council/DPC, or the Major NATO Commanders
before any specific requests or proposals for nuclear weapon use are made. In
more specific terms, it can be raised by a Major NATO Commander or a member
government through a request or proposal for nuclear weapons use or a warning
that such a request or proposal might soon be forthcoming.

3. The nuclear consultation process takes place on the basis of information
concerning the politico-military situation as available to the Council/DPC and
to capitals. It would unfold between capitals and in the Council/DPC. It
culminates with the decision by the nuclear power(s) concerned on the proposed
use.

CHAPTER 2
INFORMATION SOURCES
5. The effectiveness of the nuclear consultation process is critically dependent
on the amount of available information and on the ability of participants in
that process to exchange information and views in a rapid, secure and reliable
way. Nuclear consultation takes place against the background of the flow of
information envisaged in the process of general consultation during:peacetime
and expected to increase in time of tension, crisis and hostilities.
Furthermore, part of the context for nuclear consultation would be the basic
information available to capitals and the Council/DPC in peacetime, such as the
summary of political guidance, concepts and doctrine of NATO military forces,
and weapons characteristics and effects. The Situation Centre of NATO
Headquarters has the continuing task of supporting the Council/DPC in its
consultation role and acting as a clearing house for the exchange of data and
intelligence that forms the essential common data base for consultation(1). In
fulfilling the latter task, the NATO Situation Centre is assisted by the Current
Intelligence Group (CIG), which sifts and collates the political, military,
economic and technological information that is made available to NATO
Headquarters from many sources such as national capitals, the NATO Military
Authorities and public information media, and produces regular summaries of this
information for circulation both within the Headquarters and throughout the
Alliance.

6. In time of war, political authorities will need to be kept fully informed of
developments that may foreshadow the use of nuclear weapons. This requirement
will extend from the early stages of a conflict when only very general
indications are likely to be available through later stages when specific
release requests may be made.

7. In the early stages of a conflict, the main sources of information are
"Intelligence Summaries" on enemy activities, "Situation Summaries" concerning
the operation of our own forces and "Assessments". Assessments can be
evaluations of the military situation (prepared either by MNCs or by the
Military Committee) or politico-military appraisals developed by individual
governments or by the Council/DPC. Such assessments might contain general
indications of the possible need for use of nuclear weapons by NATO or
indications of impending Warsaw Pact use, as reflected in the evolving
situation.

8. At later stages of a conflict, the information required by political
authorities to consult on specific proposals for NATO use of nuclear weapons are
provided, in large measure, by the nuclear consultation messages originated by
the MNCs, such as SACEUR's "Early Notification Messages" and MNCs WHISKEY, ROMEO
and SIERRA messages(1).

9. Detailed procedures for handling of information within the NATO Headquarters
have been developed by the Council Operations Directorate.

CHAPTER 5

COUNCIL/DPC COMMUNICATION TO THE NUCLEAR POWER(S)

20. The views expressed, through their representatives, by member governments
during the consultation concerning a specific request for use of nuclear weapons
are communicated to the nuclear power(s) concerned by the Secretary General (as
Chairman of the Council/DPC) by the most expeditious means available. This
communication, which may consist of more than one message as consultation
proceeds, should reach the nuclear power in time to enable it to take the
decision within the time limits mentioned in the message of the requesting MNC
to ensure an effective use.

21. The Secretary General’s message is not bound to any specific format. It may
refer to one or more requests for use and should identify the national views of
each country, either singly or by group of countries. In particular, the
national views of those countries which, as defined in the General Guidelines
for Consultation Procedures(1) would be the most affected by the requested use,
should be clearly identified.

BLUE DOT WHISKEY MESSAGE
This message is used by MICs to warn NATO political authorities and NATO member
governments that the military situation in one or more NATO areas is

in itself a request for nuclear weapon release: it is intended to reduce the
time needed for consultation and decision-making. For this reason, it would
contain as much of the information required by the BLUE DOT RUCIO/SIERRA (which
may follow) as is available and applicable. The WHISKEY message may be
originated by an FNC on his own initiative or as a follow-up action to a warning
message received from a subordinate commander.

BLUE DCT SIERRA MESSAGAGE
This message is used by MICs to request pre-conditioned authority for selective
release of nuclear weapons for use against the enemy when, and if, a depicted
set of conditions, or a described situation occurs. The SIERRA message may be
originated by an MNC on his own initiative or as a follow-up action to a similar
request received from a subordinate commander. It is intended to avoid severe
loss of forces that might occur during the period required to obtain
authorisation to employ nuclear weapons using the ROMEO message procedure and to
enable the timely and effective use of such weapons against fleeting targets,
when attacks on friendly forces develop very quickly. In some of these cases,
the relesec authorization must be received by the executing commander before
specific tactical contact with the attacking enemy unit(s) has been made. Time
permitting, the SIERRA message will normally be preceded by a BLUE DCT SIERRA
message concerning the same request for pre-conditioned authority for selective
release and sent to the same addressee. The consultation based on a SIERRA
message will culminate with a decision of the nuclear power concerning that
request.

Definitions:
The following terms are frequently used in the document. In accordance with a
prior NATO understanding, they are defined as follows:

1) Assessment. An unformatted message or document containing the evaluation of
the military and/or political situation by the originator, based on intelligence
and other available information. If it contains views on possible use of nuclear
weapons.

2) ATOMIC Classification. The classification covering Atomic Information made
available by the Government of the United States of America to the North
Atlantic Treaty Organisation and its member states, pursuant to the Agreement
between the Parties to the North Atlantic Treaty for cooperation regarding
Atomic Information dated 18th June, 1964. Guidance for identification and
handling of ATOMIC information is contained in CM(68)41(Revised), 24th November,
1970.

3) Early Notification Message. An unformatted message, originated by S/CUR and
directed to proper political and military authorities, dealing with the possible
impending use of nuclear weapons.

4) Executing Commander. The commander designated by the Releasing Commander as
responsible for ordering the delivery of the weapon(s) to the target(s)
authorised in the release message.

5) Follow-on Use. The selective use of nuclear weapons after initial selective
use by NATO.

6) Initial Use. The selective first use of nuclear weapons by NATO.
7) Nuclear Weapons Categories(1)
a) Ground Organic: Nuclear weapons organic to Army Groups and Marine Corps
Units.

b) Air, Ground Non-Organic and Ship-borne: All nuclear weapons to be delivered
by aircraft, non-organic ground systems and surface ships against land or sea
targets.

(1) For details, see document nos. 15 and 16 in Annex A.
