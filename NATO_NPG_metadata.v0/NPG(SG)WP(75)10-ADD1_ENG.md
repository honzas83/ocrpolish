> # Communicating NATO's Intentions - Warning Messages
> 
> This addendum to Working Paper NPG(Staff Group)WP(75)10 analyzes the strategic implications of issuing warning messages to the Warsaw Pact regarding NATO's intention to use nuclear weapons. It examines the range of possible Soviet attitudes, categorized as 'flexible and cautious' or 'rigid and doctrinal,' and evaluates whether such warnings may influence Soviet leadership. The document notes that while a warning might encourage a political solution or a cessation of hostilities, it risks triggering a pre-emptive tactical nuclear strike by the Warsaw Pact. It further explores the 'spectrum of warning messages,' ranging from general terms at the outbreak of hostilities to blunt terms at the point of nuclear decision. The text also discusses the channels for communication, suggesting that nuclear power hotlines are the most practicable channel due to the urgency of a crisis. The analysis includes a specific example from the Denmark Study regarding the use of nuclear weapons to deter amphibious landings. The document concludes that the overall effectiveness of a warning message is dependent on the unknown calculations of the enemy and the the Warsaw Pact's perception of NATO's resolve.
>

8. Flexible and Cautious Approach. The term "flexible and cautious" Warsaw Pact
approach is not susceptible of precise definition as it encompasses a range of
possible Soviet attitudes to the war.

The Warsaw Pact Study should be consulted for an extensive description.
For the purposes of this paper, it can best be illustrated by a range of
possible Soviet perceptions with regard to NATO's willingness to use nuclear
weapons: either NATO would not have the nerve to go nuclear and NATO Europe
could, therefore, be overrun by conventional means alone; or while NATO might
well make limited tactical use of nuclear weapons, this risk could appear
acceptable to the Warsaw Pact for limited territorial gains and with a ceasefire
after NATO initial use; finally, the Warsaw Pact expectation might be a limited
tactical nuclear exchange after which NATO resistance would collapse.

9. The question that poses itself is whether the Soviet leadership would, in any
of these cases, be swayed by a warning message. This question cannot be answered
in advance. All that can be said is that the warning may have the desired
effect; for instance, if the Soviet leadership was divided, a strong warning
might tilt the balance towards seeking a political solution. Thus, there may be
advantage, but this possible advantage may even then only be partial. Thus, as a
consequence of the warning the Warsaw Pact may cease his advance, but, in
keeping with his calculations, sit tight on the territory he had gained; NATO
might, therefore, still be obliged to use nuclear weapons. Against these
possible advantages must be set the possible disadvantages. Clearly, the
principal danger of any advance warning

is of causing the Soviets to initiate tactical nuclear warfare. This must be
considered particularly probable if the Warsaw Pact would expect a collapse of
NATO resistance after a limited nuclear exchange.

10. However, it is most improbable that NATO's political leadership could make
such a concrete analysis in an actual crisis because of the difficulty of
knowing just what were the enemy calculations. It might even be impossible to
assess whether the enemy had decided on a "flexible and cautious approach" as he
might well wish to project himself as an implacable foe who would stop at
nothing. It is, therefore, necessary to draw on the preceding analysis to give
guidance to political and military authorities in a war where the Soviet
intentions could only be guessed at. This can be done as follows:

(a) if the Warsaw Pact are adopting a "rigid and doctrinal" approach, a warning
message is unlikely to have any beneficial effect; it may trigger a widespread
nuclear attack, but no different from their response to NATO's (presumably
limited) initial use; thus, in this case, the end result of a warning message is
neutral; events just happen sooner than they would without;

(b) if the Warsaw Pact are adopting a "flexible and cautious" approach, their
response to a warning message (if, indeed, they react at all to the message:
they might well just prefer to continue their advance conventionally) might
range through the following:

- cessation of hostilities and withdrawal;
- no further advance, but holding on to territory gained;
- tactical nuclear action which would not unnecessarily exceed what was needed
to discount in advance NATO's nuclear action and secure the continued advance of
the WP forces.

11. In short, the warning message may cause heavy and widescale pre-emption,
but, if so, this would have happened in any case (the "doomsday case"), may,
alternatively, have all or some of the desired effect, or may cause initiation
of tactical nuclear warfare on what the Warsaw Pact feels to be a militarily
necessary level. Thus, the principal calculation that would have to be made
(putting aside the possibilities of the doomsday case) is whether the
disadvantages of possible limited tactical warfare initiated by the Warsaw Pact
rather than NATO outweigh the possible and perhaps partial beneficial effects.

Other Disadvantages of Warning Messages

12. Without any doubt, the risk of causing Soviet nuclear attacks is the
principal disadvantage of a warning message. But there are others. The Soviet
response to the warning message might be ambiguous or deceptive, causing NATO to
lose valuable time while the conventional battle was going from bad to worse. It
is also possible that the enemy could take military counter-measures, such as
dispersing his forces further or abstaining from air attacks if he learned that
nuclear-air defence weapons were under consideration.

The Spectrum of Warning Messages
13. The warning messages might range in time from before or at the outbreak of
hostilities (and thus, presumably, couched in general terms) up to the stage of
a conflict when the nuclear power or powers have reached a firm decision to use
nuclear weapons (in this case, the message would probably be cast in specific
and blunt terms). It is probably fair to say, in general, that, on the one hand,
the dangers and, on the other hand, the likelihood of success of a warning
message would increase in intensity from the start of the war to the point of
nuclear decision, because the Warsaw Pact would not unnecessarily use nuclear
weapons if the conventional battle was going well, but it was clear that NATO
was some time away from being forced to use nuclear weapons.

14. However, this is a generalisation rather than an invariable rule, and there
may well be cases where it would not apply. For instance, drawing on the example
of the Denmark Study, Follow-on Use(Study(1)), a firm and early statement by
NATO that it intended to use nuclear weapons against foreseen amphibious
landings in Denmark, where the enemy could make no nuclear reply directly
related to that particular engagement, might well deter the enemy from
committing himself to the operation.

15. In general, we believe that the likelihood of success of a message sent at
an early stage of a conflict would be less, but obviously an early end to
hostilities might be considered such a prize as to make the attempt worthwhile.
On the other hand, common sense tells us that it would be disadvantageous to
NATO to issue too many warning messages.

Warning: Messages and Follow-on Use

16. The foregoing has been written mainly with NATO's initial use of nuclear
weapons in mind. In general, it would also apply mutatis mutandis, for follow-on
use if it were decided in the Alliance to make any warning further to that
implicit in initial use. However, there would be additional considerations.

17. If a warning message had been issued before initial use and had been
ignored, the Warsaw Pact would, at least, have learnt that NATO did not make
warnings as a bluff. Thus, if NATO made a warning before follow-on use, the
Warsaw Pact would be sure that it would be followed by use of nuclear weapons on
a scale to cause significant disruption of military operations, either because
the message said so or because of their knowledge of NATO strategy. It follows
from this that the Warsaw Pact response to the message could hardly be passive.
Either they negotiate and reduce conventional operations to a level which would,
in their view, not cause NATO to go ahead with the threatened use or they seek
to pre-empt NATO's follow-on use.

Channels for Warning Messages

18. A warning message might be issued as a communiqué by the North Atlantic
Council and disseminated by all public means available to the Allies. By
allowing public knowledge of the warning message sent to the Warsaw Pact, the
Alliance might demonstrate – especially to non-aligned countries – its desire to
hold back from nuclear war. On the other hand, this kind of publicity might not
allow the Alliance to offer to the enemy face-saving de-escalatory options, and,
