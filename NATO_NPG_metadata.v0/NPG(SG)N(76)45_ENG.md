> # Articles Related to Nuclear Defence
> 
> This document is a notice from the Chairman of the Nuclear Planning Group (Staff Group) dated May 26, 1976, providing extracts from recent editions of Aviation Week & Space Technology and Foreign Affairs. The first annex, titled 'Targeting Flexibility Emphasized by SAC', discusses the Strategic Air Command's support for a limited or double nuclear weapons response-to-attack doctrine. It details the U.S. strategic force, including B-52 and FB-111 bombers, Minuteman III and Minuteman 2 ICBMs, and submarine-launched ballistic missiles, detailing the current limitations in covering the target list in the Single Integrated Operational Plan (SIO). The text emphasizes that while the U.S. has sufficient warheads to cover high-value targets in a nuclear exchange with the Soviet Union, it does not have enough to destroy an enemy seven times over. It also discusses the importance of targeting areas of the Soviet and Chinese economies essential for reconstruction to ensure the U.S. is not inferior after a strike. Additionally, it describes technical improvements in Minuteman guidance, the Mk.12/A warhead, and Hybrid Explicit guidance software to increase targeting flexibility and the future role of the Rockwell International B-1 bomber.

ANNEX A to NPG(Staff Group)N(76)45
Targeting Flexibility Emphasized by SAC
CMR A.B. S—Strategic Air Command is supporting the national requirement for a
limited or double nuclear weapons response-to-attack doctrine by providing an
accurate force with a 97% reliability factor capable of delivering a specific
number of warheads on targets without committing the entire U.S. strategic
force.

Even with the surplus of precision now available in weapons delivery to support
flexible response, the U.S. triad with its components of bombers,
intercontinental ballistic missiles and submarine-launched ballistic missiles
can cover only about three-fourths of the targets on the list in the Single
Integrated Operational Plan (SIO) according to Gen. J. M. Keck, SAC's vice
commander.

U.S. strategic force available to cover the target list includes:

○ Boeing B-52 heavy bombers—420.

○ General Dynamics FB-111 medium bombers—66.

○ Boeing Minuteman III ICBM—550, with the IRB's each

○ Boeing Minuteman 2—450, with single RVs

○ Then 2: 54, with single RVs

○ Lockheed P-3 submarines launched ballistic missiles—100, with three MIRVs
each.

○ Lockheed Pershing C-351 B1M—490, each with up to 14 MIRVs.

Senator SAC officials are eager to dispel the myth, often expressed in Congress,
that the U.S. has enough nuclear weapons to destroy an enemy seven times over.

Competed war game studies of the U.S. capability and that of the principal
adversary, the Soviet Union, by the Joint Strategic Target Planning Staff,
organized and acting under the guidance of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, reveal the
U.S. has sufficient warheads to cover all of the high-value targets in a nuclear
exchange with the USSR.

Gen. Russell F. Dougherty, SAC's commander in chief, and the situation is
analogous to a duck hunter. The hunter is armed with a full choke 12-gauge
shotgun with number four shot. Gen. Dougherty said, and theoretically, he has
enough power with one shell in his weapon to destroy 100 ducks. But when the
ducks fly over, the hunter can only fire at one. Gen. Dougherty explained.

In the war game studies of a nuclear exchange between the U.S. and USSR, the
target list in the SIO calls out targets with a priority value and damage
assessment is determined. In some respects, the U.S. fares better than the
Soviet Union because there is a broader base of targets to aim at in the USSR,
but that is balanced by the fact that they are more widely scattered than
targets in the U.S., and protected by a very active Soviet surface-to-air
missile defense program plus civil defense and dispersal of military and
industrial targets to remote areas. Better accuracy of U.S. strategic delivery
vehicles becomes another factor in the equation, but, according to senior SAC
officials, the Soviets are gaining ground with accuracy improvements and the
advantage they have in missile throw weight.

Nuclear war is survivable, SAC professionals believe. Because neither the U.S.
nor the USSR has sufficient nuclear weapons to annihilate the other, the Joint
Strategic Target Planning Staff and SAC are studying targeting areas of the
Soviet and Chinese economy that would aid in a rapid recovery from a nuclear
weapons exchange—areas like food stockpiles, fertilizer plants to aid
agriculture and widely dispersed industrial complexes. SAC officials are letting
potential enemies know that, even though some form of survival is possible, by
targeting those items essential for reconstruction, the U.S. is making certain
that it will not be inferior to an enemy after a nuclear strike.

The U.S. must match Soviet momentum to have an assured nuclear deterrent force.
SAC officials said. They base this on measurements of thermal radiation and
blast effects of U.S. weapons calculated in the studies against the requirement
to destroy all of the high-value targets on the list.

Weapons system reliability is added to the computation along with circular error
probable (that 50% of the strategic warheads fall within a specified circle
around the target area) and the overpressure created in the target zone. The
size of the U.S. strategic force is thus determined by the percentage of the
assurance of target destruction, and SAC believes it now does not have
sufficient weapons to destroy all targets on that list.

While the U.S. has long had some level of flexibility in delivering some of its
nuclear weapons on a selective basis, that capability has been given priority
and has rapidly expanded.

The use of manned bombers carrying Boeing short-range attack missiles (SRAM) and
nuclear gravity weapons, with differing yields on both missile and bomb
warheads, offers the greatest possible flexibility in responding, and that is
one of the reasons why officials in headquarters here are anxious for the
Defense Dept. and Congress to approve production and funding requests for the
Rockwell International B-1 bomber. Its low-level penetration capability and
survival characteristics increase the flexible response options, SAC officials
said.

A shift in the targeting doctrine, which refines the mutual assured destruction
philosophy upon which the U.S. strategic deterrent is based, plus developments
in computer hardware/software and the advent of the command data buffer system
permitting more rapid retargeting capability of the Boeing Minuteman 3 force
have aided in developing a limited nuclear response.

A number of programs to further refine and support the limited targeting option,
and the larger assured destruction capability are in development. They include:

○ Minuteman guidance improvement program—A software development effort to
improve Minuteman 3 accuracy by reducing guidance errors. This program is
expected to be operational within the next three years.

○ Mk.12/A warhead—Development of a higher yield reentry vehicle within the same
space in the Minuteman post-boost control system by improvements in arming and
fusing technology. The yield of the Mk.12/A will be larger providing still more
flexibility in targeting.

○ Hybrid Explicit—Guidance software that will permit an increase from three to
four in the number of target sets that can be stored in each Minuteman 3 armed
with its three multiple independently targetable reentry vehicles (MIRV). It

Four Services Represented
The Joint Strategic Target Planning Staff is located here at Ottawa, and Gen.
Dougherty is director of the organization. The Joint Staff has the
representatives of all four services. The unified commands in Europe involved
with nuclear weapons, and the Supreme Allied Commander-Europe coordinating the
North Atlantic Treaty Organization war plan with that of the U.S.

The staff has two divisions. They are:
• Intelligence—the function that is used to prepare the national strategic
target list. This list identifies necessary targets within the SIO.

• Operations planners—the target list is matched by this section against various
warheads and delivery systems to develop the tactics and timing used in the SIO.

The SIO is reviewed constantly by war game procedures on computers located at
SAC headquarters. The SIO is updated daily and is completely revised
periodically.

Data Buffer Operational
Command data buffer now operational with some Minuteman 3 units is used to erase
executably programmed execution plans in the memory module of the weapons system
controller in the command capsules. Now Hybrid Explicit is becoming operational.
Less memory capacity is devoted to target program computers. More memory space
can be used for additional execution plans. Those 100 or 200 execution plans can
refer only to target sets already available and stored in the onboard missile
computer memory. However, with command data buffer, the target location in each
of the target sets can be changed to a new target location. Without Hybrid
Explicit, the Minuteman 3 missile has the capacity for three sets of targets for
each of its three RVs. Hybrid Explicit will expand that capability to four sets
of onboard target data for each of as many as three RVs because the algorithm
used by Hybrid Explicit requires less space in the onboard memory module of the
NS-20 dedicated to target constants.

A target set equates to a latitude and longitude for each RV, providing for each
target island designated ground zero.

The execution plan for all 50 missiles in a Minuteman squadron is called
preparatory launch command-A and is used to select one of the three target sets
stored in each missile and to establish release time for the missile’s flight. A
variety of delays in missile launch are built into the war plan.

A key turn by the combat crew commander in the launch control center and his
deputy is the last act in the launch sequence. The two keys are separated by a
distance of 12 ft. They must be turned within 2 sec. of one another and held for
2 sec. in order to register a valid launch vote in the missile system. It is
physically impossible for one man to turn both keys fast enough to cause a
launch signal. The missile must also receive a second valid launch signal from
one of the other launch control centers within the squadron before the launch
can occur.

If all ground control to the missiles has been lost, the second vote or both
votes can be provided from an airborne command post aircraft or from other
specially equipped Boeing EC-135 aircraft called airborne launch control
centers.

In missile tests from Vandenberg AFB, Calif., SAC through March has launched 34
missiles using the airborne launch system at distances varying from 70 to 120
naut. mi.

Each launch control center is responsible for 10 missiles. Targeting changes
normally accomplished by one of the launch control centers in a squadron with
one of the others monitoring and verifying target changes. The other three
centers perform this task routinely and maintain a control over these functions.
Targeting is generally rotated among a unit’s control centers to provide
experience for the crew.

Preparatory launch command-B is used to enter an execution plan for a single
Minuteman ICBM, while the A command simultaneously issues the execution command
for all 50 missiles, setting up the launch delay sequence for each and telling
each which set of stored target data will be executed.

The execution plans can refer only to the target sets stored in the missile’s
onboard Autonome D-37 computer.

Three-Phase Trajectory
ICBM trajectory consists of three phases—boost, ballistic flight and reentry.
The missile takes off from 25 to 35 min. to strike its target located somewhere
between 1,800 and 7,500 naut. mi. away, depending on the location of the silo
and the target area.

During the flight, an ICBM can reach heights of as great as 1,200 naut. mi.
above the earth. The boost phase lasts for about 3 min. with the three solid-
fueled stages producing a maximum thrust of 225,000 lb. to place the missile in
the proper ballistic trajectory.

Boosting is critical, because the trajectory is shaped through the interaction
of an onboard computer and guidance and control unit. There are as many as
800,000 mathematical calculations accomplished by the onboard computer during
the first 9 min. of missile flight. The most critical part of the powered flight
is just before third-stage burnout. At this point, the ICBM is traveling faster
than 16,000 mph, and an error of 1 fps. in cut-off velocity can result in a
target miss of more than a mile.

The ballistic portion of the trajectory begins at third-stage burnout for a
single RV like the Minuteman 3, and as much as 6 min. later for a MRV system in
the Minuteman 3. At this point, gravity is the primary force affecting the
system. Apogee is between 450 and 1,200 naut. mi., and reentry occurs around
250,000 ft. During ballistic flight, the MRV system has the capability to deploy
the three independently targeted warheads hidden in trains of chaff clouds.

During reentry, atmospheric conditions have a marked effect on the speed and
horizontal range of an RV. Only 139 sec. elapsed while the RV is slowed from
about 14,000 mph to 240 mph. With some reentry vehicles, impact velocity depends
on such variables as reentry angle, range, target elevation and warhead shape.

In each revision of the SIOC, SAC's 544th Aerospace Reconnaissance Technical
Wing located here, must produce missile target kits for the ICBM force, except
for those Minuteman 3 missiles with command data buffer. The sequence begins
with target selection by the Joint Strategic Target Planning Staff. After
selection, the IJTPS staff selects launcher/target combinations and the 544th's
Trajectory Div. uses an IBM 360/RS computer to produce the target kits.

Production of target data is accomplished through an operational targeting
program, a very large and complex digital computer software program. It operates
in two modes: targeting and verification. There is a unique program for each
missile weapon system, and each requires two types of data—fixed and variable.
Fixed data is constant for a given missile system and is basically hardware-
dependent. The variable data are supplied by the Joint Strategic Target Planning
Staff for each launcher/target combination.

The target mode computes as many as 750 guidance constants in 1-2 min. After the
constants are produced, the verification mode is used to simulate the actual
trajectory using the constants produced in the target mode. The calculated
impact point must fall within 200 ft. of the desired target coordinates of the
tape.

With command data buffer entering the missile force, the process begins with the
trajectory division providing the missile unit with a validated operational
target program and the necessary fixed data in the targeting base. The SAC staff
initiates the target change by issuing the target order directly to the missile
unit instead of to the trajectory division.

Constants Generated
The missile combat crew then generates the guidance constants using a small
computer called the weapons system controller. Once the constants are produced
and verified, they are transmitted directly into the onboard computer.

With this system, computation and transmission are simplified and no physical
penetration of the silo is required. With Hybrid Explicit entering the SAC
missile force, the trajectory division will verify some flight tapes in addition
to the checkout and verification of the new operational targeting program.

The difference between the standard guidance scheme now used, and referred to by
SAC as delta guidance, and Hybrid Explicit is that with delta, when the missile
is off course it corrects back to track. With Hybrid Explicit it corrects to
intercept the destination or target. With the command data buffer system, a
formal message can be sent over existing SAC communications lines. The combat
crew has a copy of the formal in the launch control center and can use the
message to change targeting data.

Normally, the message would be sent from SAC headquarters here to missile launch
control centers involved.

With the Delta guidance program, the guidance constants on the Mylar tape, or
the constants computed at the command data buffer launch control facility,
define a unique trajectory from the silo to the target. That information is pre-
programmed into the digital control unit in the airborne guidance equipment to
control the flight. During powered flight the current missile position is
compared with the precomputed trajectory which is known as the 'nominal'
trajectory. Whenever a deviation from the nominal trajectory is detected, the
missile's guidance and control unit immediately directs the missile back to the
nominal path.

Equations Used
With Hybrid Explicit guidance which is being installed in the Minuteman 3 force,
no reference trajectory is inserted into the digital control unit. There are,
instead, physical motion and steering equations in the digital unit of the
missile guidance set. The computer uses current trajectory position and velocity
data and predicts the impact point. Computations are also made to determine the
precise velocity needed to go directly from the current position to the target.
This is done throughout the flight until it reaches a point in space where the
trajectory coincides with that to the target. The post-boost control mechanism,
a liquid-fueled fourth stage, allows for the relocation of the reentry vehicle
platform and deployment of the reentry vehicles against their separate targets.

With Hybrid Explicit guidance now being installed on most of the Minuteman 3
force, no reference trajectories are inserted in the digital control unit. There
are, instead, physical motion and steering equations in the digital unit of the
missile guidance set. With this guidance, the computer uses current trajectory
input and predicts the impact point. This is done throughout the flight until it
reaches a point in space where the trajectory coincides with that to the target.
The post-boost control mechanism, a liquid-fueled fourth stage, allows for the
relocation of the reentry vehicle platform and deployment of the reentry
vehicles against their separate targets.

With Hybrid Explicit guidance techniques have been adapted for the speed and
capacity of the D-37 computer in the NS-20 guidance system. During flight, the
missile guidance set computes impact predictions and the missile position
relative to required trajectory.

The onboard memory module of the NS-20 dedicated to target constants.
A target set equates to a latitude and longitude for each RV, providing for each

The execution plan for all 50 missiles in a Minuteman squadron is called
preparatory launch command-A and is used to select one of the three target sets
stored in each missile and to establish release time for the missile's flight. A
variety of delays in missile launch are built into the war plan.

A key turn by the combat crew commander in the launch control center and his
deputy is the last act in the launch sequence. The two keys are separated by a
distance of 12 ft. They must be turned within 2 sec. of one another and held for
2 sec. in order to register a valid launch vote in the missile system. It is
physically impossible for one man to turn both keys fast enough to cause a
launch signal. The missile must also receive a second valid launch signal from
one of the other launch control centers within the squadron before the launch
can occur.

If all ground control to the missiles has been lost, the second vote or both
votes can be provided from an airborne command post aircraft or from other
specially equipped Boeing EC-135 aircraft called airborne launch control
centers.

In missile tests from Vandenberg AFB, Calif., SAC through March has launched 34
missiles using the airborne launch system at distances varying from 70 to 120
naut. mi.

Each launch control center is responsible for 10 missiles. Targeting changes
normally accomplished by one of the launch control centers in a squadron with
one of the others monitoring and verifying target changes. The other three
centers perform this task routinely and maintain a control over these functions.
Targeting is generally rotated among a unit’s control centers to provide
experience for the crew.

Preparatory launch command-B is used to enter an execution plan for a single
Minuteman ICBM, while the A command simultaneously issues the execution command
for all 50 missiles, setting up the launch delay sequence for each and telling
each which set of stored target data will be executed.

The execution plans can refer only to the target sets stored in the missile’s
onboard Autonome D-37 computer.

ASSURING STRATEGIC STABILITY:
AN ALTERNATIVE VIEW
By Jan M. Lodal

In the coming months, the Ford Administration must decide either to offer the
Soviet Union compromises on the Vladivostok SALT Accord, permitting completion
of the agreement as a permanent treaty, or to face the prospect of a prolonged
period of strategic competition with the U.S.S.R., unconstrained by formal
limits on strategic offensive forces. If the agreement is completed, the
Congress must then decide on ratification or rejection. While this issue will
occupy center stage in the strategic debate until it is resolved, the United
States also faces a second major decision regarding its strategic program:
whether to respond to the ongoing Soviet deployment of new, large, land-based
missiles equipped with multiple independently targetable reentry vehicles
(MIRVs). This Soviet deployment is not affected by the Vladivostok Accord. Thus,
if it is important to respond by adjusting our strategic program, we will have
to do so whether the agreement is completed or not.

In the January issue of Foreign Affairs, Paul Nitze recommends that, in addition
to ongoing programs to improve accuracy, two specific major actions be taken by
the United States to ensure strategic stability in the next decade: the
deployment of a "multiple launch-point" land-based intercontinental ballistic
missile system (more frequently called a mobile ICBM), and a greatly expanded
civil defense program. He bases these recommendations on a series of
considerations, including the nature of recent Soviet strategic force
deployments, his view of Soviet motives concerning detente, and his view of the
overriding importance of missile throw-weight in determining overall strategic
force capability.

With regard to the Vladivostok Accord, Mr. Nitze points out that the agreement
will not change the basic nature of the current Soviet force buildup. While
apparently resigned to its completion, he nevertheless implies that the Accord
is probably worse than no SALT agreement at all; he clearly believes it to be
worse than the type of agreement he would have preferred—primarily because it
fails to limit Soviet missile throw-weight—and finds "without adequate
foundation" the general belief that while strategic stability may not be [Paul
H. Nitze, "Assuring Strategic Stability in an Era of Detente," Foreign Affairs,
January 1976, pp. 207-32. While advocating expanded civil defense, Mr. Nitze
notes that "such a program does not appear to be politically possible at this
time." Ibid., p. 237.]

While this situation makes it clear that the process of establishing a better
Soviet-American relationship is not an easy one, I believe that Mr. Nitze's
concern that the Soviet Union might be close to obtaining strategic superiority
is not justified. In my view, he has considerably overemphasized missile "throw-
weight" as an indicator of aggregate strategic capability. Furthermore, Mr.
Nitze's recommendations for deployment of land-based mobile ICBMs and large
civil defense programs are, in my opinion, both ill-advised; neither should be
pursued, either on its merits or in the light of other more modest measures that
are available to meet the situation.

Finally, with regard to the Vladivostok Accord, while the agreement contains
provisions which are clearly less constraining to the arms buildup of the two
sides than many persons had hoped for, its terms are relatively favorable to the
United States. If it is completed, it should provide a reasonably good basis for
controlling the strategic arms competition between the two sides in the longer
term.

To refute this view, let us examine some specific combinations of size and

kilotons each. If we assume its accuracy is approximately 0.15 nautical miles,

United States to deploy the Mark 12A.

Moreover, when accuracies get better than 0.1 nautical miles, as essentially all
experts predict they will by the late 1980s, throw-weight becomes almost
completely irrelevant. A 500-kiloton weapon with a 0.06 nautical mile CEP has a
95 percent probability of destroying a missile silo, targeting only two weapons
per silo—essentially as high a probability as one could ever obtain. Thus, in
this situation additional throw-weight, even enough to permit the deployment of
one- or two-megaton MIRVs, is of no advantage whatsoever. Even relatively small
weapons, such as the 100-kiloton warhead projected for the new Trident-1
submarine-launched missile, can have quite good anti-silo counterforce
capabilities at these accuracies.

In sum, there is only a very narrow range of accuracies in which throw-weight is
important to a hard-target counterforce capability. Unless accuracies are better
than about 0.2 nautical miles, no reasonable MIRV system can have much of a
counterforce capability; once accuracies are better than 0.1 nautical miles,
essentially any size missile, even those of relatively low throw-weight, can
destroy silos. Since the technology is clearly in hand to permit both sides to
obtain accuracies better than 0.1 nautical miles in the late 1980s, limiting
throw-weight is probably unnecessary for strategic stability.

IV
Turning now to the Vladivostok Accord, I believe it is first important to review
precisely what was settled there and the negotiating history led to the
agreement reached in November 1974.

The Accord calls for an equal numerical limit on two important quantities: the
number of strategic delivery vehicles (missiles and strategic bombers), which
are limited to 2,400, and the number of missiles equipped with MIRVs, limited to
1,320, with both numbers effective through 1985. As has been pointed out, 2,400
is only slightly below the Soviets' present force levels (about 2,500), and is
above what the United States plans to deploy anyway (unless "mothballed"
strategic bombers are counted in the total). Furthermore, 1,320 is dramatically
above the present Soviet MIRV force level (zero at the time the Accord was
reached and only 50-100 today), and also above the number of missiles with MIRVs
the United States plans to deploy, even assuming all existing Posidion
submarines are retained in the force as the new Trident submarines come on line.
Thus, it is clear that neither side gave up much, if any, of its present
strategic programs.

principle was somewhat ambiguously embodied in the "Jackson Amendment" (to the
Joint Resolution approving the Interim Agreement), which provided that "the
President seek a future treaty that, inter alia, would not limit the United
States to levels of intercontinental strategic forces inferior to the limits
provided for the Soviet Union."

based not much on the military importance of this measure, which was
acknowledged to be minimal, as on its political significance. It was argued that

Based on the analysis presented earlier, I am considerably less concerned with
the Soviet throw-weight advantage than is Paul Nitze. Nevertheless, no one can
deny that Soviet capabilities are increasing and that Minuteman is becoming
vulnerable, albeit more because of improvements in accuracy than because of
increases in throw-weight. Thus, the United States must decide whether to react
to the Soviet buildup by adjusting its own strategic program; and Mr. Nitze's
suggestions for how we should respond deserve consideration even if one
disagrees with his reasons for recommending a response.

Mr. Nitze's primary recommendation is that the United States employ a shelter-
based land-mobile ICBM system to offset the Soviet throw-weight advantage. He
argues that such a system would "absorb" the Soviet throw-weight advantage; if
the Soviets were to attempt a first strike against our strategic forces, they
would be required to devote a large proportion of their own force to attacking
the mobile missiles, reducing their surviving throw-weight.

Again, this argument is open to question on Mr. Nitze's own assumptions
concerning Soviet attack strategy. He apparently does not assume that the
Soviets would carry out futile attacks against U.S. submarines at sea; why,
then, should one assume that they would "waste" their throw-weight in an equally
futile attempt to destroy dispersed land-mobile missiles? Yet, if they should
not do so, the net Soviet advantage after the assumed attack—Mr. Nitze's main
criterion—would not be greatly affected.

On the other hand, the deployment of land-mobile missiles would substantially
increase the absolute amount of surviving U.S. retaliatory capability. Thus, for
reasons somewhat different from Mr. Nitze's, I agree that a mobile land-based
system, by reducing the vulnerability of U.S. strategic forces to a counterforce
attack, could help to prevent the Soviets from ever attaining strategic
superiority. But even on this basis Mr. Nitze's proposed program would be
extravagant and untested, going beyond the degree of threat that now appears
likely, and far more costly than other measures, at least as feasible, to
improve survivability. Furthermore, it might well set off a new competition in
which the Soviets would have all the advantages. Let me address these points in
order.

systems. They require either very hard moveable capsules to protect the
missiles, or very cheap hard shelters, neither of which has yet been proved

If we continue with the strategic programs I have discussed above, it seems
highly unlikely that the Soviets could mount an effective threat to our

political perceptions in the rest of the world concerning the relative strengths

designed to protect our deterrent capability, and explain carefully why we have
decided to forgo a "throw-weight race" or a "megatonnage race," I see no reason

VI
Two other suggestions have been made for a U.S. response to the Soviet buildup:
further improvements in the accuracy of American missiles, and the development
of new, larger throw-weight, silo-based missiles to replace Minuteman. Both of
these programs are being pursued at an active, though not terribly vigorous,
pace by the Pentagon. Critics charge that they are destabilizing to the
strategic balance, in that they would increase the American threat to the Soviet
retaliatory force, giving the Soviets more incentive to strike first. My
judgment differs. While we have no particular need for greater overall throw-
weight, I see little reason for us to impose upon ourselves a unilateral
restraint on the accuracy of our missiles. First, no one has yet developed a

conceive of a situation in which the Soviets might use such a capability, but
there would be even less reason for the Soviets to attempt such a strike if they
knew the United States had a comparable capability and could respond in kind.

Finally, perhaps the most compelling argument against a unilateral American
effort to limit the accuracy of its missiles is a technical one. As mentioned
earlier, extremely high accuracies are not likely to be obtained using the
missile guidance technology now being pursued, i.e., purely inertial guidance
systems. However, very high accuracies are likely not only to be feasible, but
to be relatively easy to achieve once sophisticated satellite navigation systems
are perfected. If these systems permit the determination of location as
precisely as they promise, it will be possible to obtain accuracies of a few
hundred feet. There are feasible and not terribly expensive ways of ensuring
that guidance systems based on these satellite navigation systems are both
highly survivable and essentially impossible to jam electronically. Since these
systems are being developed for a host of navigation purposes unrelated to
warhead accuracy, they will go forward in any event. Thus, as much as we may
believe we can restrain our own programs for improving accuracy, the basic
technological developments which will eventually lead to high accuracy are sure
to come.

VII
To conclude, the United States has reasonable strategic force alternatives which
can ensure our retaliatory capability, deny the Soviets any potential military
advantage, and yet which do not require a return to a massive civil defense
program or deployment of a costly and perhaps technically infeasible land-based
mobile ICBM system. We need not match the Soviets in missile throw-weight, nor,
within quite broad limits, need we even worry about their advantage. At the same
time, our government must adopt complementary rhetoric, making it clear why we
have chosen not to place great emphasis on throw-weight, lest the growing Soviet
advantage in this one respect create an adverse perception of the strategic
balance throughout the world.

With respect to the Vladivostok SALT Accord, I believe that it should be
completed, even if this means compromising our positions somewhat on the
Backfire and cruise missile issues. The Vladivostok Accord may well have been
disappointing relative to what many of us had expected as a follow-on SALT
agreement: it does not limit the threat to the survival of the land-based
missile forces of either side, nor does it provide for significant reductions in
the size of existing strategic forces. Rather, it will undoubtedly permit a
significant increase in the overall destructive capability of the forces of the
two sides. These facts make it easy to criticize the Vladivostok Accord, since
one can always compare it to the kind of agreement most of us would have
preferred. Furthermore, it is easy to place the blame on the Soviets, ascribing
to them a strong effort to obtain strategic superiority.

What is much more difficult is to work out a viable alternative to the
Vladivostok Accord. Most of the specific suggestions critics have made for
alternative agreements were tried vigorously in one form or another during the
three-year period leading up to the Accord. Low limits on Soviet MRIVs were
unacceptable to the Soviet Union for the obvious reason that this would have
left the United States with a militarily significant superiority in this area.
Limits on throw-weight were unacceptable because such limits would have
effectively prohibited significant MRIV deployments, given existing Soviet
technology. Significant reductions were not of interest to either side, perhaps
more than any other reason because neither the Soviet leadership nor the U.S.
Administration was willing to undertake the internal bureaucratic struggles
necessary to decide which component would have to reduce. Finally, the Soviets
even today remain quite concerned about U.S. forward-based systems, as we remain

Given this background, I believe the only serious alternative to completing the
Vladivostok Accord is to abandon the SALT process for the time being, at least
with respect to offensive forces, hoping that the two sides will trust each
other enough to keep their force deployments under control by mutual example,
without the benefit of formal agreements.

Such a hope is likely to be disappointed. With no agreements, the probability is
that the Soviets will continue to increase their strategic spending, leading to
ever-widening disparities in force size. While the thrust of this article has
been that many of these disparities are much less meaningful than they are made
out to be, at some point the disparities could become large enough to have
political, and perhaps even military, consequences. We would then have to
increase our force size to keep up, probably beyond the ceilings contemplated at
Vladivostok, certainly beyond our present programs.

Some might argue that we need not match the Soviet buildup even roughly. But
unless we did so we could hardly expect the Soviets, in renewed negotiations, to
accept even Vladivostok-level limits, since they would then be at a far higher
level and probably at a comparative advantage at least in numbers.

In short, abandonment of the SALT process, even for a time, would mean
unnecessary extension of an arms competition that has gone on too long. Such an
extension could only increase Soviet-American tensions and, in my judgment,
decrease rather than improve our security.
