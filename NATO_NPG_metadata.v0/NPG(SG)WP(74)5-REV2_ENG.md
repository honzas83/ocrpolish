> # Study on Warsaw Pact Politico-Military Strategy and Military Doctrine for the Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons
> 
> This document is a draft report by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) regarding a study on the Warsaw Pact's politico-military strategy and military doctrine for the tactical use of nuclear weapons. The report emphasizes the importance of understanding the Warsaw Pact's potential responses to NATO's tactical nuclear use to avoid uncontrolled escalation. It notes that a study team composed of representatives from Canada, Denmark, the Federal Republic of Germany, the Netherlands, the Netherlands, the United Kingdom, and the United States was developed under the leadership of the Netherlands. The report suggests that the study be submitted to the Defence Planning Committee (DPC) for further consideration. It highlights that the the Warsaw Pact does not subscribe to a formal strategy of graduated nuclear response, increasing the risk of abrupt escalation in the event of NATO nuclear use. The document also acknowledges that the Warsaw Pact possesses the capability to execute a wide range of nuclear options on a controlled and selective basis, though they lack the same range of delivery systems and yields as NATO. The report concludes that the conclusions of the WP Study do not call into question the validity of current NATO strategy and basic policy guidance.

NUCLEAR PLANNING GROUP
STUDY ON WARSAW PACT POLITICO-MILITARY STRATEGY
AND MILITARY DOCTRINE FOR THE TACTICAL USE OF NUCLEAR WEAPONS
Draft Report by the Permanent Representatives
During their consideration of the ACLANT Follow-on Use Study(1) at their meeting
in Ankara in May, 1973, the Ministers noted that political authorities of the
Alliance, in consulting on a selective release request, would have to give very
serious consideration to the Warsaw Pact (WP) response that such use might
provoke and to the risk of escalating the conflict that such use entails. In
this context, they also noted the suggestion(2) in the ACLANT Study that some
insight on this question might be gained in peacetime by assessing the actual
capabilities that the WP possesses and by seeking as clear a knowledge and
understanding as possible of current WP politico-military strategy and military
doctrine which is likely to govern WP tactical use of nuclear weapons. The
Ministers therefore invited(3) the Permanent Representatives to arrange for a
study on this subject to be prepared for their consideration at a future
meeting.

2. The study(1) was developed by an ad hoc Study Team composed of participants
from Canada, Denmark, the Federal Republic of Germany, the Netherlands, the
United Kingdom and the United States, under the leadership of the Netherlands.
The Study Team was assisted by members of the International Staff, International
Military Staff and SHAPE. Participation by national or NATO representatives does
not imply that the WP Study necessarily reflects the views of the countries or
staffs concerned.

3. Since the primary purpose of the study effort was to expand the basis for
NATO's political decision-making in actual conflict situations, the approach
taken in the WP Study is necessarily different, in terms of objective, point of
departure and rules of analysis, from other approaches used within the Alliance.
Therefore, the WP Study goes beyond the assessments of WP strength and
capabilities reflected in the NATO agreed military intelligence base. We believe
the WP Study is an important and useful contribution toward the objective of
reaching a deeper understanding of WP politico-military strategy and military
doctrine for the tactical use of nuclear weapons. In our opinion, it should be
given a larger distribution within NATO. We suggest that this be accomplished by
submitting it to the Defence Planning Committee(DPC). At Annex is a draft NPG
report to the DPC, containing some comments on the WP Study, which is intended
to be used for this purpose.

A study developed under NPG auspices to contribute toward meeting this need is
attached. Our comments in the following paragraphs should be read in conjunction

III. COMMENTS
General Evaluation
3. - In developing its analysis, the WP Study drew(1) heavily on the assessment
of WP military capabilities and the summary of the strategic military
considerations affecting Soviet decision-making with regard to use of military
power contained in the 1973 agreed NATO military intelligence base(2).
Nevertheless, in seeking to reach a deeper understanding of WP politico-military
strategy, the WP Study attempted to go beyond the agreed intelligence assessment
to consider the political attitudes, the motivations and the possible
perceptions of NATO intent which might influence Soviet and WP conduct and
actions in an actual crisis, and in particular the possible impact these
unquantifiable factors might have on WP tactical use of nuclear weapons(3).
While fully recognising the need and value of the NATO agreed military
intelligence base, we agree with the WP Study that an examination of these
unquantifiable factors is desirable, since it expands the basis for NATO
decision-making.

4. We note the WP Study conclusion that it is not possible to be dogmatic about
the course of action the WP might take in an actual conflict, particularly with
regard to tactical use of nuclear weapons, and we agree that NATO decisions in

WP Tactical Nuclear Options
5. As mentioned earlier, the WP Study drew upon the 1973
NATO agreed military intelligence assessment which indicated, among
other things, that the WP might resort to pre-emptive tactical use
of nuclear weapons, or to wide-scale retaliation in the event of NATO
tactical use, since the WP does not subscribe to a formal strategy
of graduated nuclear response(2). In this context, we note that the
1974 version now states that Soviet military doctrine apparently does
not subscribe to a strategy of graduated response. If NATO were to
make use of nuclear weapons, even in a tactical situation, the risk
of an abrupt escalation in scale, nature of target, area or any
combination of these would be great, though it is believed that the
Soviets would at least initially limit their response to the theatre

7. The WP Study points out that there are probably two distinct (and sometimes
conflicting) influences that will have a critical impact on WP politico-military
strategy and on WP decisions during an actual conflict: the aggressive stance
compatible with Communist revolutionary aims, and the defensive attitude against
a perceived Western threat. With regard to the latter, the WP Study notes that
the Soviets might regard NATO's deterrent strategy as an intimidating threat to
their politico-military system, just as the NATO view of the WP concentrates on
the threat it implies for Western security(1). This, in our view, highlights the
need for NATO to ensure that its own policy and actions avoid possibilities of
misinterpretation.

the image its "deterrent posture" portrays to the WP, through such elements as
the strategy, policy and guidance made known to the public or to the

Implications for Conflict Termination
9. The WP Study observes that the most satisfactory WP response (in NATO's eyes)
to NATO initial use, and one which fits with an assumption of caution on the
part of the Soviet leadership, would be a fulfilment of NATO's deterrent
strategy by a Soviet decision to halt the aggression(1). We note, in this
context, that the Provisional Political Guidelines for the Initial Defensive
Tactical Use of Nuclear Weapons have stated that the fundamental objective of
the tactical use of nuclear weapons and the threat of further escalation is to
induce the enemy to make the political decision to cease his attack and to
withdraw(2). NATO's threat of further escalation in this case would have to be
such that deterrence during any conflict would not only have to work against any
intensification or continuation of enemy action but would have to operate also
in a way which contributes, in addition to other political and military steps,
to the final enemy withdrawal from NATO territory.

Concluding Remarks
10. We believe the analysis in the WP Study and the comments in this report may
be of interest to the DFC and of potential value to other NATO bodies and
agencies in the conduct of their planning and activities. The WP Study has
highlighted the fact that it is not possible for there to be any dogmatic and
specific predictions in this field, not only because there are limitations to
our sources of information, but also because the factors affecting WP policies
may change. A meaningful understanding of WP politico-military strategy and
military doctrine, particularly with respect to tactical use of nuclear weapons,
should therefore be enhanced by continuing efforts to assemble and analyse
current and authoritative information.

11. In the light of all the evidence available, the WP Study's main conclusion
is that NATO decisions in the event of hostilities with the Warsaw Pact should
not be based solely on a single assumption of the manner in which the WP might
respond. We endorse this conclusion and suggest that the subject of the Study
merits continuing attention in view of its relevance to NATO's peacetime
deterrent posture and to possible NATO courses of action in time of tension or
conflict.
