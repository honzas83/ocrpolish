> # Conceptual Analysis of Nuclear Aspects of Deterrence
> 
> This document is a working paper and draft report by the Permanent Representatives of the Nuclear Planning Group (NPG) regarding the conceptual analysis of nuclear aspects of deterrence. It originates from a request by NPG Ministers in May 1973 to consider the value of such an analysis, following discussions on the implications of the non-use of nuclear weapons and the potential impairment of the NATO deterrent. The paper argues that while deterrence is a complex subject involving both concrete factors like force structure and abstract factors like aggressor perception, a one-time conceptual analysis would be unlikely to provide an enduring answer. Furthermore, it notes that deterrence is already being addressed through other NPG projects, such as the work of the Phase II Study Team on the Follow-on Use Work Programme. Due to the limited resources and scale of effort available for NPG work, the document concludes that initiating a separate, dedicated conceptual analysis of the nuclear aspects of deterrence is not warranted at this time.

I. PURPOSE
This report discusses the question of whether a conceptual analysis of the
nuclear aspects of deterrence should be initiated by the Nuclear Planning Group
(NPG).

II. BACKGROUND
2. At their meeting in Ankara on 15th and 16th May, 1973, during consideration
of an illustrative study dealing with demonstrative use, the NPG Ministers
discussed, inter alia, the implications of non-use of nuclear weapons. Noting
that the credibility of the deterrent could be seriously impaired if NATO failed
to use nuclear weapons in its defence when such use would be militarily
unavoidable, the Ministers invited(1) the Permanent Representatives to consider
the value of a conceptual analysis of the nuclear aspects of deterrence for
Ministerial consideration at a future meeting.

III. DISCUSSION
3. Deterrence is a complex subject, an understanding of which must take into
account not only such concrete factors as NATO force structure, deployment,
readiness and declaratory policy but also equally significant, highly abstract
and often intangible factors such as a potential aggressor's perception of the
NATO deterrent posture and his risk assessment. Because of the fundamental
relationship of deterrence to Alliance strategy, it is nevertheless important to
develop as clear an understanding as possible of the general workings of
deterrence, with particular emphasis on the nuclear aspects insofar as the NPG
is concerned. This is a difficult and challenging task to which the Alliance as
a whole and the NPG in particular have devoted considerable attention in the
past. It also promises to be a continuing task because understanding will need
to be adjusted as conditions change and as new insight is gained. In other
words, a one-time conceptual analysis is unlikely to provide an enduring answer
to the need.

4. Moreover, deterrence is not only an important subject in its own right, but
it impinges upon, and is reflected in, many other aspects of NPG work. This is
illustrated by the annexed Working Paper on "Aspects of Deterrence" that was
prepared by the ad hoc Study Team currently developing a report on Phase II of
the Follow-on Use Work Programme. Written by the Phase II Study Team to provide
a general background to other papers prepared in the course of its work, the
Working Paper was made available by the

Study Team in the belief that it might be of some value in resolving the
question of whether to undertake a conceptual analysis of deterrence. In doing
so, the Study Team pointed out that its Working Paper does not attempt to
provide a comprehensive treatment of deterrence, since its primary purpose was
to identify some of the important factors which the NPG will need to take into
account when considering the problems of follow-on use.

5. In spite of its specific orientation, the Working Paper makes a very useful
contribution toward understanding the general workings of deterrence. It also
provides a good example of the way in which consideration of deterrence aspects
in the course of other NPG work can strengthen our understanding of the subject.
Most of the projects now underway or under consideration in the NPG offer the
potential to add in one way or another to our knowledge of deterrence.

6. A practical aspect that must also be considered in this context is the
overall limitation on the resources and scale of effort that can be devoted to
NPG work. Viewed from this standpoint, a conceptual analysis of the nuclear
aspects of deterrence would seem to have less of a claim on the limited
resources than other projects of interest to the NPG.

IV. CONCLUSION
7. In view of the need to pay continuing attention to sustaining and refining
our understanding of deterrence, the likelihood that a good part of the current
and prospective effort of the NPG in other fields could be exploited to meet
that need, and the requirement to husband the resources that are available for
NPG work, it is concluded that the initiation of a separate conceptual analysis
of the nuclear aspects of deterrence is not warranted at this time.
